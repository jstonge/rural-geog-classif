## Economic Development, Racialization, and Privilege: 'Yes in My Backyard' Prison Politics and the Reinvention of Madras, Oregon

## Anne Bonds

Department of Geography, University of Wisconsin-Milwaukee

This article draws from geographic engagements with theories of racialization and NIMBYism to explore connections between economic development and the relational construction of racial identities. I investigate the discourses of local white leadership surrounding two interconnected economic agendas crafted with the goal of remaking the central Oregon town of Madras into an upscale, white community, including (1) entrepreneurial prison development, and (2) an urban renewal project emphasizing high-income residential construction and the removal of 'blighted' housing. Community leaders framed these developments as essential to changing perceptions of Madras based on its racial makeup and entrenched poverty. White officials promoted prison recruitment and upscale housing development through a normative racial framework that reaffirmed the privileged status of whites, stigmatized Latinos and Native Americans, and (re)produced unequal spaces. Through this empirical focus, I call attention to the centrality of race in economic practices, emphasizing how racialized privilege and marginalization are reproduced through development agendas that give shape to geographies of opportunity and (dis)advantage. Key Words: economic development, housing, NIMBY-ism, prison, race.

C ity boosters in Madras, Oregon are proud to boast that the newly constructed correctional institution in their community is a 'city unto itself.' The expansive institution is nestled cleverly in a valley off to the side of the road, as prisons often are, just such that you might not see it if you didn't know where to look. Once spotted, however, this imagery powerfully resonates. Located on 453 acres just five miles east of downtown, the facility is a men's prison that includes both minimum-security and medium-security facilities.

Among the high desert landscape of scrubby juniper trees and sagebrush stand stadium lights and fences of barbed wire, and the white 'pods' that contain prisoners stretch along the horizon. Strangely enough, just down the road from the prison, land has been cleared for a much different sort of development. A master-planned community, complete with a celebrity-designed golf course, is in the early stages of development and a few recently built homes dot the landscape. It is deeply paradoxical that these two communities sit adjacent to one another: one, a large, state correctional institution that has the capacity to (ware)house more than 1,800 people; the other, an upscale, new urbanist housing development designed to eventually include 1,500 to 1,700 single-family homes.

Adding to the seeming contradiction, these projects are profoundly interconnected. These intertwined developments were conceptualized by area leaders as solutions to a stagnating local economy characterized by entrenched poverty and persistently high unemployment. In fact, the housing development and the prison were something of a package deal. Singing an all-too-common tune about corrections and economic development, officials anticipated that the prison would bring in new workers and put pressure on a tight housing market. Thus, a new luxury housing development, made possible by the forced relocation and incapacitation of hundreds, was necessary 'to inspire workers to live in the city' (personal interview 2006). This was despite the long-standing claims of local social service providers who assert that affordable housing is perhaps the most pressing issue for the community's poor. This need for low-income housing has persisted, first, as regional amenities-based growth and real estate speculation fueled escalating housing prices in central Oregon through 2007, and then after, when the housing recession beleaguered the area's builders and timber market.

In this article, I build from research on racialization and NIMBYism to argue that yes in my backyard (YIMBY) prison development initiatives are galvanized to maintain geographies of racialized privilege. Drawing from the YIMBYism underscoring local efforts to remake Madras, I demonstrate the inextricable connections among racialization, privilege, and economic development. I argue that the specific articulation of race and class in Madras gives rise to YIMBY prison development to illustrate that, like NIMBYism, YIMBYism is a particular form of racism. Through this lens, my analysis interrogates how redevelopment agendas flow through and legitimate local geographies of racialized poverty, inequality, and dispossession. I describe how the con- stellation of redevelopment strategies animated by entrepreneurial prison development in Madras simultaneously institutionalize and normalize carceral expansion at the same time that they sustain local race and class positions. My analysis underscores the ways in which local economic development is produced through and productive of relations of race, status, and privilege.

To focus on any aspect of mass incarceration requires an acknowledgment that prisons represent a specific iteration of racism. Whether viewing mass incarceration as a contemporary social construct emerging from a long history of violent, racist state structures that have spatially bound and dispossessed African American communities (Wacquant 2001; Davis 2003; Alexander 2010) or as a labor market institution that artificially depresses unemployment rates and systematically warehouses the most socially and economically marginalized (Western and Beckett 1999; Peck and Theodore 2008), the racialized implications of carceral expansion are profound. A focus on prison-led development requires an understanding of the ways in which this process is entangled within the truly remarkable, and specifically racialized, growth in incarceration. Rural prison development efforts therefore clearly reflect and reproduce 'a particular racist formation' (Pulido 2000, 12).

My analysis focuses on the community of Madras, centrally located in Jefferson County, Oregon. With a population of 9,776, 1 Madras is the largest city in the county and operates as the retail center for surrounding agricultural areas and smaller communities. Situated in one of the poorest and most diverse counties in the state, the city has a distinct racial makeup that is 64 percent white, 36 percent Latino, and 6 percent Native American (U.S. Census Bureau 2010). The fact that these percentages do not add up is, in and of itself, a reflection of the socially constructed nature of racial categories and the politics of counting racial groups. The census counts Hispanics as an ethnic category, rather than a racial group. Consequently, individuals may identify as Hispanic and also as members of another racial category. In what follows, I situate my analysis in relation to research on geographies of incarceration. I then discuss geographic engagements with theories of racialization, focusing especially on the ways in which race and privilege have been theorized in relation to NIMBYism and economic processes, emphasizing Wilson's (2009) important call for increased scholarly research on the intersections of political economy and processes of racialization. I then turn to my empirical focus on the prison siting and proposed

upscale housing development in Madras, Oregon, introduced in the beginning of the article. Through the example of YIMBY prison development policies, I call attention to the centrality of race-class formations in economic practices, emphasizing how racialized privilege and marginalization are reproduced through policies, institutions, and markets that give shape to geographies of opportunity and (dis)advantage.

## Producing Race and Carceral Redevelopment

The dynamics of incarceration have remained underexplored in geography until relatively recently. This new research examines state power and incarceration (Martin and Mitchelson 2009; Martin 2012), the space of the prison and the ways in which incarceration is experienced (van Hoven and Sibley 2008; Moran 2011), as well as prison siting dynamics (Che 2005; Gilmore 2007; Glasmeier and Farrigan 2007; Bonds 2009). Carceral geographies have also been interrogated in relation to punitive neoliberal policies that have eroded social entitlements while simultaneously rolled-out probusiness agendas have further funneled investments away from the poor (Peck 2003; Gilmore 2007; Bonds 2009). This scholarship calls attention to the spatiality of incarceration to demonstrate how the prison is necessarily insular and outwardly dependent, requiring walls to demarcate and separate at the same time that it necessitates connections to places and peoples to perform its purpose.

Rural prison growth in the United States is facilitated by processes of racialized dispossession that channel mostly urban and mostly non-white inmates into remote nonmetropolitan correctional facilities. Since the 1990s, depressed rural communities have identified prisons and other correctional facilities-immigrant detention centers, federal detention centers-as a growth industry (Che 2005; Gilmore 2007; Glasmeier and Farrigan 2007; Bonds 2009). As is well documented in the literature, from the rural development perspective, prisons are an inevitable, if unfortunate, component of social life, and the racial politics and meanings of mass incarceration generally remain unproblematized by those recruiting prisons (Bonds 2008).

The taken-for-granted racial project of rural prison expansion connects to geographic research on racism and racialization that theorizes the ways in which racial formations are actively (re)produced through shifting ideologies of difference and material processes embedded within a range of social and spatial practices, institutional contexts, histories, and everyday activities (Omi and Winant 1994; Pulido 2000, 2006; Gilmore 2002; Barraclough 2009, 2011). By emphasizing the spatial contingency of the social construction of race, geographers bring a distinct focus to the grounded situations in which race is constructed and contested and accentuate the importance of geographic contexts in the formation and operation of race and racisms. This scholarship challenges misconceptions about racism, particularly those that equate racism singularly to hostile acts of prejudice or racially motivated violence, to emphasize the ways in which racism, as a system of power operating through a range of institutions, ideologies, and practices, is reproduced through everyday actions that might or might not be intentional (Pulido 2000). For example, although prison recruitment campaigns are generally articulated through economistic claims rather than explicitly racialized language, as is the case in Madras, this development strategy cannot be uncoupled from systems of power and the racism of mass incarceration, operationalized through a vast nexus of norms, policies, laws, and institutions that have stigmatized and marginalized of people of color (Wacquant 2001; Davis 2003; Gilmore 2007; Alexander 2010).

Of particular significance in examinations of prisonled redevelopment strategies are critiques of white privilege that highlight the sociospatial construction of whiteness as a racial category and the normative geographies associated with white identities (Bonnett 1997; Pulido 2000; Hubbard 2005). These investigations underscore the role of white identities in place-making and the ways in which whiteness is constructed and reproduced both through everyday practices and through racist actions-intended or otherwise-in defense of exclusionary white spaces (Bonnett 1997; Pulido 2000; Hubbard 2005). Like all racial identities, whiteness is historically and relationally constructed, forged through cultural practices and ideological norms, although the transparency of whiteness as a racial category means that white identities are often naturalized and normalized. That is, just as racisms are produced through deliberate acts as well as unintentional, mundane, ordinary activities, so, too, are privileges.

Indeed, all places are racialized, not just 'the inner city,' 'the border,' and 'the reservation' (Delaney 2002, 6). Racial geographies in the United States are the outcome of white supremacy, slavery, exploitation, genocide, and the conscious design of immigration and naturalization laws (Haney-Lopez 1996). The legacies of these institutions are embedded within all

contemporary landscapes, even those ostensibly free of racial conflict (Schein 2006). Seemingly 'white places'-in this case, a rural Western community-were made so through legally sanctioned mechanisms of racial exclusion, including indigenous colonization and reservation containment, housing covenants and mortgage lending practices, zoning and land use planning, employment discrimination, labor exploitation, and violence and bigotry (Delaney 2002; Barraclough 2009, 2011). The geography of the American West was established through imperialism and white supremacy, and the material processes and racial hierarchies established through this history are reflected in contemporary racial politics and economic inequalities. These regional race-class hierarchies intertwine with local economic conditions and social dynamics to produce particularly spatialized race relations that cast and position groups differently (Pulido 2006; Lawson, Jarosz, and Bonds 2010; Lai 2012). The specific racial geometry of Madras, Oregon, discussed in more detail in the subsequent text, is rooted in the white supremacy enshrined in land use practices that dispossessed indigenous peoples and redistributed lands to white settlers and the dynamics of capital accumulation fundamentally tied to the exploitation of Latino farm workers. Contemporary agendas to remake Madras are imagined through and embedded within this history.

Pulido (2000) and Hubbard (2005, 52), among others, have interrogated the ways in which the politics of NIMBYism-'not in my backyard' campaigns marshaled to prevent developments identified as dangerous, unhealthy, or undesirable-have been mobilized to defend white privilege and white spaces. The racialization of NIMBYism involves 'the series of structures, institutional practices-which may or may not be intentionally racist-but that serve to maintain the privileged status of white places' (Hubbard 2005, 52). I contribute to these discussions by demonstrating instead how 'yes in my backyard' prison development politics are galvanized to maintain geographies of racialized privilege. Indeed, I argue that the particular articulation of race and class in Madras gave rise to YIMBY prison development there. Rather than opposing the siting of a state prison on the basis of protecting exclusionary white space, city leaders in Madras actively pursued a correctional facility with the seemingly contradictory goal of remaking the town into a white upscale recreation and retirement community, an idea that rests on the taken-for-granted logics of incarceration. In other words, local 'redevelopment imagineers' (Lai 2012, 154) entrepreneurially recruited a prison in a racialized and classed effort to remake the city. This strangely incongruous plan depends on and reinforces the racism of mass incarceration and simultaneously seeks to attract new wealthy, white in-migrants to the town.

Recently, Wilson (2009) has advocated for increased research and more nuanced analyses of the reciprocal relations between race and political economy in geography that takes seriously the ways in which conceptions of race flow through economic practices, processes, and political institutions. Such research, what he terms 'racial economy perspectives,' should work to uncover the ways in which racialized poverty and marginalization are reproduced through institutions and markets-land markets, housing markets, commodity markets, service markets-that 'structure how urban space and people are used and regulated through economic processes' (Wilson 2009, 140). Following Wilson (2009), Pulido (2006), Barraclough (2009, 2011), and Lawson, Jarosz, and Bonds (2008, 2010), I focus on the ways in which social difference is connected to political economic processes, emphasizing the linkages between material historical processes and contemporary geographies of racism and privilege that have systematically dispossessed and disadvantaged communities of color in Madras.

I contribute to conversations in geography on political economy and processes of racialization by focusing on rural spaces in what has been a decidedly urbanfocused discussion (but see Lawson, Jarosz, and Bonds 2008, 2010; Barraclough 2009, 2011; Bonds 2009). An emphasis on the rural contributes to discussions about the racialization of space in several significant ways. It complicates normative assumptions about whiteness and rurality by marking rural spaces as complexly racialized spaces, emphasizing the sociospatial construction of racial identities and highlighting the specific processes reproducing these specifically racialized rural geographies (Winders 2003; Barraclough 2009, 2011; Leitner 2012; Lawson, Jarosz, and Bonds 2010). Rural prison-hosting communities are often, perhaps inadvertently, portrayed as predominantly white communities pandering to lock up people of color for economic gain. Although the racism and class privilege involved in the process of prison recruitment should be accentuated, it is critical to situate and scale such discourses to reveal the complexity of prison-hosting communities and the people that inhabit them. Moreover, an explicit focus on the YIMBY politics of rural prison sitings emphasizes the rural-urban connections between criminalization and the carceral expropriation of poor

urban communities of color and the entrepreneurial recruitment of correctional facilities.

Additionally, I respond to Wilson's (2009) call by bringing racial economy perspectives in geography in conversation with Kim's (1999) concept of racial triangulation as a means to theorize the mutually constitutive nature of economic and racialization processes. Kim (1999) established the notion of a 'racial geometry'-or a 'field of racial positions'-to theorize relational racialization (Barraclough 2011), or the differential racialization (Pulido 2006, 23) of particular communities (see also Lai 2012). Racial geometries are constituted in a given time and place through economic processes, public discourses, long-standing stereotypes, and representations of racial groups and their relative status. The chief 'architects' of racial geometries are major opinion-makers and gatekeepers-white elected officials, journalists, community leaders, business elites-although these topographies are also contested, reworked, and reinforced through mundane actions and everyday life (Kim 1999, 107). Through this process, groups are racialized in relation to one another materially and discursively in ways that confer power and insider status on some groups while marking others as inferior, and outsider: 'As a normative blueprint for who gets what, this field of racial positions profoundly shapes opportunities, constraints, and possibilities with which subordinate groups must contend, ultimately serving to reinforce White dominance and privilege' (Kim 1999, 107).

I investigate the discourses surrounding two interconnected economic strategies that have been the center of the community leadership's strategy to remake Madras, including (1) entrepreneurial prison development, which I define as the competitive efforts of community leaders to recruit a prison since 1995, and (2) an urban renewal project emphasizing the creation of an upscale housing development. City leaders have promoted these agendas as essential to changing 'perceptions' of the community based on its racial makeup and entrenched poverty. I interrogate how these discourses narrate local racial identities to analyze the reproduction of local geographies of exclusion and difference. I contend that community development and economic growth policies flow through and rework long-standing racial tropes about Latino immigrants and Native Americans in ways that reproduce local racial hierarchies.

The data presented here were gathered between 2005 and 2010. My in-depth case study approach combines archival research with interviews and participant observation to elicit linkages among local political economy, community development agendas, and race-class relations. Interviewees were drawn from a sample of city leadership, including elected officials, business leaders, social service providers, and community boosters, to examine the narratives produced by central actors involved in the creation of economic development policy. This focus allows me to emphasize the discourses of the locally powerful, whose messages and understandings of local development and race-class relations have a very real impact on the recursive material and discursive positioning of groups. I bring these interviews together with city planning documents and newspaper commentaries to examine how community development agendas connect-explicitly or inadvertently-to local racial politics. Although my examination concentrates on the representational tactics of influential officials-the 'architects' of racial geometries-racialized hierarchies are continuously negotiated across racial and class positionings (Kim 1999, 107). Comments about local renewal efforts were not always expressed through explicitly racialized language, but my analysis also focuses on histories that have systematized particular racial relations as well as silences and omissions to argue that visions of redevelopment are predicated on long-established power relations and assumptions that negatively position Latinos and Native Americans while reaffirming the social and economic status of whites (Kim 1999; Pulido 2006; Barraclough 2011; Lai 2012). I focus on economic development discourse to highlight how local agendas create a normative framework for 'who gets what,' which in turn shapes the 'opportunities, constraints, and possibilities' (Kim 1999, 107).

## Race, Restructuring, and Crisis in Central Oregon

Positioned in close proximity to outdoor amenities and other booming recreational communities, Madras is located on the edge of gentrifying central Oregon, where in-migration and tourism have fueled rapid increases in land prices and real estate speculation (personal interview 2006). Agriculture and manufacturing, specifically wood processing, continue to make up an important part of the local economic base in Jefferson County, and key crops include wheat, garlic, potatoes, mint, alfalfa, and vegetable and flower seeds (City of Madras Urban Revitalization Action Plan [CMURAP] 2007). Madras's industrial base, however, is encounter-

ing challenges as local crops and seed production face increased competition and traditional manufacturing industries withdraw their local investments. More recently, city and county leaders have aimed to develop Madras into the sort of recreational tourist destination that surrounding communities like Bend, Redmond, and Hood River have become over the past decade. This economic development goal seems to contradict Madras's prison recruitment agenda but, in fact, the correctional facility and local agendas to remake Madras into a recreation destination go together hand in glove.

With nearby communities like Bend leading the way, central Oregon witnessed the spectacular highs of the housing boom as well as extraordinary declines precipitated by the subsequent crisis. Bend, in particular, has experienced rapid gentrification driven by unprecedented levels of investment and growth. Between 2001 and 2005, the median home value in Bend increased by an astounding 80 percent (Brookes 2009) and leading up to the height of speculative development in the area, local studies found that 92 percent of the available housing was priced above what was considered affordable for households in the region (Fisher 2006b). As housing prices escalated, regional economists and social service providers repeatedly highlighted the pressing need for affordable worker housing for those employed in the area, as opposed to the continued focus by area builders, developers, and community leaders on retiree and vacation housing (personal interview 2006; Fisher 2006a). When the housing market slumped nationally, the trend reverberated through central Oregon.

Although Madras, located just forty-five miles from Bend, experienced only marginal economic growth in the wake of Bend's speculative real estate boom, the community did encounter rising housing costs and an affordable housing crisis of its own as it became one of the few viable places in the area for low-income people to live. A social service provider in Madras explained that Madras was becoming 'a bedroom community for Bend,' arguing that 'affordable housing is going to be key' and that 'it's getting harder and harder for people to afford it' (personal interview 2006). Despite the need for moderately priced housing, local leadership in Madras hoped to benefit from housing speculation in Bend by promoting a high-end housing development to attract tourists and wealthy retirees to the community. In fact, city leaders in Madras tied their visions of highend housing development to the prison, anticipating future gains following the Bend model of growth.

In addition to being bypassed by the booms experienced by surrounding gentrifying central Oregon communities like Bend, Jefferson County and the city of Madras are distinct in several other important ways. Significantly, the racial geographies of the towns are strikingly different. Jefferson County is the most racially diverse county in Oregon, with a population that is 80 percent white, 19 percent Latino, and 17 percent Native American (U.S. Census Bureau 2010). Once part of the territory of Tenino Indians, the county's racial makeup is a legacy of an array of racial exclusion laws in Oregon and the imperial history of the West, dating back to the 1855 authorization to clear the indigenous peoples from the Oregon Territory. After being forced to relinquish nearly 10 million acres of land, the Warms Springs and Wasco peoples were confined to the Warms Springs Reservation established in Jefferson County in the Treaty of 1855, a process that fundamentally altered indigenous livelihoods, dramatically limited territorial and fishing access, and commenced the imposition of federal assimilation programs (The Confederated Tribes of Warms Springs 2001).

The reservation land occupied by The Confederated Tribes of Warms Springs is primarily located in Jefferson County, and the tribal community and the town of Madras are closely connected, in terms of proximity, but also politically, economically, and culturally, as a growing portion of the tribe lives or works in Madras. Indigenous colonization in Oregon systematically denied Native Americans basic social, cultural, and economic freedoms, and the deep wounds resulting from this violent historical geography remain significant today. One in four tribal members on the reservation are out of work, with one in three children living in poverty and unemployment rates exceeding 50 percent, particularly in the winter when seasonal work opportunities sharply decline (Dash 2007). Moreover, the ongoing consequences are reflected in racial attitudes between groups and the racism and reservation stereotypes that permeate local discourses.

The significant Latino population in Madras is tied, in part, to the importance of agriculture in central Oregon and the historical dependence of area growers on migrant and seasonal farm workers. The number of Latino inhabitants in the area has fluctuated through various periods of labor recruitment, repatriation, and deportation, with a significant population having settled in Madras and Jefferson County. Streams of migrant labor to the area intensified from 1910 to 1925 when Mexican workers were first contracted to work in sugar beet production and then again between 1942 and 1947 when more than 47,000 workers were brought into the state through the Bracero

Program to work in agriculture and on the railroads (Gamboa 1987; Nelson 2008; Stephen and Mendoza 2008).

From the 1940s onward, Mexican and Mexican American farm workers became an essential component of rural agribusiness across the Pacific Northwest, moving through Oregon, Washington, and Idaho to work different harvests. In spite of their centrality and regular movement through the region, farmworkers were socially and economically marginalized and spatially isolated, often contained in camps located on the farms where they labored (Nelson 2008, 46). During the past four decades, the number of Latinos in Oregon has increased and Latinos are now the second largest population group in the state (at 9.9 percent), with population growth particularly high in rural areas with concentrated agricultural activity like the Willamette Valley and central Oregon (Stephen and Mendoza 2008). Jefferson County is well known for its seed production and the acreage devoted to vegetable and flower seed continues to expand (Oregon Child Development Coalition [OCDC] 2006). Central to the racial geography of the county is the fact that, unlike the production of other agricultural commodities, mechanization in seed production does not diminish the need for labor (OCDC 2006).

Ahost of other political, economic, and social transformations have contributed to the marked growth of Latinos in Madras and in Jefferson County since the 1980s. First, the decline in employment opportunities in traditional immigrant gateways has given rise to the expansion of Latino immigration in a wider range of locales across the United States (Zu˜ niga and Hern´ andezLe´ on 2006). Robust amenities-based growth across the Pacific Northwest throughout the 1990s and 2000s contributed to a significant increase in Latinos in Oregon employed in low-wage services connected to tourism (Lawson, Jarosz, and Bonds 2008; Stephen, Mendoza, and Maga˜ na 2008). Indeed, rural gentrification has fueled Latino immigration (Nelson and Nelson 2011). Moreover, the permanent settlement of Latinos in Oregon is also connected to the political geographies created by the passage of the 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA), which, among other provisions, granted legal residency to primarily male seasonal agricultural workers. This facilitated the immigration of the immediate family members of individuals granted permanent residence through the IRCA, and many of these families permanently settled in rural communities throughout Oregon (Stephen, Mendoza, and Maga˜ na 2008). In addition, the establishment of social, cultural, and political spaces for Latinos by earlier generations of migrants encouraged new immigrant families to settle in these areas (Stephen, Mendoza, and Maga˜ na 2008). In Madras, the increase in Latinos is particularly pronounced, with the city's Latino population more than doubling between 1990 and 2000. Indeed, this growth has played a key role in racialized anxieties expressed by the white local leadership.

Oregon's racial mythology is tied to the imagery of manifest destiny and rugged white pioneers taming the land (Taylor 2007, v). This rendering is fundamentally racist and incomplete, denying the presence of indigenous peoples and the rich histories of other racial and ethnic groups in the state. Nonetheless, the history of white settlement in Madras is importantly tied to the racial project of homesteading. 2 Initial homestead claims were few and far between because the arid, craggy landscape made agriculture incredibly difficult. The building of railroads that connected rural towns across the state-made possible by the labor of Mexican, Japanese, and Chinese immigrants-and, later, new irrigation techniques, however, supported the developmentof central Oregon and white homesteaders flocked to the high desert and took up residence on 'conquered territories' (Barraclough 2011, 235) offered by the government 3 (Tonsfeldt and Claeyssens 2004).

Contemporary white spatial privilege was established through this settlement history, forged through racist policies at a variety of scales that facilitated land appropriation and labor exploitation (Xing et al. 2007). Whites took ownership of the area's most productive land and built their local infrastructure and economy with Japanese, Chinese, and Mexican immigrant labor (Xing 2007). These efforts were bolstered early on by racial exclusion laws passed in 1849 and 1854 that prevented African Americans from living in Oregon Territory and in the state's first constitution, in 1859, which further codified this restriction, making it the only free state in the Union with a black exclusion clause (Xing 2007). Chinese immigrants moving to the state to work in mining, fishing, and the railroads were prevented from owning land or holding a mining claim when statehood was declared in 1859. The passage of the Chinese Exclusion Act in 1882 contributed to an increase in Japanese immigrants to Oregon, who later also faced land ownership restrictions (Xing 2007; Wilson 2011). Additionally, the state's 1901 antimiscegenation statute nullified and criminalized marriage between whites and people of color, making the offense punishable by imprisonment. After decades of white hegemony in land ownership, town planning,

and leadership, it is perhaps not surprising that elite, culturally and discursively powerful local positions continue to be dominated by white individuals. 4

In addition to its racial diversity, Jefferson County, and Madras in particular, has long been known as one of the most impoverished areas in the state. Census estimates identify county and city poverty rates far exceeding state and national trends. Between 1990 and 2000, poverty rates in Jefferson County increased by 10 percent, reaching 16.2 percent in Madras (compared to the national poverty rate of 13.5 percent and the state poverty rate of 13.6 percent; U.S. Census Bureau 2000; OCDC 2006). By 2005, poverty rates in Madras climbed to 18.9 percent. One in four residents of the county is on food stamps, a rate that far exceeds levels elsewhere in the state, and this statistic does not include separate programs offering support to tribal members (Cole 2009). As a local social service provider explained, 'I've seen statistics that a third of the population here in [Madras] is Hispanic. And if you look at the school, it's pretty evenly divided. One-third Hispanic, one-third Native American, one-third White. And with 85 percent of them on free and reduced lunch, we know that most of the people here in Madras are poor' (personal interview 2006). The numbers characterizing conditions on the reservation are much more dramatic: A recent tribal study found that unemployment on the reservation sat at 63 percent, with 79 percent of eighteen- to twenty-five-year-olds jobless (Read 2009).

Not only has the central Oregon region experienced a precipitous drop in home construction and already low homeprices following the housing crisis and subsequent economic downturn, but this dynamic has been redoubled in Jefferson County and Madras as a consequence of the local economy's dependence on wood products and lumber industries, a sector closely tied to home building. Lumber prices have declined to a historic low and area mills have dramatically cut back workforces (McDonald 2009). In early 2008, Madras's largest private employer, a wood-processing firm with more than 1,000 workers eliminated 140 jobs (CMURP 2007; Ahern 2008). The tribal lumber mill, famed for once receiving a nod from the Reagan administration as a model of 'tribal free enterprise,' nearly closed its doors, laying off the majority of its employees in the same year (Read 2009).

These spatial and economic dynamics are specifically racialized. Native Americans and Latinos are overrepresented among the county's working poor and are disproportionately represented in low-paying seasonal agricultural employment and service jobs associated with summer tourism. Both sectors, hard hit by reces- sional shifts and declines in disposable incomes, have cut back on seasonal hiring, making already precariously employed workers more vulnerable. These declining employment opportunities are further augmented by the increasing demand and decreasing availability of social supports as overburdened social service agencies endeavor to serve the growing numbers of the working poor alongside recently displaced white middle-class workers from the housing and construction sectors.

Against this backdrop of local race-class relations and economic dynamics, I now turn to economic development agendas in Madras, specifically centered on entrepreneurial prison recruitment and luxury housing construction, to theorize how the racial geometry in Jefferson County is reproduced through both representations and material processes.

## 'Yes in My Backyard' Politics and the Racialized Remaking of Madras

[Madras] is on the move. What is most exciting, though, is our town is bashing through the negative stereotypes that have dogged it. (Ahern 2005a)

Since the mid-1990s, Madras has been involved in prison recruitment efforts. Like other states in the region, the Oregon state prison system has experienced phenomenal growth in the past two decades and has been engaged in massive prison expansion projects to accommodate overcrowding in its existing facilities. Since 1994, when voters approved controversial Measure 11, a law that mandates much longer sentences for certain crimes, Oregon's prison population has more than doubled, even as crime rates have declined in every category (Goldsmith 2009). With 14,000 people now in prison 5 and 35,000 under the supervision of the Oregon Department of Corrections (ODOC), the state's corrections budget has increased by 20 percent each biennium since 1995 (Goldsmith 2009). Stemming from the brisk growth in incarcerated populations and Measure 11-driven prison overcrowding, the state declared a state of emergency that facilitated the passage of a supersiting measure designed to 'fast track' the prison siting process by making prisons exempt from typical land use and environmental review processes (personal interview, 2006; SB 5531 2005).

Following the state's announcement of plans to commence new prison construction in 1995, rural community leaders across the state submitted applications to host the new facilities in their towns. In the case of Madras, a local coalition made up of town business

leaders and city officials was instrumental in submitting the application (Ahern 2005b), and this initial process took place with little public discussion or a venue to vet the possibility within the community (personal interview 2006). After a failed attempt to win the state's bid for a women's prison, the community received news in 2001 that the state would build an even larger facility, a mediumand minimum-security men's institution, in Madras. Ground was scheduled to be broken for the prison in 2003, but delays-facilitated by budget uncertainty and legislative politics impeding the passage of the Senate Bill authorizing the bond sale and funds for prison construction-prolonged the process for several years (Ahern 2005b; personal interview 2006). Although the process started in 1995, it was not until 2006 that construction on the facility actually began.

In a discussion about the siting process, one city official noted that while other more 'upscale' communities have opposed prison sitings, '[Madras], for the most part was like, yes, we'll take it! . . . Yes, we were happy to have a prison in our backyard' (personal interview 2006). In fact, although the local growth coalition formed early in the process might have recruited and welcomed the facility, there was community opposition to the prison that in many ways reflects the racialization of traditional NIMBYism. Narratives of local opposition came primarily from long-term, white residents and ranged from concerns about corrections spending and prison growth in the state (Kollen 2005), apprehension about local involvement in the process (Foster 2002), anxieties about community safety and property values (Foster 2002; Lindsey 2007), and angst about tax increases and local spending in support of the facility (Hoff 2005). In spite of bureaucratic delays and community concerns, the city invested heavily in the prison long before ground was broken for the facility, with infrastructural improvements, school and hospital expansions, and beautification efforts designed to 'make the city of [Madras] appearing more attractive for prison employees to live' (Gill 2005b). As another high-ranking city leader clarified, however, this decade of delays in siting the facility helped to silence local prison antagonisms:

Of course there were some people who really weren't [on board with the prison]. At first, it was pretty controversial. There were a lot of concerns. But the whole decade of preparations was kind of nice in some ways [laughs] . . . . We were able to work through lots of issues and people had time to ease into being more comfortable with it. So I think it will be fine. There are still some people who are grumbly. But no one is real vocal about it anymore. It's not going to be stopped. It's a done deal.

The prison itself was tied to a series of connected economic growth agendas, including upscale housing and urban revitalization and blight removal. As one town leader made clear, 'The prison was just another economic development tool. We didn't want to put all of our eggs in one basket, prison-wise. It's not the end-all' (personal interview 2006). In fact, it was the siting of the correctional facility that provided the capital necessary to pursue the upscale housing agenda and that animated the development discourses surrounding efforts to remake the community. Funds used to purchase the lands for the luxury housing development originated from ODOC's first payment to the city in 2002 (Gill 2005a). Furthermore, once the state's plans for a prison in Madras were made public, one of the city leaders most active in recruiting the prison claimed that the time was right for Madras to 'reinvent the town's look, its attitude, and its image' (Lundgren 2001, A17). Another leader publicly noted '[t]he prison has put us on the radar' (Lundgren 2001, A17). One of the key ways that community leaders planned to operationalize this image reinvention was through a $ 14 million urban renewal process that involved the revitalization of the Main Street corridor to attract new visitors to 'stop urban flight and deal with 'blight'' (personal interview 2006; CMURP 2007).

Interviews and analysis of local commentary revealed that desires to 'reinvent' the image of Madras and to '[bash] negative stereotypes that have dogged it,' (Ahern 2005a), as the quotation at the beginning of this section describes, are deeply racialized and classed. The subtext of local interviews and media remarks makes manifest tensions about the tribal community, the presence of migrant farmworkers, and the growing Latino population settling in the area. It becomes particularly salient here how local planning agendas, informed by long-standing racial tropes, work to reproduce racialized poverty and spatial inequalities. Echoing the need to reinvent the image of Madras, an area community booster explained that the city is working hard with its marketing plan and redevelopment efforts to change assumptions held about the community based on its historically entrenched poverty and its racial mix, noting that 'people assume that because of the Indian and Hispanic communities, it's got to be bad here' (personal interview 2006). Another leader reiterated this theme, explaining:

[The perceptions are] based on the last at least 25 years. And when you have that kind of a battle in front of you, especially being connected to a Native American tribe, when you're connected to that, whatever reputation that particular tribe has, the city next to it holds as well . . . . Mix that in with the busing in of migrant workers to camps and then they leave when the harvest is over, to all of a sudden they're here year round. Now you've added another element that is an unknown. We need a perceptual [ sic ] shift. (personal interview 2006)

Several aspects of these statements are important. First, these quotations lay bare the negative sentiments held by city leaders regarding the community's racial makeup. According to these two influential community officials, Latino and Native American populations are the source of negative viewpoints and 'unknown' elements that must be managed to better promote the city. Furthermore, these comments link negative perceptions about the community to tribal reputation and the transitioning Latino population, a framing that squarely faults the othered groups, themselves, rather than broader policies and economic, political, and race-class structures that have disenfranchised, entrenched, and spatialized poverty in the community. Second, this statement highlights the local racial geometry and the way that whites, Latinos, and Native Americans are positioned and differentiated within local racial hierarchies (Kim 1999; Pulido 2006). Although whites make up a significant portion of the community's poor, poverty is mapped onto local communities of color and a clear 'cultural distance' is established between Madras's white community and local Latinos and Native Americans (Pulido 2006, 24). This framing invisibilizes white poverty, stigmatizes and negatively positions impoverished communities of color, and reaffirms the social and economic status of local whites.

Tellingly, in the preceding quotation, the community development director refers to an 'influx' of migrant workers with 'unknown' impacts. This framing suggests that the presence of Latinos in Jefferson County is a relatively new phenomenon and, echoing a common euphemism about immigrants and contagion, portrays Latinos as an invading population. Although the number of Latinos living in Madras has sharply increased since 1990, Latinos have a long history in the community. It has only been with the recent growth of Latinos settling in town, however, that white residents began to actually see the social reproduction of those workers who have long been critical to the local agricultural industry (Nelson 2008). When asked about the growing Latino community, another city administrator claimed, 'They're [Latinos] only staying here a little while and then moving on' (personal interview 2006). Referring to the city's demographic changes, another white city leader described Madras as '[a] community in transition. Our population is becoming more urbanized' (personal interview 2006). Here the term urbanized stands in as coded language to refer to communities of color fundamentally different from an idealized, rural white norm. Such language highlights a deep unease about the growth of Latinos and the 'the browning of Oregon' (Taylor 2007, v). Indeed, throughout interviews, Latinos were constructed as both new and transitory members of the community. Representing the Latino population in this way has significant effects by denying the historical presence of Latinos in the communityandtheimportantrole that Latino labor has played in the viability of the local economy and capital accumulation. Moreover, marking Latinos in this way not only devalues and conceals historical and contemporary labor dependencies but also situates Latinos as impermanent outsiders and legitimates the disassociation of white leadership from the needs of local Latinos.

Whereas the discourses of the white leadership position Latinos as transitory, the Native American community, by contrast, is represented as a fixed, permanent liability in city efforts to remake Madras. Enduring stereotypes about Native Americans were repeatedly invoked in interviews, illuminating longstanding resentments about the tribe's sovereign status and access to federal resources. In the following response from a town leader, when asked about how economic development efforts would seek to address tribal poverty, the Native American community is represented as simultaneously debauched and wealthy:

They are well off. But that doesn't stop the fact that we have generation after generation of unborn babies that say that we owe them. And they go out and preach that. They maintain that they want that separation of their nation and the United States. They want that autonomy, but then they want the same things that we enjoy as a nation. Our nation. They demand rights and so forth that are the same, but they still want their autonomy. They want their cake and eat it too. And they know it. We all know it. But it's not politically correct to talk about. And so you have that element going, where a lot of them do not like to work. They don't want to work. They've never had to work. And they get paid, and that's, through their tribal receipts and revenues, a lot of them do. (personal interview 2006)

This remark underscores a common theme that emerged in interviews. Repeatedly, the tribal community was

identified as wealthy, with one city leader even asserting that it was 'one of the most affluent in the nation' (personal interview 2006) because of revenues generated from its casino and lumber processing industry. Yet the notion that the tribe is wealthy belies the actual circumstances on the reservation, where poverty rates are estimated to exceed 50 percent (Dash 2007; Read 2009). In fact, gaming revenues have been more than offset by declines in the tribe's lumber products industry, which has fallen sharply since the mid-1990s (Dash 2007, 205). Further, such commentary identifies the economic challenges facing the tribe as stemming from personal choices and depravity, rather than from histories of racism and the dispossession of lands and livelihoods. Native Americans are stigmatized and unfavorably positioned within local racial geometries by commentary that culturally constructs them as morally corrupt and unwilling to work. Materialities borne out of the extermination and containment of indigenous peoples and the incremental white economic and social advantages generated through this history are dismissed as having legitimate implications for contemporary economic geographies. Rather, the myth of white meritocracy is upheld and white accountabilities are allayed by the enduring stereotype of Native American poverty as perpetuated by deviance.

The statement also uncovers profound resentment over Native Americans accessing services and governmentprogramsaswellashostility toward their sovereign tribal status and the constitutional rights that come with this sovereignty. Another city leader further called on this political demarcation to explain the deep divides andlackofcommunicationbetweencityandtriballeaders. '[They're] a sovereign nation out there that's trying to build their own future. And they have a complex set of problems that we don't have. So to intermingle all of that stuff can be tough sometimes. And so even though the reservation is within our county, there is none of it in the city limits' (personal interview 2006). These representations relate tribal sovereignty as a 'special right' and a marker of spatial separation. In so doing, they fail to observe that 'sovereignty recognized by treaties and the US constitution does not represent something given to the tribe; rather, it is what tribes have retained throughout the tragic history of colonialism' (Ishiyama 2003, 130). Whites control local social, political, and economic institutions and invoke the reservation border and the rights granted from tribal sovereignty to legitimate Madras's exclusionary racial geography. By placing Native Americans, many of whom live and work in Madras, outside of 'the city limits,' local leaders distance themselves-culturally and geographically-from both the tribal community's deep need and from the history of colonization and oppression that forms the basis for this inequality.

Even as Latinos and Native Americans are seen as existing outside of the community via their tribal sovereignty or impermanent status, community leaders were also quick to exoticize their presence as a means to attract people to Madras. Highlighting this theme, a town booster remarked that the community 'sells its racial diversity' (personal interview 2006). Describing how the community 'leverages' its racial diversity in local marketing agendas, a community official noted

And that [racial diversity] puts Jefferson County in such a unique situation. But I think one of the big things of us, is that we'll stand in front of God and country and call ourselves culturally diverse. We admit it. We talk about. We leverage it. (personal interview 2006)

Striking a remarkably confessional tone, this statement simultaneously confirms normative whiteness and local racial hierarchies. Although local whites can 'admit' to a shared racial landscape, such recognition reaffirms a relational racial geography where people of color are raced and whites remain unmarked and unseen. The privileged status of whites is maintained through power relations that afford white leadership the ability to name others, to 'leverage' and market non-white populations, and whiteness remains the unmarked category against which difference is constructed. Thus, although local communities of color are at once othered and blamed for the town's entrenched poverty-the source of 'negative perceptions'-these communities simultaneously represent a local marketing strategy.

It is through this kaleidoscope of racial positions that the city claims the need to reinvent the community. The social production of race and class in Madras is reinforced and reproduced by economic development agendas that devalue and marginalize particular subjects and spaces while protecting and enhancing others. The representational tactics of white community leaders translate into material consequences through the implementation of policies and the allocation of local resources. Efforts to remake Madras reflect this relational interplay between the material and the discursive. Together with prison recruitment, the community's efforts to engender 'a perceptual shift' regarding the town's entrenched poverty have included efforts to transform the city into an affluent community featuring 'high-end housing and unparalleled access to the area's amenities' (personal interview 2006).

This focus on high-end residential construction was prominently discussed in all interviews and is explicitly stated in the city's comprehensive plan, as well as the city's urban revitalization agenda:

So that a reasonable mix of housing types on a variety of lot sizes are available for both existing and future residents, the City shall encourage the development of housing types that are suitable for high income households. To be competitive with housing in the region that accommodates high income households, the encouraged housing type should include amenities appropriate for high income households, such as a golf course . . . . With the addition of more housing targeted at high income buyers, the City will grow into a more diverse, vibrant community. (City of Madras 2003, 96)

Underlain by local race-class hierarchies affixing poverty to Latinos and Native Americans, this emphasis on affluent housing is specifically racialized as a means to draw in new wealthy and middle-class, white in-migrants and also to keep privileged white residents in the city. Indeed, not long after prison construction plans first surfaced, developers held a public meeting to outline their plans for an 800-acre master-planned community, including a celebrity-designed golf course and 'view lots,' paid for by the prison and ironically located just across the road from the facility (Fisher 2006a). Targeted homebuyers were identified as 'second home and retirement buyers from Central Oregon and metro Portland and working families with incomes tied to the future prison in [Madras]' (Fisher 2006a). Developers, some with suspect connections to city politics, acquired the newly incorporated land from the city of Madras in a sweetheart, below-market-value deal (Ahern 2007b). It is important to emphasize here that the prison was framed throughout commentaries and interviews not as an economic boon for its potential in creating jobs for existing town residents in need of employment but as an opportunity to bring in new residents. 'Since talk of the prison in [Madras] first emerged in the 1990s, the real economic buzz on the [the facility] wasn't necessarily that you or your friend might get a job at the prisons, but rather the community was excited about the side benefits it would bring-spark more retail, lure more well-paid residents and inspire housing developers' (Ahern 2007a).

Community leaders framed alleged inevitability of population and economic growth associated with middle-class prison workers as a means through which to advance the city's focus on high-end housing. The city's comprehensive plan is informative in illustrating this connection:

The City's existing housing includes a generous supply of housing that is affordable for low and moderate income families, but there is a deficit of housing that is commensurate with the financial capabilities of existing and future high income families. The Department of Corrections Facility is expected to create high-income jobs (i.e., jobs that will raise household incomes in excess of 120% of the MFI [median family income]), and the City desires to attract these employees (and maintain high income families) as residents. (City of Madras 2003, 96)

Such language highlights the way that the prison animated city claims about the need for high-income residential growth and formed the basis for city boosters' promotion of the agenda. Throughout interviews, community leaders reiterated this concern, proclaiming an increasing 'need for rooftops' in the city to accommodate both prison workers and wealthy new in-migrants (personal interview 2006). This exchange with an influential town leader further underscores these themes:

Participant: We're just really short on medium- to upperincome housing. We're really focusing on that.

Interviewer: You're lacking middle-class housing?

Participant: Really upper. We have some middle and lots of lower-class housing. [laughs]

Interviewer: So the goal is to build new housing for future prison employees?

Participant: If we can make the housing available. It will be a fair price compared to other communities, especially compared to neighboring communities. So we're trying to get that housing stock.

An explicit focus on the high end was also articulated in the following statement from a community booster in 2001. 'Whether we do anything or not, now we're going to grow. Now do we want more mobile homes or do we want nice neighborhoods?' (Lundgren 2001, A17). This individual further claimed, 'If you build junk, you get junk. You need to plan nice streets, nice neighborhoods' (Lundgren 2001, A17). Another local official described this city-described 'need for rooftops' in the following manner:

One of the things that the city did, and it's hurt us in the long haul, is that they went after housing early on in the eighties, when the federal government had programs for low-income housing and they were building low-income housing in communities that would accept it. And so our government, our local government, said hey we'll take it.

It's construction, it means population. Bring 'em on. We have a predominant inventory in low-income homes and housing here. So we basically said give us your poor. And then all of a sudden one day ten years ago, everybody said, 'Shit, look at our inventory! We're out of balance. No more low income! No more Section 8 homes, no more government subsidy type situations. Let's focus now on upper end.' (personal interview 2006)

This proclamation reveals much about the city's racialized and classed redevelopment agenda. It demonstrates a clear shift in how the poor have been utilized by community leaders to build wealth and privilege. After embracing a subsidized low-income housing agenda supported by state transfers in the 1980s, the new strategy relies instead on YIMBY prison politics involving the procurement of the poor as incarcerated to maintain and grow white privilege through economic development centered on high-end residential construction. Rather than planning with existing needs and residents of the community in mind, this focus is built on a desire to bring in new populations and transform Madras into a privileged, white space. The incongruence of this vision with Madras's actual political economic landscape-one defined by high rates of poverty and unemployment, economic restructuring, and industrial decline-is remarkable.

Efforts to reinvent the city through the part-prison, part-high-end-housing economic development agenda depend not only on prison-generated revenues and incomes but also on expunging low-income housing units through 'blight eradication' efforts (personal interview 2006). Indeed, the areas targeted for blight removal, including mobile home parks and the Section 8 housing explicitly referenced in the preceding quotation, are predominately occupied by low-income communities of color and migrant workers unable to afford rental units elsewhere (Tello 2006; personal interview 2006). A 2006 study found that seasonal farmworkers in the area earned less than a third of the area's median income and experienced great difficulty locating places to reside (McDonald 2008). Unable to afford other housing options, multiple families often must live together in single-family housing and apartment structures. Many of the units catering to farmworkers are thus overcrowded and the Central Oregon Regional Housing Authority estimates that 341 units are needed to meet this demand (OCDC 2006, 25). Overcrowding, together with years of city neglect, have contributed to residential decline. Additionally, language barriers and landlord discriminatory practices, primarily exercised through the refusal to rent to workers with no local previous address (OCDC 2006), further intensify and spatialize community segregation by delimiting housing options for low-income and seasonal workers.

Thus, as local white leadership promotes high-end residential construction to attract the wealthy to the community, the city's concurrent emphasis on 'eradicating blight' threatens to even further diminish the availability of inexpensive housing in the area. Even as an affordable housing crisis grips the region, local leaders claim a saturation of high-density and Section 8 housing associated with low-income communities of color and encourage residential development 'suitable for high-income buyers' (City of Madras 2003, 96). This agenda directs limited city resources toward this focus and away from low-income communities in need in ways that both reflect and reproduce existing hierarchies of race and class. In spite of the claim that more high-income housing will create 'a more diverse, vibrant community' (City of Madras 2003, 96), this approach, in fact, reinscribes spatial divisions between the wealthy and the poor, between white and non-white neighborhoods.

## Conclusion: YIMBYism, Racialization, and Economic Development

The year 2007 was significant for Madras. First, the minimum-security segment of the newly built prison in Madras began incarcerating inmates. Second, the city also welcomed its first non-white mayor, prominent for noting 'I see myself as a choice away from developers and the status quo . . . . Low-income, middle-income, high-income-[government] should be for all of us, not just the ones paying the most taxes' (Gill 2006). Although this mayor served just one term, his election was largely seen as a referendum on the prison-housing development agenda pursued so avidly by politically and economically interested local gatekeepers. Indeed, the outcomes of this vision of redevelopment have been disastrous. The medium-security portion of the facility was supposed to have opened in February 2008. The ODOC, however, made the decision to keep the medium-security facility closed longer as a result of state budget crises and after prison population reports showed a downward trend in medium-security inmates (Ahern 2008). Although this delayed opening was intended to be temporary, the facility remains closed today. Moreover, whereas city leaders anticipated 450 workers to be employed at the prison, it currently employs less than 200 and new layoffs have just been announced. Just

down the street from the prison, the master-planned, luxury housing community has seen the construction of just seven houses, only one not built as a spec home, in spite of the tax burden and city resources funneled into infrastructural support for this residential development.

The recruitment of a prison might seem to contradict traditional analyses of NIMBYism that would presumably identify a prison as an unwanted facility, particularly borne from concerns about declining property values and white fears about the convergence of black and brown incarcerated bodies and their families in prison-hosting communities. Indeed, it was on these grounds that many opposed the prison. Yet, in Madras, rather than oppose a prison on the basis of its undesirability or the racialized threat of procuring non-white outsiders, city leaders instead marshaled a YIMBY politics and entrepreneurially recruited a correctional facility. Undeniably, rural prison recruitment has become so ordinary that the notion of YIMBYism seems more commonplace with respect to correctional facilities than NIMBYism. But, like NIMBYism, this YIMBYism in Madras also represents a spatial effect of racism (Nast 2000, cited in Hubbard 2005, 54). White local leaders mobilized in support of a prison with the explicit objective of enhancing privilege via the remaking of the town. Although yet unrealized, this carceralsupported reinvention was designed to bring in wealthy whites through high-end residential construction.

I draw from the example of YIMBYism in Madras to illustrate how economic development is a value-laden, fully racialized project that is articulated through local racial geometries. As new rounds of capital accumulation and disinvestment transform central Oregon, local leaders seek to rebuild the economy through entrepreneurial prison recruitment, luxury housing, and blight eradication. Efforts to remake Madras into a privileged, white community at once deny the area's actual racial geography and political economic structure (high poverty, declining employment, new correctional facility) and also depend on the erasure of neighborhoods occupied predominantly by low-income communities of color. These efforts are emblematic of the differential construction of racial and class identities that materially and discursively define what it means to be white, Latino, and Native American in Madras. White officials have promoted prison recruitment policies and upscale housing development through a normative racial framework that sustains the social and economic status of whiteness, stigmatizes Latinos and Native Americans, and (re)produces unequal spaces. Low-income communities of color are thus challenged not only by economic crisis but also by local power relations of race and class that further marginalize by constructing them as irresponsible, inferior outsiders. These constructions both shape access and opportunities and reinforce the valorization and transparency of whiteness and validate economic policies crafted in support of privilege. These discursive and material processes sustain a particularly racialized field of positions, condition the flow of city resources, rationalize particular development policies, and reinforce social and spatial segregation.

By examining locally produced stereotypes and representations of Latinos and the tribal community in Madras, I uncover the ways in which racial constructions underlie debates about economic development. I also point to the ways in which the local white leadership has pursued polices in their own class and race interests. Discourses about development and economic growth flow through and rework long-standing racial tropes about Latino immigrants and Native Americans in ways that both invisibilize whiteness and white privilege and reproduce local racial hierarchies and segregated landscape. My analysis emphasizes that race is produced through the discursive construction of difference and material social relations. Economic practices reinforce racial categories, racisms, and white supremacy, even as racialization is a fundamental component in shaping economic processes themselves. I theorize the economic as produced through, and productive of, a field of racial positions-a racial geometry-through which economy and racialization are articulated relationally in a mutually constitutive looping process. Although the creation of economic policies or community development agendas might not be undertaken with the direct intention of consolidating racial and class differentiation, the effect of enhancing privilege and maintaining particularly racialized hierarchies means that these processes are inextricably linked to race. Economic power and privilege, perhaps best made manifest through the accumulation of wealth and the power to craft and implement economic policy that shapes access to resources, flows through racialized actors and is fundamentally bolstered and maintained by formations of race.

1. The city's population has grown by 52 percent since the year 2000 (U.S. Census Bureau 2000, 2010); this increase is part of a long trajectory of population growth in central Oregon. Indeed, Madras's population expanded by 233 percent between 1980 and 2000 (CMURP 2007). It is notable, though, to highlight that 774 of the town's new residents are incarcerated in the minimum-security prison that opened in 2007 (ODOC 2012).
2. The federal Homesteading Act of 1862 was implemented to promote westward expansion. Homesteaders were offered 160 acres of 'free lands' to 'improve' Western territories that had been cleared of indigenous peoples (Bell 2012). The Act's offering of these 'conquered territories' (Barraclough 2011, 235) had profound implications: It privatized 270 million acres of land (10 percent of U.S. territory) and significantly eroded American Indian culture (Bell 2012). In Oregon, 10,513,945 acres (17 percent of total lands) were homesteaded following the Act and its subsequent iterations (OBLM 2012).
3. See Wilson (2011) for a detailed discussion of the racial implications of the connections between irrigation and homesteading in Oregon.
4. At the time when interviews (2005-2006) were conducted in Madras, an all-white city council and a white mayor served the town. In 2007, the city's first non-white mayor was elected to office, which I discuss in more detail in the Conclusion. After completing this term, the mayor was replaced by another well-established white city leader who continues to serve in this position today.
5. ODOC demographic reports indicate that 9.1 percent of those in prison are black (although African Americans make up just 1.8 percent of the state's population), 14.1 percent are Hispanic (in contrast to the 11.7 percent of Oregon residents who identify as Hispanic), 2.6 percent are American Indian (with just 1.3 percent of the state's population being American Indian), 1.4 percent are Asian (while Asians represent 3.7 percent of the state's population), and, finally, 72.6 percent are white (although whites make up 84 percent of state population totals; ODOC 2012; U.S. Census Bureau 2010). Of course, these numbers are fraught because many individuals who identify as two or more races are not reflected in census and ODOC numbers.
