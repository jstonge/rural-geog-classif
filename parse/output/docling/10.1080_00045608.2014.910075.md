## Unintended Developments: Gender, Environment, and Collective Governance in a Mexican Ejido

Heidi E. Hausermann

Department of Human Ecology, Rutgers University

This article examines the unintended outcomes of a neoliberal program designed to privatize Mexico's communal lands. Although postrevolutionary agrarian law excluded women from official landholding and leadership positions, steps toward land privatization inadvertently increased women's access to land, government resources, and political power. Using ethnographic and survey data collected in a Veracruz ejido, I demonstrate how Mexico's agrarian counterreforms triggered novel subjectivities and practices. While men acted as self-imagined private property owners and decreased participation in traditional governance institutions, women became registered land managers and leaders for the first time in the ejido's history. These interlocking processes stopped the land-titling program in its tracks and reinvigorated collective governance. Even state actors charged with carrying out ejido privatization were implicated in the empowerment of rural women and failure to fully privatize land. This research contributes to nature-society debates by arguing neoliberalism does not always end economic self-determination and communal governance in agrarian contexts. Rather, I demonstrate the ways in which processual policy, subjectivity, authority formation, objects, and environmental narratives combine to produce new political trajectories with positive implications for rural women and the environment. Key Words: environment, gender, governance, Mexico, neoliberalism.

O n a September morning in 2008, Adela 1 waited in the lobby of Registro Agrario Nacional (RAN) 2 in Xalapa, capital city of Mexico's Veracruz state. Adela took over all aspects of coffee production when her husband died in 1999. Nine years and many bureaucratic procedures later, she was finally reregistering a two-hectare plot from her deceased husband's name to her own. The individuals gathered that morning reflected Veracruz's diverse agrarian landscapes and included lowland sugarcane growers, a Totonac leader from a vanilla-producing community, and several coffee producers. The crowd also represented Mexico's long history of gendered agrarian policies: Of fifty-four people waiting to speak with officials, only three were women.

Adela's presence at RAN was an unforeseen outcome of constitutional amendments designed to privatize Mexico's communal landholding system, the ejido. Adela's ejido entered the privatization program in 1997. Following initial steps toward privatization, women gained access to land and leadership positions and helped reassert collective governance. In other words, a policy designed to privatize communal lands not only played out differently than technocrats intended; it helped produce new subjectivities and authorities, ultimately stopping the land-titling process in its tracks.

I do not advocate for top-down development interventions, but I am interested in the unintended outcomes of such impositions and how they might help create new spaces for political change. More often than not, nature-society scholarship focuses on the damaging outcomes of capitalist development for people and environments: new forms of dispossession, gendered and raced exclusions, increased environmental contamination, and so forth. As Heynen and colleagues (2007) put it, 'While neoliberal policies are of course varied and unpredictable in their outcomes . . . we believe it is still reasonable to anticipate that they are more likely to produce negative than positive outcomes' (289). Researchers have demonstrated neoliberalism is anything but an ideal type; instead, policies possess unpredictable and variegated implications in different socioecological contexts (Bakker 2010; see also Larner 2003; Radcliffe 2005; Fisher 2009). This research contributes to this body of scholarship by demonstrating how state-led efforts to privatize communal land helped undo historic exclusions of women from leadership and landholding positions.

This research is based on twenty months of fieldwork in Veracruz. In contrast to the intent of Mexico's agrarian counterreforms, I discovered the national ejido privatization program helped produce unexpected subjectivities and commons-oriented practices within one rural community. I situate this work within Mexico's long history of masculinist agrarian law to demonstrate how steps toward ejido privatization triggered new positions for women as political leaders and registered ejido members. These unfoldings also inspired new tactics to reassert collective governance. I thus make empirical links between steps toward ejido privatization, production of new gendered subjectivities and authorities, and reinvigoration of collective governance. I then illustrate how these interlocking processes shortcircuited the original intent of Mexico's neoliberal counterreforms (i.e., full title privatization of communally managed land). The next section places this research within recent scholarship on nature-society dynamics in the context of neoliberalism.

## Nature-Society Dynamics in the Neoliberal Era

In the last thirty years, neoliberal economic development-led by governments, lending institutions, and other powerful organizations-became aligned with fiscal restraint, free trade, and privatization. Worldwide, agrarian livelihoods were deeply implicated in these efforts as diverse people and agroecosystems were labeled 'inefficient' and 'unviable' in terms of their ability to compete in liberalized markets. Property rights became the focus of attempts to make natural resource management, and human subjects, 'modern,' 'efficient,' and 'competitive' through privatization and enclosure measures.

The outcomes of neoliberal development policies became a major focus of nature-society geographers (e.g., Mansfield 2004; McCarthy 2005; Perreault 2009; Bakker 2010; Robertson 2011; Lave 2012; Radcliffe forthcoming). Many researchers revealed unexpected consequences of privatization and enclosure efforts. For instance, as property regimes in Mexico became messier despite state attempts to privatize resources (Perramond 2008), water and other nonhuman natures resisted commodification and control (e.g., Bakker 2005; Robbins and Luginbuhl 2007). Researchers have also identified the need to better understand the variegated outcomes of neoliberal projects in terms of gender (Harris 2009), nonbinary understandings of agency and power (Bakker 2010), and state practice (Ferguson and Gupta 2002). Thisresearch builds from these calls to demonstrate how attempts to privatize communal lands unexpectedly

yielded political power and resource access for women. Women, moreover, used their new positions to reinvigorate communal practices around shared understandings of the environment, particularly water catchment and quality. The formation of new, ejido-level authorities was critical to these processes.

Local authority formation is particularly complex in postcolonial contexts wherein multiple institutions and understandings-ranging from contemporary land use policies to customary rights-coexist to sanction land claims (Sikor and Lund 2009; see also Hagberg 2006; Lund 2006b). In Latin America and beyond, even the mundane practices of ordinary people shape new authorities mediating land use and access (Lund 2006a, 674; see also Hausermann 2012). For instance, Ghanaian landholders reproduced the government's understanding of private property to reinforce resource claims and build local authority (Lund 2009). In contrast, religious cults in Benin used customary land rights to develop political power among disparate frontier communities (Le Meur 2006). The relationships between authority formation and land tenure are key to understanding property arrangements and access in the neoliberal era. To understand how authority is formed and wielded in everyday social encounters, and the broader implications for property and governance, the processual aspects of authority formation require examination (Lund 2006a; see also Rose 1999). I follow this tack here, linking neoliberal counterreforms to new forms of authority, subjectivities, and reclamation of communal arrangements. To do so, I build from scholarship on subject formation under neoliberalism.

Nature-society scholars have examined how people internalize elements of neoliberal capitalism. While capitalist subjects emerged with exclusive fishing rights in Alaska (Mansfield 2007), migrants self-disciplined in heteronormative ways to access resources enclosed in Indonesian forests (Elmhirst 2011). And based on fieldwork in Nepal, Nightingale (2005) detailed how community members appropriated and resisted neoliberal expertise and market relations along lines of gender, education, and caste, producing new subjectivities in the process. Sundberg (2003, 2004), moreover, showed how a conservation organization intervened in rural Guatemala with preconceived ideas about the Maya, prompting indigenous conservation subjects to perform in ways that met those expectations. Existing research thus suggests that individuals and communities come to self-govern in insidiously subversive ways, privileging the interests of neoliberal development, government, and environmental conservation.

This research has greatly furthered our understandings of neoliberalism's complex implications. Yet, the tendency exists in nature-society scholarship to treat neoliberal development discourse and practice as having colonized entire economic landscapes, leading to the end of economic self-determination and traditional governance. As St. Martin (2009) has argued, noncapitalist processes are almost always represented as threatened or disappearing as the 'logics' of capitalism conquer and expand. Although neoliberal reforms clearly catalyzed dramatic changes in many settings, such 'crisis' situations often inspire novel and creative action, transforming the world in the process (Deleuze and Guattari 1987). New political configurations can indeed emerge from moments of profound change.

Marston, Jones, and Woodward (2005) are helpful here, positing that things in the world are material actualizations resolved through heterogeneous interactions of different objects-ranging from coffee plants to human bodies and government policies. By examining interactions of differential combinations we can see repetitiveness and capacity for newness. These complex potentialities help us understand how diverse relations (both actual and potential) produce different sites and orders, with politically productive unfoldings (Marston, Jones, and Woodward 2005, 425). Following this line of thought, capitalist processes, such as a national land privatization program, might indeed possess diverse implications for peoples and places and create politically differentiated trajectories. It is thus important to examine how policies interact with gender, historic institutions, subject formation, and authority without a priori understanding of one element possessing greater spatial reach than others. Indeed, in the 'illimitable et cetera' of actually existing difference, we find new departures for politics (Butler 1990, 196). Such a view allows researchers to carefully examine interplays to understand outcomesandpossibilities, most of which, given the vast socioecological difference that makes up the world, are unpredictable.

I found Mexico's neoliberal agrarian counter reforms unexpectedly triggered an undoing of women's historic exclusion from local leadership and landholding positions. Following the ejido's entrance in a land privatization program, men identified as private property owners and became apathetic toward traditional governance while women developed political authority and helped reinvigorate collective practices. These processes, moreover, kept the ejido from being fully privatized. In other words, new interlocking subjectivities and forms of authority undermined the land-titling

process itself and communal practices were reclaimed in new contexts. Before describing these findings in greater detail, I provide background on Mexican agrarian reform and its undoing.

## Agrarian Reform and Gender in Mexico (1917-1992)

Between 1917 and 1992, the Mexican government expropriated large landholdings throughout the nation, redistributing resources to small-scale farmers, pastoralists, and fisherfolk in the form of communally managed ejidos. 3 Ejidos are often a combination of solares (urban plots), parcelas (farm plots), and a preagreed percentage of tierras de uso com´ un (commonsland and resources). In the decades following the Mexican Revolution, 28,000 ejidos and agrarian communities were created, occupying more than half of Mexico's arable land surface (Instituto Nacional de Estad´ ıstica y Geograf´ ıa [INEGI] 1990). By 1992, when constitutional amendments brought an end to Mexican land reform, millions of ejido members (hereafter ejidatarios ) were registered in these communities.

Postrevolutionary agrarian laws were designed to create and protect ejido land from concentration. Ejidatarios never legally possessed fee-simple titles to land. Instead, the state granted usufruct access, the right to use property belonging to someone else, in this case federal land and water. Even in ejidos where members work individual parcels, producers possess land exploitation rights in usufruct form (Procuradur´ ıa Agraria 2007). Put differently, whereas ejidatarios could pass land to others through inheritance, they could not legally lease or sell land, although many did through de facto practices (e.g., Sheridan 1988; Nu˜ nez-Madrazo 2005; LastarriaCornhiel 2011).

Agrarian laws also mandated collective decision making,requiring ejidos possess a general assembly comprised of all registered members. 4 This regularly convened assembly is the venue for collective decision making: establishing rules, elections, solving disputes, and so on. Within the assembly, elected leaders serve three-year terms. 5 While promoting collectivity in land management and local governance, Mexican agrarian laws systematically excluded women from official entitlement and political participation.

Women could only become registered ejidatarias as widows or a household's sole economic provider, the latter being difficult to prove. Until 1971 a woman was required to forfeit land rights and official status if she married an ejidatario. Constitutional amendments in 1971 reworked the status of women; women would no longer lose ejido membership upon marriage. Inheritance rights were also created, meaning that land could be willed to individuals irrespective of sex. Deere and Le´ on (2001b, 71), however, argued that the 1971 amendmentsdidlittle to promote women's roles in ejido decision making. Brunt (1992, 82) demonstrated that when women did gain access to ejidataria status, men rarely allowed them to serve as leaders (see also De Walt and Rees 1994; Esparza Salinas, Su´ arez, and Bonfil 1996). Despite significant roles in agricultural and social (re)production, women account for under 18 percent of registered ejido members (V´ azquez-Garc´ ıa 1997; Radel 2011). By extension, local leadership positions requiring ejidatario status are dominated by men. Women are also often excluded from government programs tied to official standing, such as credit and agricultural extension services.

While fostering male dominance in resource access and politics, the ejidal sector became an important site of state formation, ecological transformation, and cultural symbolism in postrevolutionary Mexico. By the end of 1987, though, largely due to the 1982 oil crisis and massive borrowing, the Mexican government had accumulated US $ 107.6 billion of foreign debt (Hern´ andez-Estrada, Villa-Issa, and Quintana Loya 1989). Foreign lending institutions began to mandate austerity measures, including privatization and government cutbacks. During this period, the World Bank began discussing agricultural reform with Mexican technocrats, including changes to the ejido sector (Teichman 2004). Land-titling in ejidos, the bank argued, would create incentives for farmers to enhance productivity and provide collateral for further investment (Deininger and Binswanger 1999, 249).

The 1992 constitutional amendments (to Article 27 in particular) were designed to open ejidos to privatization. Land previously held in usufruct could now be transformed into private property and leased or sold to firms or individuals (both foreign and national). At the time, President Carlos Salinas de Gortari (1988-1994) argued that the changes would result in government withdrawal from the agrarian sector and empower peasants to make decisions as 'legitimate owners of the land' (Craib 2004, 256). Federal legislation created Programa de Certificaci´ on de Derechos Ejidales y Titulaci´ on de Solares (PROCEDE) 6 to facilitate the process. Through this ongoing program, ejidos can opt to transform 'usufruct fields' into official 'parcels,' via a bewildering array of bureaucratic tr´ amites (procedures), involving cartography, notaries, and other forms of certification. 7 If an

ejido's general assembly votes to enter PROCEDE, ejidatarios can obtain land titles to urban plots, farm plots, and a preagreed percentage of the ejidal commons (Nuijten 2003). Ejidatarios can choose to title any or all of these land uses. For example, some ejidos pursued ownership of individual parcels while retaining communal control of resources such as forests and grazing lands (Perramond 2008).

By entering PROCEDE, ejidatarios must allow INEGI, 8 Mexico's cartographic agency, to measure and map urban and agricultural plots. Once parcels are mapped, ejidatarios receive a certificate verifying boundaries. Yet, even after this laborious mapping strategy, individual parcels are not considered private property by the state. Before land is officially privatized-thereby legalizing land sales, rentals, and so on-the ejido's general assembly must vote to proceed with privatization and disband the ejido. If 20 percent or more of ejidatarios abstain or vote to discontinue PROCEDE, the entire community's privatization progress is stalled. Put differently, no land is legally considered private property until 80 percent of ejidatarios agree, in vote, to finalize PROCEDE, achieving dominio pleno (fee-simple ownership). Until dominio pleno is reached and the ejido disbanded, ejidatarios are technically subject to ejido rules and regulations. I argue that the processual nature of PROCEDE combined with new subjectivities and practices to derail steps toward full privatization.

The 1992 amendments and creation of PROCEDE inspired significant criticism. Critics warned that the changeswouldresult in further dispossession of the peasantry, resource consolidation, and monopolies (e.g., Foley 1995; Harvey 2003). There was also speculation the amendments would further alienate women from resources and decision making (Stephen 1993, 1996; Zapata 1995; Bonfil S´ anchez 1996; Esparza Salinas, Su´ arez, and Bonfil 1996; Appendini 2001; Hamilton 2001; De Ita 2007). Deere and Le´ on (2001a) projected that PROCEDE would negatively affect women, as ejido parcels, held largely in men's names, transitioned into maleowned private property. So far, however, the policy shifts have not produced the sweeping effects proponents desired and critics feared.

Two decades have passed since constitutional reforms allowed for ejido privatization. By 2006, 97 percent of Mexico's ejidos had entered PROCEDE (Perramond 2008; Kelly et al. 2010). Yet, despite widespread steps toward privatization, the ejido sector has experienced little private investment (Cornelius and Myhre 1998; Perramond 2010). Very few communities have achieved dominio pleno. Scholars suggested that farmers are reluctant to finish PROCEDE because of fees associated with dominio pleno (201 pesos/title; currently about US $ 16) or in avoidance of property taxes (e.g., Deere and Le´ on 2001b; Wilshusen 2010). Researchers demonstrated that land tenure is more complex now than before PROCEDE (e.g., Nuijten 2003; Haenn 2006; Kelly et al. 2010; Perramond 2010; Wilshusen 2010). Yet, we know little about the relationships among privatization efforts, gender, subjectivities, and commons-oriented practices. Before I turn to empirics to untangle these relationships, I discuss the study site and methods used to conduct this research.

## Study Site and Methods

This article is the result of fieldwork carried out in an ejido called La Grullita located in central Veracruz, on the eastern flanks of shield volcano Cofre de Perote, near Coatepec (Figure 1). The community has a population of approximately 1,529 inhabitants, 156 of whom are registered ejidatarios (INEGI 2005). Except the urban area and a scattering of pasture plots, shadegrown coffee dominates land use in polyculture fi ncas (coffee orchards). Ecologists have shown that central Veracruz coffee systems provide several environmental benefits, including water catchment, nutrient cycling, and carbon sequestration (e.g., Greenberg, Bichier, and Sterling 1997; Moguel and Toledo 1999; Manson, Contreras Hern´ andez, and L´ opez-Barrera 2008). Veracruz plantations also possess levels of biodiversity similar to less altered forests in the region and serve as habitat corridors for migratory bird populations.

Between June 2003 and November 2008 I conducted twenty months of fieldwork in Veracruz. I was introduced to La Grullita in June 2006 during predissertation fieldwork and the ejido was one of two case study communities selected for my doctoral research. It quickly became clear that PROCEDE, women leaders, and collective tree-planting efforts were important ongoing topics of discussion within the ejido. To understand these dynamics in greater detail, I employed an ethnographic approach. Within La Grullita, I conducted twenty-four semistructured interviews with local leaders, male and female ejidatarios, and other individuals. 9 Twenty-one additional interviews were carried out with state actors, including officials from agrarian reform, agricultural, and development agencies. All interviews were transcribed and coded during the fieldwork period.

## Results

## Breaking the Rules: Self-Imagined Private Property Subjects

La Grullita was declared an ejido in 1936 and since possessed various rules about local governance and land use (Table 1). Until 1997, when the assembly voted to enter PROCEDE, the ejido largely functioned in accordance with these internal rules and agrarian laws. La Grullita initiated the privatization process for 472 hectares, already divided into individual parcels, yet subject to usufruct rights under agrarian law. The ejido elected to keep its sixty acres of commons land out of PROCEDE and in collective control. RAN officials were quick to visit the community to discuss the transition. INEGI surveyors followed to measure and map. According to former community leaders, INEGI t´ ecnicos were impressed by the ejido's quantity of water, both surface and underground. Ricardo, ejido president at the time, remembered an INEGI surveyor pulling him aside:

He told me, 'We traveled over the entire country and have never seen an ejido with as much water as this one. Be very careful selling the land . . . and if you sell, sell for a good price.'

Others remembered similar reactions from the INEGIt´ ecnicos. One fifty-two-year-old ejidatario recalled:

We walked the boundaries of my parcel and that of my brother. They had their equipment and wrote everything in notebooks. . . . They commented a lot about the water . . . how much there was, how clean it was . . . they asked me a lot of questions about water.

INEGI bureaucrats worked as double agents in neoliberal policy implementation. On one hand, they enacted the first steps toward ejido privatization: boundary delineation and mapping. On the other hand, these state actors cautioned ejidatarios on potential exploitation resulting from PROCEDE and educated people on potential land values. And as we will see in the following pages, the t´ ecnicos' perceptions of the ejido's prolific water resources triggered an environmental narrative around which women later worked to reinvigorate commons-oriented practices.

Following INEGI's mapping efforts, farmers were provided with certificates indicating parcel size and perimeters. If the ejido voted to continue through PROCEDE's hoops, these certificates would be used to apply for individual titles. Lund (2006a) argued that although development interventions claim to be technical and discrete, they are often political and engender structural change (see also Craib 2004; Mitchell 2002). INEGI's mapping efforts catalyzed important political reshufflings within the ejido. Ejidatarios should follow regulations until achievement of dominio pleno, but many behaved as if they already possessed titles, marking a clear shift from how transactions unfolded in the ejido's past. Although not legally permitted by agrarian law, community members agreed that intra-ejido land sales and rentals occurred prior to 1997. But ejidatarios followed local protocol for such exchanges, obtaining permission from the Executive Committee prior to land sales. Entrance into PROCEDE altered these de facto procedures.

Male ejidatarios acted as self-imagined private property owners following certificate dispersal. Mario, who sold a large portion of his farm to a cousin in 2001, explained his decision:

I sold land to my cousin . . . he wanted to build a trout farm and my parcel is in a good location, close to the road and stream. . . . I sold the land for a good price, although I didn't charge too much. I built a new room [on the house] and put the other part [of the money] in the bank.

Indeed, many community members noted increased land sales following PROCEDE. Respondents estimated that 25 percent of ejidatarios sold portions of land since PROCEDE, mostly to family and community members. People largely believed that these transactions did not pass before the ejido assembly. Many men admitted that they did not seek permission to divide or sell land. This contrasts with people's pre-PROCEDE recollections: Although land sales did occur occasionally, individuals always presented transactions to the assembly for approval. Now, however, with certificates in hand, many men acted in their own self-interest, bypassing customary channels. Ejidatarios used certificates to back up their desire to act autonomously with regard to land use decisions. The certificates, in other words, became objects for making claims and justifying particular behaviors.

Postcertificate land sales in La Grullita reflect the emergenceof rational, profit-seeking individuals following land-titling projects (Mansfield 2007, 396; Wolford 2007). Kelly et al. (2010) noted similar dynamics as ejidatarios in San Luis Potosi assumed that parcels would eventually convert to dominio pleno. The production of identities and practices linked to private property, in other words, prelude land privatization itself. Because postrevolutionary agrarian laws ensured that most ejidatarios were men, certificate dispersal, and subsequent land use practices, largely influenced male subjectivities.

Yet, although men fancied themselves private property owners and used elements of the neoliberal state to

back up their actions, many found that they still needed ejido institutions, particularly when autonomous control of private property did not work out as planned. As Mario explained:

The trout farm didn't happen. My cousin didn't have all the money . . . he wanted me to buy [back] the land. It turned ugly. . . . I went to Enrique [ejido president] so he could support me.

Mario was not alone: There were several conflicts following land sales, mostly disputes about payment. But now, if the assembly was not consulted prior to the transaction's initiation, quarrelling parties must resolve their own conflicts. Enrique, the ejido president, explained:

They [ejidatarios] sell land without the assembly's permission. Maybe later they have a problem . . . they want more money or their land back. Then they come to me for help . . . but if they didn't come to the assembly to begin with, my hands are clean.

Men liked the convenience and autonomy corresponding with their status as private property owners. Treating certificates as titles, many men engaged in land sales, including those based on future speculations like trout farming profit. Yet, such practices put informal sellers in a bind when people, like Mario's cousin, could not pay or wanted to renegotiate initial terms. Men also wanted to continue participating in traditional governance as they saw fit, and according to their needs. The production of male subjects as self-imagined private landowners, moreover, coemerged with apathy and decreased participation among male ejidatarios toward traditional institutions.

## Breaking the Rules: Decreased Participation in Traditional Governance

The initial meeting with PROCEDE officials, sixteen years ago, was the last assembly attended by all ejidatarios. Shortly after parcels were mapped and certificates received, assembly attendance waned. Survey respondents noted declining participation in communal works projects, assembly attendance, and elections, including a decrease in their own involvement (Table 2). Decreased participation in local governance was partially due to livelihood diversification.

## Unexpected Authorities

As men became increasingly disinterested in collective decision-making processes, political opportunities were created for women. By 2008, women-for the first time in the ejido's history-held three of the six local leadership positions. All three women were under forty years of age and shared committee positions with older men, an alliance that would have been unlikely fifteen years ago (Table 3). As Miriam, secretary of the Executive Committee, recalled:

Men were not interested in [committee] positions when the last election came . . . I went to Jos´ e [the former president] and asked if I could run as secretary. He told me, 'Well, no woman ever had that position, but it could be done.' He encouraged me. I think it made him happy someone was interested. So I put my name on the ballot.

Miriam encouraged two other women, her cousin Claudia and childhood friend Lupe, to run for the remaining positions without candidates. All three women inherited land from their fathers and were already registered ejidatarias. All participated in coffee production and regularly attended assemblies. And the three womencompletedjuniorhighschoolandreadandwrite well. Lupe explained her reaction when Miriam encouraged her to run for office:

When she told me she wanted to be secretary and that I should run, too, I thought she had gone crazy. But then I thought . . . I go to the assemblies. I have ideas about how to make things better . . . and I always did well in math.

Literacy was an important factor in Miriam, Lupe, and Claudia's political ascendance. The three women perceived reading, writing, and working with numbers as ensuring success as community leaders. Literacy, in other words, became an important tool mobilized by the women to access new authority positions. All three womenweresurprised by the support they received from family, neighbors, and other community members. Lupe recalled the dynamic with her husband when she confessed her desire to become treasurer:

He was surprised at first. He was secretary several years ago and told me it would be a lot of work . . . I explained I already attend the assembly . . . I told him the ejido needs a system for noting [land] transactions. . . . We were in agreement and he said he would support me . . . I think he was proud of me and that gave me a lot of encouragement.

Confident in her vision to record land transactions and create an efficient monitoring system, Lupe lobbied for her husband's approval. In the end she was rewarded, and motivated, by his support. Indeed, although social relationships can produce resistant, uncooperative subjects, they can also produce subject positions that work collectively (Nightingale 2011). In the wake of PROCEDE and men's decreased participation in local governance, women looked to family and community members to support their desire to run for office.

The political mobilization of young women was an unforeseen consequence of the ejido's step toward privatization. As men decreased participation in traditional governance, the three women used vacated political spaces to end the ejido's history of masculinized authority. Although the filling of leadership positions by the women does not necessarily reflect a long-term shift in gender politics, the turn away from masculinized forms of local authority does represent a moment of profound historical change in La Grullita.

Scholars have documented the roles development organizations and government programs play in attempts to 'empower' women in land rights and the frequent failures of such interventions due to essentialized understandings of gender (e.g., Lastarria-Cornhiel 1997; Deere and Le´ on 2001a; Ahlers and Zwarteveen 2009; Deere, Lastarria-Cornhiel, and Ranaboldo 2011). The new female authorities in La Grullita happened autonomously, however, with no outside intervention other than PROCEDE's initial triggering effects. The

women,moreover, wielded authority to help others gain ejidataria status, creating new positions for women as registered land users and ejido members.

## Becoming Ejidatarias

Ejido leaders helped women gain access to official ejidataria status. In several cases, women inherited land from fathers or husbands but never registered parcels in their names. In another case, the ejidatario did not farm or contribute to household income. With the help of ejido leaders, his wife, Rosa, proved that she was the household's sole economic provider, and the parcel was reregistered in her name. As Claudia described:

These women manage the fincas alone. If registered [as ejidatarias], they can have titles in their own names. This provides more options . . . to access subsidies . . . and government programs. In some cases it wasn't easy, but we all helped. Enrique went with Rosa thirteen times to Xalapa. Can you imagine? Thirteen trips to register one woman!

Women leaders not only inspired other women to obtain official status; they motivated male colleagues, like Enrique, to help. Adela explained:

When my husband died, I thought about registering the parcel in my name, but I knew it would require going to Xalapa and there would be many forms. . . . Last year Miriam went with me to RAN for the first time. The next time Enrique accompanied me and we completed the notarization. Now I go alone, or with the other women [who are registering].

Adela's fear of travel and officialdom discouraged her from registering the parcel in her name following her husband's death. Nearly a decade later, with support from community members, she began the registration process. Although she expressed frustration with bureaucratic procedures, Adela's initial fears about official documents, government spaces, and travel to the capital were eased. She acknowledged changes in her own capacities and abilities and credited local leaders for initiating the process. Literacy also plays an important role here: Leaders helped less educated women understand and complete forms, access notarizations, and negotiate paper-shuffling procedures. For Adela and others, becoming ejidataria was not an independent practice; rather, it was a process inspired and nurtured, both in verbal support and physical presence, by other community members.

Enrique, the seventy-two-year-old ejido president born and raised in La Grullita, reflected on women's sudden and increasingly important role in local politics and decision making:

If I can speak openly, I didn't know what to think when I learned I'd be serving with the women. It was the first time women were appointed to these positions. The assemblies were always full of sombreros. But in the last two years I became a feminist [laughing] . . . I'm proud of what we've done.

No longer full of sombreros, the gendered dimensions of authority and leadership in the community changed. Enrique admits initial apprehension about serving with the women. Yet, after two years of the new arrangement, he laughs at his previous anxiety. He is proud of what they accomplished together. Indeed, analysis of social relationships mediating resource access and ownership should not only focus on conflict but also identify collaborative forms of gender relations (Ahlers and Zwarteveen 2009).

As of November 2008, thirty-nine women were registered ejidatarias in La Grullita, accounting for one quarter of all members, above the national average. As official ejidatarias, these women now vote in assemblies. Official status also gives them access to resources such as government extension programs and subsidies. By late 2008, as a result of their official status, many new ejidatarias were receiving resources ranging from federal farm subsidies to free coffee plants distributed by state agricultural extension agents. In other words, there are material and political benefits of becoming ejidatarias.

These women also learned to negotiate the bureaucratic maze that helps make the Mexican state. Secor (2007) has shown how money and personal connections allowed individuals to break from cycles of waiting in state-legitimizing procedures in Turkey. Although Adela indeed circulated through bureaucratic hoops, she did so with the support of local leaders, both women and men. In contrast to the work of Secor and others, no bribe was paid, nor were personal connections with government officials required to register parcels. Rather, women became ejidatarias through social relationships rooted in care.

Whatstate agrarian officials encountered is quite different from what PROCEDE's designers had in mind: Women, supported by community members, registered ejido parcels in their names. When I asked one RAN official what he thought of the women registering as ejidatarias, he responded, laughing:

Well, it is not what I expected. I was trained to process PROCEDE documents, not register [ejido] parcels . . . but

[these women] have everything in order . . . papers, notarizations . . . so I process their requests.

Until the community has passed through dominio pleno, reaching full title privatization, even state officials hired to facilitate PROCEDE are beholden to postrevolutionary agrarian law. This particular agrarian reform official helped produce ejidatarias through title registration, revealing how practices of state actors can also diverge from policy objectives. This was indeed an unforeseen outcome of the neoliberal reform designed to dismantle ejidos and highlights how PROCEDE's processual nature opened spaces for unpredictable subjectivities and practices, including those of state actors. Most recently, new strategies were developed to reignite participation in collective institutions. As part of this process, leaders created strategies ranging from fines to tree planting to reinvigorate commons-oriented governance.

## Reclaiming the Commons: Enforcing Ejido Rules

In recent years, community leaders stepped up 'vigilance' as a way of coping with weakened institutions and unapproved land sales. As Claudia explained:

After the certificates . . . people sold land without permission and they didn't come to meetings. . . . How can we make a decision with ten, eleven people at assemblies? And there were other problems, too . . . for people who sold land and for us [leaders] when they complain. We needed a better system for enforcing ejido rules.

Newstravels fast in La Grullita, and leaders now keep detailed records of land transactions, both assemblyapproved and clandestine. For land sales that bypass official channels, both the seller and buyer are fined and presented with a document stating the transaction is not valid per ejido law. 11 Ejidatarios are even fined for not attending assemblies or satisfying other responsibilities such as participation in communal works.

Local leaders not only work to reignite participation in collective governance practices, they also hope to inoculate themselves from future conflicts by issuing the carefully worded documents. If rogue ejidatarios-for instance, those who sold land illegally-have a problem in the future, they will receive little help from the assembly. In other words, the benefits of being an ejidatario, such as assistance from leaders in negotiating agrarian bureaucracy or settling a dispute, are only for those who abide by the rules. Enrique described the reasons behind the new disciplinary strategies:

Twelve years ago we did little enforcing . . . if someone broke a rule they were sometimes fined, but mostly people just gossiped [about the person]. Now we fine ejidatarios . . . for not coming to assemblies, for selling land illegally . . . we even fine them for not paying the fines. We do it to hold them responsible, to remind them [this community] continues as an ejido . . . but we also do it for protection . . . if someone comes to us later with a complaint, we can say, 'Look, that wasn't a legal transaction.'

Between January 2006 and October 2008, the Executive Committee fined fifty-six ejidatarios. By comparison, ejido records from 1990 showed nine individuals fined in a three-year period. All survey respondents noted increased enforcement of ejido rules in recent years. Although some ejidatarios simply disregard the fines and documents, several were motivated to renew participation in collective processes. Nearly a third of survey respondents claimed that they recently increased participation in local institutions, such as assemblies and communal works. Reasons respondents gave for reigniting participation included pressure from family members and neighbors, fear of fines, and acknowledgment that the 'old system' served important purposes.

Strategies to reinvigorate collective decision-making processes and protect local institutions represent an attempt to rein in what local leaders perceive as the negative outcomes of PROCEDE. Young women and older men work together-to develop rules, logs, and official documents-in these new arrangements. Indeed, 'state qualities of governance,' or the abilities to define and enforce collectively binding decisions, are not exclusively nested in government institutions (Lund 2006a). In La Grullita, leaders evoke history and ejido institutions to knit together authority claims about how space should be managed and decisions made. Adherence to traditional systems of collective decision making is at the heart of leaders' actions. In other words, new authorities make claims on institutions developed by the postrevolutionary state (e.g., ejido assembly) to repair what are perceived as negative outcomes of neoliberal state interventions (e.g., clandestine land sales). In the struggle to integrate collective practices with new realities, community leaders also united individuals around environmental narratives.

## Reclaiming the Commons: New Environmental Narratives and Practices

Water-the fincas's ability to soak it up and its perceived future scarcity in nearby urban areasbecame a dominant object around which ejidatarios

developed new environmental understandings and practices. Since INEGI officials visited in 1997 and expressed how impressed they were by the ejido's water resources, community members reproduced this narrative. People attributed shade-grown coffee to creating the high quality and quantity of water and sometimes criticized neighboring ejidos for not performing as well. Afifty-two-year-old ejidataria stated:

Herewetakegoodcareoftheland . . . not like those higher [elevation] communities where they chop down trees. We maintain our coffee well . . . look at the shade [canopy] we have, it's very healthy! This helps with the water . . . to take it in and clean it.

In early 2006, Enrique learned of a 'forest conservation' project administered by Mexico's forestry commission, Comisi´ on Nacional Forestal (CONAFOR). He thought it would provide an excellent opportunity for capitalizing on existing land use and water catchment. Enrique remembered:

I was in Coatepec at the Consejo [regional coffee organization] and heard someone talking about this conservation project. They said CONAFOR would pay ejidos to plant trees . . . we already know a lot about planting [trees] and taking care of water.

Enrique and Lupe visited CONAFOR and received details about the program. Back in La Grullita, they held a meeting to ask community members to participate. Leaders were excited by the possibility of accessing state funds, but they also hoped the project would be a way of uniting community members. As Miriam said in 2006:

It will be good for the ejido . . . it might put something in [people's] pockets, but I think it will also be good for morale, for the spirit of community.

Approximately sixty people showed up for the meeting. Lupe recalled what she said to participants that day in early 2006:

We already know how fertile the soil is . . . if you spit watermelon seeds in the finca, the next time you have a plant. And there is so much water . . . the trees capture it and we can see the quantity flowing in the streams. When the licenciados [college graduates] from INEGI came, they saw it, too. They told us this land is important, that we have a lot of water.

In addition to the meeting, Lupe, Claudia, Miriam, and other women went door-to-door to drum up enthusiasm for the project, claiming household incomes and the environment would benefit. Women saw the project as a way of bringing people together for a positive, and perhaps income-boosting, activity. Miriam laughed as she remembered her sales pitch for the project, 'You plant the tree and it does all the work.'

In March 2006 the ejido began a forestry project with CONAFOR. CONAFOR committed 1,000 pesos per hectare for tree planting but required its engineers oversee the project, and they took a cut. In the end, CONAFORpaid900pesosper hectare. Approximately 150 people participated. One woman recalled:

Myhusband didn't want to do it so I said I would plant the trees myself . . . and I did, every one! I went every Saturday to our finca. It made me very happy to plant those little trees.

In the end, thousands of saplings were planted. Approximately forty hectares of commons land were 'enhanced' with trees; for this land, CONAFOR monies were placed in an ejido development fund. Other saplings were planted on farmers' parcels and the money went directly to individuals. Cedar and mahogany were intercropped with coffee in the lower elevations, and pine, cypress, sweet gum, and oaks were planted in the upper limits. Based on conversations with CONAFOR engineers, ejidatarios thought they were entering a longterm relationship with the agency. Forest diversification and shade canopy maintenance, they believed, would yield future subsidies while promoting a sense of beingin-common centered on environmental stewardship.

In 2008 the ejido submitted a new proposal for the project's next phase. Ejidatarios expected to receive 400 pesos per hectare per year for maintaining the canopy. 12 La Grullita proposed to register 474 hectares, most of the ejido's land, in the project. Shortly before I left Veracruz in 2008, the ejido learned that CONAFOR rejected its proposal. The news came as a shock to leaders who followed the agency's protocol to a tee. CONAFOR claimed that the ejido possessed two small patches of pasture (less than 100 m 2 ) and this was ground for denying La Grullita's participation. Some speculate that CONAFOR's decision to exclude small communities stems from intra-agency competition. Federal resources are distributed to CONAFOR's regional offices depending on the surface area each office enrolls in forestry projects. This fosters competition among regional offices for resources (Veracruz does very well), creating a bias as large landowners and big ejidos are enrolled to maximize surface area.

Ejidatarios in La Grullita were disappointed and confused by their proposal's rejection. As Enrique explained:

I took the map, I took the folder with all the documents, I took everything they asked me for . . . and now they tell me the resources will never come.

Yet, shared understandings of La Grullita's ecological importance and the need to work together to protect the environment seem to have taken hold. At the assembly where leaders broke the bad news, ejidatarios' responses largely focused on understandings of the environment: Didn't the government know how important the ejido's land was for future water supply? Without the fincas, water scarcity at lower elevations would surely occur. CONAFOR should support La Grullita to protect the environment. Secretaries recorded claims and arguments launched by frustrated community members. Leaders scheduled an appointment with CONAFOR, planning to confront officials with their case, which hinged largely on the fincas's ecological benefits. The meeting, moreover, had the best attendance in over a decade.

People's environmental perceptions, along with treeplanting practices and CONAFOR's unexpected rejection, helped build a platform from which La Grullita ejidatarios are, at least for now, reuniting. In October 2008, the ejido voted unanimously to add a new stipulation to Article 7 of the ejido's internal regulations: Ejidatarios are now required to 'protect the watershed' and 'conserve the water that helps maintain the ejido's ecosystem equilibrium.' New environmental understandings of water catchment and supply worked their way into the ejido constitution.

Community members planned to use their environmental knowledge and concern to lobby CONAFOR to reconsider their proposal or, at the very least, provide an adequate explanation of its rejection. Shared understandings of the relationship between coffee forest and water catchment, moreover, appear to be a more effective tool for rebuilding local dialogue and participation than increased vigilance. Ironically, these new environmental understandings and collective practices were largely inspired by INEGI officials nearly fifteen years before. Community members built on the observations of cartographic extension agents to make claims on CONAFOR and rethink community priorities. In this way, too, PROCEDE inspired, through unofficial observations and warnings of INEGI t´ ecnicos, unintended forms of being-in-common as community members rallied around environmental understandings and modified the ejido's constitution. In other words, the comments of state actors charged with carrying out the first steps toward privatization inspired the planting of thousands of trees on usufruct land years later. Indeed, PROCEDE helped reshape La Grullita's gendered authorities as well as its physical and political terrain. It also inspired practices of caring for place.

## Conclusions

PROCEDE did not end collective governance arrangements in La Grullita. Despite entrance in the program fifteen years ago, the ejido did not fully enact dominio pleno. Land is still managed under usufruct rights and has not been legally transformed to private property. External investment and ejidatarios' credit access have not increased dramatically. Shadegrown coffee continues to dominate land use. Yet, PROCEDE, and parcel certification in particular, did catalyze changes within the ejido.

Dispersal of certificates inspired various subjectivities and practices. Men sold land as self-imagined private property owners and decreased participation in traditional governance. Miriam, Claudia, and Lupe seized political openings, undoing historically masculinist forms of local authority. Other women in the community, moreover, became ejidatarias and registered land managers with the help of new ejido leaders. Indeed, unpredictable subjectivities and authorities emerged as people created identities for themselves as leaders, ejidatarias, allies, tree planters, and lobbyers of the neoliberal state. PROCEDE's progress, moreover, was stalled as new subject positions and practices were enacted. State actors, too, became implicated in the implosion of ejido privatization efforts. Government cartographers, impressed with water catchment and flow, warned ejidatarios to sell high and inadvertently inspired tree-planting efforts on communal land years later. Agrarian reform officers, trained to process PROCEDE documents, were surprised to find themselves registering women as ejidatarias. The emergence of women in positions of political power played an important role in shaping these unexpected outcomes.

Miriam, Claudia, and Lupe possessed access to the commons, including the vote in assemblies, prior to La Grullita's engagement with PROCEDE. All three women were also confident with reading, writing, and math skills; had they not been educated ejidatarias who already participated in assemblies at PROCEDE's onset, ejido governance could have turned out differently. Access, even if limited, to land and decision making became important tools that inspired the women's actions and political reconfigurations. Researchers

interested in understanding exclusionary political arrangements and their undoing should examine not only how tools are put into practice but also how practices are enabled by the tools available (Butler 1990, 199). In other words, education and access to the commons can help produce new forms of gendered authority and political trajectories in the neoliberal era (see also Nightingale 2005).

The processual nature of PROCEDE hindered full privatization as community relations were reconfigured. As collective governance was reinvigorated, through disciplinary tactics and environmental narratives, the vote required to complete dominio pleno was not achieved. Progress in the privatization process was thus derailed by new subjectivities and practices ushered in by PROCEDE itself. Indeed, although most of Mexico's 28,000 ejidos and agrarian communities have entered PROCEDE, a small percentage has fully converted to dominio pleno since 1992. The progressive, consensusachieving procedures built into PROCEDE help prevent full transformation of ejido parcels to private property. This finding portends important implications for scholars interested in the variegated outcomes of neoliberalism. To better understand surprising failures of official policy, it is important to examine the ways procedural titling programs combine with other bodies, subjectivities, and environmental narratives to derail and transform neoliberal visions. Analyzing messy entanglements and coproductions reveals the degree to which bodies, objects, and practices intertwine to shape authority and political transformation. Indeed, even seemingly passive instruments such as parcel certificates and ejido titles become objects generative of change (Meehan forthcoming). Scholars might look for transformative coproductions among various actors and things to better understand land reform and gender politics in the neoliberal era.

Finally, by carefully examining the unpredictable outcomes of particular policy interventions, we might find economic certainties and generic stories to be moot. Adela, who waited at RAN that September morning in 2008 for her ejido papers, was not alienated from land and resources through PROCEDE, nor was the parcel privatized. Rather, the staunchly neoliberal reform unleashed the unexpected: Adela and other women are now ejido members who vote and participate in collective decision making. PROCEDE reconfigured La Grullita's socioecological terrain in unforeseen ways. Yet, it did not end the ejido's economic self-determination or communal governance mechanisms. The empowerment of women as ejidatarias and local authorities following PROCEDE, as well as continuation of collective practices, were not what I expected to observe in the field. To understand these unforeseen outcomes, it was necessary to abandon my a priori assumptions about what neoliberal development does. By examining the complex interplays of objects and bodies, I revealed a different story, one with positive implications for rural women and the environment. As nature-society researchers, to be creatures of the mud and not the sky helps reveal the unexpected (Haraway 2008). It is in these spaces we might find novel trajectories, with new forms of politics and pathways to equity.
