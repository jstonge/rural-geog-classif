## Assessing Urban -Rural Differences in Disaster Evacuation Vulnerability Among Minority Populations: Insights from a Recent Survey in Florida

Md. Shaharier Alam a , Mark W. Horner a , and Eren Erman Ozguven b a Florida State University, USA; b Florida A&amp;M University -Florida State University College of Engineering, USA

Minority populations frequently face heightened vulnerabilities compared to other racial groups during disasters. This research investigates how the responses to disaster issues of minority populations vary across rural and urban areas. The study examines differences in disaster evacuation vulnerabilities among minority populations by analyzing their evacuation status and choices of evacuation destinations. The 2023 survey employed a two-target groups approach, sampling minorities of all ages in both urban (eighty people) and rural areas (eighty people), and collected a total of 160 responses from northwest Florida. Our study could be influenced by various disasters, but we assume that people respond based on their perceptions of hurricanes. The survey was conducted using Pollfish, an online tool specializing in mobile application surveys, based on diverse eligibility criteria. Twelve logistic regression models were developed, examining evacuation status and future evacuation destination as dependent variables, with independent variables spanning socioeconomic, health, and past consequences dimensions. The results show that 61.3 percent of urban minorities and 81.3 percent of rural minorities evacuated at least once in past disasters. The study identifies statistically significant variables, highlighting evacuation behavior differences between urban and rural minorities, with implications for emergency management to address marginalized populations ' evacuation needs more effectively. Key Words: evacuation, minority population, Pollfish survey, urban -rural difference, vulnerability.

D isasters might not discriminate, but they can unveil preexisting inequalities within our society, highlighting how various demographic groups experience the same disaster in distinct ways (Van Willigen et al. 2002). Minority populations face elevated mortality rates and substantial obstacles due to underlying inequalities during disasters (Downey 2016; Sharpe and Wolkin 2022). Mortality rates during disasters in the United States were significantly higher for non-Hispanic Black individuals (1.87 times) and American Indian/Alaska Native individuals (7.34 times) compared to non-Hispanic White individuals, based on mortality data analysis from 1999 to 2018 (Sharpe and Wolkin 2022). Black/African American and Hispanic communities have experienced more adverse mental health outcomes during hurricane evacuations than their White counterparts (Messias and Lacy 2007; Davidson et al. 2013). The aftermath of hurricanes has been particularly devastating for low-income minority communities in places such as New Orleans, with greater damage, higher unemployment rates and, increased psychological distress compared with White communities (Elder et al. 2007; Messias, Barrington, and Lacy 2012; Lewis et al. 2019). Evacuation policies favoring private transportation have hindered African Americans ' ability to prepare and evacuate during storms, leading to disparities due to perceived vulnerability, financial constraints, neighborhood crime, and

Sometimes, undocumented immigrants face even greater barriers during emergencies, including language barriers, limited social networks for support, financial constraints, and fears of legal repercussions tied to their immigration status (Messias, Barrington, and Lacy 2012; Wilson and Tiefenbacher 2012).

The challenges faced by minority populations can differ in urban and rural settings, as both areas possess unique sets of advantages and disadvantages, rendering them both vulnerable and resilient in distinct ways (Carpender et al. 2006; Kapucu, Hawkins, and Rivera 2013; Cutter, Ash, and Emrich 2016). Rural communities are vulnerable to disasters due to their less diversified economy, geographic isolation, inadequate transport infrastructure, poverty, decreasing population, weaker planning, inequitable access to recovery resources and administrative capacity, and so on (Davies et al. 2009; Kapucu, Hawkins, and Rivera 2013; Horney et al. 2017). Additionally, rural areas often have smaller and less diverse workforces, which can lead to difficulties in evacuating and providing disaster assistance (Ye et al. 2010). The lack of roadside assistance (emergency services to fix vehicle mechanical or electrical issues), amenities, and traffic control services further complicates matters and makes it difficult to evacuate safely (Ye et al. 2010).

Although urban areas are advantaged with all the facilities just mentioned compared to rural areas, they have different challenges, making them vulnerable to disasters as well. The vulnerability of urban areas to disasters stems from rapid population increases, fragile physical environments, housing scarcity, large numbers of marginalized populations, urbanization-induced flooding, evacuation congestion, and declining economic and social structure (Comfort 2006; Sterzel et al. 2020). Urban areas lack self-sufficiency, knowledge of and connections to natural resources, strong social capital, and a sense of community that is common in rural areas (Straub et al. 2020). At the same time, disasterimpacted areas and victims in urban areas receive more attention in terms of quick response, lifesaving service delivery, medical accessibility, and long-term recovery (Shan, Yu, and Wu 2017; Wang et al. 2022).

In recent decades, though, a substantial amount of scholarly attention has been directed toward comprehending urban vulnerability, whereas the unique risks present in rural areas are often overlooked (Cutter, Ash, and Emrich 2016). Also, there are no studies that endeavor to measure and contrast the disaster vulnerability of minority populations residing in urban and rural environments. Therefore, this research is the first to investigate how minority populations perceive and respond to disasters, shedding light on their vulnerabilities and the disparities in urban and rural settings. Specifically, this research examines differences in disaster needs and vulnerabilities among minority populations by analyzing their evacuation status and choices of evacuation destinations during hurricanes. The research focuses on the Florida Panhandle, a highly hurricane-prone region. Although our study encompasses a range of disasters, respondents are assumed to be responding based on their perceptions of hurricanes.

This study defines the minority population as including all non-White groups, which includes African American, Asian, Hispanic, Latino, Arab, and multiracial populations. To accomplish this objective, we investigate the following research inquiries: Which factors influence the evacuation status and future evacuation destination choices of minority individuals across the urban -rural divide?

The remainder of the article is organized as follows. We first provide detailed descriptions of the background literature, before delving into the study area and the classification of urban and rural regions. We then outline the methodology, encompassing the survey and statistical approach. After a thorough discussion of the results of the regression models, we discuss the key findings, policy implications, and limitations. Finally, the article concludes with key takeaways and an overarching holistic perspective.

## Background Literature

## Disaster and Vulnerability

Disaster is an evolving concept and mostly defined as social disruption resulting from the convergence of a hazardous event and the societal response or the absence thereof (Perry 2018). Hoffman and OliverSmith (2002) described disasters as the points where geophysical events intersect with vulnerable populations, arising from ongoing natural and social processes over time. The extent of the disaster is not only determined by the severity of the hazard but also by how well society manages and mitigates its impact (Kusenbach, Simms, and Tobin 2010; Kelman et al. 2016).

Understanding the vulnerability to natural disasters is complicated because of its conceptual complexity (Cutter 2003). Broadly, the vulnerability to natural hazards has been viewed from two major perspectives (Wang et al. 2022). The first group viewed disaster from the lens of structural or physical characteristics. Early research in the field of disasters viewed disasters as an outcome of geophysical processes and predominantly focused on the hazard ' s physical characteristics (Blaikie et al. 2004). This paradigm led to government interventions that focused more on structural solutions, which eventually failed to manage this situation properly (Thomas et al. 2009). Researchers and policymakers eventually realized that only reducing physical or structural exposure to disaster cannot solve the underlying issues. Since then, vulnerability to natural hazards has been investigated from a different viewpoint: the societal perspective (Flanagan et al. 2011). The paradigm of considering the societal response to natural hazards started in the 1970s but did not gain enough attention at that time (Kates 1971; O ' Keefe, Westgate, and Wisner 1976). Later the multiple dimensions of vulnerability, such as social, economic, and so on, were more emphasized by many other researchers in the early twenty-first century (Cutter 1996, 2012; Neil Adger 1999; Birkmann 2006). Over time, the researchers seem to have reached common ground in propounding vulnerability as the complex interaction of biophysical and social aspects that vary across space and time (Cutter 1996, 2003; Cutter, Ash, and Emrich 2016; Bolin and Kurtz 2018; Straub et al. 2020; Tate et al. 2021). The United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction described vulnerability as the traits shaped by various physical, social, economic, and environmental elements or processes that heighten the likelihood of individuals, communities, assets, or systems being affected by hazards (Gabel et al. 2022). Vulnerability is not constant, and it varies over time and space among different social groups. Whereas measuring physical vulnerability is uncomplicated from the previous hazard ' s knowledge,

social vulnerability analysis is more complex because of its temporal and spatial variability (Cutter and Emrich 2006; Straub et al. 2020). Two demographic groups, such as the elderly and the general population, living in the same area, can experience varying degrees of vulnerability during and after disasters (Alam, Kim, et al. 2024).

To understand this interaction, Cutter (1996) introduced the hazards-of-place model, which defines the vulnerability of a location as a combination of social characteristics (represented by specific socioeconomic demographics) and geophysical risk (represented by probabilities of occurrence). This research analyzed the societal elements of disasters that influence specific social groups and their spatial variation, particularly in the context of evacuation. Although focused on infrastructure aspects that influence evacuation, Yorke et al. (2015) argued that evacuation potential of a place largely determines the vulnerability of that place to disasters; therefore, it is important to incorporate evacuation-related factors into the concept of place-based vulnerability. Vulnerability in the context of evacuation refers to the degree to which individuals, particularly those who are transportation disadvantaged, are exposed to danger and have difficulty evacuating due to personal limitations or inadequate public transportation (Bian and Wilmot 2017). It is the complex interaction among multiple vulnerabilities including social, physical, and psychological vulnerabilities, that is faced primarily by the socially vulnerable groups of society.

## Social (In)Equity and Vulnerable Populations

Social equity is a broad concept with varying definitions across academic disciplines, but it generally refers to ensuring fairness and equal treatment for different individuals or groups within society, including socially vulnerable populations (Rivera and Knox 2023). In the context of disaster management, social equity refers to ensuring that disaster-vulnerable or affected individuals have equal access to resources and opportunities to meet their needs, regardless of race, ethnicity, gender, income, or other social stratifications (Renne 2006; Emrich et al. 2020; Dhakal, Zhang, and Lv 2021; Rivera and Knox 2023). To ensure social equity during disasters, researchers must deeply understand the specific needs and vulnerabilities of different socially vulnerable groups who are already disadvantaged in society. Existing literature on social equity in disaster contexts mostly focuses on areas such as social vulnerability (Cutter 2003; Flanagan et al. 2011), equitable recovery (Emrich et al. 2020; Griego et al. 2020), social justice (Dom  ı nguez and Yeh 2020; Gurtner and King 2021), and social resilience (Coleman, Esmalian, and Mostafavi 2020; Dhakal, Zhang, and Lv 2021). The geographical variation of social (in)equity during disasters, however, such as urban -rural differences, is not adequately studied, overlooking how social inequality can vary across different geographic spaces. Although some studies have explored differences in disaster resilience (Cutter, Ash, and Emrich 2016), preparedness (Chai et al. 2021), or recovery (Park, Yao, and Ukkusuri 2024) between urban and rural areas, they tend to be broad in scope and do not specifically address how the evacuation vulnerability of minority populations in urban settings differs from the vulnerability those in rural areas. This research fills this gap in understanding how the evacuation vulnerability of urban minorities can differ from that of rural minorities.

The definition of minority populations can include a wide range of factors such as race, ethnicity, religion, and socioeconomic status, and can differ depending on geographic context (Bolin and Kurtz 2018). In the context of disaster management in the United States, though, the term minority predominantly refers to racial and ethnic minorities, specifically non-White populations (Fothergill, Maestas, and Darlington 1999; Davidson et al. 2013). Thus, this study also defines the minority population as including all non-White groups, which includes African American, Asian, Hispanic, Latino, Arab, and multiracial populations.

## Study Area

This study was carried out in the Florida Panhandle, known for its significant concentration of minority populations, and recognized as one of the most disaster-prone regions in the United States. Florida is vulnerable to disaster due to its tropical climate, exposure to oceans from three sides, and low sealevel elevation (Ghorbanzadeh et al. 2021; Miller, Arias, and Alvarez 2021). After two consecutive hurricanes -Hermine (2016) and Irma (2017) -the Florida Panhandle ' s oceanfront counties faced near devastation from Hurricane Michael (2018), one of the four most powerful hurricanes in U.S. history (Senkbeil et al. 2020). Florida is also ranked as the tenth most racially diverse state and is also home to a significant minority population and a large number of undocumented immigrants (Pew Research Center 2019; U.S. Census Bureau 2020).

This research was conducted in the eighteen Panhandle counties of northwest Florida: Escambia, Santa Rosa, Okaloosa, Walton, Holmes, Washington, Bay, Jackson, Calhoun, Gulf, Gadsden, Liberty, Franklin, Leon, Wakulla, Jefferson, Taylor, and Madison. We categorized the region ' s constituent zip codes into urban and rural categories, following the classifications outlined by AbdelMagid et al. (2023). To ensure accurate representation of rural populations, Florida ' s rural zip codes were identified using the definition of rurality provided

by the Florida Department of Transportation Office of Policy Planning. Employing the zip code files from the Federal Office of Rural Health Policy and the Census Bureau ' s 2018 American Community Survey zip code geography, the Florida Panhandle counties were classified with seventy-five rural and fifty-none urban zip codes (Figure 1).

## Survey Approach

We developed a questionnaire related to disaster scenarios in Florida, covering different aspects of disaster and approved by the Institutional Review Board of Florida State University. For this survey, we created two audience groups in Pollfish. The first group includes individuals who are non-White (Black/African American, Hispanic, Latino, Arab, Asian, or multiracial), age eighteen and older, and living in urban zip codes. The second group has the same criteria, except that they reside in rural zip codes. We collected 160 samples, evenly distributed between urban and rural minorities, with a 95 percent confidence interval and 7.8 percent margin of error. ## Statistical Methods

This study explored the factors influencing evacuation behavior among urban and rural minorities while exploring the impact of multiple binary independent factors on our decision variable for both groups. Initially, we examined whether there is a relationship between evacuation status during past disasters and future evacuation destination choices for these groups. To achieve this, we employed a chi-square test to determine if the evacuation behavior of respondents is associated with their urban or rural status. The chi-square test checks the independence of categorical variables and assesses how well an observed distribution fits an expected distribution (Das et al. 2023).

Additionally, to understand the strength of the relationship between urban and rural minorities concerning their evacuation status and destination choices, we used the Phi ( u ) coefficient and Cram  er ' s V measures. The Phi coefficient is used for 2 -2 contingency tables, whereas Cram  er ' s V is used for larger contingency tables. Scores for both measures range from 0 to 1, where 0 indicates negligible association and 1 indicates a strong association (Das et al. 2023). These measures provide insight into the strength of the relationship between categorical variables, complementing the chi-square test, which only indicates the presence or absence of a significant association.

Later, we employed logistic regression models, such as binary logistic regression and multinomial logistic regression, to analyze which factor is influencing these urban -rural differences. Respondents were asked whether they evacuated due to past disasters and their potential destinations if future evacuation becomes necessary. Twelve logistic regression models were developed, centering on evacuation status and future destination choice, as dependent variables. The independent variables included socio1economic factors, health-related aspects, and variables related to the consequences of disasters. Variables were selected based on relevant scholarly literature findings. Before running the regression models, we addressed multicollinearity issues among the independent variables by checking the variance inflation factor (VIF) values and assessing the normality of data distribution for each variable. We evaluated the VIF values for the entire data set and also separately for urban and rural subsets (see Appendix for details). As supported by much of the literature, a VIF value of 10 is commonly regarded by practitioners as indicative of severe multicollinearity (O ' Brien 2007; James et al. 2021). Therefore, using this VIF threshold, we reduced some independent variables to mitigate multicollinearity. The finalized independent variables are presented in Table 1.

Binary logistic regression examines the relationship between a binary dependent variable and one or more independent variables (King 2008). Six binary logistic regression models have been developed to analyze evacuation status (yes -no) as a binary dependent variable. Three models are developed for urban areas and three for rural areas. For urban areas, the first model includes socioeconomic factors as independent variables, the second model incorporates health factors, and the third model focuses on disaster consequence variables. Similarly, three corresponding models have been created for rural areas. The dependent variable remains the same across all six models. ## Urban -Rural Differences in Evacuation Behavior of Minorities

In the Florida Panhandle, 61.3 percent of urban minorities and 81.3 percent of rural minorities were compelled to evacuate as a result of the disasters they faced. Both urban and rural minority populations demonstrate similar evacuation behavior before hurricane landfall. A majority of the urban minority respondents evacuated within six hours before the landfall (32.7 percent), closely followed by those who evacuated within six to twelve hours before the landfall (28.6 percent). Similarly, the majority of rural minority respondents evacuated within six hours (35.4 percent) and six to twelve hours (35.4 percent) before the landfall. Interestingly, urban minorities showed a higher tendency to evacuate more than twenty-four hours before landfall (16.3 percent) compared to rural minorities (9.2 percent).

The analysis also reveals that a significant number of respondents favored seeking shelter in the homes of friends (24.72 percent), and a comparable percentage opted for accommodations in hotels, motels, or Airbnb (25.84 percent). Similarly, an equal proportion of respondents chose to evacuate to public hurricane shelters (25.84 percent) and specialized facilities for individuals with special needs (21.35 percent). A small percentage of respondents explored alternative evacuation options (2.25 percent). A majority of rural minorities (31.88 percent) opted to seek shelter in the homes of friends, and a significant proportion chose accommodations in hotels, motels, or Airbnb (23.19 percent).

The model indicates middle-aged minority people (thirty to forty-four years old) were less likely to evacuate during previous hurricanes than other age groups in urban areas. Age is a significant variable frequently examined in research on evacuation intentions. Gladwin 2017; Alam, Horner, et al. 2024). ## Discussion

Understanding urban -rural differences in disaster evacuation vulnerability is crucial for developing tailored, efficient, and equitable disaster management strategies that address the specific needs of each area (Carpender et al. 2006; Cutter, Ash, and Emrich 2016). One significant yet often overlooked aspect by disaster managers is the vulnerability of minority populations living in both settings. This study focuses on the Florida Panhandle, a hurricane-prone region with a significant minority population, to assess disparities in hurricane evacuation challenges among minority groups in urban and rural areas, examining differences in evacuation status and destination choice. Data were gathered using Pollfish, an online survey platform, allowing for quick and comprehensive collection. The research findings provide critical insights into the factors influencing the evacuation behavior of urban and rural minorities.

In urban areas, evacuation behavior among minorities is primarily influenced by economic factors, with unemployment being a significant predictor. Despite financial challenges, unemployed urban minorities are more likely to evacuate and often opt for hotel, motel, or Airbnb accommodations. This preference might arise from the vulnerabilities associated with unemployment, such as a potential lack of access to a personal vehicle for long-distance evacuation, having limited social networks, or undocumented immigration status (Adeola 2009; Golshani et al. 2019), which preclude one from choosing public shelters or staying with families or friends. Evacuation can be expensive, although hotels in Florida are legally required to keep prices

constant or sometimes offer distress rates during hurricanes. As such, unemployed individuals without access to a car in urban areas might choose affordable sheltering options among hotels, motels, or Airbnbs. Also, previous evacuation studies focusing on undocumented immigrants have found that they might often avoid government-provided shelters due to fears of being identified and deported after a disaster (Wilson and Tiefenbacher 2012). Given this trend, communication strategies should ensure clear information about evacuation routes and available shelters for unemployed individuals. Additionally, resources should be allocated to assist with transportation and other necessities, and policies should include financial assistance and support services during evacuations. The Disaster Unemployment Assistance (DUA) program by the Federal Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) offers financial support to those who became unemployed by major disasters, but many undocumented minority populations are ineligible due to restrictions limiting benefits to U.S. nationals and qualified aliens (Morey 2012). Policies should focus on more inclusive financial assistance programs for minority populations to reduce the vulnerabilities.

Housing issues, particularly for those in assisted living conditions, play a pivotal role in evacuation behavior. Urban minorities in assisted housing tend to prefer public or special needs shelters due to factors such as chronic illness, limited social connections, and financial constraints (Henderson, Roberto, and Kamo 2010; Malik et al. 2018). Past evacuations of elderly individuals from assisted living facilities have faced challenges due to inadequate preparedness, communication, and transportation (Castro et al. 2008; Brown et al. 2015). With the growing demand for assisted living, these facilities must be integrated into disaster preparedness and response plans. Effective strategies should cover sheltering in place and evacuation, with policies emphasizing evacuation plans, drills, and specialized transportation for chronically ill minorities.

The evacuation behavior of rural minorities is significantly influenced by social and health-related factors, with gender and race playing crucial roles. Rural female minorities and Black/African American individuals exhibit distinct evacuation patterns. Women in rural areas are less likely to evacuate, possibly due to privacy concerns and past experiences of vulnerability (Lowe, Lustig, and Marrow 2011; Kyne et al. 2020). To address this, policies should ensure that evacuation centers are safe, secure, and private, and prioritize additional support services such as mental health support and childcare facilities. Outreach and education efforts should focus on addressing safety concerns and improving access to secure public shelters for women.

Conversely, rural Black/African American minorities tend to prefer public shelters over staying with friends or relatives, likely due to limited social capital and connections (Aldrich and Meyer 2015; Hong and Frias-Martinez 2020). To support this group, policies should focus on strengthening social networks and ensuring equitable access to resources for effective evacuation planning. This includes increasing community engagement, developing community-based evacuation plans, and providing financial assistance when needed. Effective disaster communication channels should also be established. Community leaders, local organizations, and faithbased organizations can play a critical role in providing timely information and support for evacuation.

Health-related factors significantly affect evacuation behavior in both urban and rural areas. Primary caregivers, whether in urban or rural settings, are less likely to evacuate due to concerns about suitable facilities and transportation options (Blanchard and Dosa 2009; Ambrose, Cardei, and Cardei 2010; Christensen and Casta ~ neda 2014; Icenogle, Eastburn, and Arrieta 2016). Policies should provide comprehensive support, clear guidelines on shelter facilities, and accessible transportation tailored to their needs. Special evacuation plans should be developed for primary caregivers who are less likely to evacuate due to their responsibilities. Shelters should be equipped with resources and facilities to support caregivers and their dependents, addressing their concerns about health care, equipment needs, and treatment continuity. Furthermore, minorities facing health challenges, such as chronic illnesses, predominantly choose public or special needs shelters. Enhancing these shelters to accommodate specific health-care needs is crucial.

Past disaster experiences also shape evacuation behavior. In urban areas, past negative experiences with temporary housing were also found to negatively affect evacuation decisions. Past experiences with relocation might influence their perception due to various factors such as discomfort, privacy issues, safety concerns, or potential harassment in temporary housing (F  elix, Branco, and Feio 2013; Dostal 2015; Glass et al. 2022). Establishing and enforcing high standards for temporary housing is crucial to ensure accommodations are comfortable, safe, and dignified. This includes providing on-site mental health services to address the trauma and stress associated with evacuation and temporary displacement. Furthermore, it is essential to ensure that temporary housing respects the cultural and social needs of diverse communities. Conversely, in rural areas, those who experienced employment loss during past disasters are less inclined to evacuate. Previous studies support these findings, showing that many, especially minorities, often do not evacuate during hurricanes due to fear of losing their jobs (Messias and Lacy 2007; Burke, Bethel, and Britt 2012). Policies should implement job protection to ensure evacuees can return to work postdisaster. A focus

should also be on creating temporary employment opportunities in disaster recovery and offering financial support to alleviate the economic burden of evacuation, encouraging compliance with evacuation orders.

These findings underscore the need for tailored policy interventions that address the unique vulnerabilities of urban and rural minority populations. For urban areas, financial assistance, support services, and comprehensive evacuation plans for assisted housing residents are crucial. In rural areas, targeted outreach, education, and improved shelter accessibility are key. Addressing health-related vulnerabilities and the influence of past disaster experiences requires enhancing public and special needs shelters and providing clear, comprehensive support for primary caregivers and individuals with chronic illnesses. Understanding these nuanced differences in evacuation behavior is vital for developing effective disaster management strategies that ensure the safety and well-being of all minority populations in both urban and rural areas.

Nevertheless, the study does have certain limitations that can serve as opportunities for future research. This study examines hurricane evacuation behavior among minority populations. It does not, however, directly analyze the reasons behind these behaviors, instead relying on past literature to explain potential reasons why minorities might or might not choose to evacuate, and the destinations they opt for. Future research could benefit from more comprehensive data collection and in-depth analysis to better understand these evacuation patterns.

Also, the study faced limitations with the Pollfish platform, where the study analyzed a small but representative sample of 160 survey responses from minority populations in the Florida Panhandle with a 7.8 percent margin of error and a 95 percent confidence interval. The overall smaller sample size was due to resource constraints. A margin of error from 4 percent to 8 percent or even up to 10 percent is considered acceptable in survey research, specifically with a small number of responses (Okoye 2018; Kennedy 2021; Burrill 2023). Moreover, similar sample sizes are commonly found in previously published studies related to hurricane evacuation. For instance, Christensen, Richey, and Casta ~ neda (2013) surveyed 186 individuals, including Alzheimer ' s patients and caregivers, in South Florida to study hurricane evacuation behavior. Rosenkoetter et al. (2007) surveyed 139 older adults to examine hurricane evacuation perceptions in two counties in Georgia. Lazo et al. (2010) surveyed eighty individuals to understand hurricane evacuation decision-making in Miami, which has a much larger population overall than the Florida Panhandle.

Generally, collecting survey data from specific racial or ethnic groups is challenging due to their often smaller population sizes, possible more limited internet access for online surveys, language barriers, and lower response rates (Ahlmark et al. 2015; Kappelhof 2017). For example, White et al. (2007) conducted 110 interviews with African Americans after Hurricane Katrina in New Orleans. Li et al. (2008) surveyed 113 Vietnamese Americans and sixty-nine African Americans after Hurricane Katrina in New Orleans. Messias, Barrington, and Lacy (2012) interviewed sixty-five Latinos to explore social network dynamics after Hurricane Katrina. Peguero (2006) surveyed 165 Latino homeowners in Florida. All these studies highlight the challenges of data collection from minority groups, particularly in the context of hurricanes. Considering the literature on minority populations during disasters, we believe this study surveyed an adequate sample to yield meaningful insights into evacuation vulnerability and key issues in understanding the perceptions of urban and rural minorities. Future research could benefit from a larger sample size and perhaps a different study area for even more comprehensive insights and validate our findings.

Additionally, comparing the evacuation behavior of minority populations with other vulnerable groups, such as the elderly, could provide a deeper understanding of the unique challenges. Koloushani et al. (2022) surveyed 398 older adults in Florida to assess factors that might affect older adults ' decision of evacuations; however, they did not fully focus on a comparison with other minorities. Another potential limitation of this study is the divergence between individuals ' stated intentions and actual behavior during a disaster. Reliance on self-reported responses regarding evacuation plans might not accurately reflect real-life actions (Kang, Lindell, and Prater 2007; Reinhardt 2017; Whitmer and Sims 2021). To address this limitation, future research could employ longitudinal studies to observe changes between initial intentions and actual behavior during a disaster. Moreover, it is important to acknowledge that minorities living in close proximity to the ocean might exhibit different evacuation behaviors compared to those residing farther inland, which is not explored in this study, a potential limitation. Nonetheless, this study enhances comprehension of minority populations ' hurricane evacuation behavior, providing a basis for more effective disaster management strategies.

## Conclusion

This research analyzes evacuation behavior of minorities, highlighting urban -rural disparities and revealing the socioeconomic, health, and consequence-related challenges that necessitate context-specific disaster preparedness and response strategies. The study underscores the importance of recognizing the distinct challenges faced by urban and rural minorities,

challenging the conventional one-size-fits-all approach to disaster management (Steinfort 2017; Adepoju et al. 2022). Socioeconomic and health factors shape evacuation behavior for minorities differently in urban and rural areas. Tailored policies and improved shelter access, especially for health needs, are essential to address these disparities. The insights from this study can inform disaster management strategies beyond hurricane evacuations, applying to floods, wildfires, and earthquakes where minority populations face unique vulnerabilities. By addressing the needs of marginalized communities, policymakers can create more inclusive and effective emergency response plans. Future research should also continue exploring how socioeconomic, health, and prior disaster experiences shape evacuation behavior. Longitudinal studies can offer insights into how these factors evolve over time and across regions. Expanding research to include other vulnerable groups, such as the elderly, immigrants, people with disabilities, and low-income households, would deepen our understanding of evacuation challenges and improve disaster management.

These findings have applications in public health, urban planning, emergency management, and social services. Urban planners can design more inclusive housing, emergency managers can develop tailored evacuation plans, and public health officials can create accessible health-care services during disasters. This research aligns with FEMA ' s National Preparedness Goal, the Sendai Framework, and the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals, particularly those addressing gender equality, reduced inequalities, and sustainable communities. Focusing on minority evacuation challenges can reduce vulnerabilities and strengthen community resilience. Overall, this study emphasizes the need for context-specific disaster management strategies that address the unique challenges of urban and rural minorities, enhance resilience, reduce disproportionate impacts, and emphasize equity and inclusiveness in future research and policies to protect all populations during disasters. /H17039

## Multicollinearity Tables

To calculate the VIF for our categorical variables, we created dummy variables representing each category as separate independent variables. We created k -1 dummy variables for each categorical variable with k categories. For example, we created two dummy variables for the housing category: one for ' Housing (Apt./house/condominium) ' and another for ' Housing (assisted housing living), ' assigning binary values to each. We then performed regression analysis using all dummy independent variables in SPSS and generated VIF tables for each variable. We evaluated the VIF values for the entire data set and also separately for urban and rural subsets. As supported by much of the literature, a VIF value of 10 is commonly regarded by practitioners as indicative of severe multicollinearity (O ' Brien 2007; James et al. 2021).
