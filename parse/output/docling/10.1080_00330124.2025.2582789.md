## Geographical Disparities in Energy Access: Challenges and Opportunities for Ghana ' s Energy Transition

Akwasi Adu-Poku a , Ivy Gladys Appiah b , and Francis Kemausuor a a Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology, Ghana; b Multigeomatics Land, Mining, and Engineering Surveyors, Ghana

This study examines geographical disparities in energy access across Ghana, analyzing the spatial patterns and socioeconomic drivers that influence energy distribution. Using a mixed-methods approach combining geospatial analysis with statistical evaluation of regional data, the research reveals significant disparities between northern and southern regions, as well as between urban and rural areas. Although Ghana maintains an impressive national electrification rate of 89 percent, access varies dramatically from 99 percent in the Greater Accra region to as low as 61 percent in the Savannah region. The study identifies key factors contributing to these disparities, including proximity to power generation facilities, population density, and economic activity levels. Analysis of clean cooking fuel access shows similar spatial patterns, with liquified petroleum gas adoption ranging from 69 percent in Greater Accra to minimal levels in northern regions. The study proposes pathways for a just energy transition, emphasizing the role of decentralized renewable energy systems, public -private partnerships, and targeted policy interventions. Drawing lessons from successful initiatives in Kenya and Rwanda, the study provides actionable recommendations for addressing spatial inequities in Ghana ' s energy landscape. The findings contribute to the broader discourse on achieving universal energy access while highlighting the importance of geographical considerations in energy policy and infrastructure development. Key Words: clean cooking, electricity, energy access, energy transition, geographical disparities.

A ccess to modern energy services is a critical determinant of socioeconomic development and a cornerstone for achieving global sustainability targets (IRENA 2023). To measure progress toward United Nations Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 7.1 and for our future analysis, the International Energy Agency (IEA) defines energy access as a household ' s ability to reliably and affordably access both electricity and clean cooking facilities (IEA 2020). This access should provide enough energy to meet basic needs and, over time, reach the regional average for essential energy services. Official measures of access, however, often count only formal grid connections and registered energy providers, which could overlook the informal or illegal connections (e.g., shared meters, independent solar kits) that many Ghanaian households rely on.

Although significant progress has been made globally in expanding energy access, many regions, particularly in sub-Saharan Africa, continue to face profound challenges in bridging the energy divide (Bishoge, Kombe, and Mvile 2020; Omole, Olajiga, and Olatunde 2024). The 2024 SDG reports show that sub-Saharan Africa now accounts for 83 percent (or 570 million people) of the global access deficit in 2022, an increase from 50 percent in 2010 (United Nations 2024).

In recent years, there has been growing recognition of the importance of spatial dimensions in energy policy, highlighting how geography influences the distribution and accessibility of energy resources. The global initiative to achieve universal energy access by 2030, as outlined in SDG 7, underscores the necessity to tackle energy access disparities between and within countries (United Nations 2024). Ghana is a leader in sub-Saharan Africa regarding electricity access, with an impressive national electrification rate of 89 percent (Energy Commission 2024). This achievement has resulted from decades of deliberate investments in energy infrastructure, policy reforms, and international collaborations. Ghana ' s energy sector has implemented significant initiatives, including the deployment of solar photovoltaic (PV) systems in the Northern, Upper East, and Upper West regions, which capitalize on the country ' s high solar irradiation. Additionally, the introduction of solar-powered minigrids in island and lakeside communities, such as those in the Volta Basin, has expanded electricity access to remote areas that are challenging to connect to the national grid (Adu-Poku et al. 2023). In the Ashanti region, the commissioning of the K1 Thermal Power Plant (formerly known as the AMERI Power Plant in the Western region) in 2024 marks another significant

This development aims to reduce transmission losses and enhance grid reliability in the middle belt of Ghana. Despite these advancements, substantial disparities persist in electricity access, particularly between northern and southern regions and between urban and rural areas.

Southern Ghana, home to major economic hubs such as Accra and Kumasi, has achieved nearuniversal access to electricity due to its proximity to energy generation facilities, higher population density, and concentrated economic activities (Energy Commission 2024). Conversely, northern Ghana continues to grapple with lower electrification rates, limited access to modern cooking fuels, and underdeveloped infrastructure. Similarly, whereas urban areas across the country enjoy relatively high levels of energy access, many rural communities remain underserved, facing both infrastructural and socioeconomic barriers to reliable energy, mirroring the subregional trend in rural electrification (Omole, Olajiga, and Olatunde 2024).

These spatial disparities in Ghana ' s energy landscape are influenced by a range of geographical and socioeconomic factors. Proximity to the national grid, population density, and levels of economic activity play pivotal roles in shaping energy access patterns. Access to clean cooking fuels such as liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) also follows a similar spatial pattern, with southern and urban areas enjoying better availability and affordability compared to northern and rural areas. These disparities not only highlight the inequities in Ghana ' s energy transition but also underscore the broader challenges of ensuring sustainable and inclusive energy development.

Globally, there are several successful initiatives aimed at bridging energy access gaps, many of which hold valuable lessons for Ghana. For instance, Rwanda ' s Renewable Energy Fund (REF) demonstrates the effectiveness of targeted public -private partnerships to expand off-grid solar plants in rural communities, a model that could be adapted to Ghana ' s northern regions (Climate Investment Funds [CIF] 2022). Similarly, Kenya ' s electrification efforts, through programs like the Last Mile Connectivity Project (LMCP) and Power Africa Off-grid Project (PAOP), particularly its focus on off-grid solar solutions, have significantly increased rural energy access, reducing dependence on fossil fuels and raising national access rates (USAID 2020; Davis 2021). In Bangladesh, the Solar Home Systems (SHS) program has transformed the energy landscape in remote areas by providing affordable and reliable solar energy solutions to millions of households (World Bank Group 2021). These initiatives underscore the importance of targeted, context-specific strategies that address the unique challenges of underserved regions while leveraging local resources and capacities.

Despite Ghana ' s progress and the wealth of global examples, gaps remain in the understanding and addressing of geographical disparities in energy access within the country. Existing research and policy efforts have often focused on aggregate electrification rates, overlooking the nuanced spatial patterns that characterize energy access and consumption (Mensah, Kemausuor, and BrewHammond 2014; Gyamfi, Modjinou, and Djordjevic 2015; Kemausuor and Ackom 2017). Furthermore, there is limited analysis of how geographical factors such as proximity to infrastructure, resource availability, and socioeconomic conditions shape energy access in different parts of Ghana. This gap in understanding not only limits the effectiveness of current policies but also constrains the ability to design interventions that are equitable, efficient, and sustainable.

This study aims to address existing gaps by examining the geographical disparities in energy access in Ghana and identifying pathways toward a just energy transition. Official statistics in Ghana, however, often rely on formally registered customers and energy service providers, potentially underrepresenting the true extent of energy access by excluding informal or illegal connections, which are common in slum areas and among low-income groups. Such informal access, often through unregulated or unsafe means, is prevalent in Ghana and complicates the energy access landscape. Although this study primarily uses official data due to availability, it acknowledges that actual disparities might be more pronounced when informal access is considered, highlighting the need for a nuanced understanding of energy access in policy planning. By combining geospatial analysis with statistical assessments and policy evaluations, the study seeks to provide a thorough understanding of how spatial factors affect energy access patterns. Additionally, it will explore strategies to bridge these gaps and contribute valuable insights to the ongoing discourse on energy transition, offering actionable recommendations for policymakers, energy planners, and other stakeholders.

## Methodology

Study Area

Ghana, situated in West Africa, spans latitudes 4  44 0 N to 11  11 0 N and longitudes 3  11 0 W to 1  11 0 E, encompassing a total land area of 238,535 km 2 . The country ' s administrative structure comprises sixteen regions, each characterized by distinct geographical and socioeconomic features that significantly

influence the distribution of energy infrastructure and patterns of energy access.

The country ' s electricity infrastructure is predominantly concentrated in the southern regions, with major power generation facilities distributed across strategic locations. The hydroelectric infrastructure includes the Akosombo hydroelectric plant (1,020 MW), Kpong hydroelectric plant (160 MW), and Bui hydroelectric plant (404 MW; Energy Commission 2024). Thermal power generation is centered around industrial hubs, with significant installations in Tema, Takoradi, and Aboadze. The transmission network extends approximately 6,472 kilometers, with sixty-eight bulk supply points consisting of high-voltage lines operating at 161 kV (76.5percent of the total circuit length), 69kV (3.4 percent of the total circuit length), 225 kV (1.5 percent of the total circuit length), and 330kV (18.6 percent of the total circuit length), whereas the distribution network comprises mediumand lowvoltage lines serving various communities across the country (GRIDCo 2022).

## Data Collection

The study used the most recent disaggregated data available at the regional level, secondary data from multiple authoritative sources to examine electricity access patterns and cooking fuel usage across Ghana. Electricity access and demographic data were primarily sourced from the Ghana Statistical Service (GSS) Population and Housing Census 2021 (GSS 2022). Supplementary data on national and regional electrification rates were obtained from the Ghana Energy Commission and the Ministry of Energy ' s annual reports. These sources provided granular information on household energy usage patterns, differentiated by region.

Cooking fuel usage data were extracted from the 2021 Population and Housing Census, which provided detailed information on primary and secondary cooking fuel choices and accessibility to LPG distribution networks. This data set was particularly valuable in understanding the spatial distribution of clean cooking fuel adoption across different regions.

Infrastructure data were obtained through technical reports from GRIDCo and annual reports and maps. These documents provided comprehensive information about power plant locations and capacities, transmission line routes, distribution network coverage, and the spatial distribution of LPG marketing companies (LPGMCs) throughout the country. Solar resource potential was assessed using data and maps from Global Solar Atlas 1 and Solagis. 2

## Analytical Framework

The analytical framework adopted for this study combines geospatial and statistical methods to examine the spatial patterns and socioeconomic drivers of energy access disparities in Ghana. This dual approach allows a comprehensive understanding of energy access ' s physical and socioeconomic dimensions.

## Geospatial Analysis

Geospatial analysis was to map and visualize disparities in energy access. Specific geospatial techniques, such as proximity analysis, were employed to examine the relationship between infrastructure availability and energy access levels. For instance, the locations of power plants were georeferenced using official coordinates from GRIDCo and other verified sources. The regions ' central points (centroids) were calculated, and proximity to power plants was determined using straight-line distances. These calculations were cross-validated with official infrastructure maps and reports from the Ghana Energy Commission to ensure accuracy.

The analysis also incorporated renewable energy potential data to assess the feasibility of deploying decentralized energy solutions in underserved regions. Solar irradiance data were spatially analyzed to identify optimal regions for renewable energy projects, particularly in northern Ghana, where grid extension is economically and logistically challenging.

## Statistical Analysis

Statistical analysis was conducted using Python to explore the relationship between energy access and socioeconomic factors. Correlation analyses were performed to quantify the influence of variables such as population, population density, and proximity to power plants on electricity and clean cooking fuel access. This analysis provided a nuanced understanding of the drivers of energy access disparities.

## Clean Cooking Access and LPG Distribution

Clean cooking access remains critically low at a national average of 37 percent, with stark regional disparities. Southern regions such as Greater Accra (69 percent), Western (38 percent), and Central (37 percent) lead in LPG adoption. These regions benefit from robust LPG distribution networks and higher household incomes, making clean cooking fuels more accessible. Greater Accra, for example, hosts 188 LPGMCs, far exceeding the numbers in northern regions such as Savannah (three) and North East (one), even though the Savannah and North East regions are several times larger than the Greater Accra region by land size.

In northern Ghana, households predominantly rely on traditional biomass fuels like firewood and charcoal. Savannah (95 percent), North East (95 percent), and Northern (88 percent) regions exhibit heavy dependence on firewood. This reliance, as shown in Figure 1, contributes to deforestation and indoor air pollution, exacerbating health and environmental challenges. Electricity for cooking remains minimal nationwide, with Greater Accra showing the highest adoption (10,116 households) due to its advanced electrification and economic status. Expanding LPG infrastructure and promoting alternatives like biogas are essential to addressing these disparities.

As shown in Figure 2, the correlation analysis provides valuable insights into energy access disparities. Proximity to power plants exhibits a strong negative correlation with access (Pearson ' s r ¼ -0.84), indicating that regions closer to generation sites have higher electrification rates. Regions like Volta and Eastern, located close to hydropower plants (80 km) and thermal plants (150 km), achieve high electricity access rates of 93 percent and 91 percent, respectively. By contrast, remote regions such as Savannah and North East, located 400 to 550 km from power plants, report access rates as low as 61 percent and 74 percent. This disparity arises from transmission losses, higher infrastructure costs, and voltage drops over long distances.

## Key Insights on Ghana ' s Energy Transition

Spatial disparities in energy access significantly challenge Ghana ' s sustainable development goals. For instance, the lower level of electricity access in northern Ghana limits educational opportunities as schools struggle to provide lighting and power for digital resources. Health-care delivery is also hindered, with clinics facing difficulties running essential equipment and vaccine refrigeration. Furthermore, limited energy access curtails economic development, as businesses and industries in these regions cannot operate efficiently or expand. These combined impacts illustrate how uneven energy distribution perpetuates regional inequalities and undermines national development efforts. The results highlight how regional inequities in electricity and clean cooking fuel access undermine socioeconomic development and limit the country ' s

progress toward achieving SDG 7. Regions in northern Ghana, such as Savannah, Upper East, and North East, lag in electricity access, with rates as low as 61 percent. These disparities are compounded by the physical remoteness of these regions, logistical challenges, and limited economic activity. Conversely, southern and urbanized regions like Greater Accra and Ashanti benefit from concentrated infrastructure investment and policy prioritization, achieving electricity access rates of nearly 100 percent.

In addition to geographical, socioeconomic, and infrastructural factors, political dynamics significantly shape energy access disparities in Ghana. Politicians often finance energy infrastructure projects as part of vote-seeking strategies, leading to development in remote locations with low population density and limited economic activity, which might not align with demographic or economic justifications. For instance, electrification projects under the National Electrification Project (NEP) and Self-Help Electrification Program (SHEP), by which communities and governments shared the cost of electricity access, have been influenced by political agendas, with resources sometimes disproportionately allocated to regions aligned with ruling parties. The SHEP was for communities within 20 km of existing 11/33 kV grid lines; where communities had to organize themselves and contribute some materials, like electricity poles for grid connection (IEA 2021). Towns vied with each other to be on the SHEP scheme, with communities putting up signboards that read ' Noelectricity, no vote. ' This politicization can both mitigate and exacerbate disparities, depending on whether investments prioritize politically strategic areas over those with greater energy poverty. Transparent and equitable energy planning, guided by objective criteria such as energy access needs, is essential to address these challenges effectively.

The findings align with global patterns observed in similar contexts, as highlighted in the literature. For example, the politicization of energy access in sub-Saharan Africa, as discussed by Briggs (2012), often leads to selective allocation of energy infrastructure, entrenching regional inequalities. MacLean et al. (2016), in their comparative analysis of Ghana and Uganda, also highlighted how divergent political histories have shaped citizens ' expectations for electricity access. For instance, the political dynamics highlighted by Cuesta Fern  andez (2018) are evident in our findings where subnational units significantly influence core -periphery linkages in electrification efforts. This is particularly apparent in the distribution of LPGMCs and grid infrastructure, which shows clear regional biases favoring southern and urban areas. The concept of electric territorialities proposed by Cuesta Fern  andez (2018) helps explain how these disparities become entrenched through political and infrastructural decisions.

## Policy and Implementation Gaps

The study ' s findings reveal significant limitations in Ghana ' s energy policies, particularly in addressing regional and urban -rural disparities. Despite substantial progress in national electrification, policy frameworks often lack spatial considerations, focusing predominantly on aggregate access rates.

One critical gap is the uneven distribution of LPGMCs, which exacerbates clean cooking access disparities. Whereas the Greater Accra region hosts 188 LPGMCs, Savannah and North East regions have only three and one, respectively. This imbalance reflects broader policy failures to ensure equitable infrastructure deployment. Despite government efforts to promote clean cooking adoption, including the Clean Cooking Action Plan and the Rural LPG Promotion Program (RLP), which provides LPG stoves at no cost, evaluations have shown low sustained usage among beneficiaries (Broni-Bediako and Amorin 2018; Carri  on et al. 2021; Clean Cooking Alliance 2021). Moreover, the high upfront costs of clean cooking technologies, coupled with limited subsidies and financing options, further hinder adoption in rural areas (Dongzagla and Adams 2022).

Another limitation is the reliance on centralized energy systems, prioritizing urban and periurban regions due to their economic viability. This approach neglects remote and sparsely populated areas, where grid extension is economically unfeasible. Decentralized energy solutions, such as solar minigrids, remain underutilized despite their proven efficacy in addressing similar challenges in countries like Kenya, Rwanda, and Bangladesh.

Policy inconsistencies and political interference also undermine long-term energy planning. As demonstrated by Brass, Harris, and MacLean (2019), access to electricity has complex implications for political participation and citizen engagement. For example, Briggs (2012) reported that electrification projects under the NEP in Ghana were often influenced by political agendas, with resources disproportionately allocated to regions aligned with ruling political parties (World Bank Group 2010). Such practices resulted in the neglect of oppositionaligned regions and led to inefficiencies in energy infrastructure deployment. This inconsistent prioritization not only delays progress toward universal access but also deepens regional inequalities in energy access. Strengthening institutional frameworks and ensuring continuity in energy planning are essential to overcoming these challenges.

## Energy Access and the Energy Transition

Achieving a just energy transition in Ghana requires strategies that prioritize equitable access to energy for all regions and socioeconomic groups. Decentralized renewable energy systems, such as solar minigrids and home systems, are critical for improving access in

remote areas, particularly in northern Ghana, where solar potential is high (4.06 -4.45 kWh/kWp compared to 3.70 -4.10 kWh/kWp in the south). Challenges such as unreliable minigrid or off-grid electricity (Adu-Poku et al. 2023) and the capital costs of solar systems alongside energy-storage systems affect their adoption. A significant concern is the potential abandonment of decentralized systems on grid extension, as observed in contexts like Indonesia and Sri Lanka, where communities prefer centralized grid electricity due to social perceptions of reliability or cost (Tenenbaum, Greacen, and Vaghela 2018; Segura-Rodr  ı guez, Bhandari, and Sanogo 2025). In Ghana, grid unreliability and billing issues further complicate this dynamic, particularly in rural areas.

To ensure that decentralized renewable energy contributes to both energy access and a sustainable energy transition, policies must facilitate integration with the national grid. This includes technical standards for interconnection, feed-in tariffs to allow excess energy sales, and incentives for maintaining renewable systems after grid extension. Lessons from countries like India, with established minigrid regulations, and Kenya ' s off-grid energy policies suggest that hybrid systems combining grid and renewable sources can enhance sustainability. In urban areas, where grid access is high (e.g., 99 percent in the Greater Accra region), renewable energy adoption driven by cost declines and energy independence desires can reduce grid pressure, supporting the transition. A comprehensive strategy integrating rural and urban contexts, addressing grid reliability, and promoting equitable policies is essential for a just and sustainable energy transition in Ghana.

Furthermore, the Rwanda REF project highlights the importance of targeted and inclusive strategies in promoting private-sector-led off-grid renewable energy development (CIF 2022). This initiative provides lines of credit and creates an enabling environment for off-grid solar electrification. At the core of the project is the creation of lines of credit in local currency for financial institutions. These lines of credit are used to finance solar home systems, minigrids, and the solar companies that offer these solutions (CIF 2022). Local financial institutions leverage these funds to support private solar companies, enabling them to provide households with offgrid solutions through decentralized methods. Rwanda ' s solar power projects, such as the Agahozo Shalom Youth Village solar field, the first utilityscale, grid-connected solar project in East Africa. This demonstrates that intentional efforts can effectively close the energy access gaps faced by many communities. Kenya ' s electrification program also offers instructive lessons. Through initiatives like the LMPC, Kenya increased electricity access from 37 percent in 2013 to 79 percent in 2023 by connecting 9 million rural households (IEA 2025).

Concurrently, Kenya became a global leader in offgrid solar: In 2023 it accounted for nearly three quarters of all solar home systems sold in East Africa. These outcomes show the power of blending grid expansion with market-driven solar deployment. Ghana can emulate this approach by scaling up minigrid projects and innovative financing models (e.g., pay-as-you-go solar) alongside continued grid extension, ensuring both strategies work in tandem.

Renewable energy deployment should also be complemented by targeted policies that address socioeconomic barriers. Subsidies and financing mechanisms for clean cooking technologies, such as LPG and biogas, can enhance affordability and adoption in rural areas. Kenya ' s National Cooking Transition Strategy offers valuable lessons, demonstrating how multifaceted approaches involving policy implementation, private-sector participation, and innovative financing can drive progress (Ministry of Energy and Petroleum 2024). This approach has transformed Kenya ' s cooking sector into a sustainable and profitable industry, intending to achieve universal access to clean cooking technologies by 2028. Ghana can adopt similar models to mobilize resources and expertise for its energy transition.

International support is equally crucial. Development partners and multilateral organizations can provide technical assistance, concessional financing, and capacity-building initiatives to support decentralized energy projects and infrastructure development in underserved regions. By aligning these efforts with Ghana ' s broader development agenda, the country can achieve sustainable and inclusive energy growth.

## Conclusion

This comprehensive study of geographical disparities in Ghana ' s energy access reveals complex patterns of inequity that must be addressed to achieve universal energy access and sustainable development. The research demonstrates that although Ghana has made remarkable progress in electricity access, achieving an 89 percent national electrification rate, significant regional disparities persist. These disparities are particularly pronounced between northern and southern regions, with access rates varying from 99 percent in Greater Accra to 61 percent in the Savannah region, reflecting deeper structural and socioeconomic inequalities.

The analysis reveals that proximity to power generation facilities, population density, and economic activity levels are key determinants of energy access. Urban regions benefit from concentrated infrastructure investment and economies of scale, whereas rural and remote areas face persistent challenges in grid connectivity and clean cooking fuel access. The study ' s findings on clean cooking access are

particularly concerning, with stark disparities in LPG distribution networks and adoption rates between regions. The research also identifies significant opportunities, however, particularly in northern Ghana ' s superior solar resource potential. With specific PV power outputs ranging from 4.06 to 4.45 kWh/kWp, compared to 3.70 to 4.10 kWh/ kWp in southern regions, there is substantial potential for decentralized renewable energy solutions to address access gaps in underserved areas.

Drawing from successful examples in Kenya and Rwanda, the study demonstrates that targeted interventions and innovative approaches can effectively address spatial inequities in energy access. Success, however, requires sustained political commitment, robust institutional frameworks, and careful consideration of local contexts and needs. Future research should focus on developing more detailed implementation strategies for specific regions, evaluating the effectiveness of various policy interventions, and exploring innovative financing mechanisms to support energy infrastructure development in underserved areas. Additionally, continued monitoring and evaluation of progress toward energy access goals will be crucial for ensuring the effectiveness of implemented solutions.

The findings of this study contribute to our understanding of the spatial dimensions of energy access and provide insights for policymakers and planners. It highlights that achieving SDG 7 requires not only technological deployment but also deliberate attention to where and how energy services are provided. By addressing geographical disparities, Ghana can advance its energy transition in a way that is sustainable and inclusive, offering lessons for similarly situated countries in the pursuit of universal modern energy access. /H17039
