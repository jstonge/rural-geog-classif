ABSTRACT. Research on Amazonian communities has focussed more often on rural-urban linkages than on links among rural communities. This is unsurprising, given the low density of population, limited intercommunity commerce, and importance of direct city-market relations. Social relations among rural communities are also important in shaping rural livelihoods and lifeways. We report on the findings of a large-scale census of communities in the Napo River basin in northeastern Peru (n=174). Data were gathered on intervillage crop seed acquisition and cooperative labor sharing as two key inputs in agriculture, and on intervillage soccer matches, which are integral to rural social life. We analyze the sociospatial networks of each practice, paying attention to settlement patterns, community ethnicity, and differential access to the uplands. We find that seeds and labor flow along soccer network lines. Rural social networks appear to be structured strongly by ethnicity--homophily---and reflect important complementarities between upland and lowland communities (weak ties). Keywords : Amazon, social networks, PARLAP project, peasant agriculture, rural life.

In Latin America, as elsewhere in the Global South, social networks are of considerable importance for all sectors of society, but they are especially key among traditional societies and poor sectors of the population (Chuang and Schechter 2015). Informal personal networks help people cope with economic, health, and environmental shocks and to gain access to food, resources, and information essential to their livelihoods and security (Wydick and others 2011; Kasper and Borgerhoff Mulder 2015; Mertens and others 2015). Social interaction through these networks facilitates cooperation and helps to shape identities and lifeways more broadly (Peña-Venegas and others 2014; Porro and Porro 2014). People invest time and resources to build, maintain, and enhance such relationships both for altruistic and utilitarian reasons (Patton 2005; Kasper and Borgerhoff Mulder 2015), but in many cases also because spending time with others is enjoyable (Uzendoski 2004).

Research on indigenous and folk peoples in Amazonia has shown the importance of kinship and other social relations both in prehistory and in the present. Kin relations (consanguinity, affinity, and filiation), in particular, are considered to be the building blocks of society, group identity, and subsistence within Amazonian communities and ethnic groups (see Bergman 1980; Eakin and others 1986; Gow 1991; Santos-Granero 1991; Descola 1994; Lenaerts 2004; Uzendoski 2004; Walker 2013a). Relations with 'others' are sometimes seen as a means of turning strangers into kin or close friends (McCallum 1990; Gow 1991; Johnson 2003; Uzendoski 2004; Santos-Granero 2007; Killick 2009). Mobility and migration also play a formative role in the making of social relationships (Feather 2009).

Ethnographies and studies on rural life and livelihoods in Amazonia have made important contributions to our understanding of a wide range of social exchange relations, including food or manioc beer sharing (Harris 2000; WinklerPrins and de Souza 2005; Franzen and Eaves 2008; Killick 2009; Welch 2014), transfers of knowledge (Henley 1996) and planting material (Boster 1986; Cole and others 2007; Cabral de Oliveira 2008; Heckler

Such studies, however, either privilege relationships and interactions within a single community or a sample of households from a small number of communities, often of the same ethnic group, or connections between rural communities and the city, including market relations, urban-ward migration for education and jobs, and the multisited nature of Amazonian life (WinklerPrins 2002a; WinklerPrins and de Souza 2005; Padoch and others 2008; van Vliet and others 2015).

The low density of population in rural Amazonia, limited intercommunity commerce, and importance of direct urban-market relations, all suggest that rural-urban linkages are of paramount importance. We argue, however, that relations among rural communities, which often have been overlooked, are also influential in shaping rural livelihoods and lifeways. A recurrent concern in this literature is whether traditional linkages based on sharing, trust, and reciprocity are disappearing with the expansion of markets (Behrens 1992; Minzenberg and Wallace, 2011; Takasaki and others 2014). In spite of the long history of studying social relations in Amazonia, only in recent years have scholars began to adopt a social-networks perspective (Kawa and others 2012; Peña-Venegas and others 2014, Abizaid and others 2015, 2016), and unlike other research settings such as the Andes, South Africa, and Thailand, the spatial configuration of such networks remains little studied (Faust and others 1999; Zimmerer 2003; Schramski and Huang 2016).

## &lt;&lt;A-HEAD&gt;&gt; CONCEPTUAL APPROACH

This study is informed by concepts from social network theory to examine intercommunity connections. Social network theory focuses on relationships or ties among actors (in this case communities), rather than attributes, to explain particular outcomes (Borgatti and others 2014). Ties may be divided into four interrelated types: similarities (colocation, shared attributes); social relations (kinship, friendship); interactions (transactions, exchanges); and flows (Borgatti and others 2009). For instance, people may interact more closely with people similar to them, choose to live close to family or friends, or withhold information from people they dislike. Specifically, we draw on theories of homophily and Granovetter's strength of weak ties. Homophily refers to the propensity of people to have greater contact with people from the same group---ethnicity, race, gender, age, religion---and is often linked with trust and group cohesiveness, or strong (bonding) ties (McPherson and others 2001). Although homophily is highly instrumental at times, it may not always be indicative of strength in a network. Granovetter's strength of weak-ties theory rests on the notion that weak ties---unique ties with others outside one's group---can provid e nonredundant

information or different resources to the individual and the group, and thus, can serve as bridges between groups (Borgatti and Halgin 2011).

The primary unit of analysis in our regional-scale study is the 'community' recognizing that community-level outcomes are an aggregate or emergent property of decisions made by households therein. Whereas the locus of decision making for soccer partnering is the community, seed transfers and labor-sharing decisions are made by individual households and are typically studied at the household level. In this study, however, we examine links among communities, regionwide, rather than the underlying social exchange among households, by characterizing partnering communities. A 'partner' community is one in which households receive seeds or labor from or play soccer with households in another community. By studying links among communities, we provide a novel perspective that promises to complement household-level studies.

## &lt;&lt;A-HEAD&gt;&gt; THE PARLAP PROJECT AND STUDY AREA

PARLAP is an international ongoing collaboration focussed on the understanding of rural poverty among indigenous and folk peoples in western Amazonia. This multiyear project is based on the most extensive community census and household survey yet undertaken in Amazonia. Working along four major rivers of the Peruvian Amazon---the Amazon, Napo, Pastaza, and Ucayali, an area more than twice the size of Costa Rica---our field teams completed a census of 919 communities, and surveyed just over 3900 households in 213 communities. The project combines the use of field data, with remote sensing, GIS, and historical information to examine the links between rural livelihoods and poverty in the Peruvian Amazon. As an important component of the PARLAP project, the present study examined the connections between rural social networks and rural livelihoods, focusing

exclusively on community-level data from the Napo River basin in northeastern Peru (Figure 1), which will inform subsequent analysis for all four rivers in the PARLAP study area.

The Napo River is a large northern tributary of the Amazon River, flowing southward from the Ecuadorean Andes. By the time it enters Peru, the Napo has dropped to under 200 m above sea level (Laraque and others 2009), running some 600 km before joining the Amazon, 70 km downstream from Iquitos (Pop: 437,400, INEI 2015). Covering an area of 44,992 km 2 in Peru, the basin is dominated by the main stem of the Napo River, its tributaries, and associated wetlands. According to projections for 2015, 37,200 people live in the three districts that comprise the bulk of the Napo basin in Peru---Mazán, Napo, and Torres Causana (INEI 2009)---and the population is primarily rural in character (&lt; 30 percent of the population lives in the district capitals).

At the time of Francisco de Orellana's expedition that culminated with the European discovery of the Amazon River in the sixteenth century, the Napo River was dominated by the Omaguas, who engaged in trade with the Quijos at the foot of the Andes (Reeve 1993). Today, traces of pre-Columbian settlement are hard to find, as are communities founded initially as Jesuit missions in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries. The rubber boom of the late-nineteenth century had a profound impact on settlement patterns with the largest towns and most of the oldest communities established as rural estates during that period (Barham and Coomes 1996; Santos-Granero and Barclay 2000). Almost one quarter of all communities in the Napo were established as estates, some of them established by patrones from Ecuador and Colombia, who brought in Naporuna natives from Ecuador (SantosGranero and Barclay 2000). Since then, settlement along the Napo has 'in-filled', with new communities being founded between older ones  and roughly 60 percent of them being established since the 1970s (Miller 2014).

Present-day rural communities are more numerous in the Lower Napo than in the headwaters. The majority of communities are predominantly Amerindian, with Quichua being the primary indigenous group. Other Amerindian communities are comprised of the Arabela, Maijuna, Secoya, and Yagua. A quasi-ethnic group, referred to in the literature as ribereños, or riverbank people (Chibnik 1991), is also found in considerable numbers: they account for 42  percent of all communities in the basin and 81 percent of the communities in the Mazán district. Ribereños are the mixed-descendants of Europeans, mestizos, and Amerindians, many dating back to the rubber boom or earlier (Padoch 1988; Hiraoka 1989). They have a deep understanding of the local environment and although it is sometimes claimed that they are more oriented towards the markets, their livelihoods are often similar to those of their Amerindian counterparts. Three communities have been founded by colonos recently arrived into the region. Official Peruvian statistics show that the bulk of the population of Loreto, where the Napo is situated, are either Catholic or evangelical Christian of various denominations (INEI 2008), including the Ataucusi (Israelitas), who have significant presence in the border region with Colombia and Brazil (Désilets 2008).

Iquitos is the regional market and administrative center of Loreto, with a handful of small towns serving as secondary markets and often district capitals (that is, Pantoja, Santa Clotilde and Mazán).  Beyond these towns, 174 small kin-based communities (mean = 44 households, 280 individuals), each of them organized around a soccer field, line the Napo River and tributaries (Table 1). People in these communities often have kin living in Iquitos or in neighboring communities. In the absence of roads---apart from a 3.6 km dirt tract across the isthmus between the Napo and Amazon River at Mazán---the river is the 'highway'. Whereas residents in the Mazán district---closest to Iquitos--- rely on canoes and small boats to take their produce to market, people living further upstream also use river launches, or barter/trade with itinerant river traders locally known as regatones (Salonen and others 2012).

River transportation and accessibility are affected by seasonal variations in water levels, as well as by the direction of the river flow (Tenkanen and others 2015). Although many communities have access to a radiophone, a public telephone and cell phone coverage, their primary use is for communication with the city and district capitals rather than between rural communities.

Roughly half of the rural communities are located on bluffs overlooking the river (upland or terra firme ) above river flood levels, and the remainder are situated in the lowland on the river floodplain where they are vulnerable to annual flooding; few communities are situated in the interfluves. Rural residents along the Napo make a living from a varying mix of agriculture, fishing, timber and non-timber forest products (NTFP) extraction, and hunting, much like riverine people along the Amazon River (Chibnik 1994). A diversity of crops is planted using traditional technology. Rice, an important cash crop, is typically planted on silt bars that are exposed between the floods though these are not as extensive as those that form along the Ucayali or the main stem of the Amazon. Maize is grown on other alluvial areas (low levees and backswamps) and is also sold at regional markets. Manioc and plantains, the main local staples, are grown at higher elevation on levees and the upland. Local farming households rely primarily on labor within the household and through cooperative labor, mainly from kin and friends within the same village (Takasaki and others 2014), but also from others in neighboring communities (Abizaid and others 2015); cooperative labor is used primarily for forest clearing (Takasaki and others 2014). Importantly, households obtain seeds and other planting material through informal seed networks within and outside of their communities to replant fields following major floods and to secure new crops (Ban and Coomes 2004; Perrault-Archambault 2005; Coomes 2010; Stromberg and others 2010;

Soccer is an exceptionally popular sport and an important pillar of rural life in Amazonia (Walker 2013b). In riverine communities, houses are typically arranged around a soccer field, where men play for fun in the evenings after work. Women and children, gather around the pitch to watch and visit with relatives and friends. Most communities have one or more soccer clubs (Chibnik 1994). In some communities of the Peruvian Amazon, members of such clubs sometimes form labor-sharing groups (Abizaid, unpublished data) and in Bolivia, soccer matches have been reported as an important way to connect with other communities (Alcorn and others 2007). Intercommunity soccer matches take place when a team, or a schoolteacher of a community. invites the team of another community to visit and play, though tournaments involving multiple teams are also organized, usually around local festivities such as Mother's Day, San Juan festivities, village or patron saint celebrations, and Fiestas Patrias ) (Walker 2013b). Individual players and teams pay a registration fee and bets are sometime placed on the results, and matches are followed by meals and dancing (Chibnik 1994; Walker 2013b). Typically, a visiting community will extend a similar invitation in the future and ultimately they form a league of sorts. Community delegations include not only the players, but their families, who travel to cheer on their team(s) and to see relatives and friends living in other communities. Such gatherings help people to maintain and create social ties, and have been reported to serve as a forum for people to exchange produce, planting material, and agricultural knowledge (Ban and Coomes 2004; Coomes and Ban 2004; Miltner and Coomes 2015).

## &lt;&lt;A-HEAD&gt;&gt; DATA COLLECTION AND ANALYSIS

Data were gathered in 2013--14 through a census conducted by a local field team of all communities in the districts of Mazán, Napo, and Torres Causana (Figure 1). Communities were identified based on the Peruvian National Census 2007, survey data on indigenous communities from the Instituto del Bien Común (IBC) (Smith and others 2003), satellite imagery from Google Earth and local enquiries in the field, and compiled in a spatially explicit database (Webster and others 2016).  Using Iquitos as a base, the field team travelled by boat along the Napo, Curaray, and Mazán rivers and other tributaries, close to 600 km upriver, to the border with Ecuador. A total of 177 communities, including the towns of Pantoja, Santa Clotilde and Mazán, were visited over the span of thirteen months. In each community, the field team sought out the local authorities and conducted a focus group-based interview following a comprehensive, structured questionnaire to collect community-level information.  The nine-page questionnaire solicited information on community characteristics, communication and transportation, history of settlement and relocation, and primary economic activities and conditions. We did not collect information on religion, which has been shown to be relevant for environmental governance in other parts of Amazonia (Otsuki 2013).

As part of the focus-group interviews, respondents were asked about linkages with other communities in terms of seed acquisition, cooperative labor sharing, and sporting events (that is, soccer matches). Specifically, respondents were asked to identify communities from which community residents typically receive planting material (maize and rice seed, manioc cuttings, and plantain suckers---up to three sources for each type of planting stock), and cooperative labor (up to six sources). Similarly, they were asked whether they play soccer against other communities, and to list the names of up to ten partners and the frequency of matches per year, starting with the community with which they play most often.

Information on trading partners was also collected during the focus-group interviews through questions about the two main markets where they buy basic household items (for example, rice, sugar cooking oil, soap, batteries, fuel) and sell produce (for example, plantains, manioc, rice, fish. and game meat). In addition to the interview, the field team recorded the community location (using a Garmin GPS at the center of the community soccer field) and prepared a brief report on the community's founding and economic history. Our analyses focus on all Amerindian (native) and ribereño communities and towns in the basin (n=174), while the three colono communities (not Israelitas) were not included.

Data were organized into matrices that represent linkages between pairs of communities for relational analysis of seed transfers, cooperative labor sharing and soccer matches among communities. Of particular interest in this setting are potential homophily effects and intergroup interactions (weak or bridging ties) between upland and lowland communities, and between Amerindian (defined in terms of self-identification regardless of their formal status according to the state) and ribereño communities. Also of interest is the role of distance between communities and distance to market. The literature and our own field experience point to the potential of both homophily and weak-tie effects in intercommunity interactions along ethnic lines (Labeyrie and others 2016), differential access to land in the uplands and distance (Coomes 2010). Specifically, we hypothesize that intercommunity soccer matches, seed transfers, and labor sharing are more common among communities of the same group (native or ribereño) (homophily). Our second hypothesis is that in this setting access to resources in both the uplands (low productivity/low risk) and the floodplain (high productivity/high risk due to flooding) is important (Denevan 1996), suggesting the potential of weak-tie effects across communities. Finally, we conjecture that the density and patterns of settlement in the basin mediate the role of homophily and weak-tie effects, because they affect the possibility for interaction within and across groups.

We examine the structure of these networks at a regional scale through descriptive statistics on levels of participation, number of partners, within and across group interactions, and analyze potential associations between networks through correlation analysis of pairlevel data using the quadratic assignment procedure (QAP) function in UCINET (Borgatti and others 2002). QAP correlations measure the association between networks through an algorithm that first computes the Pearson's correlation coefficient between corresponding cells of two data matrices and then randomly permutes the rows and columns of one matrix and recomputes the correlation and other measures hundreds of times in order to determine the proportion of times that a random measure is larger or equal to the observed measure obtained in the first step. We then visualize these networks using GEPHI (Bastian and others 2009) and NetDraw (Borgatti 2002), two open-source software packages designed for network visualization and interpretation. Finally, we use GIS to map, visualize, and analyze the spatial configuration of these networks, paying particular attention to the role of distance based on the river route most likely taken (see Webster and others 2016). Specifically, we focus on the distance between neighboring communities, between network partners (that is, seed, cooperative labor, and soccer), and to the main markets, with the assumption that along the Napo, river distance may be an important impediment for social and economic interaction among communities.

Although a similar share of communities in the basin reported receiving cooperative labor from people in other communities (36 percent) (Table 2), the structure of the cooperative labor-sharing network is much more fragmented than the seed network (Figure 2A, 2B). ## &lt;&lt;C-HEAD&gt;&gt; SOCCER

Figure 2C shows a much higher degree of connectivity among communities than for seeds or cooperative labor sharing (Figure 2A, 2B). Indeed, except for a couple of communities (one in the Napo and one in Torres Causana), participation in the soccer network is universal (Table 2). On average, communities in the Napo basin play soccer with six other communities, and play an intervillage match every other week. The nature of the soccer network, however, is different in Mazán relative to the upper reaches of the Napo. Communities in Mazán tend to have fewer soccer partners, but play with them more frequently (that is, they tend to have closer ties with a smaller number of communities). In turn, communities further upstream, in the Napo and Torres Causana districts, have more soccer partners, but play with them less frequently (they have less intense ties with a more diverse pool of partners). When we examine the spatial configuration of the soccer network, we see that although communities tend to play soccer with neighboring communities, soccer matches among distant communities are also common. For instance, some communities in the Mazán district listed communities located more than 40 km as their main soccer partners (Figure 3A), and maximum distance to a partner is 79 km. This is striking given that the average distance to their closest neighbor is only three km.

## &lt;&lt;B-HEAD&gt;&gt; COMMUNITY TYPE AND RURAL VILLAGE NETWORKS: SEEDS AND COOPERATIVE LABOR

The structure of the networks of upland and lowland communities is also distinct. With regards to crop-seed acquisition we find that a larger share of lowland communities relies on seed obtained from external sources (45 versus 30 percent). Upland communities are the main sources of seed for upland and lowland communities alike (homophily among upland communities and weak ties for lowland communities) (Table 4), accounting for both subsistence (manioc and plantain) and cash crops (rice and maize). Furthermore, lowland communities have a more diversified pool of seed sources in comparison to upland communities (1.4 versus 1.7 partners). Similarly, the average number of seed partners is highest in the Napo and the Mazán districts, suggesting that seed is readily available from multiple sources closer to markets in Mazán and Iquitos. Upon closer examination, however, we saw that Iquitos and the district capitals are the main providers of rice and maize seed, and that rural upland communities are the primary sources of manioc cuttings and plantain pseudostems.

Whereas 42 percent of upland communities receive cooperative labor from other communities, only 29 percent of lowland communities do so, which is not surprising given the greater demand for labor for forest clearing in the upland. Upland communities are the main recipients of intervillage labor across the three districts in the Napo (66 percent of all cooperative labor ties), and almost three quarters of that labor comes from other upland communities (homophily) (Table 4). Similarly to the seed networks, cooperative labor tends to flow from upland to lowland communities, but in a less pronounced way and cooperative labor sharing among lowland communities is the least common (weak ties). ## &lt;&lt;B-HEAD&gt;&gt; DISTANCE, DENSITY OF SETTLEMENT AND RURAL VILLAGE NETWORKS ALONG THE NAPO

In the Napo basin, as elsewhere in the Peruvian Amazon lowlands where roads are practically absent and boat travel is slow and service is variable both spatially and seasonally, spatial factors are likely to shape the prospects for social interaction and rural village networks. Indeed, our results suggest that the distance between communities constrains the ability of communities to share seeds, work together, and play soccer with one another. As shown in Figure 5, the average distance between seeds, labor and soccer partners is relatively small (&lt; 25 km) vis-à-vis the average distance to the markets ( &gt; 100 km). The fact that these distances are more similar to the mean distance between rural communities suggests that rural village networks respond to a different logic than the commercial ties that bind rural communities with larger towns (district capitals) and cities. Propinquity is most important for cooperative labor sharing. Indeed, whereas the mean distance between labor partners is twelve km, the distance between seed and soccer partners is nineteen and twenty-four km. The greater distance travelled for soccer over seed or labor acquisition may reflect the fact that the cost of soccer travel is pooled, whereas travel for seed/labor is not.  Interestingly, communities do not always interact more intensely with their closest neighbors. Why so remains unclear, but possible explanations include a history of intercommunity disputes over land or resources, social differences among neighboring communities (closest neighbor is of a different ethnic group, or kin members live further away), or residents seek out seed not available locally or

We also contend that the density of settlement along the Napo basin is important in understanding rural village networks. We would expect more densely settled areas to offer greater opportunity for interaction because of the larger number of communities within a given distance creates a larger pool of potential partners. ## &lt;&lt;A-Head&gt;&gt; DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION

This regional-scale study from the Napo basin in Peru sought to explore connections among rural communities through seed transfers, labor sharing, and soccer matches---three important phenomena in the social and economic lives of riverine people. Based on a census of communities along the Napo River, we found that participation in intercommunity seed transfer and cooperative labor is high and is practically universal for intercommunity soccer playing. Our results confirm the prevalence of seed networks and suggest that intercommunity labor sharing among Amazonian riverine communities is more common than previously thought (Takasaki and others 2014; Abizaid and others 2015). Our study also

Accepted Article

This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

provides a first glimpse of the importance of sports (soccer) as a marker of rural life in the Amazon, and on how communities, similar or different (upland/lowland, native/ribereño), relate to each other, and demonstrates the potential contribution of community-level studies to the understanding of seed (and labor) sharing at a regional scale.

Our study contributes to understanding of rural life and livelihoods in Amazonia in four specific ways. First, our study points to significant overlaps between informal social networks and lends support to the hypothesis that inter-community soccer matches help to bind rural society in Amazonia and may be instrumental for rural dwellers to secure seeds, cooperative labor, and agricultural knowledge from family and friends in other communities (Ban and Coomes 2004; Miltner and Coomes 2015). This finding is consistent also with Brisson, who finds that residents of Nuevo Miraflores, a lowland community along the Marañón River, play soccer, share seed, and work together with kin in nearby communities (2003). As mentioned earlier, however, it is also possible for communities to play soccer with their seed and/or cooperative labor partners. Even then, intervillage gatherings around soccer matches serve as a setting that further enables important social interaction. Our study points, ultimately, to the need for a better understanding of the nature and functioning of community sports and other formative networks such as communication and religious networks in rural Amazonia.

Second, our results suggest that interethnic interaction among rural communities is more limited than previously acknowledged (Cole and others 2007; Alexiades and Peluso 2009; Clement and others 2009), which may reflect cultural barriers that impede interethnic interaction. Indeed, we found strong homophily in intercommunity relations, that is, rural communities share seed, work together through cooperative labor, and play soccer more closely with communities of the same cultural group: native with native, and ribereño with ribereño. Such barriers can have profound implications, especially for communities belonging

Accepted Article

This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

to small ethnic groups in more remote areas, which are the most isolated socially and may face difficulties in establishing bridging ties with communities from other groups. Ribereño communities and towns are the main sources of crop planting material, even for native communities, suggesting that ribereño communities provide a form of informal insurance to native communities (weak ties) and that they may be important also in the broad social diffusion of plant varietal diversity (Emperaire and de Cabral de Oliveira 2011). Similarly to seed flows, intergroup cooperative labor is provided by ribereño communities to native communities. This latter finding is surprising given the history of exploitative forms of appropriation of indigenous labor (Santos-Granero and Barclay 2000). It is not clear whether social isolation of small ethnic groups in more remote areas is the product of intertribal conflict, or if greater interaction between small ethnic groups and ribereño communities in the lower reaches is facilitated by better market access. Further research is needed to better understand the nature of interethnic ties in western Amazonia and the barriers that inhibit intergroup relations (weak ties), especially for small, ethnic groups in remote areas. Specifically, it will be important to grasp why some smaller ethnic groups are more socially isolated and the implications for their cultural survival.

Third, our results highlight the importance of upland resources for people living in the floodplain and are consistent with Denevan's model of riverine bluff settlement (Denevan 1996; Tournon 2002; WinklerPrins 2002b). Similar to earlier works (Coomes 2010), we found that in spite of their greater productivity, lowland communities are dependent on seed and cooperative labor supplied by relatives and friends in upland communities (weak ties). Unaffected by flooding, upland communities function as a safety net for lowland communities, even if they are less productive. With greater fluctuations in flood levels expected with climate change and a growing emphasis on agriculture over forest-based economic activity among rain forest communities (Coomes and others 2016), food security

Accepted Article

This article is protected by copyright. All rights reserved.

and ultimately the viability of lowland communities may well become increasingly dependent on the ability to secure planting stock from upland communities.

Finally, our results indicate that the density of settlement shapes informal rural networks in Amazonia and that better access to market does not attenuate---and may augment---rural-rural interaction. Indeed, our study found that rural networks are denser and more integrated in the Mazán District, which has a significantly higher density of settlement and is significantly closer to Iquitos in the Amazon. Three important implications of this are: that the lower density of settlement in the headwaters reduces the ability of communities to interact with each other (that is, fewer neighbors located further apart), which is crucial given that the weak telecommunication network among communities in the region; that informal rural networks play a complementary role to rural-urban linkages; and that informal institutions have important functions under imperfect market conditions as those found in rural Amazonia (Takasaki and others 2014). This finding suggests the need to better understand the role of community location in shaping regional social interactions in western Amazonia.

This study provides a first look into the complex web of relations that bind rural communities in Amazonia and will help to inform more quantitative analyses of how rural village networks form, using econometric techniques in social network analysis, which have been recently used to explain cooperative labor and seed networks within communities in the Peruvian Amazon (Abizaid and others 2015, 2016).  Ultimately, the types of intercommunity linkages explored here---soccer, seeds, and labor---as well as others (communication and religious networks) deserve greater attention from both scholars and development practitioners. Aside from learning more about how rural networks form and evolve, it will be important to better understand the role of social networks as a safety net and their linkages with and implications for mobility and migration.
