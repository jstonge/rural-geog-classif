## CREATIVE DESTRUCTION AND TRIAL BY SPACE IN A HISTORIC CANADIAN VILLAGE*

CLARE J. A. MITCHELL and JULIE VANDERWERF

abstract. Since the ďś±ďśąďś·ďś° s,  stakeholders have attempted to revitalize selected rural communities across the developed world. The resulting initiatives have contributed much to the growing multifunctionality of rural space. Geographers have conceptualized this transformation in at least two ways: as a process of creative destruction and as a process of trial by space. In this article we demonstrate the merit of uniting these concepts to enhance our understanding of the evolution of historic towns and villages. We illustrate this unification in  a  study  of  Creemore,  a  picturesque  village  in  the  province  of  Ontario, Canada.  Our analysis reveals that the transformation of this locale, from rural service center to heritagescape, has been fraught with controversy as various stakeholders have struggled to establish  a  dominant identity. We conclude that unification of these concepts does provide a useful framework for unraveling the evolution of historic communities within rural space. Keywords: Canada, creative destruction, heritage, historic villages, identity, postproductivism, trial by space.

T owns and villages in the developed world have experienced significant changes since ďś±ďśąďś·ďś° . These initiatives have contributed much to the growing multifunctionality of rural space.

Recent literature has drawn on the seminal works of Joseph Schumpeter and Henri Lefebvre to conceptualize the transformation under way in rural locales (Schumpeter ďś±ďśąďś´ďśł ; Lefebvre [ ďś±ďśąďś·ďś´ ] ďś±ďśąďśąďś± ). ## Creative Destruction

In his ďś±ďśąďś´ďśł work Schumpeter introduced the phrase 'creative destruction' to the field of economics. He used the term to illustrate the transformative process that normally accompanies a radical innovation. According to Schumpeter, the creation of new innovations by profit-driven entrepreneurs results in the destruction of companies whose profits are based on older technologies. The process of creative destruction, he argued, initiates new cycles of economic growth in a regional economy.

Since this seminal work the concept has permeated many strands of academia (Huang, Wall, and Mitchell ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś· ). Evolutionary economists, for example, owe much to Schumpeter's early ideas regarding the fundamental role of innovation (Andersen ďś±ďśąďśąďś´ ). Students of globalization have embraced the concept to describe the impact of Western creative activity on the destruction of national cultures (Jameson ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś° ). Geographers and other scholars interested in the transformation of space have also benefited from Schumpeter's contribution (Barnes and Hayter ďś±ďśąďśąďś˛ ; Page ďś±ďśąďśąďśą ; T onts and Grieve ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś˛ ;  Chang and Huang ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďśµ ).  This is perhaps most apparent in the work of David Harvey ( ďś±ďśąďś¸ďś¸ , ďś±ďśąďś¸ďśą , ďś±ďśąďśąďś° ), who suggested that the creation of new innovation has spatial implications. For Harvey, the actions of entrepreneurs give rise not only to new cycles of capital accumulation but also to new landscapes that ultimately destroy those based on older innovations.

Several scholars have used this concept to describe the transformation of communities that commodify, or sell, local heritage (Mitchell ďś±ďśąďśąďś¸ ; Mitchell and Coghill ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś° ; Mitchell, Atkinson, and Clark ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś± ; T onts and Grieve ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś˛ ; Chang and Huang ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďśµ ; Huang, Wall, and Mitchell ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś· ; Fan, Wall, and Mitchell ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś¸ ; Mitchell and deWaal ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďśą ). In our research we have suggested that various motivational drivers are responsible for the ongoing transformation of these locales (Mitchell and deWaal ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďśą ). New place identities accompany these physical transformations. Building on an earlier article (Mitchell and deWaal ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďśą ), we suggest that three different identities emerge with each new landscape of accumulation (see Table I). For local residents living in a productivist townscape, place identity is a function of both their quotidian economic and social activities. For those who are attracted to a heritagescape, this space represents a place of commodified heritage, one that provides heritage seekers both tangible commodities and intangible experiences reflecting a bygone, preproductivist era. For individuals who are drawn to the leisurescape, this space is a place of entertainment where myriad venues meet the recreational and consumptive needs of the mass tourist market.

Each new place identity ultimately challenges existing identities associated with earlier landscapes of accumulation. At greatest risk is the identity held by some local residents who value the space for its social cohesion and small-town atmosphere. As stakeholders transform and reproduce or create the space, this place identity  may  be  compromised or destroyed. To understand more fully why this destruction occurs, we now turn to the concept of trial by space.

## Trial by Space

Lefebvre, the renowned social theorist whose work has contributed much to academics' understanding of space, coined the phrase 'trial by space' ([ ďś±ďśąďś·ďś´ ] ďś±ďśąďśąďś± ). He suggested that the production of space involves confrontations among actors with

varying values and ideas who are vying to establish an identity as a means to achieve significance and power. This identity only emerges with physical presence. If values or ideas have no tangible expression, they will lose identity and evolve, as Lefebvre observes below, into mere abstract descriptions. Trial by space is the process by which this identity is established:

Nothing or no one can avoid trial by space. . . . It is in space on a worldwide scale, that each idea of 'value' acquires or loses its distinctiveness through confrontation with the other values and ideas that it encounters there. Moreover ď›ž and more importantly ď›ž groups, classes, or factions of classes cannot constitute themselves, or recognize one another, as 'subjects' unless they generate (or produce) a space. Ideas, representations or values which do not succeed in making their mark on space, and thus generating (or producing) an appropriate morphology, will lose all pith and become signs, resolve themselves into abstract descriptions, or mutate into fantasies. (Lefebvre [ ďś±ďśąďś·ďś´ ] ďś±ďśąďśąďś± , ďś´ďś±ďś¶ -ďś´ďś±ďś· ; italics added)

Halfacree defines these as follows: Rural localities are 'inscribed through relatively distinctive spatial practices, linked to production and/or consumption activities.' Formal representations of the rural are those expressed by 'capitalist interests, cultural arbiters, planners and politicians.' Everyday lives refer to the daily behaviors of rural residents, which are 'inevitably subjective and diverse, and with varying levels of coherence/fracture' ( ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś· , ďś±ďś˛ďś· ).

## Creative Destruction and Trial by Space in Historic Towns and Villages

Integrating the concepts of creative destruction and trial by space provided us with a framework for understanding the transformation of historic communities located within rural space (Table III). For this conceptualization we tracked the potential transformation of a historic center through three congruent forms, each of which reflects the landscape and identity of the space at a particular point in time. Each of these spatial forms comprises the three facets of space ď›ž locality, representation, and everyday lives ď›ž which cohere with the dominant identity of the space. A period of contradiction or chaos may precede each state as stakeholder groups, driven by various motivations, attempt to find a physical expression of their values and ideas. In some cases this trial by space may be relatively smooth, if what is imposed on the space is relatively consistent with what is already in place. In contrast, chaos may erupt, if inconsistent values or ideas permeate the space.

## Creemore as Lived Space

More simply put, in this widely promoted consumptive space we expected to find local residents and visitors interacting in a relatively harmonious manner.

## Trial by Space in Creemore

In Creemore, two types of conflict have accompanied the creation of this heritagescape. The first is that which emerged between those who seek to promote economic development through commodification and those who hope to preserve the community's small-town, productivist landscape. The second is that which surfaced more recently between those who desire to preserve the heritagescape and those

who aspire to accumulate surplus value through the development of Creemore's residential sector. We consider each of these trials by space.

Local residents opposed the creation of this heritagespace throughout the ďś±ďśąďśąďś° s. A local business owner revealed this resistance in ďś±ďśąďśąďś° when he argued that two local  tourism-development promoters had no right 'to tell the businessmen of Creemore that they should make the faĂ§ades of their buildings look like turn-ofthe-century buildings. Some of us don't want our buildings to look like they did ďś±ďś°ďś° years ago' ( cs ďś±ďśąďśąďś° ). Soon a battle between those in favor of restoring the village and those against the historic facade erupted. In a letter to the editor, one of the local promoters responded that 'the BIA Board has actively encouraged businesses to maintain, restore, or replicate the turn-of-the century architecture. . . . It is done because numerous studies have shown that people today have a strong appreciation for the heritage look as witnessed by the success of Niagara-on-the-Lake. . . . The projection of an image for the village to attract new customers only works if everyone cooperates' ( cs ďś±ďśąďśąďś° ).

In ďś±ďśąďśąďśł considerable opposition again emerged, this time against the sidewalk initiative. Many believed that the money could be better spent, as revealed in this resident's comment: 'I can't believe the village of Creemore is going to spend $ ďś±ďś¸ďś° , ďś°ďś°ďś° on brick sidewalks when the water is in dire need of help' ( cs ďś±ďśąďśąďśł ). A year later another resident voiced his comments on Creemore's new identity: 'What we now must do is play the role of entertainer for visitors. . . . Though we may not be collectively thrilled with the idea, we are aware that our area depends to a great extent on the infusion of dollars from tourists. . . . We are a small town caught in the economic no-man's-land between genuine small town life and the pre-packaged faĂ§ade that we are being forced to sell' ( cs ďś±ďśąďśąďś´ ).

Opposition again surfaced in ďś±ďśąďśąďś¸ over proposed plans to build a ďś±ďśµďś° -seat theater in the village. One resident observed that 'Creemore is a quaint little village and I think we should be careful not to build a white elephant we can ill afford' (Campbell ďś±ďśąďśąďś¸ ).  He continued, 'It seems that the more thriving, lovely, and authentic a community is, the quicker are the minions of progress to try to destroy it and to bully its residents into believing that such destruction is in their best interests' (p. ďś´ ). Opposition to this initiative caused the promoters to back down, and the facility was never constructed.

These examples reveal that Creemore's emergence as a heritagescape came about after  a  period  of  conflict  that  pitted  promoters of tourism development against those who sought to preserve Creemore's existing place identity. Promoters believed that commodification would lead to downtown revitalization and generate revenue. The initiatives taken to re-create the 'preproductivist' setting were designed to take visitors back to a simpler time. For some local residents, however, these initiatives were not an accurate reflection of the 'real' Creemore. To these preservationists, the village was a place where residents converged for commerce ď›ž albeit somewhat limited commerce ď›ž and social interaction. The creation of this 'faux-authentic'

heritagescape conflicted greatly with their individual ideas, values, and, ultimately, place identity.

Over time, however, local residents appear to have accepted this new identity as real, or authentic. The relatively harmonious situation that appeared to exist in ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś· , particularly between newcomers and visitors, suggests that the majority of local residents see this commodified landscape as the 'real' Creemore. This acceptance gave rise to a relatively congruent space. However, as we dug deeper we found that another trial by space is under way, one that may eventually compromise Creemore's identity as a commodified heritagescape.

The more recent conflict in Creemore is between a development company and local Creemore residents who are seeking to preserve the community as a heritagescape. Alliance Homes, a company based in the Greater Toronto Area, initiated this battle in ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś¶ when it announced plans to construct a ďś´ďśąďś¸ -unit subdivision on a ďśąďś¶ -acre parcel on the outskirts of the village. At two public meetings more than ďś¶ďś°ďś° residents expressed opposition to this proposal, on the grounds that the dense development was 'not in character with the village' (Rusk ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś¶ ). The development company decided to appeal to the Ontario Municipal Board when the township failed to come to a decision on this proposal. The board approved the proposal in ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś¸ , with development expected to take several years to complete (Gennings ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś¸ ). What impact this development will have on Creemore's current identity remains to be seen.

Creemore's future lies largely in the hands of local politicians. If they are driven by a desire to promote growth and/or enhance profit, future subdivision-development proposals  will  probably  be  approved.  These  initiatives  will  help  spur  this postproductivist heritagescape to a state of neoproductivism, one that will likely take on a residential identity that will appeal to former Toronto urbanites or retirees.  On the other hand, if those in power are motivated by a desire to preserve Creemore's small-town charm, future applications will be denied, thus halting the village's trek along the path of creative destruction. This outcome will not emerge quickly, however, for, as Lefebvre observed, 'nothing, or no one, can avoid trial by space' ([ ďś±ďśąďś·ďś´ ] ďś±ďśąďśąďś± , ďś´ďś±ďś¶ ).

## Reflections

Geographers have used the concepts of creative destruction and trial by space to unravel the transformations under way in rural space (Halfacree ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďś· ; Mitchell and deWaal ďś˛ďś°ďś°ďśą ).  Each of these concepts has individual merit; however, as our case study of Creemore shows, their unification also has value. Trial by space reveals the process that occurs in the production of space; that is, contestation among stakeholders with varying ideas and values (Lefebvre [ ďś±ďśąďś·ďś´ ] ďś±ďśąďśąďś± ). Creative destruction, in contrast, reveals the outcome of this process; that is, the creation of new landscapes of accumulation (Harvey ďś±ďśąďś¸ďśą ). Thus their unification offers a more complete understanding of both how and why space is transformed.

This merger provides insight into the evolution of historic communities. The presence of stakeholders motivated by profit, preservation, and/or promotion may give  rise  to  three  landscapes  of  accumulation:  productivist  townscape,  postproductivist heritagescape, and neoproductivist leisurescape. New landscapes and identities emerge often after a period of conflict, as stakeholders' ideas and values vie for spatial expression (see Table III). Newer identities and landscapes replace older identities and landscapes as spatial expression is attained. Our research demonstrates that Creemore transformed from a productivist to a postproductivist landscape and that the actions of preservationists, profiteers, and promoters of growth facilitated the evolution. Contestations created a landscape of commodified heritage, where the three facets of space ď›ž locality,  representation, and everyday lives existed in a relatively congruent state at the time of our fieldwork. Recent development proposals, however, have initiated a new trial by space, the outcome of which cannot be foretold.

In this article we presented the progression of historic space as a three-stage process, one that sees specific communities move from a landscape of local service provision, to a heritagescape, and finally to a leisurescape of mass consumption. Our findings indicate that a leisurescape is only one of several possible landscapes that may emerge in communities that base their development on the commodification of heritage.

The impending construction of a large residential subdivision on the outskirts of Creemore suggests that outsiders are imposing a new identity on the village. If similar proposals are approved in the future, this identity may come to dominate. And if this scenario unfolds, the community may evolve into a state of neoproductivism, albeit one whose identity ď›ž as a place of exurban residence ď›ž may deviate from what was originally proposed ď›ž as a place of entertainment. In either case, the ongoing transformation will provide further evidence of the emerging multifunctionality of rural space. Additional investigation will likely reveal that creative destruction and trial by space extend well beyond this historic village in southern Ontario.
