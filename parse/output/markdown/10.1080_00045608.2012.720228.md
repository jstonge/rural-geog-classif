## The Power of Mapping: Considering Discrepancies of Polish Cadastral Mapping

Francis Harvey

Department of Geography, University of Minnesota

In his seminal work on cartography, Brian Harley develops insights and raises questions about the power of maps. This article takes up these insights and questions through an anti-essentialist reading of Foucault's later thinking merged with concepts from grounded theory. The research examines the interrelated economic, strategic, legal, and technical aspects of mapping activities that use and create cadastral (property) maps. Using multiple scales, I analyze cadastral mapping activities through decomposition into interactions, boundary objects, and contextual factors to establish how empowerment and limitation take place. The examples come from fieldwork in Poland that explores cadastral mapping discrepancies. Following the demise of socialist forms of government and property in 1989, studies from the 1990s found differences (by area) between officially mapped ownership and actual property usage of up to 40 percent in rural areas. Although most Polish cadastral maps from rural areas were then, and still are, officially accepted as accurate, many remain functionally unusable. Further, the high level of discrepancies failed, and still fails, to systematically disrupt cadastral mapping, farming, and other economic activities. Considering this background, the article explores the processes of making property rights and the limits of state-centric constructions of territory. Reflecting the increased use of geographic information systems in cadastral mapping and the controls of Common Agricultural Policy disbursements, the article also analyzes different meanings of accuracy and the limits of cadastral mapping's calculative power. Key Words: cadastre, governance, mapping, Poland, power.

C onsiderations of the power of maps, beginning with Harley's (1989) seminal contributions to cartography and geography, have become a touchstone in cartography, critical geographic information systems (GIS), cadastral studies, political economy, and other subfields of geography. Geographers and cartographers also widely acknowledge that maps do not represent place as static mirrors of the world (Rorty 1981; Gelernter 1991) but, rather, provide selective constructions of dynamic activities and changes that exercise power through their visual representations, with explicit and implicit semiotic references permeating each cartographic text (Pickles 1991, 1997, 2004; Kwan 2007; Brown and Knopp 2008; Crampton 2010; Ettlinger 2011). As Harley and others acknowledge, map power is quite complex. The rich body of work that follows Harley's deconstructionist approach to mapping offers significant insights into the role of maps in state government (Ettlinger 2011), which more recently has been referred to as the state-led construction of calculable territory (Crampton and Krygier 2005; Crampton 2010; Rose-Redwood 2012). Government maps are regularly created, replaced, archived, and ignored. It follows that a map empowers in many ways when in use. What about the power of the forgotten, but still valid, map in an archive, the administrative map shown only once to a prominent international visitor, the map forgotten under the seat, the 'official' map of such dubious accuracy that it becomes irrelevant in praxis, or maps in conflict?

This article takes up these questions by engaging Harley's rejoinder that one considers how the numerous and vast mapping activities of the state enable and limit or, in other words, how activities of mapping manifest power. The power of mapping activities is sustained in the many interactions that enable and limit other activities. This article focuses on examples from cadastral mapping and its role in delineating property rights (Blomley 1998) as connected to the modern state's construction of calculable territory (Elden 2007a; Hannah 2009). The state maps, but it certainly relies on multiple mapping activities, which are widely known to be in disagreement with other government mapping activities or simply out of date. The accuracy of territorial inscriptions can be limited and even ambiguous, reflecting an incalculable territory, but still facilitate state control through the significance of mapping in administrative activities.

Cadastral mapping is one of the most common state mapping activities, given the central importance of property relations in modern geographies, but is acknowledged to be fraught with problems in many parts of the world (Thrower 1966, 1972; 'Oil and Land Rights in Peru' 2009). Cadastral mapping empowers and limits many economic and social activities in myriad ways. Blomley and other geographers have contributed innovative scholarship examining the role of the cadastre in state attempts to control its territory, often in conjunction with colonization and asserting control over nature (Blomley 1998; Scott 1998; Mitchell 2002). The significance of cadastres, with emphasis on the genesis of the modern states of Western Europe and in colonization, also has been widely explored (Andrews 1975; Buissert 1992; Kain and Baigent 1992; Lampland 1992; Smucker, White, and Bannister 2002; Unruh 2002). Indigenous resistance to demarcation of common lands into private property, and discrepancies between official records and indigenous land tenure (Kyem 1999; Lumumba-Kasongo 2000; Watts 2000; Augustinus 2001; Barry and Fourie 2002; F. Harvey 2003; Weiner and Harris 2003), provide rich examples revealing that cadastres are far from neutral and unitary instruments of governance organizing natural rights to private property. Fundamentally, cadastral mapping comes up against challenges that arise in the differences between governance and actual activities.

In spite of their significance, recent participatory use of cadastral materials in environmental and urban conflicts, and the empowerment of indigenous resistance, geographic studies of cadastral mapping activities are few in number. To examine how empowerment and limitation come through cadastral mapping activities, this article inverts the focus of the analysis of map power from the map to its use. Following an anti-essentialist reading of Foucault's writing and lectures on power, governmentality, and other relevant concepts and taking up concepts from grounded theory, particularly from infrastructure studies and boundary objects, the article develops a novel theoretical framework to study geographic relations and the empowerment and limitations arising through mapping activities.

Thecadastral mapping activities this article describes are derived from a multiyear research project in Poland that examined how cadastral (property rights) mapping enables and limits rural residents, local authorities, local surveying and mapping agencies, and the responsible national government agency when discrepancies as large as 40 percent exist between the officially mapped area of ownership and the actual area used by the person or people (Figure 1). This inverted focus speaks to the challenge of accounting for local governance mapping activities as technologies offer new potential to state government agencies (Schwarz and Thompson 1990). In the tradition of skepticism from which antiessentialist grounded theory and its various offshoots developed, examining empirical evidence from activities moves grounded theory toward facts and addresses the conundrums and contradictions of mapping activities' empowerment and limitations.

The article consists of the following sections. The next section begins with a review of theoretical work from geography and science and technology studies on power and the literature on cadastral infrastructure, boundary objects, and strategic logic. This review leads to the formulation of a theoretical framework for the analysis of enablement and limitations in mapping activities. A consideration of methodological issues for social interaction research involving mapping infor- mation technologies concludes the section. The third section provides an overview of the organization and background of the cadastral infrastructure in Poland, as well as relevant historical, cultural, economic, and social factors for the decomposition of cadastral mapping activities into interactions, boundary objects, and contextual factors. Drawing on this background and using the theoretical framework, I analyze two types of discrepancies involving mapping interactions. The first results from internal administrative coordination issues that speak to the challenges of sustaining empowerment and limitations. The second arises from differences between formal records and informal practices. These discrepancies persist and lead to conundrums and contradictions between the cadastre and land tenure but also reflect a strategic logic that developed as a form of resistance to colonization and military government. The fourth section analyzes the coexistence of incommensurate concepts of accuracy and the concluding section summarizes the findings and contributions. It also points to open issues for future research, including attention to cadastral infrastructures in studies of property rights and state mapping programs through more thorough ethnographic-based analyses of mapping activities.

## Mapping Power, Property Rights, and Calculating Territory

## Studying Power in Activities

The inversion of analysis of mapping power from the map itself to its use draws considerably from Foucault's engagements, particularly his later work (Elden 2007b) and his lectures on power. It also takes up concepts from infrastructure studies and grounded theory, especially the emphasis on the duality of interactions and boundary objects. Beyond dialectical processes of mapping, the concept of strategic logic, introduced by Foucault, provides the conceptual apparatus for the explanation of apparent inconsistencies and contradictions in cadastral mapping activities and helps ensure that the decomposition of infrastructure interactions and the elements of thematic domains reveal the empowerment and limitations that come through cadastral mapping.

## Key Concepts in Review

Following a recent emphasis on studying power arising in relations, this research takes up the challenge in Harley's insights (Harley and Laxton 2001) that the

geographical consideration of maps requires a deeper engagement with the activities of creating and using these maps.

In other words, the theoretical framework for the study of mapping activities accounts for how they both empower and limit these activities as well as others. Material collected from actual activities explains processes of empowerment and limitation. A rich body of geographic literature drawing on Foucault's work analyzes historical and current uses of cadastral maps and their centrality as attempts at assuring the means of control (Biggs 1999; Withers 1999; Hannah 2000; Pickles 2004). The wide-ranging use of Foucault's work reflects his frequent admonition that others work freely with his insights.

Mapping is an activity with power. As Elden related in detail (Elden 2007a, 2007b), power is made evident by resistance. Mapping power triggers many forms of resistance, often first noticed in the bureaucratic challenges to creating and maintaining cadastral materials, pointing to the capillary nature of mapping power. The myriad roles and forms of resistance to cadastral mapping power point to its importance in sustaining governmental power over its territory and the spatial regulation of economic activities. Mapping has been and remains a key element in the dramatic expansion in the scope of government and is a central component of state attempts to control territory (Andrews 1975; Dale 1976; Niemann 1984; Lampland 1992).

Recent work in geography has offered interesting analyses of the calculative techniques of mapping and their role in the state's construction and maintenance of territories (Hannah 2000; Crampton 2006; RoseRedwood 2012). As Elden (2007a) discussed, calculation, in Foucault's analysis, provided the means for political science to support post-Treaty of Westphalian rulers in the eighteenth century. Calculation supported the political strategies of rulers, which became topics for the education of petit-bourgeois serving on the court and remain topics of advanced education today, althoughwithdifferent names: political arithmetic, statistics, and political economy, among others. Elden's more recent work examines how territory continues to be a calculative category that is dependent on the existence of a range of techniques and political technology (Elden 2010). His analysis also reflects work by Hannah, who studied the microlevel relations of sovereignty through archaeological studies that examined how technologies of order were applied in the emergence of territory for the modern state (Hannah 2009). As Hannah explained, the application of calculative technologies is key to making territory legible and is a part of hierarchical, spatial inscriptions that can consolidate stillfragmented, but calculable, territory.

## Analysis of Cadastral Mapping

The theoretical framework for a consideration of property rights issues and the creation of calculable territory through the lens of mapping activities is the foundation for the analysis of these activities as interactions, boundary objects (e.g., parcels, surveys), and contextual factors (Figure 2). Power will be viewed as the capacity for these and contingent activities. Focusing on mapping activities means conceptualizing maps as artifacts that enforce their vision through inscription (Akrich 1992). Users often define quite different roles and complex relations to mapping, but ones that break the hierarchical institution's system for assigning and determining power. The privileging of sight in maps (Blomley 2003) reflects mapping's importance as the only means of seeing for those who cannot know the complex roles and relations it depicts firsthand. There are also changes, erasures, errors, and multiple cadastral maps to account for in cadastral mapping, however. Cadastral maps hold significance to empower and limit through relationships that meld multiple memories and infrastructures (Bowker 2006) into boundary objects that can facilitate coordination and overcome resistance. Each contention in cadastral mapping enacts struggles over property rights, conflicts involving administrative cadastral mapping activities, and state attempts to make territory calculable and legible.

The boundary objects concept is central to the theoretical framework and links the anti-essential reading of power with artifacts and concepts of mapping (Star and Griesemer 1989; F. Harvey and Chrisman 1998; Clarke and Star 2003; F. Harvey 2006). It offers a pragmatic, theoretical framework for analyzing interactions involving humans (people) and nonhumans (things) in mundane daily exchanges and more complex interactions positioned within complex relationships involving institutions and infrastructures (Neumann and Star 1996; Nardi and O'Day 1999; Star 1999). In the analysis of interactions, boundary objects are actors-nonhuman-involved in activities of the cadastral mapping infrastructure that interact with people and artifacts. In conjunction with Foucault's insight that power originates in chained relationships, boundary objects in cadastral mapping empower and limit through their usage in these interactions.

As the boundary objects in chained relationships are artifacts that can be used in different roles and additional relations, cadastral mapping activities are multidimensional (Figure 3). The legal validity of a parcel boundary for one transaction might be sufficient for pursuing other activities, such as issuing a building permit. Later revocation of that legal validity, however, might leave the building permit untouched but put it in conflict with other legal requirements. Strategic logic (Foucault and Senellart 2008) accounts for multidimensionality of cadastral mapping activities and of boundary objects by pointing to the validity and importance of outcomes in dynamic networks of relations that allow participants to both agree and disagree. Foucault explains strategic logic in distinction to linear logic and dialectical logic as a way of acting wherein an individual or group can accept apparently contradictory outcomes as a strategic outcome that keeps possibilities open. In terms of Hegelian dialectics involving thesis, antithesis, and synthesis, strategic logic allows multiple syntheses to be valid in conclusion. The exercise of strategic logic is exemplified in situations where the involved parties agree to one thing while retaining separate and distinct understandings and abilities that are in conflict and become obscured by the new agreement. A boundary object, such as a revised cadastral map with new boundaries, is an example. The chaining of state cadastral mapping activities contributes to making territory legible as an infrastructure (Bowker 2006; Nardi and O'Day 1999; Star 1999), a network of interactions of arrangements to support activities and institutions (F. Harvey and Chrisman 1998).

## A Brief Background to Polish Cadastral Mapping and Partitions, Colonization, and Occupations

Whereas western European history tends to focus on the consolidation and conflicts of western European nation-states from the mid-eighteenth century until the late twentieth century, during the same 250 years, Polish history can be characterized as a time of

colonization, partitions, wars, unrest, tragedy, and immense loss of life, which, of course, is also true for other areas of central and eastern Europe (Kotkin 1989; Rothschild and Wingfield 2000; Johnson 2002; Snyder 2004), but in comparison to neighboring states the conflux is unique in Poland. To put this in context, that period, however, follows an era of Polish history, when in contrast to the so-called late Middle Ages in western Europe, Poland was a leading European empire (Davies 2001). Following expansionist activities, conflicts exacerbated by uprisings of Ukrainians; the ascent of the Prussian, Austro-Hungarian, and Russian empires; and unscrupulous Polish nobility weakened the country. The loss of large areas to these empires continued through the eighteenth century until the Polish empire was completely partitioned into the surrounding three empires in 1795. From then until 1918, Poland did not exist as a state, and the Polish language and culture were suppressed to varying degrees in each of the three partitions. Each colonizing power created different administrations and policies. With the Treaty of Versailles following World War I, Poland was reconstituted as an independent state after 122 years. After a surprising victory over Bolshevik forces at the gates of Warsaw in 1921, Polish economic development took off, due in some measure to sweeping land reform (Davies 2001) that divided many large estates and distributed the lands among local peasantry. German and Russian political leaders' ambitions led to the disappearance of Poland at the beginning of World War II (1939), fulfilled through three short-lived partitions into German territory, Russian territory, and an occupied area under German administration. Nazi occupiers prepared to resettle Aryans in the area, commencing with programs to eradicate the Jewish population and other undesirable groups. They weakened Polish society by imprisoning elites, closing higher educational institutions, and forbidding education beyond the grammar school level (Romaniszyn and Nowak 2002).

At the end of World War II, Poland again was recreated as a sovereign state following the Yalta agreements, but its territory was moved westward by around 150 km (see Figure 5). The war was devastating for its people, economy, and infrastructure. In 1944, when the first Polish postwar government was formed in Lublin, Poland had lost 16 percent of its prewar population, its urban areas were largely destroyed (in the case of Warsaw, 85 percent of all structures), and rural areas remained in upheaval, as 2 million people moved from previously Polish areas in the east to the new western territories. A civil war in southeastern Poland with Ukrainian nationalists in the following years led to the forced migration of several hundred thousand people from that area to the new territories. The socialist government, under a Soviet umbrella, embarked on what was, in many ways, the successful rebuilding and reindustrialization of the country. Programs to collectivize the agriculture were less successful, except in areas gained through the Yalta treaty. Collectivization was abandoned in 1956 (Davies 2001; Ba´ nski 2006). The broad persistence of rural private land ownership in most of the country was unique among socialist countries of central and eastern Europe. This was the result of failed political calculations. Gomułka, the Communist Party leader and prime minister at that time, along with party leadership, felt that, with party control over the agricultural economy, the rural peasantry would slowly but surely fade away, and the Communist Party would avoid the catastrophes it had witnessed in Ukraine during the 1930s (Davies 2001).

Increasing crises in the 1960s and 1970s undermined Poland's economic and political stability. With increasing economic problems and failed government interventions, the trade union Solidarno´ s´ c grew into a national force. Its political ascendance and the perceived threat of Soviet invasion in response led to the declaration of martial law in 1981. The ongoing inability of the Socialist Party leadership to move the country beyond economic and political malaise, connected to the increased internationalization of the Polish economy (Hardy 2004), led to meetings with the former Solidarno´ s´ c leadership in the late 1980s and the election of opposition politicians in 1989 that effectively marked the demise of forty years of socialism (Garton-Ash 1983). All collective forms of ownership organized under the socialist government were dissolved in late 1989 as one of the first acts of the newly elected government. A new set of elites came to dominate political, economic, and cultural life in Poland, a process similar to that in other European countries experiencing transition at this time (Burawoy and Verdery 1999). In spite of challenges connected to internal political crises and the shock therapy designed by Balczerowicz and Sachs in the 1990s (Lipton and Sachs 1991; Balczerowicz 1996), increasing foreign investment and neoliberal policies paved the way for a growing European Union (EU) and interest in Poland becoming a member (Smith and Pickles 1998; Smith 2002; Stenning 2005; H¨ orschelmann and Stenning 2008). Poland joined the EU in 2004.

## Contexts of Cadastral Mapping in Poland: Informal Arrangements and Generations of Subversion

Experiences and civil society arrangements that developed during partitions; three-way colonization by the Austrian, Prussian, and Russian governments; war; and attempts to impose a socialist agriculture led to a deep mistrust of government. This mistrust has been the ground for developing ways of organizing society that at first restricted the impact of colonizing agencies, later evolved to undermine occupation authority, and then grew to subvert socialist policy. In almost 180 years of partitions and colonization, world wars, and the imposition of socialist policies, Poles developed what Davies (2001) called the 'wide-spread disrepute of political authority' (213). His analysis makes the trenchant point that 'Political authority was perceived as an alien imposition in partitioned Poland on several counts' (213).

Asafunctionofthestate, cadastral administration faced considerable opposition and, although widely used, was undermined in a variety of ways (Roszkowski 1991; Blobaum 1995; Romaniszyn and Nowak 2002). Related to cadastral mapping activities is Davies's further observation that 'the pattern of land ownership, for example, was altered, but not overturned' (2001, 215). For example, the novel Plac´ owka (Prus 1886), which is frequently required reading in schools, paints cadastral mapping as a means of German colonization and honors subversion of the cadastre (Sander 1995; F. Harvey 2006). In many Polish films about rural society, the cadastre figures as an aspect of an ongoing uncertainty and deeply rooted problems. The discrepancies of the Polish cadastral mapping figure in these movies as a geographical reference for the inconsistencies of Polish life, the necessity for subversion of government cadastral mapping, and unspoken neighborly cohesion among Poles in the face of overwhelming state power and bungling, capricious, and corrupt administrations. ## Polish Cadastral Institutions: Background and Current Status

Despite overt and subvert resistance, and contrary to Marxist ideology, during the socialist period (1944-1989) the Polish state supported the updating and maintenance of the cadastre in all of Poland, including in the collectivized areas (Federowski 1974). Actual cadastral-related updating and maintenance varied as the cadastre was the responsibility of each commune. Although the cadastre was often maintained and even updated, however, many transactions and discrepancies went without resolution. Historical discrepancies from the wars or earlier survivals remained (see Figure 6). The socialist cadastral records often rested on materials created by the previous colonizer. Some contemporary rural areas of Poland still rely on cadastral materials surveyed and entered in the cadastre 80 to 120 years ago.

## Research Project Design and Methods

This article covers a portion of results from a research project using a comparative approach on data collected over three years for longitudinal comparisons centered on cadastral mapping, boundary objects, and interactions. Three counties ( powiaty ), each located in a different historical partition of Poland, and distinguished by socioeconomic conditions and characteristics, were selected with the assistance of Polish geographers. The counties were chosen to maximize the historical, economic, and cultural diversity found in Poland within a small number of sites. After a preliminary visit to each county, I chose three villages in each county representative of the local diversity of agricultural activities and the different trajectories of transition since 1989. Ten interviews with randomly chosen rural residents were conducted yearly in each village. The nine village mayors of all communes ( W´ ojt ) were also interviewed yearly, along with the three directors of the county surveying offices, and staff from regional offices of the agricultural agency responsible for restructuring, modernizing, and administrating direct payments: the Agency for Restructuration and Modernization of Agriculture (ARMA). Finally, at the national level, interviews were held with Poland's chief surveyor and the head of the section of ARMA responsible for the Land Parcel Information System (LPIS) for auditing agricultural direct payment applications. Some additional interviews planned at regional-level agencies responsible for cadastral activities were not held because the individuals contacted refused to agree to an interview over the three-year duration of the project; in some cases it was possible to conduct some of the desired interviews but not within the yearly cycle. In total, during the three-year period, 532 interviews were held (see Figure 1 for a map showing the approximate location of the three sites, which have been given pseudonyms following the terms of human subjects protection for the project).

All interviews used the same set of questions. In the three years (2006-2009), Polish students conducted the interviews of rural residents. Powiat Chałupy is located in the northwest of Poland an area that was German territory from the close of the eighteenth century to the end of World War II. It is in a peripheral area of Poland but has seen steady agricultural investment since 1989. Powiat Bałycz is in the center of Poland and was part of the Russian partition until the end of World War I. It has become a very specialized and wealthy agricultural area. Powiat Anu´ scin is in Poland's southeast, previously part of the Austro-Hungarian partition. It is also peripherally located, with largely agricultural investments since 1989, and also experiences increasing, but still limited, tourism.

Although this article contributes to geographic studies of cadastral (property relations) mapping, the research design has some limits. Primarily, the design of the interview instruments focuses on comparisons and does not allow for a detailed analysis of specific boundary objects and interactions about the long-term dynamics of specific cadastral mapping processes through which individuals address discrepancies.

## Cadastral Mapping Activities Between Formal Bureaucracy and Informal Reliability

This analysis uses translated and coded interviews to study cadastral mapping activities that empower and limit property rights and state mapping programs. Discrepancies between the cadastre and actual property rights are broken down into interactions, boundary objects, and contextual factors. The more than 400 interviews provide rich examples of the innumerable problems, quarrels, and legal battles related to cadastral mapping discrepancies, broadly understood to include all activities related to property rights. Most interviewees were reticent about providing details to the outsiders conducting the interviews-an expected, historically conditioned attitude that keeps ambiguity high and protects relations. The decomposition of interview materials from multiple years led to the identification of many discrepancies, cases of empowerment and limitations, and inconsistencies and contradictions (Figure 7). Complexity, reticence, and contradictions add to the difficulty of the analysis of interview material and limit unequivocally lucid and concise examples to but a few. This section, therefore, reviews a set of exemplary cases to document the decomposition of cadastral activities.

I had just started the second-year interview with the mayor and assistant mayor in the village of Borowy, located in County B. After their welcome statements had been translated, the latter spun a one-liner to the mayor and me: 'Did you hear about the man who went to his field to cut his hay and found it covered in manure?' The mayor and the assistant burst out laughing. I smiled and said, 'Yes, that's a good joke,' silently noting during the translation of my response that the joke pointed to the ambiguity of boundaries in the area. I then asked if this kind of mistake happens often. 'Oh yes, regularly,' was the response. The frequency of such unclear situations might appear to be the crux of the joke, but as the interview continued, I realized that the initial reaction was a simplistic interpretation. As the interviewees discussed their difficulties in arranging land surveys and administrative documentation, I understood that the joke's twist was actually that the farmer hadn't coordinated with his neighbors about the use of that particular piece of land. The legacy of crafting informal

arrangements over many years meant that the only way to know to whom a parcel of land actually belonged and its usage was to engage neighbors and find agreements. The existence of such interactions was borne out in three years of interviews from this village and the surrounding area. The interviews made it plain that rural residents have established informal institutions to assure property rights with their own boundary objects; for example, identifying markers in fields and crafting agreements to coordinate which areas are used for fodder. This example also offers insight into a local culture's didactical methods to teach each other and introduce newcomers to the centrality of informal arrangements that maintain the reliability of informal approaches and sustain the cadastral mapping discrepancies.

Although the prevalence of and types of discrepancies and informal arrangements are in constant flux, discrepancies of this nature are commonplace in all three areas. In more than 350 interviews with farmers asking about the accuracy of maps showing field boundaries and location of fields, between 7 percent and 34 percent acknowledged discrepancies. These results reflect village mayors' and county surveyors' assessments as well. Differences among the three sites can be connected to historical factors. The lowest number of discrepancies was reported in County A, which, after World War II, completed a survey using archival materials and the very stable monumentation left by Germans. The highest number of discrepancies was reported in County B, which possessed a Russian title-only survey in the years up until 1918 and had never completed a full cadastral survey in the interwar period. The number of discrepancies ranged greatly in various areas of County C, which had been Austrian-Hungarian territory until 1918 and had also experienced depopulation following World War II in some areas (Figure 8).

Cadastral mapping activities exhibit a cleft between administrative arrangements and informal arrangements. Rural residents in all three counties have found ways to create arrangements that, for all their failings, remain the dominant way of regulating property rights. For the villagers, it is easier and cheaper to settle discrepancies by informal agreement, but the mapping discrepancies, transitions in agriculture, and increases in the value of agricultural land sometimes cause people to pursue more formal administrative arrangements. Also problematic are sales transactions and land leases. Cadastral mapping, faced with these pervasive discrepancies and challenges for administrative resolution, is often not rational, nor does it employ rigorous implementation of state and private calculative technologies.

Broadening the scope of the decomposition to include state administrative activities points out the variable role and power of cadastral mapping in addressing discrepancies. Administrative laws and regulations for cadastral mapping are homogenous across Poland; yet, interviews with local mayors and county surveyors led to the documentation of parcels with similar boundary objects but exhibiting very different interactions in the resolution of discrepancies. Cadastral mapping can readily produce territorial boundaries that are at odds with actual uses. The discrepancies can be the result of many issues. Some result from ambiguous resolution of ownership following the 1970s demise of many smaller cooperatives or their legally required closure in 1989. Many discrepancies arise from limited state support for organizing cadastral systems and updating older maps and registry entries. Costs of updating can be higher than the value of the land and must be borne by the property owner in most cases. In addition to these discrepancies, there are those between ministries' cadastral registries and other registries that I pointed out in reviewing the organization of Polish cadastral systems. The resolution of discrepancies requires tackling cadastral mapping technical issues featuring labyrinthine complexities. Many inconsistencies and contradictions surface when surveyors must return to historical materials related to parcel boundaries to assure the providence of current boundaries. Because of the complexity, cost, and anxiety over potential losses due to the resolution of discrepancies, the systematic resurvey of boundaries is rarely pursued. As a result, in one county, one village had a 1990-era cadastral map, and a nearby village was still using the cadastral mapping from 1917 as the formal records of property rights. The title registry in this area also contained records recording ownership rights of individuals who had last used the property at an unknown time but at least forty years earlier.

Technical guidelines, regulations, and laws address cadastral mapping issues. Accredited Polish surveyors must follow legal prescriptions lest they risk losing clients or employment. Each identifiable problem involves at least one boundary object and an interaction but usually many more. The underlying challenge in resolving discrepancies is the semantic interpretation of the legal concepts that define the objects used in rational and calculated approaches of cadastral mapping. A surveyor explained, 'All boundaries that are registered are the legal boundaries.' Interviewees clarified what this means: Multiple valid, legally registered boundaries can exist for what is the same actual boundary on the ground. Resolving the issues can be a complex and costly process. More fundamental issues, however, are the interpretation of what defines a boundary and the correspondence between a boundary as defined in a registry and said boundary on the ground. The surveyor in County Chałupy stated in an interview that what people call a boundary on the ground is just a convention; it is not legal, and not binding, but hopefully coincides with a legal boundary.

The discrepancies are cases wherein people acknowledge problems. The number ranges from 5 percent to 40 percent among the three counties. Residents and surveyors will address them, but the resources are limited, and the number of cases overwhelms capacity. The interactions, boundary objects, and contextual factors are extremely complex. For instance, after just completing a series of interviews with mayors who complained about the problem of getting useable cadastral materials, the surveyor of County Bałycz responded at length to my question about the reliability of the records. She said that the 20 percent to 30 percent of county cadastral maps show discrepancies with actual uses and boundaries. The contextual factors and boundary objects are known. Under the Russian administration (before 1918), no accurate cadastral maps were prepared; only titles were recorded. After World War II, people didn't care about the exact location of boundaries in the orchards that predominated. These issues should be dealt with through mandatory

checks of all cadastral records every ten to fifteen years, but the funds aren't available, so checks are only done if requested by landowners. An additional administrative hindrance is that the Polish National Treasury owns the cadastral records, so county cadastral data must be requested from its offices before the county can work on the material. In all cases, though, when a person or party brings an error to the county surveyor's attention, a legal process is begun to clarify the source of the error. Half of the cases are paid for by private individuals and the other half by the county administration. Cases are regularly filed, but processing depends on funds. Concluding her comments, the surveyor added that if there are considerable problems in an area, the county surveyor can also work on new maps as resources permit.

The persistence of discrepancies and resulting inconsistencies and contradictions of cadastral mapping activities, including the collection of property taxes (a minor, often trifling, amount for individuals in relationship to incomes), cumulatively remain an important portion of local government revenue. Mayors recognize the problems; for instance, one mayor stated, 'Generally, it's [discrepancies] a very big percentage. People do not want to touch this problem. It comes out when there is a work on infrastructure, like electrical posts, roads. New measures make people realize the size of the problems: maps, uses, boundaries do not correspond. The situation is bad.' The bad situation hinders, but never obstructs, the collection of taxes, however. A village mayor told of receiving a list of tax records so they would have the legal names of persons responsible for paying taxes. It turns out that it doesn't matter if the names are those of real people, and as many as a third of the records can be wrong. Nonetheless, the bills are sent out to those names, and as long as the bills are paid and legal requirements fulfilled, things are okay. The few people who are behind in paying their taxes can be contacted by staff, neighbors, or council persons. Other mayors confirmed this approach to working around the breakdown of cadastral mapping.

## Accuracy: Mapping's Empowerment and Limitations

The persistence of discrepancies in the preceding examples between the formal cadastre and informal arrangements of property rights might seem to point to a limited significance of state efforts to control territory, which is in fact very far from Polish reality. Formal, notarized property transactions and officially endorsed cadastral records remain a necessity in Poland. Although the correspondence of the records to actual boundaries and parcels is often limited, these discrepancies fail to systematically disrupt most administrative and rural resident activities. Facilitating legal interactions to resolve discrepancies involves both formal and informal spheres in complex negotiations that begin with informal resolutions accepted by all involved parties. Afterward, formal arrangements can be made with or without resolution of the specific discrepancies.

The interviews elicited examples of discrepancy negotiations that often followed strategic logic. The issue of accuracy from cartography and GIScience is an excellent example to demonstrate how strategic logic explains the persistence of discrepancies and the simultaneous empowerment and limitations of cadastral mapping. On the face of it, persistence of discrepancies means greater flexibility for rural residents' nonbureaucratic arrangements, while administrative agencies retain their rational and calculative administration and its self-referencing metrics. The interactions and boundary objects, however, point to far more complex concepts of accuracy that empower and limit cadastral mapping in important ways and offer examples for the limits of calculative approaches.

The complexity can be traced to Poland's accession to the EU in 2004 and the implementation of EU agricultural policy, the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP; Leo and Lemoine 2001; Bogaerts, Williamson, and Fendel 2002; Departament Ewidencji Gospodarstw 2004). Although the implementation of CAP actually began many years prior to Poland's joining the EU, and CAP implementation in EU member states can be vastly different, the underlying use of cadastral mapping, in principle for auditing applications for CAP support, is the same. In a system called the Integrated Administrative Control System (IACS), national agencies responsible for CAP and the EU have created this administrative capacity (Leo and Lemoine 2001; EU 2004). In Poland, although the creation of IACS involved the use of cadastral data from the ground and buildings registry, errors and inaccuracies impeded the use of these data. Consequently, the auditing relies on interpretations of cadastral data, aerial photography, and spot checks. Around 7 percent of all applications for assistance from the EU are audited annually. Accuracy is important in the audits because, under the directive for IACS, if the threshold of errors specified in

regulations and agreements of the applications is crossed, the member state is responsible for paying a penalty based on a further analysis.

Accuracy, of course, has been a central issue in cadastral mapping activities and also occupies an important position in cartography and GIScience (MacDougall 1975; Goodchild 1978; Chrisman 1987; Veregin 1989). The focus for this section is on this question: Does the use of geographic information connected to discrepancies between existing cadastral maps and actual boundaries change the significance of accuracy in cadastral mapping? The analysis of interview material points to two divergent concepts of accuracy. For rural residents the meaning of accuracy lies not in the degree of correspondence between measurement and phenomena or location but rather in the degree to which rural residents think the administration believes its cadastral data is valid. For example, asked about the accuracy of cadastral maps of their areas, respondents in rural areas stated that the maps were accurate when they also believed that the local administration regarded the maps as sufficient. Scientific concepts of accuracy held relevance for administrators, and the administrative concept places an emphasis on measures of correspondence to internal specifications, instead of correspondence to the location of features on the ground. This distinction became more relevant with the introduction of geographic information technologies facilitating more rapid collection of field data, but cadastral mapping still requires extensive, archival research to determine the providence of different surveys. Internal administrative conflicts related to mapping accuracy involve the administrative power of the cadastre to determine which boundaries and survey materials empower and limit ownership, rights, and obligations (Dale 1976; Dale and McLaughlin 1999). Within the administration, the relationship between established practices and new sources of information has begun to change (see discussion in the previous section), but larger changes can be anticipated in the future.

To help minimize the impact of resolving internal administrative discrepancies, rural residents and communal administrators have developed a third concept of accuracy that makes it possible for the scientific and rural resident concepts of accuracy to coexist. An example of strategic logic would be the situation wherein, for generations, there has been a boundary object acknowledged by both rural residents and administrators. For administrators it is a line on the map, but for residents it is a divider of variable width, usually 20 to 50 cm wide. Returning to the issue of accuracy and cadastral mapping power, the impact of a recent GIS-based program (LPIS) for the distribution of agricultural direct payments has been limited (Harvey 2009). Unsurprisingly, among rural residents who received considerable support through direct payments, the responses suggest that the maps provided through LPIS are more accurate than the cadastre. In the years from 2006 to 2008, among 120 rural resident interviewees, a stable adherence to this strategic logic is evident. In 2006 most (72 percent) of the respondents reported that the areas of their farm indicated on maps that they received from the agency implementing CAP programs corresponded to the area they farmed. In 2007 and 2008 the responses to the question about LPIS map accuracy were 78 percent and 76 percent, respectively. The perception of county survey map accuracy is also consistent among rural residents. In 2006 only 80 percent of the rural residents interviewed indicated that county survey maps were accurate. In 2007 the response was 88 percent and in 2008 it was 87 percent. In all years, most of the interviewees said that they had seen the maps from LPIS and the county surveyor, but one third of these respondents had not seen the county survey maps. Through the three years of interviews, data suggest that County Chałupy has both accurate county and LPIS maps and degree of correspondence; County Bałycz has both the lowest accuracy overall for both maps and lowest correspondence between LPIS and county maps. Rural residents in County Anu´ scin indicate that the LPIS maps have high accuracy, whereas the county maps have low accuracy.

Consideration of responses to questions about cadastral mapping accuracy and interactions with cadastral mapping administration in the interviews illustrates the evolution of cadastral mapping boundary objects and interactions in response to the introduction of GIS-based technologies. The interview material suggests that perceptions of higher mapping accuracy actually reflect greater ability to enter into arrangements with neighbors, informally agreeing to the boundaries of land in spite of formal records. In all years, a small number of the rural residents referred to conflicts surrounding boundaries and fallow land, and a good number of interviewees in each area offered contradictory responses to related questions in the interview. Contradictions in responses to questions about the accuracy of cadastral maps and LPIS maps increased slightly each year. As doubts waned about the administration's ability to check self-reported field size, a growing sense set in that the LPIS mapping is actually not as accurate as administrators claim, but the inaccuracy doesn't matter.

## Mapping Power Comes Through Activities

Theory is when you know everything, but nothing works. Practice is when everything works, but nobody knows why. In this office we have managed to combine theory and practice-nothing works and nobody knows why.

- -SigninEnglish, CODGiK office, Warsaw, Poland, 2005

To conclude this article, this quote seems ideal for expressing the complexity of interactions, boundary objects, and contextual factors of mapping power. The workers in an office responsible for distributing government maps who made the sign are acknowledging that the government project of cadastral mapping faces limits that people, including themselves, don't understand. Yet mapping continues. This is also the stance taken toward the discrepancies of cadastral mapping of many interviewees in rural Poland. Indeed, for everyone, cadastral mapping activities are full of problems that nobody fully understands, but mapping still works. Attempts to resolve discrepancies require great effort to arrange activities that bring formal and informal institutions into some measure of agreement-mapping must continue to satisfy state ideology and Western rationale commitments. Indeed, much of the cadastral mapping I examined for this research leaves incalculable territory. As the decomposition of interactions, boundary objects, and contextual factors indicates, however, mapping power often might be limited, but it still remains a key component of inscribing (Latour 1987) power through nonhuman intermediaries. Cadastral mapping governanceactivities often foreground theoretically relevant qualities of performing state functions with only limited connections to actual situations, however. Motivations remain for allowing the discrepancies to continue to assure that various groups and individuals retain the resources and relationships to which they now have access for their economic activities.

The theoretical framework introduced in this article provides an analysis of mapping activities characterized by the enablement and limitations of mapping power where incalculability dominates. In this framework, mapping activities hold power predominantly through administrative activities that influence geographic relations for state control, rather than through capabilities of rational calculation. Strategic logic connected, for

example, to activities involving different understandings of the accuracy of mapping by multiple actors facilitates the continued coexistence of incalculable territory with rational mapping. The anti-essentialist approach of this research also can help provide traction for other studies of mapping practices. The theoretical framework can help untangle the conflicts in the construction and use of geographic information by recognizing the often noted contingency and power of cartographic materials and geographic information (Monmonier 1993, 1995). Further, despite apparent totalizing, cadastral mapping incalculable territory suggests a potential for the subaltern (Sparke 1995; Mignolo 2000; Escobar 2001) to undermine the state attempts to control people and territory through mapping.

The ongoing processes of governance create constant needs for mapping information that empowers and constrains. Cadastral maps instantiate state authority to facilitate activities against the constant challenge of bringing maps and, mainly now, geographic information into agreement with changing situations. The limits that mapping power faces reflect the long-held understanding of many geographers, including Ratzel in Anthropogeographie (1882; Uhlig 2007), that space is a matter of relations and that cartographic lines are fictions. Although maps can certainly precede a territory, the results of this research suggest that maps and territory stand in multiple relations best understood through strategic logic, wherein mapping activities both empower and limit interactions between the state and individuals. In spite of a plethora of cadastral maps, calculative modes of mapping power can be constrained and subverted, leaving incalculable territory, maps of limited relevance, and abundant spaces of relations.
