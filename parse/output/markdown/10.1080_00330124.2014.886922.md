## Spatial Patterns of Seasonal Level Diurnal Variations of Ozone and Respirable Suspended Particulates in Hong Kong

## Zhen Liu and Shouraseni Sen Roy

University of Miami

In this study we analyzed the diurnal spatial patterns of cycles of ozone (O3) and respirable suspended particulate (RSP) matter in Hong Kong between 2001 and 2011. We used harmonic analysis to reveal the diurnal cycle in the seasonal-level spatial patterns. The diurnal cycle of O3 is stronger in the northeast rural areas of Hong Kong than in the urban center due to the lower titration effects of nitric oxide in the rural areas. For RSP, the southern sections show the strongest diurnal cycle and the northeastern sections show the weakest diurnal cycle, which could be explained by the heavy vehicular traffic in the southern urban areas and light vehicular traffic in the northeast rural areas. The diurnal patterns of O3 and RSP both show stronger cycles in the summer months, which might be related to the diurnal rainfall pattern. The time of maximum for O3 is around 1400 Local Standard Time (LST) in the afternoon in summer, whereas a wider range in the time of maximum, between 1100 and 1400 LST, was observed in winter. The spatial pattern of the time of maximum for RSP showed a gradual progression from the center of the city to the west, between 1300 and 1700 LST, which is consistent with rush hour traffic. Key Words: diurnal, harmonic, Hong Kong, ozone, respirable suspended particulates, spatial analysis.

H ong Kong, as one of the most populated cities in the world, has very high levels of air pollution, leading to significant health problems such as asthma and bronchial infections, as people ingest a significant amount of toxic elements (Davies et al. 1990; Raghunath et al. 1999; Mielke et al. 2000; Y´ a ˜ nez et al. 2003). Increasing evidence of rising levels of air pollution is apparent as a result of heavy volumes of traffic and emissions from coal-fired power stations, which resulted in 175 days of 'very high' pollution in 2011 (this level triggers government warnings for people with heart and respiratory illness to avoid areas with heavy traffic; Ghosh 2013). In this context, it is critical to analyze the diurnal variation of air pollution levels, because human activities have a major influence on air pollution such as respirable suspended particulates (RSP) and ozone (O3). For instance, vehicular exhaust emissions are a major cause of increasing air pollution, which is occurring as a result of the continuous increase in the use of motor vehicles, especially emissions from diesel vehicles including all public buses and goods vehicles, which contribute 98 percent of all emitted particulates. These result in a large volume of emissions of RSP and nitrogen oxides (NOx; Lu and Wang 2008). As NOx is a very important O3 precursor, the high volume of traffic in Hong Kong leads to higher concentrations of RSP and O3. Additionally, Hong Kong experiences the summer and winter

monsoon, which results in entirely different seasonal wind circulations, with air masses originating from different areas and different long-range transport of pollutants (L. Y. Chan, Chan, and Qin 1998). The majority of these studies, however, are limited in the numberofstations or length of observations employed, with limited spatial analysis. Therefore, in this study, we used eleven years (from 2001-2011) of air pollution data from fourteen monitoring sites to determine the seasonal-level spatial variations in the diurnal patterns of O3 and RSP concentration in Hong Kong. O3 and RSP are two of the major air pollutants in Hong Kong with continuous hourly observation records from 2001 to 2011.

Compared to previous studies, however, our study provides more comprehensive and detailed spatial analysis of O3 and RSP patterns in Hong Kong. Most previous studies (L. Y. Chan, Chan, and Qin 1998; So and Wang 2003) only used several stations for a relatively short period to analyze the pollution pattern. We use a longer data period (eleven years, from 2001 to 2011) to reveal the diurnal variations in the spatial patterns of pollutants across Hong Kong. In addition, unlike some of the previous studies that take into consideration only one station in each category (representing urban, rural, and new towns; So and Wang 2003), our study consists of data from fourteen stations that cover the majority of Hong Kong. Furthermore, we have used harmonic analysis, which has been successfully used for efficiently detecting diurnal cycles for various climate phenomena across the world (Atkinson-Palombo, Miller, and Balling 2006; Sen Roy and Sen Roy 2011). Therefore, the results of our study enable us to better understand the spatial patterns and thus lead to better policy formulation.

## Study Area

HongKongisaninternational metropolis on the south coast of China with a population of about 7,026,400 and total area of about 1,104 km 2 (Li and Liu 2012). The majority of Hong Kong is mountainous, located at the tip of the Pearl River delta and close to several large industrial and urban centers (Figure 1). Hong Kong has a subtropical climate characterized by hot, humid summers and generally mild to cool winters. Asian continental high pressure dominates in winter, whereas low pressure prevails in summer. Therefore, there are dry easterly or northeasterly winds in autumn and winter and moist winds from the southwest in summer. ## Harmonic Analysis

The hourly air pollution data for O3 and RSP were aggregated at the seasonal level, which included March to May as spring, June to August as summer, September to November as autumn, and December to February as winter. The seasonal classification is in conformity with previous seasonal level studies for Hong Kong (L. Y. Chan, Chan, and Qin 1998; HKEPD 2009; Li and Liu 2012) and thus enables comparison of final results.

One of the most widely used methods for examining diurnal patterns in weather phenomena, such as rainfall and levels of different pollutants, includes harmonic analysis (Sen Roy and Balling 2005; AtkinsonPalombo, Miller, and Balling 2006; Sen Roy and Sen Roy 2011). ## Results and Discussion

Seasonal-Level Patterns of O3

As one of the major air pollutants in many urban areas, O3 is produced from complex photochemical reactions involving NOx and volatile organic compounds (VOCs)inthepresenceofsunlight. Irrespective of how O3 is formed, it is important to understand its spatial and temporal variations to formulate effective policies to reduce excessive O3 episodes. Between 1999 and 2005, O3 episodes increased, but anthropogenic VOC emissions, which are mainly from vehicle emissions, decreased in Hong Kong (C. Chan and Yao 2008). Results from previous studies show that both local and regional contributions to O3 episodes are from vehicular emissions and power plants in Hong Kong from 1994 to 1999 (Lee et al. 2002; Liu and Chan 2002). The analysis of O3 levels by Ding et al. (2004) and Lam et al. (2005), however, revealed that O3 episodes from 1999 to 2003 were mainly from sources outside of Hong Kong. In addition, Zhang et al. (2007) concluded that O3 episodes in Hong Kong were caused by both the regional contributions and the local emissions of air pollutants.

The results of our analysis revealed, at the seasonal level, that higher O3 concentrations were observed during the autumn and spring, compared to winter and summer (Figure 2). The relatively lower values during the summer were caused by the washout effect of the summer monsoon system in this region. This is in conformity with the results of previous studies, which indicated lower O3 levels during the summer in Hong Kong (L. Y. Chan, Chan, and Qin 1998; So and Wang 2003). Additionally, Lee et al. (2002) found only six O3 episode days during summer between 1994 and 1999. A later study by Huang et al. (2005), however, found that the number of O3 episode days gradually increased from seven days in 1999 to eighteen in 2003 and then to thirty-seven in 2004. The seasonallevel differences can be attributed to the seasonal alternation of the prevailing oceanic and continental air masses, along with the climate regime associated with the Asian monsoon system (L. Y. Chan, Chan, and Qin 1998). The spatial patterns among the different stations revealed the highest concentration at the rural station of Tap Mun (85.76 µ g/m 3 ), where there is the

## Seasonal Level Patterns of RSP

RSP in general refers to those suspended particulates with nominal aerodynamic diameters of 10 µ morless.

There are three main suspended particulates including PM1.0 (fine particles, with diameter &lt; 1 µ m), PM2.5 (fine particles, with diameter &lt; 2.5 µ m) and PM10 (coarse particles, with diameter &lt; 10 µ m). In this study, we examined PM10 levels across fourteen stations spread across Hong Kong. Emissions from vehicles and power plants are the major sources of RSP in Hong Kong. In addition, photochemical reactions of NOx and VOCs and gaseous pollutants, such as sulfur dioxide (SO2), are also significant sources of RSP (HKEPD 2009).

It is evident from Figure 2B that the roadside stations have the highest RSP concentration during all seasons, which overlaps with areas of heavy traffic. Unlike the spatial patterns observed in the case of O3, the observed levels of RSP were lowest in the rural station of Tap Mun. The average levels of RSP during

the entire study period for the roadside stations were 25.88 µ g/m 3 , whereas the average levels varied between 25 µ g/m 3 for rural stations and 31 µ g/m 3 for new town and urban stations. Similar patterns were reported by L. Y. Chan et al. (2001), who examined the spatial patterns in the levels of total suspended particulates (TSP), PM10, and PM2.5 measurements across eleven sites and found the highest daily average of all the particulates concentration in the Sham Shui Po site associated with heavy traffic. Apart from traffic, other factors such as topography, industrial, construction, and commercial activities determined the variations among districts in the same land use category. The average PM2.5/PM10 ratio is high, indicating that fine particles dominated the particle mass concentration in the roadside microenvironment (L. Y. Chan et al. 2001). Furthermore, Lee and Hills (2003) found the annual average PM10 concentrations in Hong Kong from 2003 to 2005 were about 20 percent higher than before 2003. Their results showed that some of the high PM10 episodes (with daily average PM10 concentrations higher than 180 mg/m 3 ) were associated with anticyclonic and cyclonic synoptic systems over southern China.

During summer, the clean air masses arrive from the South China Sea by the southwest monsoon, and therefore not much pollutants are carried to Hong Kong, resulting in relatively lower concentrations in summer. During the winter, however, the northeast winds transport pollutants emitted from mainland China. Particulates accumulate near the ground because they are combined from local and long-range transport sources, leading to higher RSP concentrations in winter (L. Y. Their results indicated seasonal variation, with higher levels of concentration in winter and lower levels in summer, which was attributed to the dry continental winter monsoon and the wet oceanic summer monsoon circulation patterns. Another study by Cheng et al. (2006)

## Diurnal Variations of O3

The results from previously published studies revealed a gradual buildup of O3 before noon, peaking in the afternoon hours outside urban centers, when precursor pollutants are accumulated and sunlight is strong (HKEPD 2009). Within urban areas, the lowest O3 concentrations were often observed during the rush hours, because a large volume of NOx from the rushhourtraffic can react with and reduce O3 concentration and sunlight is not strong enough for photochemical reactions to occur. The hourly plot of O3 levels through our study period revealed similar patterns of lower levels during rush hours (Figure 3). A mild peak, however, was evident in the new town residential and urban area stations a few hours before sunrise and a more distinct peak was evident during late afternoon hours around 1400 to 1500 LST. ## Diurnal Variations of RSP

The hourly average annual patterns of RSP were significantly higher (70-90 µ g/m 3 ) for the roadside stations located in the urban center, whereas the lowest levels (40-50 µ g/m 3 ) were observed in the rural station located in the northeastern part of Hong Kong (Figure 3B). The hourly variations through the day were also most pronounced for roadside stations (ranging between 49.4 and 87.1 µ g/m 3 ), whereas there were not many variations at the other stations. The diurnal cycle in the case of RSP was substantially stronger for all seasons than that observed in the case of O3 levels, as depicted by the higher values of variance explained (Figure 6). Similar to the seasonal variations observed in the case of O3, however, the strength of the diurnal cycle was greater during summer and autumn (Figures 6B, 6C) compared to winter and spring seasons (Figures 6A, 6D). The stronger diurnal cycle during the warmer months is related again to the distinct diurnal variation in rainfall, in the form of peak rainfall activity during the morning hours. The spatial variations for variance explained revealed relatively lower values for all the roadside stations located in the urban center for all seasons. As shown in Table 1, the roadside stations have the lowest elevations, which results in the unpredictable diurnal pattern of winds, leading to a weaker diurnal cycle. The rela- tively higher values of variance explained observed at the nearby urban and new town stations were mainly caused by vehicle emissions from the heavy traffic in the urban center. The time of maximum RSP concentration was consistent throughout the different seasons, ranging between 1100 and 1700 LST during summer and autumn and from 1400 to 1900 LST during winter and spring (Figure 6). Specifically, the spatial pattern for the time of maximum exhibited a gradual progression from the urban center to the west from 1400 to 1700 LST (as shown in Figure 6). During winter, the maximum time for RSP concentration in the urban areas is later than that in summer, at 1600 LST in the afternoon (Figure 6D). The later time of maximum is a combined effect of both the local rush-hour traffic emissions and the inversion effect, keeping RSP at a low elevation. Furthermore, the greater range in the time of maximum during winter is a result of the reduced washout effect in winter, leading to longer retention of RSP within the urban center. including Cheng et al. (2006), which analyzed the diurnal pattern of PM2.5 and found high concentrations in the daytime (0800-1800 LST) and low concentrations at night (2200-0600 LST). This is consistent with the changes in traffic flow. Two major peaks generally occurred in the morning and afternoon depending on the traffic counts around rush hours for each day (Cheng et al. 2006).

## Conclusions

In this article, we have analyzed the spatial patterns of seasonal-level diurnal cycles of O3 and RSP in Hong Kong using hourly air pollution data, from eleven and fourteen stations, respectively, from 2001 to 2011. At the seasonal scale, both O3 and RSP levels were lowest during the summer, which was related to the prevailing rainy season. We used harmonic analysis to determine the spatial patterns of the diurnal cycle of O3 and RSP concentrations across Hong Kong.

O3 has an overall weaker diurnal cycle in the urban center of Hong Kong than the rural areas in the northeast. The stronger diurnal cycle of O3 in the rural areas can be attributed to the lower titration effect of NO. In the case of the seasonal differences of diurnal pattern, the diurnal cycle is stronger in the summer months due to the prevalence of a distinct diurnal rainfall pattern. The time of maximum during summer is around 1400 LST, whereas the time of maximum during winter is a little later with a wider range of time maximum. This is consistent with the findings of previous studies indicating a similar midafternoon peak (L. Y. Chan, Chan, and Qin 1998; HKEPD 2009).

RSPhasarelatively stronger diurnal cycle compared to O3, as revealed by the variance explained. The value for the variance explained for RSP is much higher in the summer compared to winter and spring, mainly due to the more distinct washout effect of rainfall in summer. Similar to that observed in the case of O3, the diurnal cycle during summer was stronger than that observed during the winter. In the summer, the strength of the diurnal cycle was lowest at three roadside stations, which is a result of the low elevation where RSP could be affected by the unpredictable pattern of winds. During summer, the time of maximum RSP is consistent with daytime rush hours. The spatial pattern of the time of maximum has a gradual progression from the urban center to the west during all seasons. The maximum hour of RSP in winter is later than that in summer due to the combined effect of rushhour traffic emission and the air masses from the north.

Overall, the spatial patterns reveal the effect of regional-level atmospheric circulation processes in the form of monsoon rainfall, leading to more homogenous patterns during summer and autumn. During winter and spring, the role of local-level processes and topography played a more dominant role. The results of this study reveal the spatial patterns of diurnal patterns of O3 and RSP concentration across Hong Kong, which are broadly in agreement with previous stud- ies. The use of long-term station-level hourly data has made it possible to identify and explain the underlying processes for the resulting diurnal patterns at the local level across the study area. The results of this study could help policymakers to better understand the diurnal pattern of air pollution that is critical for this region and make better decisions about regulation in controlling the pollution.

Finally, in a broader context our study not only benefits the people in Hong Kong but also those in other large, rapidly developing metropolitan areas in the tropics. Hong Kong typifies densely populated tropical cities in Asia, and thus the results from this study also can be in generalized to other large cities with similar attributes in the tropics. Additionally, these results can be used for comparative studies about ozone and particulate matter pollution in other big cities, such as Atlanta (Mulholland et al. 1998) and Phoenix (AtkinsonPalombo, Miller, and Balling 2006). Comparing the difference of diurnal ozone patterns between the cities will help us explore the role of the differences in topography and urbanization process in the resulting spatial patterns in pollution levels. /squaresolid
