ABSTRACT. The predominant trend has been rapid and unprecedented migration from rural to urban China since the mid-1980s. This dramatic demographic change has profoundly reshaped the migrants' sense of the newly settled place. By examining the perceptual changes that have occurred in this usual context, we attempted to analyze the impact of social connection on the migrants' sense of belonging to the host cities. In order to accomplish this, we conducted an empirical study featuring 12,807 rural-to-urban migrants across eight Chinese cities. We found that  their  contact  with  residents  who  were  not  related  to  them  was  positively  linked  to  the development of a sense of belonging in the host cities, whereas contact with only nonresidents had the opposite effect. In addition, we also observed that one's sense of belonging is mostly fostered by one's direct interaction with a place, but it could be also be indirectly mediated via social  contacts.  Furthermore,  the  context  of  place  will  affect  one's  affinity  for  it.  Also,  the availability of public services generates positive feelings regarding the new place, whereas large population size has the opposite effect. This study deepened our understanding about migrants' subjective sense place and revealed the inherent complexity in this relationship.

Keywords: social contact; rural-urban migrant; sense of belonging; hukou; public services; urban China.

People frequently feel that they belong to the geographic area in which they reside, visit, or even just know about (Relph, 1976; Hidalgo and Hernández 2001). Such a feeling, often called sense of belonging, represents the perceptual, emotional, and affective relationship between humans and  a  place  (Mee  and  Wright  2009;  Ralph  and  Staehell  2011).  In  an  effort  to  develop  this connection, a certain level of spatial proximity to it must be maintained, whereas frequent travel away from it has the opposite effect. Until recently, the positive functions of human mobility have been gradually emphasized (see Lewicka 2011; Manzo and Devine-Wright 2013; DevineWright  2014).  We  will  discuss  the  psychological  and  social  aspects  of  this  phenomenon.  For example, according to Gabriele Pollini (2005), a migrant's social contact with residents or other migrants are cherished experiences along his or her journey and we realize that sense of place is not bound to a fixed physical location; likewise, Lewicka (2011) further asserts that frequent migration might mitigate one's existing sense of belonging to one place and enhance active and conscious affinity for other places. Apparently, there have been more debates than agreement on this timely and controversial topic.

To  shed  light  on  this  divergence,  we  attempted  to  explore  the  migrants'  sense  of belonging  to  their  new  homes  in  the  context  of  China's  rapid  urbanization.  This  dramatic population  change  has  profoundly  reshaped  their  perceptions  of  the  newly  settled  places. These rural-to-urban migrants ( Nongmingong in Chinese pinyin) are often referred to as 'nonhukou' due to the extreme difficulty in obtaining local government-registered urban residency, especially in large cities (Chan 2009; Wu and Wang 2014). In 2015, China's central government proclaimed that rural migrants would be granted additional rights and benefits from their host cities'  governments.  This  ambitious  plan  would  allow  approximately  300  million  migrants  to

Previous studies have indicated that migrants who maintain strong ties with their places of  origin  show  little  connection  to  the  host  cities,  whereas  those  who  actively  pursue  social contacts in the host cities show a strong sense of belonging (Du and Li 2010; Fan 2011; Zhu and Chen  2010).  However,  in  these  studies,  various  types  of  migrant-local  contact  were  lumped together  that  made  it  difficult  to  gauge  the  impact  of  migrants'  kin-based  relationships compared to those with local residents. In fact, evidence suggests that migrants' kin-oriented social contacts in the new regions actually increases their sense of belonging to their places of origin.  (Cao  and  others  2015).  Apparently,  connection  to  blood  relatives,  which  differs significantly from newly established relationships with friends, colleagues, and other acquaintances, plays a major role in migrants' attempts to forge social connections in their host cities.  In  addition,  existing  studies  have  failed  to  compare  migrants'  sense  of  belonging  in various neighborhoods or cities in China; however, development of belonging has been linked to contextual variables (Lewicka 2011) such as the size of the neighborhoods and the cities.

To address these research gaps, we investigated rural migrants' sense of belonging on two fronts.  The  first  was  the  effects  of  having  social  contacts  in  the  new  places  of  residence, specifically with three different groups: residents who are kin, nonkin, and other migrants who relocated from the same area. The second was the various characteristics of the host cities (for example, population size and the availability of public services).

The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. The next section provides a general discussion of how sense of place differs between migrants and local residents. Then, the data source  and  regression  analysis  technique  are  introduced.  In  the  final  section,  we  report empirical findings and conclude with policy implications.

## &lt;&lt;A-HEAD&gt;&gt; THEORETICAL PERSPECTIVES

## &lt;&lt;B-Head&gt;&gt; SENSE OF BELONGING TO A PLACE

Sense  of  place,  as  one  of  the  quintessential  topics  in  geography,  transcends  the  material.  It suggests a socially and psychologically constructed relationship that represents a perceptional, affective, and emotional bond between human beings and place (Relph 1976; Tuan 1977). This complex phenomenon is known by many names such as place attachment, identity, dependence, bonding with place, and the like. (Scannell and Gifford 2010; Lewicka 2011; Qian, Zhu, and Liu 2011; Zhao, Huang and Sui, 2018), and has been closely studied by many scholars from a wide variety of fields (Counted 2016).

This  multidimensional  construct  not  only  encompasses  attachment  to,  dependence  on, and  identification  with  a  place,  it  also  involves  an  individual's  affective,  behavioral,  and cognitive responses or attitudes towards it. As long as one interacts with a place, the need to belong there is provoked (Pollini 2005; Counted 2016). It can be measured through the level of attachment people feel towards a place (affective component), how dependent they are on the resources  or  activities  provided  in  a  geographical  setting  to  ensure  their  survival  (the

## &lt;&lt;B-Head&gt;&gt; MIGRANTS' SENSE OF BELONGING TO A PLACE

Migration has been regarded as a deterritorializing process that mitigates migrants' sense of belonging to a place (Tuan 1977), since mobility would impede one from developing proximity to  it  (Relph  1976).  However,  recently  scholars  have  begun  to  argue  that  migrants  construct sentimental connections by establishing social relationships and bringing spaces, objects, and elements together that represent relationships, events, and memories (Dayaratne and Kellett 2008; Lewicka 2011). As stated above, social relationships between migrants and residents are particularly important to that process, as belonging is socially defined (Ralph and Staehell 2011; Bennett 2013). Apparently, if people feel that they belong to a social group in a place, they could also perceive belonging to that place (Mee and Wright 2009; Ralph and Staehell 2011). In this sense, social contact with residents further enhances a migrant's sense of belonging to the host place (Pollini 2005).

This  feeling  is  not  exclusive,  but  multicentered.  That  is  because  migration  involves  a variety of places on multiple geographical scales associated with a variety of social, economic, and  cultural  conditions.  Hence,  according  to  Lewicka,  an  individual's  sense  of  place  is  also developed through his or her interactions with a variety of places on many levels, such as home, a  neighborhood,  district,  city,  or  even  larger  regions    (2008).  In  other  words,  a  sense  of belonging to a city is developed from multiple people-place relationships, since the city could be seen as a source of relationships with referent social groups and a site for personal experiences. Therefore, scholars have begun to advocate for a dialectical understanding of the interplay of sense  and  identity  across  areas  of  migration.  In  this  regard,  the  history  of  migration  can  be viewed as a reembodiment of the meanings of belonging to different places (Qian, Zhu, and Liu 2011).

## CITIES?

It  is  relatively  difficult  for  migrants  of  lower-socioeconomic  status  to  build  a  sentimental connection with their host cities (Wu, Zhang, and Webster 2013), especially to the residential places with which migrants are not familiar (Burns 2001; Liu and others 2014; Wang, Zhang, and  Wu  2016).  For  example,  it  is  very  challenging  for  them  to  reestablish  the  psychological bonds they had with their original homes (Qian, Zhu, and Liu 2011). Therefore, they are prone to  psychological  segregation  since  lack  of  hukou  status  restricts  their  access  to  a  number  of public spaces (Wu, Zhang, and Webster 2013) as well as social welfare programs (Huang and others 2014) and political rights (Palmer and others 2011). It is almost impossible for them to serve  on  neighborhood  committee  boards  or  participate  in  the  decision-making  process regarding neighborhood affairs (Palmer and others 2011). These factors undermine migrants' sense of belonging to their residential communities.

Furthermore, because of the lack of access to public social welfare programs, migrants particularly require the support of informal social contacts in the host cities (Wang, Zhang, and Wu  2016;  Qian,  Zhu,  and  Wang  2014),  which  in  itself  might  help  to  promote  a  sense  of belonging.  To  investigate  this  vital  link,  we  borrowed  the  classifications  of  Yue  and  others (2013), which can be categorized  into the following three major types based on when these connections were made---prior to or after the migrant's arrival: (1) kin-based and home-based contacts with other migrants in the same host city, (2) kin-based contacts with local residents (local resident of the host city who are also the kin to the given migrant), and (3) contacts with local residents (nonkin resident contacts). Then, we investigated how different types of social contacts affect the establishment of migrants' sense of belonging. Compared to the kin-based types (1 and 2) that are formed before arrival, it is  difficult and time-consuming to establish social contacts with local residents who are nonkin after they arrive. An accumulation of social contacts increases the opportunity cost of returning to the place of origin, thereby very likely strengthening sense of belonging to the host city.  Our first research hypothesis is shown below.

H1:  The  rural-to-urban  migrants  who  sustain  contact  with  kin-based  or  home-based migrants in the same host cities tend to have a weaker sense of belonging to the host city, while the ones who sustain kin-based contacts with local residents or actively pursue contact with nonkin local residents have a stronger sense of belonging to their host cities.

In addition to the individual-level social contacts, connection to a city is also affected by contextual-level factors (Inalhan and Finch 2004; Lewicka 2011). For example, in urban China, due  to the constraints of the hukou  system,  rural  migrants  are  excluded  from  social participations and welfare programs in their host cities (Wu, Zhang, and Webster 2013; Huang and others 2014), which obviously undermines any bonding with it (Du 2017; Qian and Zhu 2014; Qian, Zhu, and Wang 2011). However, more recent reforms to this system that include providing urban social welfare, neighborhood public services and activities, volunteers to assist migrants in their new neighborhoods, and insurance programs (Huang, Van Weesep, and Dijst 2017) have tended to promote social integration.  However, these reforms have not reached some  neighborhoods  and  cities  (Huang  and  others  2014;  Liu  and  others  2017).  Another contextual difference lies in the population size. Since connection to place represents a tendency to  maintain  subjective  proximity  to  it  (Hidalgo  and  Hernández  2001),  it  is  easier  to  become familiar  with  a  less  populated  or  smaller  area.  In  this  sense,  migrants  may  develop  different levels  of  connection.  In  view  of  these  contextual  differences,  we  raise  two  other  research hypotheses:

H2:  The  availability  of  public  services  has  a  positive  effect  on  rural  migrants'  sense  of belonging to the host cities.

H3: A large population has a negative effect on rural migrants' sense of belonging to the host cities.

Keeping these  hypotheses  in mind,  in  the  following  sections,  we  explore  how  various types of social contacts and geographic scales will influence the migrants' sense of belonging.

## &lt;&lt;A-HEAD&gt;&gt; DATA AND METHODOLOGY

## &lt;&lt;B-Head&gt;&gt; SURVEY AND DATASETS

We  utilized  empirical  data  from  China's  National  Migrant  Population  Dynamic  Monitoring Survey  (MDMS),  which  was  conducted  by  the  China  Population  and  Development  Research Center (CPDRC) and commissioned by the National Population and Family Planning Commission  of  China  (NPFPC)  in  2013.  The  survey  contains  questions  about  migrants' demographic information, socioeconomic status, migration experiences, social integration, and sense  of  belonging  in  the  host  cities.  The  survey  yielded  two  datasets---the  main  one  from approximately  196,000  respondents  living  in  430  cities,  and  another  smaller  one  from approximately  15,000  migrant  respondents  living  in  eight  cities  (Shanghai,  Suzhou,  Wuxi, Quanzhou, Changsha, Wuhan, Xi'an and Xianyang) that were chosen purposely by NPFPC. We surveyed migrants between the ages of 15 and 59 as of May 2013, who moved across counties (based on their hukou status) and have resided at their new places of residence for more than a month.

For the 2013 MDMS, we employed a 'probability proportionate to size' (PPS) sampling technique,  which  is  comprised  of  three  consecutive  stages.  In  the  first  stage,  we  selected primary sampling units (townships, towns or subdistricts/ jiedao ) from the eight cities based on the 2012 annual report data of the migrant population. The corresponding sampling frame was based on statistics from the national census of the migrant population in 2012. In the second stage, we selected secondary sampling units (neighborhood committees or village committees) from the primary sampling units. The corresponding sampling frame was compiled by NPFPC of each province. In the third stage, we selected 20 respondents from the neighborhood/village committees in the areas under study through a two-way method (top-down and bottom-up). To achieve this goal, one respondent out of five was randomly selected by the neighborhood/village  committee,  and  we  ensured  that  one  respondent  from  each  household was  represented.  The  final  dataset  used  for  our  analysis  contains  a  total  of  12,807  migrants from approximately 750 neighborhoods.

## &lt;&lt;B-Head&gt;&gt; VARIABLES AND MEASUREMENTS

We developed and distributed questionnaires to determine how migrants develop a sense of belonging. The design of the items was partially borrowed from existing studies that are broadly related  to  this  topic  and  include  elements  such  as  place  attachment  (Hidalgo  and  Hernández 2001; Lewicka 2010; Scannell and Gifford 2010), emotional sense of belonging (Pollini 2007; Wu  and  others  2011),  social  bonding  (Raymonda,  Brown,  and  Weber  2010),  place  identity (Williams and others 1992), place dependence (Jorgensen and Stedman 2006; Kyle and others 2004), and sense of place (Stedman 2003). For instance, the purpose of  the MDMS is to assess self-reported emotional sense of belonging through the following questions: (' I have a sense of belonging to the host city. '), place attachment (' I want to live next door to local residents. '), social bonding  (' I  want to be friends with local residents. '  ' I  want myself or other family members to marry local residents. '),  place  dependence  (' I hope that local residents accept me as part of the neighborhood. ') and place identity (' I want to be part of the neighborhood. ') (Zhu and Lin 2016). To measure interactive social contacts, respondents were asked 'After work, with whom in  the  host city do you frequently interact?' The respondents were asked to select any of the following  choices:  'relatives  who  migrated  together,'  'countryman---people  from  the  same place  of  origin---who  migrated  together,'  'relatives  who  migrated  together  and  have  already been  granted  local  hukou  status,'  'friends  who  are  all  migrants,'  'colleagues  who  are  local residents,' 'government officials,' 'classmates and friends (excluding colleagues) who are local residents,' 'residents' committee or villagers' committee/property management company,' 'no one,' and 'others.' We grouped these answers into four categories: contacts with nonresidents (relatives, countryman, and other friends who are also migrants), contacts with kin residents (relatives  who  have  already  established  local hukou status),  contacts  with  nonkin  migrants (other people who have already gained  local hukou status, including colleagues, classmates, and friends),  and  contacts  with  local  authorities  (government  officials,  and  staff  in  residents' committee/villagers' committee/property management companies). We eliminated respondents who chose the option 'no one' from the study.

In  addition  to  social  contacts,  financial  and  institutional  links  might  also  influence migrants'  sense  of  belonging  (Lewicka  2011).  To  quantitatively  measure  such  links,  we  used several variables as proxies of their connections with home, or more precisely their places of origin. The first variable is the amount of money they send to relatives back home. The higher the  remittance,  the  stronger  the  bond  with  home.  Such  remittance  may  be  used  by  migrants after  returning to their original place of residence. Thus, we expected a negative relationship between the amount of money sent home and links to the host city. The last two variables are related to homeownership and landholding in the places of origin. Specifically, the respondents of the survey were asked to report on the size of their farmlands and houses that they owned in their homeland.  Migrants are less likely to become attached to the host city if they were well established in the homeland, including owning property such as farmland or a house (Hao and Tang 2015). Moreover, to better understand the institutional links, we included the migrant's participation in urban or rural insurance programs, 1  partly because these indicators have been used in previous studies (for example, Huang and others 2014).

Moving on to the contextual factors, we included three neighborhood-level variables in the  regressions  with  the  goal  of  exploring  the  availability  and  accessibility  of  neighborhood public services to migrants (compare, Raymonda,  Brown, and Weber 2010), the level of activity of voluntary  and  charitable  events  geared  toward  migrants,  and  the  number  of  neighborhood volunteers (Liu and others 2017). These variables were derived from an objective and holistic assessment of services for rural migrants in various neighborhood committees (Liu and others 2017).

1  There are two major insurance programs in the Chinese context: one is health insurance - insurers pay a small amount each month, when if they are seriously ill the insurance companies will pay for their bills; the other one is pension- insurers pay a certain amount each month and then receives refunds above a certain age.

With respect to the city-level variables, we identified two main factors: the first was the urban areas within the city, because sense of belonging is also related to the scale of the new place (Steele 1981; Hidalgo and Hernández 2001), and the second one was the GDP per capita, which represents the economic development of the host city (Zhu and Lin 2016). The GDP, as an indicator of macroeconomic development, signifies the employment opportunities in the city. Thus, it represents how attractive a city may be to migrants, as the motive for higher income has been recognized as a principal reason for migration (Fan 2003).

As stated above, in an effort to qualitatively construct a holistic perception of sense of belonging,  we  established  a  series  of  socio-demographic  characteristics  (for  example,  age, gender, and education level) of the migrants in our study and considered whether or not family members (for example, children, parents, and spouse) of the migrant also live in the host city. Moreover,  we  employed  the  variance  inflation  factor  (VIF)  to  quantify  the  severity  level  of multicollinearity. When the value of VIF for independent variables is less than 3, it suggests that there is no severe multicollinearity among the examined independent variables.

Based on the proposed MLM approach, we first assessed the city-level heterogeneity. The ICC indicators of this level were 0.031, and the value of the between-city variance σb 2  was 0.105, meaning  than  5  percent  of  the  total  variance  (the  value  of  ICC)  was  due  to  the  intergroup differences across cities.

A high ICC value means that the spatial heterogeneity of sense of belonging in the neighborhood should not be ignored. According  to  the  model,  the  social  contact  variable  of  kin-based  relatives  and  homebased countrymen who migrated together was statistically insignificant for predicting sense of belonging. In other words, migrants who rely on social support from other migrants were less likely to feel at home in their host cities. This finding is consistent with Cooney's interpretation on  the  negative  effects  of  intragroup  social  contacts,  which  may  deter  openness  and  thereby prevent integration into the new society (Cooney 1998). In this case, one possible explanation is that  because  migrants  are  unable  to  form  social  contacts  in  the  host  city  due  to  social segregation, they are forced to turn to social support from other migrants. However, this type of social support is relatively limited and does nothing to improve the migrants' quality of life or connection with the host city.

Notably, if a migrant's relatives are residents with local hukou, the results will be more positive.  We  found  that  kin-based  contacts  with  local  residents  (kin  resident  contacts)  were positively  related  to  a  migrant's  affinity  to  the  host  city.  However,  the  results  showed  that significant interactions with local nonkin residents will lead to a greater sense of belonging by providing migrants with useful information and practical support to enhance their new lives in the host city.

Interestingly, contacts with  local authorities (government  officials and  staff  and property  management  company  committee  members)  were  found  to  make  a  significantly positive impact on sense of belonging. Although previous scholars rarely discuss this aspect, this type of interaction may provide migrants with access to neighborhood services, including health and  volunteer  services,  along  with  the  social  integration  into  the  host  neighborhood.  If  a migrant could feel comfortable about approaching committee members, he or she would be able to  acquire  information  about  the  neighborhood  and  educational  resources  for  the  children. (Huang,  Van  Weesep,  and  Dijst  2017).  Based  on  these  empirical  results,  Hypothesis  1  is supported: Rural  migrants  who  retain  only  kin-based  and  home-based  contacts  with  other migrants in host cities tend to develop a weaker sense of belonging to the host city, while those who  maintain  kin-based  contacts  with  local  residents  or  actively  establish  social  contacts  with nonkin local residents tend to have stronger ties to the host city.

As  for  the  effects  of  neighborhood-level  factors  including  services  and  activities,  we found  that  only  access  to  public  services  had  a  significantly  positive  correlation  with  the migrants'  sense  of  belonging.  In  contrast,  access  to  neighborhood  activities  was  statistically insignificant, or even had a negative effect, probably because these activities were not appealing to the migrants. Unlike public services (for example, public housing, education for children of migrants, and social assistance), these activities would not yield any immediate benefits and not improve their sense of belonging. These results support Hypothesis H2: Access to neighborhood services has a positive effect on rural migrants' sense of belonging to the host city.

In  addition,  we  found    the  population  size  of  the  host  city  to  be  negatively  related  to migrants'  sense  of  belonging  (0~1:  Shanghai-0.752;  Suzhou-0.806;  Wuxi-0.813;  Quanzhou0.776;  Changsha-0.839;  Wuhan-0.829;  Xi'an-0.789;  Xianyang-0.784),  which  is  consistent  with the findings of other scholars (Hidalgo and Hernández 2001) that it is easier to become familiar with  a  smaller  place  and  cultivate  a  sentimental  attachment  to  it.  This  result  supports Hypothesis H3: Population size has a negative effect on rural migrants' sense of belonging to the host  city. Regarding individual socioeconomic and demographic factors, we found higher education levels and family income to be significantly positively linked to a sense of belonging. This is consistent with existing research findings that migrants with higher human capital and socioeconomic status are more likely to enjoy their lives in the host city (Zhu and Chen 2010b). With  respect  to  migration  factors,  compared  to  interprovincial  migrants,  we  found  that intraprovincial migrants were more likely to have a greater connection to the host city; a longer duration in the host city is also a positive indicator, which is consistent with existing research (Huang,  Van  Weesep,  and  Dijst  2017).  Notably,  neither  age  nor  gender  was  found  to  be statistically significant for predicting sense of belonging. Moreover, it is not surprising that not being able to migrate with their children was found to be negatively correlated with attachment to the host city. However, leaving elderly relatives or a partner was not statistically significant.

In sum, the three hypotheses, if other variables are controlled, were supported.

H1:  Rural-to-urban  migrants  who  sustain  kin-based  and  home-based  contacts  with  other migrants in the host city tens to have a weaker sense of belonging to the host city, while those who maintain kin-based contacts with local residents or actively pursue contacts with non-kin residents develop a stronger sense of belonging.

H2: Access to public services has a positive effect on rural migrants' sense of belonging to the host city.

H3:  The  population  size  of  the  host  city  has  a  negative  effect  on  rural  migrants'  sense  of belonging.

## &lt;&lt;A-HEAD&gt;&gt; DISCUSSION

Our  empirical  findings  will  provide  a  unique  contribution  to  the  understanding  of  the  social nature  of  migrants'  sense  of  belonging  and  the  inherent  heterogeneity  of  the  people-place relationship.  First,  belonging  to  a  place  is  not  only  subjectively  defined  it  is  also  socially constructed (compare, Pollini 2005; Mee and Wright 2009; Ralph and Staehell 2011). It could occur directly between a person and a place or indirectly via establishment of social contacts. For  example,  making  friends  with  new  neighbors  could  further  facilitate  migrants'  sense  of belonging  to  the  neighborhood,  or  a  group  of  migrants  could  join  a  local  line-dancing  club, which could help promote a sense of community. In this regard, the people-place relationship can  be  further  categorized  into  a  person-person-place  relationship  or  a  person-group-place relationship (Lim and Barton 2010). Apparently, this classification extends the traditional twofold framework of people-place in geography to psychological aspects involving the interaction

among the  ego,  other(s),  and  the  place.  In  this  sense,  it  is  necessary  to  understand  that  the nature of sense of belonging is relational (compare, Anthias 2006).

Moreover,  Yue  and  others  have  discovered  a  'realistic'  pattern  of  migrants'  social networking, which has further extended our understanding of migrants' social contacts (2013). Admittedly, migration is a difficult process and newcomers need resources and an emotional support network to settle in a new place. However, due to the fact that institutional barriers restrict access to public goods and services, migrants must heavily rely on their existing social contacts, which are likely to be other migrants, in the host city as well as construct new contacts with residents. In addition to launching a new social integration policy in host neighborhoods (Huang, Van Weesep, and Dijst 2017), some neighborhood committees have already provided migrants  with  volunteers  and  health  services  in  order  to  promote  their  integration  into  the neighborhoods. In this  way  migrants can contact the  neighborhood committee  members and obtain relevant social support and acquire useful information on how to survive in the host city (for  example,  job  opportunities  and  access  to  public  services).  These  benefits  ultimately enhance migrants' sense of belonging.

Furthermore, we explored the geographic scales on which migrants develop connections (for example, city level, neighborhood, and individual level). It is more likely that migrants will establish  a  sense  of  belonging  in  a  smaller  region.  However,  the  subjective  bonds  at  the neighborhood level could extend to the city level. In this light, the relationship between people and place should not be  perceived as remaining  on a fixed geographical scale. Instead,  these relationships are consistently dynamic, involving internal relationships on various geographic scales that are intertwined and mutually constituted.

We suggest significant improvements to existing urbanization policies, particularly the hukou policy  to  facilitate  rural  migrants'  social  integration  in  the  cities.  These  reforms  must include not only immediate changes in hukou access system but also enhancements to social welfare programs. Moreover, there must be educational reforms for the children of migrants and social assistance to the enhance the lives of migrants in the host city. In addition to breaking the hukou barrier, measures to promote social interaction should be implemented. For example, more inclusive neighborhood housing, more public spaces, or neighborhood activities designed to include rural migrants can stimulate intergroup social interactions and build a sense of social trust. Thus, we suggest that the local governments should increase public subsidies earmarked for  care  of  migrant  children  and  the  elderly.  These  efforts  will  also  further  enhance  the interaction  between  migrants  and  residents,  thereby  not  only  increasing  migrants'  sense  of belonging but also facilitating their integration into the host city.

A  limitation  of  this  study,  however,  is  the  lack  of  analysis  regarding  the  dynamics  of urban  residents'  sense  of  belonging  since  this  study  was  based  on  a  cross-sectional  dataset instead of a longitudinal survey. A long-range study could provide needed perspectives on the development  and  the  long-term,  changing  dynamics  of  this  phenomenon.  More  efforts, therefore,  should  be  devoted  to  understanding  how  such  differences  in  sense  of  belonging among rural migrants are linked to their life experiences in cities and in their home villages. Such questions will be addressed in our future research.

## &lt;&lt;A-HEAD&gt;&gt; CONCLUSION

This  study  focused  on  elements,  such  as  social  contact,  that  may  enhance  migrants'  sense  of belonging  and  integration  into  the  host  city.  Types  of  social  contact  were  divided  into  the following categories: with nonresidents, kin residents, and nonkin residents. We then explored the effect of contextual indicators such as access to public services and population size on the migrants' sense of belonging. Specifically, the empirical results showed that the newly formed intergroup contacts and existing intragroup associations affected sense of belonging in various ways.  Specifically,  newly  formed  nonkin  resident  contacts  were  positively  linked  to  sense  of belonging, while contacts with nonresidents were negatively associated. By characterizing the differences  among  these  social  contacts,  we  found  that  inter-group  social  contacts  played  a more significant role in shaping migrants' affinity than the existing intragroup social contacts because  supportive  resources  from  local  residents  such  as  financial  assistance,  job  market information, and companionship helped the migrants integrate into the host city. Moreover, we found that some contextual factors of place increased sense of belonging, particularly access to neighborhood services . Based on these findings, we argue that sense of belonging is relational and socially constructed; it is a complex praxis involving the interaction among ego, other(s), and place.
