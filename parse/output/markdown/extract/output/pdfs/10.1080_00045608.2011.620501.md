Contentious Land Change in the Amazon’s Arc of Deforestation

Land change in the Amazon is driven by numerous factors including fiscal incentives, infrastructure, transportation costs, migration, and household decision making. Largely missing from the story to date, however, is the role of contentious social processes, including contention over land resources. By employing a case study of land conflict over a largeholding in southeastern Pará, Brazil, and a regional-scale statistical model, we describe contentious land change (C-LC) in an area with a long history of antagonism between largeholders and the rural poor. We fuse the conceptual frameworks of political ecology with the methodological approaches of land change science to show that deforestation in the area of study is enhanced due to the interaction of diverse and adversarial agents rather than the independent actions of isolated land managers deforesting according to the dictates of microeconomic optimization. C-LC is a process of global reach and must therefore be added to the topical range of land change science. A combination of the explanatory richness of political ecology with the methodological rigor of land change science greatly enhances our understanding of land change processes.
Key Words: Amazon, deforestation, land change science, land reform, political ecology.

Numerosos factores intervienen en el proceso de cambios de la tierra en el Amazonas, entre los cuales se cuentan los incentivos fiscales, la infraestructura, costos de transporte, migración y la toma de decisiones a nivel familiar. Sin embargo, hasta la fecha aparece en gran medida ignorado el papel que tienen procesos sociales polémicos, incluyendo el debate sobre los recursos de la tierra. Mediante un estudio de caso sobre un latifundio del sur de Pará, en Brasil, y de un modelo estadístico a escala regional, describimos el cambio disputado de la tierra (C-LC) en un área que tiene un largo historial de antagonismo entre latifundistas y los campesinos pobres. Fusionamos el marco conceptual de la ecología política con los enfoques metodológicos de la ciencia de transformación de la tierra para mostrar que la deforestación en el área de estudio se incrementa más por la interacción de agentes diversos y antagónicos que por las acciones independientes de aislad administradores de tierras que deforesten de acuerdo con los dictados de la optimización microeconómica. El C-LC es un proceso de alcance global y tiene por eso que agregarse al ámbito temático de la ciencia de la transformación de la tierra. Combinando la riqueza explicativa de la ecología política con el rigor metodológico de la ciencia de la transformación de la tierra se fortalece grandemente nuestra comprensión de los procesos del cambio de la tierra. Since the opening of Amazônia in the 1960s, human populations have grown precipitously, and a great deal of forest has been transformed into pastures and crops (R. Santos 1980; R. Walker et al. 2008). Estimates as of 2007 indicate that some 637,189 km² of standing forest has been removed, 17 percent of the basin’s original cover (Fearnside 2001; Instituto Nacional de Pesquisas Espaciais [National Institute for
Space Research] 2005). Rapid loss of Amazonian forest has spawned multilateral investigations such as the Large-Scale Biosphere–Atmosphere (LBA) experiment in Amazônia, which has greatly advanced our understanding of the region’s environment (Avissar et al. 2002; Keller et al. 2004; Batistella et al. 2007). Although such integrative studies have contributed significantly to our knowledge of change in the region, important questions remain, particularly about the human drivers responsible for this massive environmental transformation. This article contributes to our understanding by addressing on-the-ground social interactions expediting forest losses that are of concern to both the scientific community and general public.

Research has identified a number of factors affecting forest loss in Amazônia, with special attention on fiscal incentives, inadequate land titling, infrastructure, transportation costs, migration, and household-level processes. These factors, certainly important, leave the story only partly told, and recent research has drawn attention to the role of contentious social processes, associated with agrarian reform, in influencing land cover change (Fearnside 2001; Simmons et al. 2007; Aldrich 2009). Earlier work of a political economic orientation describes the impact of capitalist penetration on the forests of Amazônia, whereby a capitalist complex of social, institutional, and economic relations replaced the autarkic economies of Amazônia with modern modes of production, bringing the primitive north into an orbit of exploitive exchange relations, and decimating the environment by transforming the sustainable agriculture of smallholder subsistence into empty pastures (Monbeig 1957; Velho 1972; Martins 1975; Ianni 1979; Mahar 1979, 1989; Foweraker 1981; Moran 1983; Wood 1983; Sawyer 1984; Schmink and Wood 1984, 1992; Hecht and Cockburn 1990; Cleary 1993; Branford and Rocha 2002).

Southeastern Pará (Figure 1) is a prime geographic focus of development efforts undertaken by the military regime in the 1970s (Mahar 1979; Jadão 1984; Smith et al. 1995). Government incentives attracted capital from the south and landless farmers from the northeast, setting in motion an interwoven process of social conflict and environmental degradation that continues to this day (Foweraker 1981; Hall 1987, 1989; Schmink and Wood 1992; Fearnside 2001; Simmons et al. 2007). Southeastern Pará is now almost completely deforested, and fights for land there continue to claim lives (Simmons et al. 2007; Aldrich 2009; R. Walker et al. 2011).

Deforestation is a social process that unfolds over decades and involves a succession of largeholders and smallholders—often led by social movement organizations (SMOs)—who engage each other contentiously, sometimes with violence. In southeastern Pará, and in many other parts of the Amazon basin, deforestation occurs within a matrix of multiple agents and competing land claims. Such complexity is not well reflected in research on Amazonian land cover change (henceforth, land change [LC]), and a prime objective of this work is to rectify this situation, on both empirical and theoretical grounds. Specifically, we implement methodology widely utilized by land change science but do so within a conceptual framework stemming, in part, from political ecology (see Turner and Robbins 2008). Although land change science and political ecology are sometimes viewed as offering distinct, if not incommensurate, perspectives, we argue, as do others, that bridging them offers significant analytical potential (Hames and Vickers 1983; Zimmerer 2004, 803; Turner and Robbins 2008). We make our case by direct application to southeastern Pará, in many ways the epicenter of both Amazonian deforestation and rural conflict in Brazil.

We pursue our objectives as follows. In the next section we consider the historical origins of land conflict in southeastern Pará and the emergence of the current land reform movement that is gripping Brazil, especially its northern region. We follow with a brief discussion of research on Amazonian LC, then present our conceptual framework, invoking the theory of contentious politics as a complement to structural political ecology as it has been applied to comprehend resource-driven conflicts. We follow with a case study describing interactions between ranchers and the rural poor and their consequences for the environment. Regression analyses, enabled by a unique set of geographic and historical data, provide evidence that contentious land change (C-LC) is a reality in southeastern Pará. Social inequalities not only damage the social fabric of Amazônia but also play a role in its environmental degradation; we therefore conclude by discussing the implications of these findings for theory, practice, and policy.

Empirical Motivation and Prior Research

The Social Context

The foundation for environmental change and social conflict in the arc of deforestation was laid long before the military regime took power and initiated its development programs in 1964. People have long occupied southeastern Pará, including both indigenous groups and postconquest residents who came in search of livelihood from the earliest days of the colony, with gold discoveries on the Tocantins River and agricultural frontiers advancing from Goiás and Bahia. Significant populations did not grow until the first rubber boom, with migrations from the northeast spurred on by frequent and devastating drought (Ianni 1979; Jadão 1984; Dean 1987; Almeida 2006). When the rubber boom went bust, the poor of southeastern Pará switched to Brazil nut extraction, which flourished for several decades just after Malaysian rubber production destroyed the Amazonian extractive economy with low prices. These castanheiros (nut collectors), who started out as seringueiros (rubber collectors), extracted as they could from forests that were common properties, at least in the beginning, and they eked out subsistence crops on small clearings, settling on freely available lands called terras devolutas (Caldas 2008). The conditions they endured were difficult, especially as their indebtedness grew to merchants in Marabá, São João do Araguaia, and Conceição do Araguaia, port towns on the Araguaia and Tocantins Rivers (Jadão 1984). As discussed in the next section, these castanheiros were soon put under pressure to abandon their land, given strong Brazil nut prices and growing interest in this new extractive good by local capitalists. The dispersed population of these poor forest farmers, called posseiros due to their unmediated occupation of land, constituted a substantial majority in the rural areas of southeastern Pará by the 1970s—more than 95 percent in the county of Conceição do Araguaia, the largest in the region until it was subdivided (Ianni 1979, 156).

The opening of the region to development under the military regime in the 1960s brought rural unrest, as state-supported land grabs by largeholders from southern Brazil brought them face to face with the rural poor, many of them posseiros convinced of their rights to stay on the lands they had long occupied. Partly as a consequence, the Communist Party of Brazil (PC do B, Partido Comunista do Brasil) infiltrated the western banks of the Araguaia River valley in the late 1960s, as President Médici’s “hard line” took hold of the government, resulting in severe repression of dissidents in Brazil’s urban areas. Discovering this Marxist redoubt and knowing that the PC do B pursued a Maoist strategy of people’s war, the Army initiated a counterinsurgency in 1972, forcing the hand of party activists who had focused most of their attention on propaganda, not military preparations (Morais and Silva 2005). What resulted was the “Little War” of Araguaia, a two-year
conflict that ended in annihilation of the PC do B as a political force in Brazil. Possibly as many as 20,000 troops rotated through several field campaigns to root out the sixty to seventy Marxist fighters, nearly all of whom were killed (Morais and Silva 2005).

Although the PC do B was defeated, peasant resistance continued, even under the dictatorship of the generals. Perhaps because military actions had failed in this regard, the Army next followed a more conciliatory approach. It had already initiated a soft-handed approach to ease social tensions in the northeast with the onset of drought in 1970, providing part of the motivation for building the Trans-Amazonian Highway and colonization projects in Marabá, Altamira, and Itaituba (Moran 1981; Smith 1982). Locally, southeastern Pará became an area of special interest to military planners concerned about the federal colonization agency’s (Instituto Nacional de Colonização e Reforma Agrária [INCRA]) track record in resolving land disputes in the region. As a result, INCRA was abolished to make way for Group Executivo de Trabalho do Araguaia-Tocantins (GETAT), the Executive Group for the Araguaia-Tocantins, a colonization bureaucracy tasked with bringing a new face to the military’s presence in Pará in a gesture that has been described as “military populism” (Schmink and Wood 1992, 73). But GETAT and the Army’s new approach did little to staunch the growing sense of grievance on the part of the posseiros and the landless rural workers. Their defense of prior land claims escalated into armed actions in a struggle that has been referred to as the Luta Posseiro, or the “Posseiro Struggle.” The Luta Posseiro simmered through the final years of the military regime, before exploding with the transition to democracy. The decade of the 1980s brought land conflict fatalities in southeastern Pará to an all-time high, with victims no longer counted exclusively on the side of the rural poor. Adding to the tumult of the period, the discovery of gold in Serra Pelada in 1979 brought perhaps 100,000 wildcatters to the region. Those who failed to strike it rich, the vast majority, joined the ranks of the rural poor in a desperate search for livelihood and land.

With democratic transition completed by 1985, the long-dormant desire of the rural poor for land erupted into a broad social movement throughout Brazil. This movement, which has been referred to as Direct Action Land Reform (DALR) for its aggressive, often confrontational, tactics, found fertile ground in southeastern Pará, with its volatile demography and history. Dedicated SMOs soon found support in the region, politicizing the Luta Posseiro into a movement for social justice and engaging the latifúndia (wealthy largeholders), as well as the newly re-created INCRA, in a well-orchestrated fight for land. Preeminent among these were a group of often closely aligned organizations with slightly different goals, including the well-known Movimento dos Trabalhadores Rurais Sem-Terra (MST), the Comissão Pastoral de Terra (CPT), and lesser known but powerful organizations such as Federação dos Trabalhadores na Agricultura (FETAGRI) and the Movimento Luta pela Terra (MLT). SMOs such as these raised the banner of land reform and continue to hold it high throughout the twenty-eight municipalities that constitute the region. By 2002, DALR had created 247 settlements, or assentamentos, in southeastern Pará, settling 32,295 families in the process (INCRA 2003).

Implications for the Region’s Forest

In addition to enabling the transformation of the Luta Posseiro into DALR, the return of democracy to Brazil also coincided with a period of rapid forest loss in southeastern Pará (Aldrich 2009). By no means do we wish to suggest that democracy is somehow implicated in Amazonian forest loss or that LC in southeastern Pará—and Amazônia more widely—is solely attributable to DALR and its hard-worn partisans. Current basin-scale analyses point to the considerable role played by largeholders in pan-Amazonian LC processes (Alves 2002; R. Walker, DeFries et al. 2009), and southeastern Pará is no exception. Indeed, the region’s ranchers took advantage of capital subsidies and tax exemptions in their efforts to convert the forests of the region into agriculture and extractive land uses (e.g., Hecht 1985). It is estimated that land reform in general accounted for 30 percent of the basin’s deforestation between 1964 and 1997 (Alston, Libecap, and Mueller 2000, 5). Although the Brazilian government formally prohibits the formation of land reform settlements in forested areas, this is where much of it occurs (Fearnside 2001; Simmons et al. 2007; Caldas 2008). The number of families participating in DALR throughout Brazil increased from about 10,500 in 1988 to nearly 70,000 in 2007, with the State of Pará accounting for more than 23 percent of all families involved in DALR and nearly one fourth of all land involved in DALR activity nationwide between 1979 and 2007 (DataLuta 2008). Over this period, land occupied for DALR in Pará, primarily in southeastern Pará, amounted to 4,426 km² per year, a sizable fraction of the state’s annual deforestation rate estimated at 7,174 km² (INPE 2005; Simmons et al. 2010). In sum, DALR is associated with a distinct
form of LC in Amazônia, one that is firmly rooted in social processes stemming from significant injustice.

Land Change Science in Amazônia

Despite these numbers and recent social history in large parts of the arc of deforestation, Amazonian land change science typically assumes that land covers change due to the behavior of individuals acting rationally in pursuit of economic objectives via agronomic land uses. Such a conceptualization is implicit in the Thunian formulation of rent and also in notions of household production theory stemming from peasant economics (Ellis 1993). To date, Amazonian LC studies include (1) region-scale econometrics applications based on government census data (e.g., Reis and Guzmán 1994; Pfaff 1999; Anderson et al. 2002; Weinhold and Reis 2008); (2) behavioral computation and modeling that spatially replicates forest conversion (Soares-Filho et al. 2004; Soares-Filho et al. 2006; Arima et al. 2008); and (3) ethnographically grounded survey research that explains LC by explicit reference to the actions of farmers and ranchers directly engaged in land management (e.g., Pichón 1997; R. Walker 2003; Moran, Brondízio, and VanWey 2005; Pan and Bilsborrow 2005; Walsh et al. 2005). A component of the research we present here lines up methodologically with this survey-based research, given its focus on property-scale processes and empirical agents of LC. Our work also emphasizes deforestation, although Amazonian researchers have grown increasingly interested in secondary vegetation and the possibility of forest transition over large areas (Rudel 2002; Perz and Skole 2003). Forest transition in the study area is unlikely, however, given its deepening engagement in global markets for beef (R. Walker, Browder et al. 2009).

Survey research on Amazonian LC has mainly addressed smallholders and, specifically, links among migration, livelihood systems, and the use of land (de Sherbinin et al. 2008). It has generated explanatory conceptual frameworks, such as the multiphase response model (MPR), which considers the sequence of livelihood strategies adopted by new migrants to forest frontiers (Bilsborrow and Ogendo 1992). A complement to MPR that has inspired considerable Amazonian LC research is the household life cycle (HLC) model, stemming from a synthesis of Goody’s domestic cycle concept (Goody 1962; Moran, Brondízio, and VanWey 2005) and household production theory (Singh et al. 1986; Chayanov [1925] 1986; R. Walker 2003). The HLC model ties household characteristics (e.g., labor availability and family consumption) to land use and suggests that changes in these characteristics is what brings about LC (R. Walker and Homma 1996; Pichón 1997; Marquette 1998; McCracken et al. 1999; Brondízio et al. 2002; McCracken, Boucek, and Moran 2002; VanWey, D’Antona, and Brondízio 2007). An emerging alternative explanation to the HLC model is the “property life cycle” formulation, founded on the notion that land use strategies in forest frontiers are limited, unidirectional, and independent of household structure (Barbieri, Bilsborrow, and Pan 2005; VanWey, D’Antona, and Brondízio 2007). Statistical work shows that time of occupation is linked to extent of land cover change, a result suggestive of an autonomous, property-level process (Barbieri, Bilsborrow, and Pan 2005; Caldas et al. 2007; VanWey, D’Antona, and Brondízio 2007). The property life-cycle specification proves important in the results that follow.

Shortcomings of Previous Research

Political economy and land change science have added to our knowledge about environmental change in Amazônia, but they both fall short on important counts. Early accounts of capitalist penetration, such as those considered in the article’s introductory remarks, formulated LC as an aggregate outcome and focused attention on the policy dimensions of Amazonian development, not its environmental consequences. These interpretations also emphasized deforestation resulting from largeholders and their subsidy-driven conversions of primary forest to pasture. As such, they rendered, perhaps ironically, a single-agent explanation of Amazonian LC, divorced from ground-level social processes and occasionally even national politics favoring capitalist accumulation. Although LCS-inspired analyses take a ground-level view of LC, they, too, advance explanations largely based on single agents, despite efforts to provide scalar contextualization (e.g., Pan and Bilsborrow 2005). Neither political economic analyses nor land change science have specifically addressed the C-LC dynamics arising in the wake of the restoration of democracy in Brazil in 1985. Since then, as has already been discussed, SMOs dedicated to DALR have engaged wealthy largeholders in a fight for land, with implications for LC. Here we consider the LC outcomes of this contention, as they add to the impacts of corporate subsidies and household decision making on Amazonian LC. Central to this argument is the idea that LC hardly, if ever, results from the seamless decision making of a unitary agent but is instead the result of a broken
thread of social actions and reactions. This is particularly the case in large parts of Amazônia’s arc of deforestation, where LC is a material process driven by social contention, engaging a multiplicity of agents, including smallholders, largeholders, landless rural workers, SMO activists, and even government bureaucrats. LC is not simply the outcome of the household processes of poor farmers, or of subsidies provided to capitalist ranchers.

A Political Ecology of Contention in Amazônia

The work presented here advances a structural political ecology akin to that of Blaikie and Brookfield (1987) and others (e.g., Schmink and Wood 1987; Campbell and Olson 1991; Pelling 2003) in explaining LC in the arc of deforestation. We situate the ecological actions of land managers within ever-widening contexts, with expanding sets of institutional constraints and resources, which limit or enable behaviors as the case may be. The land managers of interest are largeholders, typically ranchers, on the one hand, and the rural poor, individuals who might be smallholders or landless rural workers, on the other. Both groups are deemed to possess agronomic interests in parcels of land, and both are intent on possessing these parcels to the exclusion of each other. The consequence of this competition is deforestation as well as social contention, processes impacted by a multiplicity of forces with their own scalar provenance, ranging from the immediate environment of action in specific localities to the realms of nation-states and global markets (Hecht 1985; Brondízio, Safar, and Siqueira 2002; R. Walker, Browder et al. 2009). Although we absolutely recognize the intellectual validity of recent advances in poststructural analysis made by political ecology (Escobar 1999; Castree and Braun 2001; Brown and Purcell 2005; Blaikie 2008; Rocheleau 2008), the material processes of the issue at hand invite a structural formulation, as has previously been employed to link underlying causes of LC to proximate ones, in Amazônia and elsewhere (Schmink and Wood 1987; Geist and Lambin 2001; Chowdhury and Turner 2006; Turner and Robbins 2008).

The political ecological framework we employ has specific antecedence in (1) the concept of inequality-driven resource conflict (Watts 1983; Peluso and Watts 2001) and (2) the theory of contentious politics, as elaborated by political scientists and geographers (Miller 2000; McAdam, Tarrow, and Tilly 2001; Wolford 2003a, 2004; Wright and Wolford 2003; Simmons 2005; Simmons et al. 2007). Resource conflict scholarship describes broad-scale social processes precipitating contention over land, the natural resource in question. Alternatively, the theory of contentious politics situates the form conflict takes in historical terms, provides its institutional context, and helps reveal its specific performance modalities. Our interweaving of these two approaches constitutes a political ecology sensitized to politics from above and to LC impacts occurring at ground level, as a consequence of contentious interactions among largeholders, SMOs, and their rank-and-file partisans.

Land Scarcity and Contentious Politics in Southeastern Pará

In southeastern Pará, and throughout Amazônia more generally, largeholders have enjoyed the support of state and federal governments in gaining access to land at the expense of smallholders and landless rural workers. This has created land scarcity in the midst of plenty, concentrating land in the hands of a relatively small group of individuals who have not been shy about defending what they claim to be theirs. Amazonian land scarcity, a social construct, resulted from a series of enclosures that effectively excluded prior claimants from properties they long occupied (Peluso and Watts 2001; Simmons et al. 2007). Of particular note were legislative actions in the early twentieth century taken by the State of Pará, which allowed for the leasing of public lands. These leases permitted wealthy landowners to take possession of large stands of Brazil nut in the southeastern part of the state that had long been common property resources (Ianni 1979; Schmink and Wood 1992). As a consequence, independent Brazil nut collectors were dispossessed and transformed into debt peons subsisting on informal holdings, the aforementioned posseiros. Those who benefited from the bounty of the state recomposed themselves as an oligarchy of the privileged few (Ianni 1979; Emmi 1999).

The rural poor of southeastern Pará were further impacted as both the State of Pará and the federal government awarded land titles to highly capitalized interests from the south, often in complete ignorance of prior land claims by posseiros. This began before the military’s seizure of power, with the opening of the Belem–Brasília Highway in 1960. At that time, wealthy southerners entered the Araguaia Valley in earnest and appropriated vast areas (Fernandes 1999). Two families alone (Lanari, Malzoni) claimed about 15,000 km², a significant share of the entire region, much of it already occupied
(Fernandes 1999; Almeida 2006). These land grabs precipitated a secondary round of enclosures with legislation allowing the eviction of posseiros (Araujo et al. 2009), and subsequent federal subsidies completed the consolidation of the largeholder class (Hecht 1985).

Given these extensive and long-term appropriations of land, and a poor population with strong grievances, SMOs have found fertile ground for DALR, which is readily comprehensible as a form of contentious politics given its utility in describing the pursuit of social, political, and economic objectives outside normal societal grievance channels (McAdam, Tarrow, and Tilly 2001; Wolford 2003a, 2003b, 2004; Simmons 2005; Simmons et al. 2010). Contentious politics arise for a variety of reasons (Miller 2000; McAdam, Tarrow, and Tilly 2001; Simmons 2005), but one of particular interest for the Brazilian case stems from the institutional environment governing constitutional land rights. Certainly, the land scarcity affecting southeastern Pará provides a rationale for aggressive actions on the part of the landless, but it does not explain the conflict’s specific articulation, which involves the physical occupation of properties targeted for DALR and largeholder reactions aimed at removal of DALR occupants.

This contentious process of engagement has its basis in the Constitution of 1988, which seeks to remedy the land issue that has long plagued Brazil’s rural areas. It does so by providing a mechanism for the expropriation of landed property if an owner fails to meet requirements about managing it for the social good (Aldrich 2009).1 The crux of the matter is that private property must fulfill a “social function.” This constitutional dictate, together with laws regarding labor and the environment, provide the land reform movement with legal recourse that facilitates the physical occupation and re-possession of properties deemed to have failed to meet their social function. For their part, largeholders desirous of keeping their threatened properties intact also resort to contentious tactics; it is not uncommon for largeholders to maintain permanent militia or to hire gunmen in special circumstances. Most of the violence in southeastern Pará has been suffered by posseiros, victims of ranchers protecting what they have or wanting more.

To summarize, the multiscalar articulation of state power, both local and federal, in collaboration with local elites, has created a dispossessed population poised for resource-driven conflict (Peluso and Watts 2001; Peet and Watts 2004; Simmons 2004). This conflict, in turn, has assumed its specific form by virtue of Brazilian laws, which, perhaps ironically, have provided an institutional resource that SMOs dedicated to DALR have been quick to exploit. Thus, the allocation of land in southeastern Pará, and in many parts of Amazônia, is often mediated by a balance of local power rather than market exchanges. The quest for land by the rural poor, a legitimate social goal, has evolved a strategy of dubious legality that stakes its claims outside of standard grievance channels (Tilly and Tarrow 2007). Contentious politics anticipates the implications of Brazil’s unique institutional circumstances for direct action performances in southeastern Pará.

Implications for Land Change

We have explained social contention as a function of land inequality and the institutional structure of Brazilian land law, but we have not considered implications for LC, the final step in the formulation of our political ecological framework. Here again, the analysis of contentious politics serves us by calling attention to Brazilian law, which has long sanctioned rights to possession on the basis of productive use. In the Amazonian case, this is typically demonstrated by deforestation (R. Walker, Moran, and Anselin 2000). Thus, in their efforts to show they have fulfilled constitutional requirements to possess land, DALR participants often clear forest on the occupation of largeholdings by ranchers, or the latifúndio. Alternatively, largeholders who claim prior possession preemptively take down trees in an effort to ward off DALR by making a dramatic, productive gesture to INCRA, the agency responsible for adjudicating competing claims (Simmons 2005; Bicalho and Hoefler 2008). Externalities of conflict, particularly the use of fire to destroy structures, add substantially to the ledger of forest degradation, as do liquidation harvests in the face of imminent loss of land (e.g., Opinião! Editors 2000a). All these actions lead to a loss of forest in excess of what would have likely occurred in the absence of social contention, with deforestation following in response to the strictly agronomic use of land for productive purposes.

We posit that a component of deforestation in Amazônia results from contentious social interactions aimed at land possession in addition to what we refer to as agronomic deforestation undertaken to plant crops or pasture as an economic activity. More broadly, we offer a theoretical concept to the land change science community, namely, C-LC, which is the result of social processes as opposed to the isolated behaviors of individual agents, be they subsistence farmers or large-scale cattle ranchers. In many respects, C-LC is evident in studies
with a political ecological perspective on environmental and social outcomes of unequal power relations (e.g., for Amazônia, see Schmink and Wood 1987; Ciccantell 1999). Given the global reach of land conflict and the reemergence of land reform movements, C-LC probably occurs in a wide variety of settings and offers a useful empirical category to the analytical repertoire of land change scientists.2

Contention and Land Change in the Arc of Deforestation

Contention between smallholders and largeholders in Amazônia has been described by many authors but most often via anecdote and rarely with reference to LC (Correio Editors 1991, 1996a, 1996b, 2002; Associated Press 2005; Hennigan 2008). We now turn to an explicit account to set the stage for statistical assessment and generalization. We first present a case study that epitomizes both land conflict and LC in the study region, followed by (1) a schematic representation linking these two processes and (2) a regression analysis based on a sample of properties from the Brazil nut polygon, a planning unit used by the State of Pará to keep track of largeholdings near the city of Marabá. The polygon possesses a concentration of holdings from which Brazil nuts were extracted with considerable profit in the early part of the twentieth century, as discussed earlier.

Case Study of Bala do Sul Ranch3

The case study focuses on a 9,700-hectare property referred to as Fazenda (Ranch) Bala do Sul. The details of conflict were extracted from an archive of more than 3,000 newspaper articles from two regional newspapers and supplemented with key informant interviews with ranchers who were willing to speak about the conflict. The case study is based on this property for three reasons. First, Bala do Sul serves as an emblematic test of the dedication of DALR SMOs, the clarity of laws regarding property expropriation in the social interest, and the resolve of largeholders intent on keeping what they say is theirs. Second, its majority owner, “João Machado,” is known through the region for investing heavily in his ranching operations and has always been vocal in his hostility toward DALR and the MST in particular (E. Santos 2004). Finally, the Machado clan dominated Brazil nut production and distribution in Pará during the first half of the twentieth century, an economic power they translated into local political control (Emmi 1999; Almeida 2006). The Machado power base began to erode with the arrival of southern capitalists who came with the opening of the Belem–Brasília highway (BR-010) and with the discovery of gold and vast deposits of iron in the Carajás region just to the south of Marabá. Nevertheless, the Machados remain a force to be reckoned with and a target of choice for SMOs intent on toppling the still very real political power of largeholders (E. Santos 2004).

Prior to describing conflict on Bala do Sul, and essential to comprehending the analysis that follows, it is necessary to recall features of Brazilian property law that have already been discussed and that pave the way to expropriation in the social interest. Two are of prime importance to C-LC and the case of Bala do Sul. In particular, SMOs have relied on the definition of social interest as it articulates with concerns about the environment and labor law. For Bala do Sul, environmental law violations, interpreted by the SMOs as grounds for occupation and appropriation, usually involve taking trees from the landholding’s legally mandated forest reserve. Under current federal law, all properties in forested regions of the legal Amazon must maintain 80 percent forest cover and have had to do so since 2001, when the minimum forest reserve was increased from 50 percent.4 As for labor law violations, Bala do Sul has allegedly used slave labor, the most common form of accusation pursuant to Article 186, Section 3 of the 1988 constitution, which allows for the seizure of property not “complying with provisions governing labor relations” for failure to meet the standard of social benefit (Brasil 1988, Articles 185, 186, 191). The fact that forty laborers were liberated from wage-slavery on the property between 2002 and 2004 only supported the case SMOs made that Bala do Sul should be expropriated.

The story begins in the 1980s. Dating from this point is not problematic for our purposes, given Bala do Sul remained mostly forested until the mid- to late 1990s, and SMOs expressed little interest in it until then. The first stirrings of contention included brief occupations by poorly organized occupiers and three fatalities of farm workers under suspicious circumstances. A new era of conflict began when the region’s most aggressive SMO, the MST, unexpectedly occupied the ranch on 26 March 1999. The occupation, which used a new tactic known as frente de massa, employed a group of 1,200 landless MST members and targeted the property’s legally registered forest reserve (Correio Editors 1999g; de Souza 1999a). The group set up an encampment of 300 dwellings, a health post, and a school (de Souza 1999b) but soon grew hungry, a circumstance that led to the sacking of the ranch’s fish-farming
operation and the uncompensated seizure of the ranch’s cattle for food (Correio Editors 1999i). Defensive actions by ranch hands resulted in their imprisonment by the MST, followed by a quick release (Correio Editors 1999k; Souza 2001). The Machados responded publicly by accusing the MST of illegal activity (Correio Editors 1999k; de Souza 1999b). The MST countered by asserting that the Machados had no legitimate claim to the property, given that it had originally been operated under aforamento lease (a perpetual lease agreement with the state) for Brazil nut extraction. Thus, its current incarnation as a cattle ranch rendered the lease null and void (Correio Editors 1999f).

The MST argument, made via the press in the court of public opinion, did little to convince state judges. Only forty-five days after the initial occupation, in April 1999, 400 federal military police, including 100 elite special forces, arrived to evict the occupiers (Correio Editors 1999i, 1999j; Opinião! Editors 1999k). Negotiations enabled the MST members’ voluntary departure in exchange for health care, food, and a promise that the ranch would be reevaluated for productivity and its possible failure to meet the social interest (Correio Editors 1999e; N. Santos 1999d). This truce was short-lived, however, and 850 people reoccupied Bala do Sul only a month later (Opinião! Editors 1999c; N. Santos 1999c). As a follow-up, the police arrested fifteen MST leaders on weapons charges, all of which would eventually be dropped as unsubstantiated (Filho 1999b, 1999c; Opinião! Editors 1999d, 1999h, 1999j; N. Santos 1999c). The conflict on Bala do Sul ranch began to appear intractable.

In July 1999, occupiers made a gruesome discovery that brought the conflict over Bala do Sul to the national stage. While MST members were working near their encampment on the edge of the forest reserve on Bala do Sul they stumbled across a clandestine cemetery with the remains of more than thirty people who had apparently been buried there for quite some time (Correio Editors 1999c; Opinião! Editors 1999b, 1999e). Initial accusations claimed that the cemetery represented a body dump for posseiros killed during the Luta Posseira by notorious gunmen active during that period. To the contrary, an investigation conducted by Instituto Médico Legal (IML), Pará’s official police coroner, concluded the cemetery to be legitimate, the final resting place for the mortal remains of common laborers and Brazil nut collectors who had died far from town and church (Correio Editors 1999c; N. Santos 1999b). Despite this finding, many remained suspicious given credible evidence that some of the deceased had been murdered (Almeida 1999; Opinião! Editors 1999a). The discovery of the cemetery added to a sense of mounting conflict.

Military police forces evicted DALR partisans for the second time in July 1999, but not for long (Correio Editors 1999a, 1999h; Opinião! Editors 1999a, 1999i). The occupiers returned almost immediately (Opinião! Editors 1999f, 1999l), overcoming the defenses of property gunmen by their numbers (Sousa 1999). Although no one claimed responsibility, a large forest fire destroyed many standing trees and a number of ranch outbuildings (Filho 1999d), an event that signaled an intensification of the MST’s contentious performances, which by November included road blocks of the region’s major transportation artery, PA-150 (Correio Editors 1999d; Opinião! Editors 1999g; N. Santos 1999a) and physical violence against farm workers (Correio Editors 1998; Filho 1999a, 2000). The Machados, perhaps feeling their time was limited, entered the Bala do Sul reserve to harvest trees, an action they had long resisted (Batistia 2000; Opinião! Editors 2000a, 2000b). They managed to orchestrate another eviction of MST occupiers in June 2000 (Opinião! Editors 2000c), but the end was drawing near, and rumors of expropriation despite the apparent productivity of the farm were common (Filho 2001a). By August 2001 conflict on Bala do Sul was intense, with groups of MST members blocking the nearby highway multiple times, threatening farm workers repeatedly, and setting multiple fires in and around the farm headquarters (J. Brasil 2001; Filho 2001b).

In August 2001, negotiations between INCRA, the Machados, and the MST went sour, partially due to the MST’s threatening behavior, and the Machados pursued legal eviction of the occupiers for what would be the last time. After a local judge ordered the occupiers evicted, the governor of Pará stayed that order for sixty days, hoping that the parties involved could come to some sort of settlement (Opinião! Editors 2001). The property’s status remained in limbo until April 2002, when twenty-two workers were liberated from wage-slavery on the ranch by Ministry of Labor law enforcement officers (E. Santos 2002), an action repeated less than two years later when eighteen more laborers were liberated from poor work conditions in February 2004 (Correio Editors 2004). Later that year, on 25 October 2004, after a long wait for President Lula’s signature and some discussion over a fair price, the federal government, through INCRA and the national superintendent for agrarian development, expropriated the property for the “social interest” and mandated that it be dedicated to meeting agrarian reform needs (K. K. 2004; Sousa 2004; Repórter Brasil 2007).
C-LC on Bala do Sul Ranch

Figure 2 integrates data from the digitized cadastral map of the Brazil nut polygon (discussed in the next section), satellite images, and the newspaper accounts of the fight for Bala do Sul. The panels of Figure 2 start in 1996 to capture possible preemptive actions by the Machados during the buildup to the MST occupation campaign. By this time, the MST had already occupied eleven other Machado properties in the region at some point, and three properties owned by Machado family members were occupied for the first time in early 1996.

Of interest is that as late as 1996, Bala do Sul had maintained only 42 percent forest cover, which means that significant deforestation had already taken place (Figure 2). This might have reflected preemptive actions on the part of the Machados or simply a rapid adaptation to the emerging cattle economy; subsequent to their early prominence in the Brazil nut economy, the Machados added the breeding of stud bulls to their portfolio.

On the eve of the battle over Bala do Sul, the Machados maintained 42 percent of the ranch as a forest reserve, appreciably less than the 50 percent required at the time by Law 7.803 of 18 July 1989 but probably
enough to suggest compliance, which was largely voluntary at the time (and largely remains so to this day, except when challenged by SMOs). Assuming the Machados were at least imperfectly compliant as they claimed (Correio Editors 1999b), the reduction to 37 percent forest cover by July 1999 presumably indicates an LC impact during the period of struggle precipitated by the MST’s initial occupation. In that the image presented in Figure 2 is from July 1999, the 5 percent decline in forest area covers a period during which control over the property changed five times (initial occupation, eviction, reoccupation, reeviction, and reoccupation).

Between 2000 and 2002 a further 4 percent decrease in forest cover is evident on Bala do Sul, and further deforestation took place between 2002 and 2003, although cloud cover becomes an issue (Figure 2).5 Still, significant forest loss is apparent through visual inspection, especially in the northeast quadrant of the property. During this period (2000–2004), conflict continued, involving another eviction and occupation, and many cases of vandalism, protest, and legal action in the region’s court system. Also during this time, as mentioned earlier, in June 2000, workers in the employ of the Machados were caught deforesting a 100-hectare tract of protected hardwoods (Opinião! Editors 2000a, 2000b). Given the multiple shifts in property control, both the MST (who were accused of targeting the ranch’s forest reserve in the first place) and the Machados (who were implicated in illegal deforestation) were likely responsible for the more than 10 percent decline in forest cover on the property. This deforestation in all likelihood had little to do with commercial development, which had all but halted on the property, and as such can be interpreted as C-LC.

The Schematic Representation and Inference

The case study of the Bala do Sul ranch suggests a sequencing of alternating control over land and land use, situated within a process of contention between adversarial agents possessing contradictory objectives. We now proceed to a schematic portrayal of this contentious process and its LC implications to bridge the gap between case study and statistical description. We first consider the contentious process itself, both its social dynamics and implications for changes in land cover and land use, and then move on to a regression analysis of C-LC in the arc of deforestation.

Contention and Land Change

Social Process Aspects. To simplify the exposition, it is useful to regard the actors in question as either “owners” of large properties, typically ranches (despite the fact that many do not own their land in the sense that definitive legal title is in hand, which is highly problematic throughout Brazil; see Alston, Libecap, and Mueller 1999, 2000; Brannstrom 2001), or DALR social movements (even though these movements are necessarily comprised of individuals). As the process of social contention between these actors plays out, control of property changes hands in precipitate events, leading to physical action such as property occupation by masses of movement partisans. Such actions are likely to have implications for land cover, leading to C-LC (Figure 3).

Data Description and Regression Analytics

The case study of Bala do Sul ranch and the schematic representations shown in Figures 3 and 4 suggest that contention accelerates LC beyond what would be experienced due to agronomic demands for forest clearance. To assess this claim beyond the suggestive details of the case study, we turn to regression analysis and inference, modeling deforestation as a function of contention in addition to other explanatory variables often used in LC analysis, such as distance to markets, soil quality, and so on. Our analysis starts with broad descriptive statistics considering the link between LC and the experience of contention on individual properties. We then move to regression-based assessments of LC occurring in the Brazil nut polygon over the study period from 1984 to 2003. This involves analysis of (1) total LC under successive regimes of DALR participant and owner occupation and (2) preemptive deforestation on the part of owners. This final analysis is undertaken given postexpropriation outcomes have been observed before on satellite images, but owner impacts on land in advance of conflict have not, despite speculations that they might deforest to avoid DALR targeting (Simmons et al. 2007; Aldrich 2009).

Data Sources. The analysis is developed for individual properties in the Brazil nut polygon using a variety of sources. Here, we consider satellite images, a cadastral coverage of properties, and regional newspapers, given these provide the basic information on LC and contention used in both the descriptive analyses and regression. Other data sources that feed the regression analysis are discussed later, when that component of the study is addressed.

Measures of deforestation were enabled by an archive of thirteen Landsat images covering the period from 1984 to 2003; these were assembled for the two scenes covering the Brazil nut polygon (path 223/row 64 and path 223/row 65). LC was linked to individual properties via a digital coverage obtained by the authors from an informant at Superintendência de Desenvolvimento da Amazônia (Superintendency for Amazonian Development) in the 1990s (Figure 1). The coverage is a planning document for Pará State’s region of concentrated Brazil nut extraction, much of the land between Marabá to the north and Xinguara to the south. It covers nearly 13,000 km\(^2\), with about 9,000 km\(^2\) being occupied by largeholdings. The Brazil nut polygon was about 90 percent forested in 1984, a number that dropped to 43 percent in 2003 (Aldrich, Simmons, and Walker 2008). The coverage includes boundaries for all properties as of 1990, with a set of characteristics such as owner name, titling status (aforamento lease, definitive federal title, definitive state title, usufruct, and untitled), and size. A total of 180 properties can be identified, with an average area of 4,886 hectares. As of 2003, seventy-nine had experienced contention over land resources, sixty-four had been occupied by DALR participants, and sixty-one had been expropriated in whole or part, pursuant to agrarian reform law, and dedicated to state-sanctioned settlement formation. The cadastral coverage has been used by the authors and found to be accurate (Simmons et al. 2007). The newspaper accounts, used to create records of DALR for individual properties in the Brazil nut polygon, were collected in Marabá, the informal capital of southeastern Pará, in the summer of 2007. A total of 5,286 stories were photographed from the region’s three primary newspapers (O Marabá, O Correio do Tocantins, and Opinião!) to develop a complete journalistic account of contentious incidents for the period from 1984 to 2007.

Measuring Contention. Contention in the arc of deforestation assumes many forms. DALR occupations, road blockades, verbal insults, arsons, beatings, and homicides all indicate contention and have been observed on properties covered by the cadastral map. We developed property-level measures of contention based on newspaper stories, recording the number of specific conflict incidents by type, as an attribute in the geographic information system (GIS) coverage of the landholdings of the Brazil nut polygon (Aldrich 2009). A geo-referenced region-wide tally of land conflict in the study area created a unique data set allowing for exploration of contentious events and land change on the properties that hosted them.

Other property characteristics indicate contention or its likelihood, although they might not be identified as specific actions or events. For example, a number of landholdings in the Brazil nut polygon have ambiguous land titles, a status often linked to contention given the
utility of uncertain titling status as a rationale for DALR actions (Foweraker 1981; Hall 1989; de Almeida 1990; Alston 1996; Alston, Libecap, and Mueller 1999; Branford and Rocha 2002; Wright and Wolford 2003; Simmons et al. 2007; Wolford 2010). The same holds for the presence of labor conflicts, especially debt-peonage, which in effect enslaves landless workers and is a serious problem in the region (E. Santos 2002; Folhapress 2006; Aldrich 2009). A more subjective variable, but also meaningful in this context, is intensity of class antagonism born by property owners toward DALR. A handful of largeholders are particularly hostile to land reform, and their attitudes and actions are known to all, by word-of-mouth and also, reliably, by newspapers. The struggle in southeastern Pará has a personal character in many cases, and adversaries on both sides know (and often despise) each other. Thus, the cast of characters in southeastern Pará can have a serious influence on the likelihood of contention (E. Santos 2004).

For social processes like land conflict, “official” data might be biased and difficult to come by (Klandermans and Staggenborg 2002); thus, a growing number of social scientists use newspapers to study collective action and other instances of social contention (Crist and McCarthy 1996; Earl et al. 2004). Although bias might be evident (Earl et al. 2004; Ortiz et al. 2005), the value of these data remains high (Klandermans and Staggenborg 2002; Earl et al. 2004; Ortiz et al. 2005), particularly when their “hard news” content is exploited (Earl et al. 2004). Our use of newspapers minimizes inherent biases by focusing on hard news (as opposed to editorial opinion) and by relying on three (as opposed to one) newspapers (plus additional accounts available from the CPT). Both sides of the conflict have supporters in the press and incentives to see their stories told. Our use of journalistic archives is by no means novel and follows the lead of human rights groups such as the CPT, concerned government agencies (e.g., IN-CRA; Barata 1995) and other researchers investigating land conflict in Amazônia (Brandford and Glock 1985; de Almeida 1994; Alston, Libecap, and Mueller 2000; Branford and Rocha 2002; Ondetti 2008). The archive we used possesses the same newspapers as Barata (1995) but expands its time domain. Simmons (2004) showed that Barata (1995) provides a more complete account of contentious incidents than the CPT data, which are also newspaper-based but primarily track land occupations and violence, leaving other potentially important data aside (Ondetti 2008). This is not to diminish the value of the CPT’s very important data, which a number of researchers have relied on for their analyses (de Almeida 1994; Alston, Libecap, and Mueller 2000), only to point out that their focused nature means they are limited for our purposes.

We use two regression analyses to address this issue, one for total deforestation over the period (1984–2003) and one for preemptive deforestation by largeholders (Table 2). For each analysis, we begin with ordinary least squares (OLS), consider diagnostics for spatial autocorrelation, and implement the appropriate spatial model as necessary.

The prime variables of interest are LC and indicators of contention. The regression models also implement a set of conventional independent variables, often used in LC models, reflecting the impacts primarily of market access and resource quality, especially soil capability (Geist and Lambin 2001; Lambin et al. 2001; Lambin, Geist, and Lepers 2003; Gutman et al. 2004; Caldas et al. 2007). Everything else held equal, forested sites with good soils and in closer proximity to points of market sale should experience more rapid deforestation due to agronomic expansion than those with alternative characteristics. Resource quality in the analysis focuses on soils, information that was developed from a soil map (EMBRAPA and IBGE 1999) and classified according to agricultural suitability defined by Empresa Brasileira de Pesquisa Agropecuária (EMBRAPA), Brazil’s agricultural research and extension corporation designations. Market access variables are defined in several ways for both regressions, given (1) the primacy of transport costs as a driver of LC and (2) our a priori ignorance about the nature of the transportation system in the region. Included are measures of distance to rivers (important throughout Amazônia), whether a property is located in the immediate vicinity of a highway (PA-150), and distance from an urban area. All measures were created via GIS software, using property boundaries and centroids as necessary. The distance to urban area is measured as the distance of the property from Conceição do Araguaia, located in the southeastern corner of Pará. Although the region has a number of towns, Conceição do Araguaia links the Brazil nut polygon, via PA-150 and a brief ferry ride across the Araguaia River, to the Belém–Brasília Highway (BR-010), which connects Amazônia to the economic core of Brazil. Total Period Deforestation. The two regression model specifications are essentially identical but for the dependent variable implemented and a slight change in one independent variable. For the total period model, we consider the entire magnitude of deforestation on the properties, between 1984 and 2003, and regress with variables capturing contention, soil quality, and market access. Results of both the OLS regression and spatial models are presented in Table 2. Diagnostics on the OLS residuals (Table 3) indicate the appropriateness of a spatial model (Anselin and Florax 1995; Anselin et al. 1996), with spatial autocorrelation occurring in the dependent variable, as has been observed in other deforestation studies (e.g., Caldas et al. 2007). Of considerable note is the lack of performance for the contention variables, in general. Although results are not perverse and show negative coefficients indicating less forest, none show significance, although title problems appear to accelerate deforestation in the OLS model. This effect is mitigated once spatial autocorrelation is taken into account. The soil variable, although significant under OLS, loses significance with the spatial formulation, although the coefficient remains positive, indicating good soils tend to be associated with forest, not its loss, perverse by our hypothesis. The highway binary shows similar performance, with immediate road access associated with more, as opposed to less, forest; here, significance remains marginal with the move to the spatial model. Finally, distance to urban area provides a perverse result as well, with greater distance from Conceição do Araguaia indicating less forest; however, marginal significance under OLS vanishes once the spatial model is fit. The results attain high \( R^2 \) under OLS, and the Akaike information criterion changes from 3,045.109 in OLS to 3,018.377 with the spatial lag specification, as would be expected given the diagnostics. The rho coefficient is positive and strongly significant, consistent with both diagnostics and prior research on the spatial relations of forest clearing at property scale.

Preemptive Deforestation. Our second regression analysis implements a dependent variable that measures the amount of deforestation occurring on a property prior to its first occupation. As such, it indicates the preemptive actions land owners take in the face of conflict. We assume the owner knows if he or she is about to experience DALR, which is reasonable given the openness of the movements to the press and their interest in mobilizing class actions against identifiable targets. Measuring this variable requires manipulation, given the incompleteness of the satellite image record. Our objective is to measure deforestation between the two years prior to first occupation, assuming preemptive actions manifest on a relatively short schedule prior to occupation. These in turn are hypothesized to be linked to subsequent DALR actions, which are taken to be tangible, at least in degree, prior to their manifestation, given the tense atmosphere enveloping contested lands in the region. The available data required that we use a period control when the necessary images were not available for our two-year window on preemption. This variable measures time, in years, from the first occupation to the most recent prior image, also used to build the deforestation measure. The time variable controls for additional time steps that could conceivably increase deforestation over that interval if agronomic land clearance in accordance with the property life-cycle effect proceeds up to the time of the owner’s preemptive actions.

As with the first regression model, we also fit a spatial model given diagnostics on the OLS error terms. The most important finding is that DALR actions, which in this specification include only those events before initial property occupation, and a number of other variables indicating contention show impacts on preemptive deforestation. Specifically, variables indicating contention increase the amount of deforestation, which is consistent with hypothesized effects. The result is
preserved in the spatial model for DALR actions, where we find robust performance across the specifications. The other variables reflecting resource quality and market access perform in a similar fashion to the first regression. Again, the distance to urban area appears with a significant perverse result under OLS, but this vanishes with the spatial model. The period control, not conceptually translatable to the total deforestation regression, shows a consistent impact in the preemptive model, with longer periods leading to more deforestation. As defined, this variable can be interpreted as reflecting property life cycle, as the other variables in the model would presumably account for most deforestation in the absence of an autonomous property process. This is to say, the null for the period control variable is coincident with a null hypothesis of no property life-cycle effect, which the results reject strongly.

Discussion

The case study and regression analyses provide evidence for the existence of C-LC in Amazônia’s arc of deforestation. The case study documents a rapid reduction in forest cover during the period of contention. From 1984 until 2003 average forest cover fell from 80 to 23 percent on the 180 largeholdings of the study area, and it is possible to link specific contentious incidents to deforestation events. The descriptive statistical analysis suggests a strong bivariate relationship as well. This does not stand up in the total deforestation analysis, although OLS does provide a suggestive result. Poor performance of DALR variables in the total deforestation analysis could be accounted for by failure to control for property life cycle, which is unavoidable given our record of satellite imagery does not allow us to observe initial acts of deforestation on individual holdings. If properties that avoid DALR are of long occupation by established families, then those that experience it likely begin their agronomic LC relatively late. In such a situation, C-LC, even if it exceeds agronomic LC on DALR-impacted properties, will not necessarily exceed the extent of clearance on the older, established ones, in which case variables reflecting DALR might not show deforestation impacts in regressions with both property types in the sample. Another possible reason for insignificant results in this model is sample size; the sign of the DALR coefficient is consistent with expectations, and more statistical power could well lead to rejecting the null hypothesis of no effect. Despite these observations, it needs to be emphasized that the preemptive analysis lends strong support to the hypothesis that contention functions as a driver of LC in the region, a result that could be further tested in an investigation of “natural resource liquidation” behavior, which might be apparent over shorter time periods. Here, the period control for measuring preemptive deforestation also controls for duration of land tenure by largeholders more generally and therefore reveals the presence of property life-cycle effects. This adds support to the interpretation offered about the lack of strong performance of DALR variables in the total deforestation analysis.

The results of both regressions are consistent in showing that conventional explanations of LC do little to account for LC dynamics in southeastern Pará. As for the resource quality result, this could be explained, in part, by the spatial distribution of soil, as fertile classes are concentrated in only a few areas of the region, meaning their statistical signal might be weak in this analysis anyway. Some have argued that the role of soils in inhibiting Amazonian deforestation over the long run might have been overstated (Chomitz and Thomas 2003; Caldas et al. 2007) and, in fact, soils in Amazônia are not that much different from other agricultural regions in Brazil, in which case soil quality might do little to impact agronomic decisions in aggregate (Arima et al. 2005; R. Walker, DeFries et al. 2009). The result here is perverse, which suggests, possibly, an intensification effect and the substitution of land for soil fertility in production technology. Given the extent of available land in the region, some have argued that intensification is an unlikely prospect, at least over the short run (White et al. 2001). In any event, the results invite further analysis.

As for the distance and access variables, the regression results do not agree with expectations that they should intensify LC. In fact, the road binary presents a significantly perverse result in both regression analyses, even when controlling for autocorrelation. This could be explained by strategic behavior on part of largeholders, who might deliberately maintain forest at roadsides, deceiving the Instituto Brasileiro do Meio Ambiente e dos Recursos Naturais Renováveis (the Brazilian Institute of Environment and Renewable Natural Resources, Brazil’s environmental enforcement agency) officials passing by into believing properties are in compliance with environmental laws to keep a part of the original forest intact in a reserve area (Cardoso 2000; Olmos 2006; Sant’Anna 2008). The distance variables, both to water and to urban areas, also do not perform as expected, although neither result is significant
in the spatial model, indicating no effect whatsoever. This contrasts significantly with survey-based studies along the Trans-Amazonian Highway to the northwest, where land clearance is tightly linked to distance from road (R. Walker et al. 2002; Caldas et al. 2007). As for the results reported here, they pertain to an entirely different circumstance, namely, the transport of highly marketized largeholder production in a region that possesses a much denser road network than the colonization frontiers of the Trans-Amazonian Highway. Although the cost structure for removal of goods from colonist properties in the frontier is a function of distance (see Caldas et al. 2007, note 11), it is entirely possible that truckers do not vary their charges much across the breadth of the Brazil nut polygon, in which case a distance to urban area variable is less likely to perform well.

Conclusions

We wish to draw two conclusions beyond the analysis just presented and discussed. The first relates to theory and the second to policy. Theoretically, we have already discussed how prior research has tended to conceptualize LC as a behavioral outcome of actions taken by individual agents. The results of this study suggest that such an explanatory paradigm misses an important ingredient, namely, the role of social processes involving a succession of agents whose actions and interactions precipitate the proximate causes of LC. The identification of DALR and contention more generally lends weight to an explanatory framework combining the “inequality” view of resource conflict with contentious politics. Such a framework enables the identification of social structures and institutional resources that explain both the genesis of land contention in Amazônia and the form this contention has taken. Furthermore, when agent interactions are considered, in southeastern Pará it is clear that the institution of a “social function” requirement for landholdings has given both largeholders and the SMO adversaries a constitutional justification for their claims in the struggle for land and can be interpreted by both groups as a legal imperative to deforest any land to which they have access. When deployed to explicate LC in the arc of deforestation, this framework fuses the thematic concerns and the methodological resources of land change science with the explanatory power of political ecology, thereby going beyond reductionist approaches to Amazonian deforestation that often focus on disaggregate decision makers responding autonomously to market signals and institutional incentives.

The article focuses on a single region in the arc of deforestation, but C-LC is not restricted to the study area. Rondônia has long experienced contention between DALR movements and largeholders, and Acre, of course, gained notoriety for the murder of Chico Mendes in a dispute over land (Mendes and Gross 1992). More recently, conflicts involving smallholders and largeholders have surfaced in parts of the basin new to this phenomenon. In counties like Santarém, Uruará, and Mojuí in western Pará, incidents of violence have increased, probably due to the arrival of mechanized agriculture with the soy frontier and its associated surge in land values (Araujo et al. 2009). Conflict has also come to a number of heretofore peaceful states, including Amazonas, Amapá, and Roraima (CPT 2000, 2001, 2002, 2003, 2004, 2005, 2006, 2007; DataLuta 2008). Given DALR’s widespread dispersion (see Simmons et al. 2007), C-LC probably accounts for an appreciable component of deforestation, basin-wide. With a resurgence of land reform across the continent in countries such as Bolivia, Venezuela, and Ecuador, as well as in tropical Africa and Asia and the Taliban strongholds of Afghanistan and Pakistan, C-LC might well present a highly generalized phenomenon (Petras 1998; Adams 2000; Moyo and Yeros 2005; Rosset, Patel, and Courville 2006; Sikor and Müller 2009; Trofimov 2010).

With respect to Amazônia, C-LC is likely to grow, given that conflict is endemic throughout northern Brazil and appears to be spreading. Conflict could soon be aggravated by the revival of old dreams of “modernization” via massive development. The “multi-year plans” (or Planos Plurianuais) of past presidents Fernando Henrique Cardoso and Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, as well as Lula da Silva’s “growth acceleration plan” (or Programa de Aceleração do Crescimento), have added tremendous infrastructure to the region, a process likely to continue under President Dilma (see Brazil 2010). These infrastructure investments will intensify the advance of mechanized agriculture on the region, bringing soy and other crops into areas that once were forested, and bringing along a class of Brazilian agricultural laborers looking for opportunity (Brown, Jepson, and Price 2004; Jepson 2005; Morton et al. 2006).

We have previously referred to land conflict in southeastern Pará as the Amazon Land War, given its high number of casualties and the intensity of its deforestation process (Simmons et al. 2007). With the irresistible advance of capital into Amazônia, pushed on by development plans and exploding demands for Brazilian commodities (R. Walker, Browder, et al. 2009), all indications are that the Land War will spread, from its first
cauldron in southeastern Pará to the north and west. Land-conflict-related fatalities have recently moved in that direction, with the 2003 massacre of seven rural workers and one rancher near São Felix do Xingu; the 2005 execution of Dorothy Stang in Anapu, northwest of Marabá; the May 2011 assassination of environmental activist João Claudio Ribeiro da Silva and his wife just outside of Marabá; and the emerging pattern of assassinations in Altamira and points west along BR-163, the soy highway. Evidently, confrontation between the interests of capital and the Brazilian poor will continue in Amazônia, at least over the short run (DataLuta 2008).

The work presented here describes the implications of land conflict on its forested battleground, which happens to be one of the world’s great ecological treasures, the closed moist forest that covers the heart of a continent. If the intensity of physical violence waxes and wanes (Simmons 2004), destruction of the Amazonian forest does not. This brings us to our second and final conclusion. C-LC is deeply embedded in the fight for land and livelihood in Amazônia, and only by ending it with genuine land reform can sustainability be achieved for both its peoples and environment.
