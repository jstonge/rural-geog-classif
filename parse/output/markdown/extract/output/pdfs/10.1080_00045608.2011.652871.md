Neoliberalization, Transnational Migration, and the Varied Landscape of Economic Subjectivity in the Totonacapan Region of Veracruz

Jeff Popke* and Rebecca Maria Torres†

*Department of Geography, East Carolina University
†Department of Geography and the Environment, The University of Texas at Austin

Over the past two decades, many of Mexico’s rural communities have been faced with significant challenges arising from two interrelated processes: the neoliberal restructuring of rural policy and citizenship, and the dramatic increase in transnational migration. The ways in which local communities experience and respond to such changes, however, are variable. We examine the intersection of neoliberal socioeconomic change and transnational migration in the Totonacapan region of Veracruz, highlighting their uneven regional impacts in the largely Mestizo coastal region and the more indigenous sierra. Drawing on in-depth interviews, we show how neoliberal change is filtered through local historical trajectories and cultural understandings that influence the development of migration in the region. We suggest how different conceptions of social responsibility influence the outcomes of migration, with some communities experiencing severe dislocation and others managing the process for the benefit of the common. In both cases, we argue, the new economic subjectivities arising from intensified migration can be seen as a symptom, and thus potentially a source of ethical critique, of neoliberalism’s many failures. Key Words: agrarian change, indigenous ethics, Mexican migration, neoliberal subjectivity, Totonac Veracruz.

For millions of Mexico’s rural smallholders, the past two decades have been a period of profound social transformation, and in some cases crisis, brought about by two interconnected processes. The first is a dramatic agrarian restructuring due largely to neoliberal policy shifts on the part of the Mexican state; the second is a significant increase in the transnationalization of livelihoods, as growing numbers of rural dwellers seek income-earning opportunities in Mexico’s urban areas or in the United States. Taken together, this
neoliberalism–migration nexus has led to a significant reshaping of the Mexican countryside, producing new economic strategies, familial relationships, and agrarian identities in the process.

We examine how this constellation of changes has played out in the Totonacapan, a rural region in the Gulf Coast state of Veracruz. Outmigration has increased substantially in this region over the past fifteen years or so, a period that has also been witness to a deepening of neoliberal political and economic policies, as well as new sensibilities that are consistent with neoliberal forms of citizenship and subjectivity. Although neoliberalism and migration are clearly linked in rural Veracruz, their specific local impacts vary significantly. The meanings and consequences of both neoliberal change and transnational migration are mediated by a host of local factors, including economic relationships and ethnic identity, such that communities located just a few kilometers apart can have very different understandings of, and experiences with, forces of change within the region.

We present results from a field-based collaborative research project carried out in the Totonacapan in 2007. Ethnographic investigations into the local contours of neoliberalism are not common, but we believe that they have considerable potential to add to our understanding of the ways in which neoliberal sensibilities are woven into the context of household and community life. We are particularly interested in the ethical stakes of these dynamics, and the ways in which engagements with neoliberalism intersect with local notions of individual and collective responsibility. As Ong noted (2006) “neoliberalism . . . encounters and articulates other ethical regimes in particular contexts” (9). As the Zapatista uprising has clearly demonstrated, the onset of a neoliberal sensibility in Mexico has not been without challenge from groups and discourses articulating an alternative ethical vision (Popke 2004). Although there is little overt collective resistance in Veracruz, the debates and contestations engendered by recent social and economic change serve to highlight the effects of neoliberal policies, and thereby politicize the economy and indigenous autonomy as sites of ethical agency.

Approaching Neoliberalism

Geographical scholarship has contributed a great deal to our understanding of neoliberalism in recent years, from a host of different perspectives (England and Ward 2007b; Leitner, Peck, and Sheppard 2007; Barnett 2010). Among other things, neoliberalism has been examined as a historical ideology (Peck 2008), as a political-economic model and policy regime (Peck 2004; D. Harvey 2006), as a form of political rationality or governmentality (Larner 2000; Mitchell 2006; Hiemstra 2010), and as a mode of citizenship or subjectivity (Bondi 2005; Barnett, et al. 2008). Few studies to date, however, have examined empirically the uneven local impacts of neoliberal change.

As a policy regime, neoliberalism has come to describe a set of now-familiar principles, including trade and financial liberalization, deregulation, privatization, and diminished public spending on social programs (Tickell and Peck 2003). An important dimension of these shifts in governance is the embrace, even celebration, of the free market economy as the guarantor of fair and efficient outcomes, the result of which, noted Peck (2004), is to naturalize the economy, discursively positioning “the economic” beyond the realm of political negotiation. This basic set of policy prescriptions found its way to the global South in the wake of the so-called debt crisis of the early 1980s, when trade liberalization, tight fiscal policy, and market discipline were promoted (often in the guise of a structural adjustment program backed by the International Monetary Fund [IMF]) as the best means for countries to stabilize their current accounts and resume debt payments. As a number of recent commentators have pointed out, however, the development of neoliberalism in the Global South was “path dependent” and took locally specific forms as market rationalities were filtered through local institutional arrangements and experimentations (Brenner, Peck, and Theodore 2010; Ferguson 2010). In this way, neoliberalism can be described as a “mobile technology” (Ong 2007, 5) or a “flexibly mutating regime” (Peck, Theodore, and Brenner 2010, 95), the contours of which will depend on local circumstances.

In Mexico’s case, the onset of neoliberal policies entailed, in the most general terms, a reworking of the statist and corporatist pact behind the development model that held sway during the so-called Mexican economic miracle in the middle decades of the twentieth century. Although external pressures played a role in these changes, market-oriented regulatory reform was not simply copied from the United States or dictated by the IMF; it was, rather, the outcome of political negotiation, both among governing elites and between the
Mexican government and the institutions of civil society (Snyder 2001). Specific outcomes were also influenced to a significant degree by local forms of political organization and social relations (Nuijten 2003; Getz 2008), resulting in a variegated landscape of neoliberal restructuring across geographical regions, economic sectors, and social classes (MacLeod 2004; Gilbert 2007). As Babb (2001) has written, “the history of Mexico’s neoliberal transition shows that policy reforms in developing countries can be complex and multifaceted, involving the interplay of internal and external factors” (183).

Neoliberal Subjectivity

One of the important factors in neoliberalism’s locally uneven development has to do with the ways in which it intersects not only with local policy regimes but also with existing understandings of “the social” and varying forms of economic subjectivity. The policy shifts associated with neoliberalism are justified through appeals to a set of powerful discourses of individual liberty, free and fair competition, and personal choice, which have filtered into everyday practices and encounters. Neoliberalization, then, is more than an economic model or policy regime. It also functions as a form of citizenship or subjectivity, born of a shift in the relationships between government regulatory regimes, social policy, and the expectations placed on social subjects (Mitchell 2003b; Barnett et al. 2008; Hörschelmann 2008). As a form of subjectivity, neoliberalism is best described as “an ethos of self-governing” (Ong 2006, 9) that promotes self-reliance, personal responsibility, individual choice, and “family values” while discouraging social solidarity and collective action (D. Harvey 2005). As such, a neoliberal sensibility also promotes a particular ethical vision that seeks to circumscribe the sites and spaces of the social toward which we can be said to bear a moral responsibility. As Miller and Rose (1990) put it, “citizenship is to be active and individualistic rather than passive and dependent. The political subject is henceforth to be an individual whose citizenship is manifested through the free exercise of personal choice amongst a variety of options” (24).

Importantly, however, the ways in which available options come to be imagined, and the actions and decisions that might follow from this, are dependent on a host of locally variant factors, not least the extent to which the ethos of neoliberalism aligns or conflicts with extant notions of social and ethical agency. This in turn is shaped by the characteristics of specific communities, including their histories of work and labor, forms of governance, and ethnic identity. The result in Veracruz is that neoliberal forms of governmentality react on a diverse landscape of economic subjectivity, and this shapes how individuals and communities adapt to changing circumstances, implement survival strategies, and craft new social and economic opportunities.

Transnational Migration

Increasingly, the circumstances facing social subjects in rural Mexico are shaped by the transnational processes and relationships set into motion through Mexican migration to the United States. By denoting these relationships as transnational, we follow other scholars in acknowledging the ways in which individuals and communities “sustain familial, economic, cultural, and political ties and identities across national borders, in both home and host societies” (Ehrkamp and Leitner 2006, 1593; also Mountz and Wright 1996; Vertovec 1999; Bailey et al. 2002; Mitchell 2003a; Jackson, Crang, and Dwyer 2004). Neoliberal forms of subjectivity, then, are encountered and negotiated within social and spatial contexts that extend across the U.S.–Mexican border, and are continually reworked by the agency of transnational migrants.

It should be noted here that Mexican migration to the United States predates the onset of neoliberalism, and in some parts of the country, particularly the northern and central regions, temporary outmigration has been a structural feature of communities for many decades (Durand and Massey 1992). In other regions, however, including northern Veracruz, the onset of widespread outmigration roughly corresponds to the development of neoliberal policies and the articulation of new forms of citizenship over the past twenty years or so. In this context, migration bears a complex relationship to neoliberal change (Massey, Durand, and Malone 2002; Delgado Wise and Márquez Covarrubias 2008).

In the first instance, migration can be viewed as a consequence of changing economic policies, “an attempt by both individual households and the state to manage the fallout of neoliberal restructuring” (May et al. 2008, 67). As we describe in more detail later, neoliberal policies and sensibilities have proven particularly disruptive in Mexico’s rural agricultural areas. In part, this disruption is due to shifts in agrarian policy, which have resulted in lower market prices and reduced
state subsidies for rural producers, inducing many to join the growing stream of northward migration (Bartra 2004; Nevins 2007; Binford 2009). At the same time, however, migration can be seen as a means by which neoliberal subjectivity is negotiated and enacted. Each migrant biography is a particular ensemble of aspirations, calculations, and decisions in the face of available options. Those who make the journey are exposed to the competitive pressures and consumption patterns that characterize the low end of the U.S. job market. Success requires an ability to exploit opportunity and to manage risk, as the security of family and community is exchanged for a more flexible and itinerant identity, particularly for those who lack legal status in the United States.

To some extent, then, the U.S.-bound migrant can be seen as the prototypical neoliberal subject—enterprising and individualistic, no longer an object of responsibility or ethical concern for the state or wider community. As with the case of neoliberalism, however, the meanings and trajectories of migration are contingent on local circumstances and intersect with existing notions of individual and collective life, as “migrants engage in complex negotiations of multiple and sometimes conflicting identities in relation to their old and new places of residence” (Leitner and Ehrkamp 2006, 1626). The extent to which transnational migration is facilitated by or contributes to neoliberal forms of subjectivity is therefore a locally variant phenomenon. One way to better understand this complex process, we believe, is through ethnographic investigation.

Our aim in what follows, then, is to examine how the intertwined dynamics of neoliberal change and transnational migration have been filtered through local community cultures, leading to an uneven landscape of economic subjectivity and ethical agency in rural Mexico. We seek to address the need identified by Barnett and colleagues (2008) for accounts of “how these macro-level processes are connected to micro-level process of subject-formation” (625) in different places. We characterize our approach as a regional ethnography. We mean by this term not the typical anthropological understanding of “an extended sojourn amongst a group of people” over many years (Megoran 2006, 625) but rather “a research practice that takes as its starting point participation in the everyday worlds of others and a commitment to engage with their understandings and social practice” (Hörschelmann and Stenning 2008, 343).

Our investigation is focused on the Totonacapan (Figure 1), a Gulf Coast region historically and culturally associated with the Totonac ethnic group (Kelly and Palerm 1952; H. Harvey and Kelly 1969). Our insights are derived from three months of ethnographic fieldwork, during which we carried out fifty-three semistructured interviews in four municipios within the region: Papantla and Tecolutla, located in the coastal plain and home to a mix of indigenous and Mestizo communities; and the largely indigenous areas of Cuahuitlán and Filomeno Mata, located in the highlands of the Sierra Madre Oriental (Figure 2). Interview subjects included state and regional officials, migrants and their families, farmers, and local leaders in sixteen different small communities within the region. Interviews averaged about ninety minutes and were oriented around an interview guide crafted to explore various themes relating to migration, agrarian restructuring, and community change. We also conducted seven half-day focus group sessions (two each with separate groups of men, women, and youth and one with community leaders) aimed at collecting agrarian histories and tracing migration patterns in different communities and, with

Our account is derived from a synthesis of observations and experiences recorded during our immersion in the field, supplemented by a qualitative analysis of transcripts from interviews and focus groups, which included the compilation of community-specific histories of major crops, government programs, and migration patterns, as well as a content analysis to identify recurring themes.

After many days of visiting communities in the region, however, and through both our formal interviews and additional informal discussions with local residents, we began to develop a greater appreciation for the diverse contexts within which migration takes place and is interpreted in the Totonacapan. Despite their proximity, the communities that we visited differ significantly in terms of their ethnic composition; land tenure structures and agrarian histories; and environmental, economic, and resource endowments. The result is an uneven landscape of economic subjectivity within the region, which has led to locally variant articulations of neoliberalism and migration. It is this relationship, and its implications for geographic theory, that we describe in what follows.

Rural Subjectivity in the Totonacapan

In the rural areas of northern Veracruz, the impacts of neoliberalism are filtered through two important dimensions of community life. The first is the agricultural economy, which is facing a significantly altered policy environment in the wake of neoliberal restructuring; and the second is the cultural influence of indigenous identity, which shapes commonsense understandings of the social and of individual and collective responsibility.

The agricultural economy of northern Veracruz dates back to the colonial era (Ducey 1999; Kourí 2004) but took its present shape in the early decades of the twentieth century, when new railways and hydroelectric power plants facilitated the development of manufacturing and increased agricultural opportunities (Koth 2002). The most significant regional development was the discovery of oil along the Veracruz coast, which led to new highways, roads, and bridges; brought new consumer goods; and made the region more accessible to regional and national markets (J. Brown 1993; Santiago 2006). This opening offered new opportunities for the cultivation and marketing of existing cash crops such as vanilla, as well as new crops, including Jalapeño and Serrano chiles, bananas, and papaya, which were aimed primarily at the newly connected regional and national markets (Velázquez Hernández 1996). These crops formed the bulk of the regional economy through the middle decade of the twentieth century, along with tobacco, sugarcane, and coffee, three crops that became heavily dependent on state subsidies and marketing arrangements. After the Mexican Revolution (1910–1920), some communities took advantage of the
opportunity to establish ejidos, collective landholdings assigned to petitioning groups by the Mexican government as part of a massive land reform program. Other agriculturalists remained small-scale private producers, and a growing number of landless (often indigenous) rural dwellers sought work in road building, construction, or the oil industry. When prices for both bananas and vanilla declined in the 1970s, many communities responded by switching to large-scale citrus orchards (chiefly oranges and limes), which remain today, along with cattle, the foundation of the agricultural economy in the region (Ministry of Agriculture, Livestock, Rural Development, Fisheries and Food 2005). Today, these are supplemented by smaller quantities of specialty crops, including papaya, lychee, allspice, jicama, and maize husks for tamales, which have become a significant cash crop within the region (King 2007).

Overall, then, the contemporary agricultural landscape of the Totonacapan is characterized by a messy patchwork of crops and land tenure systems. Community identity and income-generating opportunities vary according to the vagaries of land quality and availability, the markets for particular crops in any given season, access to money or credit for agricultural investment, and individual or collective farming acumen. Circumstances range from relatively successful ejidatarios growing high-demand export crops, through small-scale independent or collectively farmed milpa plots tended primarily for subsistence, to communities of landless laborers (jornaleros) struggling to find work in neighboring orchards, cattle ranches, or nearby towns. These varying material conditions help to shape the ways in which both neoliberal change and migration are viewed and negotiated in any given community. They also intersect with another salient feature of community life in this corner of rural Mexico, namely, the nature of indigenous identity.

Ethnic Identity and Regional Change

The northern part of Veracruz, along with the adjacent highlands of Puebla and Hidalgo, is one of the more ethnically diverse regions of Mexico. In addition to some 260,000 Totonacos, the region is home to significant populations of Nahuas-, Otomi-, and Tepehua-speaking inhabitants (Rouy 2005). In many parts of the Totonacapan, particularly the highland areas of the Sierra Madre Oriental, Totonac communities have until recently remained relatively isolated from the rapid pace of economic development along the coast and have thus maintained a much stronger sense of Totonac identity than their coastal counterparts. Many rural dwellers continue to speak the Totonac language, and community life is influenced by the traditional rhythms of cooperative labor and by an understanding of the social that differs in many respects from neoliberal subjectivity (Villanueva 1996; W. Smith 2004; Govers 2006). Principles of solidarity, mutual aid, and environmental respect are embedded in Totonac cosmology, which, as W. Smith (2007) put it, “defines a moral geography, a human–nonhuman sociality, an environmental ethic in the most comprehensive terms” (22; see also Govers 2006). The extent to which a given community embraces such ideals, however, varies significantly across the Totonacapan, and has much to do with the changing views and policies of the Mexican state.

In the 1970s, Mexico’s indigenous communities became the target of new social and development policies, aimed at modernizing “the Indian” and cultivating a new and progressive sense of national citizenship (Lomnitz 2001; Alonso 2004). Here’s how one local government official described this process in the Totonacapan:

There was a time when we wielded a particular educational model, a development plan. . . . Around 1970, there was a program to promote Spanish on the part of the Secretary of Public Education. They held a philosophy that said that indigenous groups . . . were holding back the country, were slowing down the country. . . . The idea was to do away with the Totonac language, because the government had said that if we were speaking Totonaco, we would be backward, that there would not be any forward development. (Engineer, Xicotlapa, Filomeno Mata).1

A number of our interview respondents recalled growing up during this time and now lament the fact that today they cannot speak Totonaco. “Our parents didn’t teach Totonaco to us,” said one, “they thought that we would be embarrassed when we grew up” (Ejidatario, La Campaña, Papantla). Said another, “My father never liked it when we spoke Totonaco . . . if he heard us speaking in Totonaco, he would let us have it! And so, we didn’t speak it” (Agente municipal, Allende, Papantla).

The industrialization associated with oil development also had an influence on the indigenous character of many communities. New transplants arrived from overseas, and labor was recruited from Mestizo areas further north and inland (Villanueva 1991). At the same time, modernization in the Veracruz lowlands served to transform the worldview of indigenous people
themselves. As a local Totonac activist described it to us, with the arrival of the oil industry:

Indigenous people began to see other things, language, way of thinking, dress . . . before, it wasn’t important to have a business, a bank account; what was important was that there was maize to be tended, for tortillas to eat . . . the reason for planting so many orange groves, why today there is so much citrus, is because the indigenous campesino wanted to be like the mestizo, wanted to have money, to buy land and build houses, businesses. This wasn’t the case before, when we had a more tranquil life, and the land supported us to a large extent. (Espinosa, Papantla)

As with agricultural production, then, indigenous identity varies greatly across the region. Some communities are almost entirely Mestizo and have been for several generations. There are self-described Totonac communities where nobody speaks Totonaco and there is little material evidence of Totonac culture. And, despite decades of rapid change, many sierra communities, and a fair number of coastal communities as well, have managed to maintain a strong sense of Totonac identity. As we will see, this uneven landscape of indigenous subjectivity has helped to shape the region’s experience with both neoliberal change and transnational migration to a significant degree.

The Development of Neoliberalism in Mexico

In the wake of the Mexican Revolution, land and labor, as embodied in the rural ejido, were defining characteristics of the ideology of Mexican nationalism (Gilly 1998). Implicit within this revolutionary nationalism was a conception of social life that valorized the collective vision of cooperative labor that was central to Mexico’s peasant and indigenous communities. Agricultural self-sufficiency and communal sensibilities were thus enshrined as core ethical principles. Over the ensuing decades, however, Mexican society underwent a profound transformation. The economy witnessed dramatic industrial growth, built on import substitution and heavy state intervention combined with rapid urbanization and technological development. In the countryside, state policy favored the Green Revolution model in the agricultural sector, and peasant families increasingly began to commercialize, leading to the steady erosion of rural autonomy in favor of reliance on a combination of markets and state intervention through subsidies and support programs (Tutino 2007). Mexico’s “economic miracle” eventually collapsed in the wake of the 1982 debt crisis. President Miguel de la Madrid was forced to accept the terms of an IMF-backed structural adjustment policy, including trade liberalization and budget austerity. Mexico joined the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade in 1986, which obligated tariff reductions on most traded goods, and generally began to transform the state along neoliberal lines (Meyer 2007).

Beginning with the regime of Carlos Salinas in 1988, and continuing through the administration of Ernesto Zedillo in the late 1990s, a new generation of U.S.-trained economists and technocrats was put in charge of key government posts, and Mexico embarked on a path of continued trade liberalization, privatization, and the downsizing of the state (Demmers 2001; O’Toole 2003; Bizberg 2005; Soederberg 2005). The long-standing dominance of the Partido Revolucionario Institucional (PRI) as Mexico’s ruling party was ended by the business-friendly Partido Acción Nacional (PAN) with the election of Vicente Fox as president in the year 2000. Fox and his successor, Felipe Calderón, have continued to promote the neoliberalization of the Mexican state and economy. The sum total of these reforms was to dramatically transform the corporatist character of state–society relations in Mexico and to develop new ways of governing subjects. In agricultural regions such as the Totonacapan, the state enacted a series of reforms that articulated new forms of governmental rationality in rural Mexico, transforming production, citizenship, and subjectivity.

Land Tenure

The most profound change, at least in terms of redefining the nature of rural society, was the now infamous repeal of Article 27 of the Mexican constitution, which sought to transform Mexico’s land tenure system. The target of the reforms was the ejido system of communal lands bequeathed by the Mexican Revolution, as well as the historic commitment to land redistribution that the ejido represented. Through the Program for the Certification of Rights to Ejido Lands (PROCEDE), farmers were encouraged to submit to a privatization process involving the parcelization and titling of land. The ostensible goal of the reforms was to modernize rural agriculture by stimulating a market for land sales and by replacing government support for agriculture with investment by commercial banks and private investors. “According to its proponents,” noted Assies (2008), the repeal “freed the ejidatarios from the dead hand of state ‘paternalism’ and would help to overcome the crisis in
the sector through increased tenure security, an inflow of capital, increased productivity and the promotion of entrepreneurial forms of organization” (52).

For the most part, however, PROCEDE has failed to spur widespread changes in rural land use patterns in Mexico. Although the majority of rural ejidos have entered into the process of certification, participation has been uneven, and in general PROCEDE has failed to induce the increased capitalization or dynamic rural land market that its architects envisioned (Haenn 2006; Ita 2006; Perramond 2008). In the Totonacapan, most ejido communities have gone through the PROCEDE process and acquired title, but we found little evidence of widespread land sales, and cultivation practices at the community level appear to have remained more or less consistent with prior practice.

The repeal of Article 27 has, however, served to articulate a new form of individual sovereignty and a new relationship of governmentality between the state and the rural producer. “Peasants,” Carlos Salinas asserted in announcing the repeal, “should be the subjects, and not the objects, of change” (cited in Hilbert 1997, 132). Under this new regime of peasant subjectivity, “liberty’ now was to strengthen individual property rights over the ejidatario’s parcel and therefore to deconstruct collective decision-making, in line with neoliberal thinking” (Assies 2008, 49). This new regime of governmentality can be viewed as the reform’s principal achievement, and served as the basis from which significant changes in agricultural policy would be initiated.

Transforming the Economy

Following on the heels of land tenure reform, the Mexican government enacted a series of policy changes that altered the fortunes of peasant agriculture in regions such as the Totonacapan. The most highly visible of these changes was the signing of the North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), which opened rural producers to competition from U.S. agricultural imports. This was accompanied, during the 1990s, by the rollback, and eventual elimination, of crop subsidies, state marketing boards, agricultural credit agencies, and commodity price support programs (Pastor and Wise 1997; Yunez-Naude 2003). These broad forms of state intervention were replaced by more targeted programs, focused on the individual producer and intended to support the commercialization and increased productivity of the rural sector. One of the more prominent such programs is Alianza Para El Campo, which has been described as “a set of programs designed to support farmers with productive potential in an open economy” (Yunez-Naude and Barceinas Paredes 2006, 219).

The upshot of these changes was to promote entrepreneurialism over paternalism in the rural sector, and to shift government resources toward competitive producers and away from “inefficient” peasant smallholders. In general, agricultural producers in the Totonacapan fall into the latter category, and few have been able to take advantage of the government’s new approach. The majority of small producers lack the capital or inexpensive credit necessary for inputs or infrastructure improvements and do not have the technical expertise or social connections needed to exploit new market opportunities (Eakin 2005). One Papantla small farmer explained, “I think that NAFTA is good, but for those who have money, those who monopolize everything. They can commercialize, but I don’t think that a rural farmer can commercialize. It’s a NAFTA for the rich, the monopolies” (Ejidatario, La Campaña, Papantla).

At the same time, the decline of commodity support programs and the dismantling of state marketing agencies have resulted in greater price instability, uncertain markets, and increased risk, such that many farmers have been forced to abandon their traditional crops. This is particularly true of sugar, coffee, and tobacco, all of which had significant state support to ensure some degree of farmer success. Here is how the former head of the regional tobacco growers’ association described the privatization of Tabamex, the parastatal intermediary between growers and cigarette companies:

Under Tabamex they gave us everything—credit, subsidies, exporting, it was profitable. We made a living for many years from that. . . . [Then] the government left us. TADES A2 came in and was like, “I control tobacco now, this is how it will be, these are my conditions.” They want us to grow for a low price . . . and in the end you earn very little. I finally left it . . . it wasn’t viable for us. . . . That is why people emigrate to the U.S. (San Marcos, Papantla)

The onset of neoliberal restructuring thus represents a period of transition for many communities in the region, as farmers shifted away from the production of long-standing state-supported cash crops and toward new crops aimed at serving local and regional markets, particularly oranges and other citrus fruits but also bananas, papayas, lychees, allspice, and corn husks.

This shift toward new agricultural commodities, however, has not guaranteed prosperity for the majority of the region’s farmers, for a number of interconnected reasons. Chief among these is price instability. Most
of the farmers we interviewed felt that the lack of a fair and stable price for their product was the single biggest reason for their lack of success. Many spoke of dramatic price fluctuations: oranges ranging from 600 to 3,000 pesos per ton; limes selling for 500 pesos per crate crashing to 30 pesos; maize prices ranging from 60 centavos to 3.50 pesos per kilo; vanilla that used to sell for 1,000 pesos per kilo now down to 20 pesos. Here is how one local official put it:

This is a citrus area, we produce a lot of citrus, oranges. The root of our problem here is that the prices for the products collapsed. It was a better option to go overseas. Then, the orchard owners began to decrease their production bit by bit, to the point where they even left the fruit to spoil in the orchards . . . the amount that farmers earn from their produce is not enough to able to maintain the orchard. . . . This is why the children of the citrus growers have gone to Reynosa, to the U.S. (Subagente municipal, Rio Verde, Tecolutla)

At least part of the reason for these shifts is the enhanced role of intermediaries (coyotes) in the purchase, transport, and marketing of crops. “Free trade doesn’t enter directly,” says one farmer, “it comes via coyotes” (Ejido treasurer, San Marcos, Papantla). Famers who lack the means to transport their crop to market, or who do not possess the specialized knowledges and social connections on which marketing and sales arrangements depend, have no choice but to sell their product to a coyote, often at a low “take-it-or-leave-it” price. Citrus fruits, in particular, might then be stored for several months until prices are more favorable, with the coyote or warehouse reaping the profit. A low and unstable market price also acts as a disincentive to make long-term investments or capital improvements that might be necessary for farmers to achieve success in a newly competitive atmosphere.

For those producers who are unable to compete in this new entrepreneurial environment, the Mexican government continues to provide some measure of assistance but now in the form of direct subsidies to individual farmers. The PROCAMPO program delivers payments to farmers of basic grains based on cultivated area. Its client is the individual subsistence cultivator, rather than the ejido or community, thus reinforcing the individualist logic of neoliberal governmentality. As of 2006, PROCAMPO subsidies were paid to around 3 million producers in Mexico on 14 million hectares of land (Yunez-Naude and Barceinas Paredes 2006). In Veracruz, most of the people we spoke to described PROCAMPO as more of a poverty-reduction scheme than a rural development strategy, a palliative in the face of declining state support. As a local clergyman put it to us:

To me, PROCAMPO seems like an aspirin, it does nothing more than soothe the pain, it doesn’t promote development. . . . PROCAMPO is supposedly for the countryside, but here it goes for other spending . . . the farmer comes, receives the money and says “Let’s go drinking” or spends it on women. So much for PROCAMPO! Around here, there are cantinas with the name PROCAMPO! (San Marcos, Papantla)

Cultivating the Neoliberal Subject

Alongside these changes to land tenure and agricultural policy, the state also transformed rural development and welfare programs. The centerpiece of the new strategy was Progresa (later renamed Oportunidades), a direct transfer program that provides cash payments to women in rural areas on the condition that they ensure that their children attend school, receive proper nutrition, and make regular visits to a health clinic (Luccisano 2004; Yunez-Naude and Barceinas Paredes 2006). The stated goal of the program was to improve “human capital formation” and it was pitched in the familiar neoliberal language of freedom and responsibility. As the program’s primary architect, Santiago Levy (2006), has written, “Progresa-Oportunidades was built . . . on the recognition that poor households had to play a more substantive role in overcoming their circumstances; to that end, households were being given both the increased freedom with regard to how they spent their income transfers and greater responsibility for taking the actions required to obtain the transfers” (17).

The Oportunidades program has been lauded by both the World Bank and the Interamerican Development Bank as a successful poverty-alleviation scheme and has served as a model for similar conditional cash transfer programs elsewhere (Bradshaw 2008; Peck and Theodore 2010). In Veracruz, more than half a million families receive support from Oportunidades, the highest number of any Mexican state. In interviews, many Totonacapan residents described the program as beneficial, but also insufficient. “Oportunidades is not enough,” declared one farmer, whose eldest son was working in Texas, “the children continue in school and they need food, clothing and everything else . . . this is what we use remittances for” (Small farmer, Valle Santiago, Papantla). An educator in another community indicated that the program was perhaps not reaching
its potential. “More than 50 percent of our students receive funds from Oportunidades,” he noted, “it’s a good program, but sometimes parents don’t take advantage of it . . . they are just waiting for funds or for remittances to arrive from the United States” (Rio Verde, Tecolutla).

We can see in this sentiment an essentially neoliberal shift away from an assumption of government responsibility and toward a conception of more active citizenship, where parents are faulted for “just waiting.” Along with concomitant changes to land tenure and agrarian policy, the new welfare approach is representative of a new conception of development and social justice in Mexico, no longer centered on the collective claims of the Mexican Revolution and instead oriented around individual, entrepreneurial—which is to say responsible—conduct (Walker et al. 2008). One result is that, as far as rural policymakers are concerned, much of the responsibility for the success and failure of rural Mexico can be placed on rural dwellers themselves. Here is how one government official described the issue:

It’s not true if a farmer says we aren’t helping. We give assistance for their child in primary school, we give for their child in secondary school, we give Oportunidades, we give them PROCAMPO, we give them Alianza Para el Campo . . . all of these resources are going to the community. If a community can’t make it, it’s because they don’t want to. . . . It’s a lie when an agricultural producer says there are no programs available . . . the only thing we don’t provide is a hammock so he can lay down and rest! This is what the countryside has been reduced to. (Papantla)

This type of image, of a rural Mexico that is dependent and unmotivated, has helped to justify the neoliberal restructuring of the past two decades, experienced by small farmers in the Totonacapan not only as a new set of policies and programs or changing market conditions; it also represents a new relationship between the state and rural dwellers, a new form of economic subjectivity. As Miller and Rose (2008) reminded us:

For neo-liberalism the political subject is less a social citizen with powers and obligations deriving from membership of a collective body than an individual whose citizenship is active. This citizenship is to be manifested not in the receipt of public largesse, but in the energetic pursuit of personal fulfillment and the incessant calculations that are to enable this to be achieved. (82)

It is here, however, where we believe that neoliberal citizenship deserves more careful scrutiny. For the dictates of an “active citizenship” can be pursued in different ways, some of which might enhance the neoliberal vision of individual agency, whereas others suggest a more collective understanding of the social. In the Totonacapan, this negotiation of neoliberal subjectivity is bound up with the “energetic pursuit of personal fulfillment” through the increasing participation of the region’s residents in circuits of transnational migration.

Transnational Migration in the Totonacapan

Agricultural policy has been tied to NAFTA, and so PRO-CAMPO and Alianza Para el Campo were created. This wasn’t just giving money to the producer, it was to purchase tools and equipment in order to increase productivity. This is what we have seen from the 90s onward. But it was never going to help. Because around the same time, the state of Veracruz began to experience migration, which has continued to accelerate notwithstanding these programs. The government was focused on these rural development policies, but there has not been a single program to reduce the process of migration. (State government official, Xalapa)

During the course of our fieldwork, it became evident that migration has become a widespread phenomenon throughout the Totonacapan over the past twenty years or so. Most of the initial migrants moved to destinations within Mexico, particularly regional urban centers such as Veracruz and Puebla, and cities such as Reynosa and Ciudad Juarez in the northern maquiladora zone. Beginning in the early to mid-1990s, however, job seekers began crossing into the United States, and international migration is now the norm in many communities. Migration, then, has grown and developed concomitantly with the neoliberalization of the Mexican countryside and can be viewed at least in part as a response to the challenges faced by farm families in the new policy environment. Indeed, when asked to explain the growth in outmigration from the region, most residents point matter-of-factly to the lack of available employment. “I believe that the government has forgotten the rural areas,” said one local famer, “the government has placed its bet on industry, but has abandoned the countryside.” Another young migrant explained:

I lived with my family at the time, I was young. I went with the same “American Dream” that all Mexicans have. I wanted to help my younger siblings. I wanted to help my family, because at that time we were frustrated because there weren’t enough jobs. (Villa Madero, Papantla)

This perceived option in the face of economic decline and insecurity means that there is now an
established culture of migration within the region, one that permeates local economic understandings and social institutions (Kandel and Massey 2002; Cohen 2004b).

This does not mean, however, that the migration experience is the same throughout the region. Both the prevalence of migration and its community-level impacts vary significantly according to, as we have suggested, the economic and cultural characteristics of individual communities and the ways in which neoliberal change has been understood and negotiated. In particular, we want to explore a tension between the neoliberal sensibilities of responsible subject-formation and longstanding networks of collective labor and mutual regard that continue to shape life in rural Totonacapan. The ways in which such tensions are managed in any given community are tied to the extent to which migration is viewed chiefly as a household issue or whether it is folded into a larger vision of community identity and change.

Reactionary Migration

Viewed from one perspective, the decision to migrate can be taken as a quintessential example of neoliberalism’s individualized, active citizenship, an outcome of the “incessant calculations” of current and future prospects for personal advancement. In the Totonacapan, this is particularly evident among the younger generation, for whom the aspirations and responsibilities of adulthood are now inseparable from the risks and opportunities of migration. In our work with students, we found that this reality permeates local schools in the region. Migration is viewed as a rite of passage, and by the time many students reach high school, they have already made their decision to leave. Stories are circulated about success, failure, or tragedy, as the transnational biographies of migrants and their families are woven into the common lore of the local community:

[Those who are currently in the United States] are recent migrants who are restless and want a better life . . . they go with the idea of “The American Dream” . . . many families have done well, those who know how to make good use of their earnings, who have used them to build a house, buy a piece of land. But the majority have not made good use . . . it has made very little difference in the well-being of their family. And some remain there to live and leave their families here abandoned. (Subagente municipal, Rio Verde, Tecolutla)

Hiding behind the American dream, and its promise of progress and modernity, is a much less rosy reality, one that has been documented by a number of migration scholars (Mahler 1995; Malkin 2004; Gutiérrez Nájera 2009). In the Totonacapan, however, the negative and dangerous dimensions of transnational lives are often filtered out, in favor of a more romanticized and appealing narrative of self-sufficiency and machismo:

Migrants are suffering over there. They have difficult jobs, hiding from la migra, living in bad conditions, eating bad food. But they come back here to Mexico and it’s like “No, we’re doing fine, lots of money, lots of dollars.” And so people are deluded, and they head off to the U.S. thinking they’re going to live next to the White House! . . . Sometimes we fool ourselves. (Educator, Rio Verde, Tecolutla)

Within this culture of migration, we can glimpse a kind of entrepreneurialism that is consistent with neoliberal citizenship. For the most part, the decision to migrate is an individual or household affair, the costs and benefits of which are also borne independently, and hence unevenly. For many individual households, migrant savings and remittances are vital to the household economy and are used for general subsistence, basic necessities, or school supplies (Goldring 2004; Airola 2007). Frequently, money earned in the United States is earmarked for new or improved housing, and many young migrants leave with the intention of remaining just long enough to build their parents a new home or to establish a nest egg with which to begin their own household. There are also cases in which migrant earnings are used for conspicuous forms of consumption, from flashy clothing to a new truck, the now-iconic indicator of a successful migration experience throughout rural Mexico. “Yes, [migration] has improved the economy in the community,” explains one local mayor:

Before, for example, if you lived here your husband would not have money to buy a stereo. Now, if your children or your husband are in the United States, you have a TV, video, DVD—things have modernized. (Agente municipal, San Marcos, Papantla)

Within this landscape of personal choice and household modernization, few community leaders felt that this flow of money had led to any collective benefit. As one local ejido member put it:

There has been no impact here to the community. As I told you, people have gone only with the idea of improving their personal quality of life, but they haven’t contributed anything to the community. They haven’t brought a single benefit. (Ejidatario, Rio Verde, Tecolutla)
Another laments, “The use of remittances is like putting money into a bag with a hole in it” (Educator, Rio Verde, Tecolutla).

It should be noted here that, for many of our study participants, there is nothing particularly surprising or lamentable about this. Migration is understood to be a risky and difficult venture, and many expressed a belief that it would be unreasonable to expect migrants to contribute their earnings toward anything beyond their immediate household needs. When asked whether remittances should be used for the betterment of the community, one respondent told us:

No, nobody talks this way. I mean, they have their daily job over there, they earn a bit more because the money is worth more over here. But, no one ever says, “I’ll send a little extra for the community.” (Agente municipal, Paraiso, Papantla)

Ideas about migration, then, seem to assume an essentially neoliberal construction of subjectivity and individual self-responsibility, one consistent with the self-reliant vision of civil society crafted by the Mexican state over the past decade: “The community here is very independent,” asserts one local official, “that is the nature of our country—this is not socialism! Here, each person works for their own well-being, for their family” (Engineer, Xicotlapa, Filomeno Mata).

At the same time, however, the negative aspects of migration were widely noted by the Totonacapan residents with whom we spoke. Indeed, in many communities, migration has been profoundly disruptive, tearing at the fabric of family and community. Migration trips initially considered temporary can drag on for many years or become permanent, leaving families in Mexico without a means of support. Many migrants who end up settling in the United States might eventually reduce or halt the flow of remittances to parents, spouses, or other family members (Jones 2009). And, migrant deaths at the border or in the United States are not unknown in the region. Returning migrants might also bring with them unwelcome change, ranging from HIV/AIDS and drug abuse to gang activity and drug trafficking (R. Smith 2006; Fitzgerald 2009).

Alongside the general acceptance of neoliberal individuality, then, we also came across more critical evaluations, which suggest that the relations between individual and collective forms of action and responsibility are very much open to contestation in rural Mexico. Consider the perspective, for example, of the director of a local secondary school:

You can tell the migrants by their clothing and their way of acting, in the style that they pick up over there, the type of speech, their behavior. They are irresponsible! The first thing that a Mexican bracero does is to work and then return with their pick-up truck, even though there may be nothing to eat in the house. It’s an attitude. And then, they oppose all of the projects and the good things about the community, because I don’t think they feel they’re from here or from there. (Educator, San Marcos, Papantla)

The ideal of responsibility being invoked here is quite different and is one that we encountered during a number of interviews. For many observers, there is a line to be drawn between self-reliance and a self-indulgent, consumer-driven form of individualism. If the former is an understandable reaction to economic insecurity, the latter is viewed as selfish behavior, which places individual wishes and desires above the needs of the family or wider community. The same educator continues:

It’s cultural. . . . Mexico is a capitalist country. In Mexico, you have freedom if you are skilled and intelligent, if you work. . . . That’s what I mean by individualism. If I am successful, and I am getting rich, it doesn’t matter if I abandon or humiliate or injure my workers or my family. Because I am getting rich. This is what we have in Mexico today. And we can see it every day, in telenovelas, in movies, in daily life. It’s a question of culture, a question of the system in which we’re living. (Educator, San Marcos, Papantla)

This heightened sense of possessive individualism has been noted in studies of rural change in other regions of Mexico. Lewis, for example, in her examination of the Costa Chica region of Guerrero and Oaxaca, noted that “respect and reciprocity have given way to self-centeredness, to a less ‘communal’ set of values” (2006, 822). Similar patterns, oriented around conspicuous forms of consumption, have been identified in Tlaxcala and Puebla states (Rothstein 2005; Lee 2008).

Another commonly articulated complaint in Veracruz has to do with the refusal on the part of some recent migrants to participate in projects requiring collective labor, known in this part of Mexico as faena:

In the past . . . nobody said no. But now . . . this tradition has changed, progress had made us change our ideas. Now nobody wants to participate in faenas or volunteer for the community. There is no respect for the laws or the authorities, and now they don’t even participate in traditional festivals. (Agente municipal, San Marcos, Papantla)

In many communities, then, there is an evident tension between neoliberal understandings of individual
self-realization and long-standing practices of collective labor and communal obligation (cf. Muttersbaugh 2002; Cohen 2004a; Farr 2006). The outcomes of this negotiation of subjectivity are locally variant, but one result, we would suggest, has been to expose in stark fashion some of the consequences of neoliberal restructuring in Veracruz.

As we traveled throughout the region, we heard a common refrain that economic conditions for rural dwellers, particularly farmers, had changed for the worse. Few were apt to label this change “neoliberalism,” but many—including most local leaders and a good many farmers—believed that government policy shifts and the promotion of free trade had played a large role in their declining fortunes. As one local Totonac advocate described it:

[President Fox] speaks of the macro-economy, a strong peso. But he doesn’t say that it’s in the hands of a few Mexicans, and the great majority have nothing. It’s a macro-economy of the few. This is the problem, each time there are fewer people with money, and the people with money every day have more. This is proven by the fact that salaries have not increased, have not improved the well-being of the workers. This is a contradiction within NAFTA, effectively there are more products, and now agricultural products are going to have more competition, because we have an abandoned countryside. (Espinosa, Papantla)

In more or less specific terms, these concerns were repeated throughout the Totonacapan, and it is at least in part the phenomenon of migration, we would suggest, that has brought these issues to the fore. The culture of migration in the region has brought with it a much more heightened sense of national and regional disparities, and of shifts in government support away from rural agriculture in favor of the agribusiness and maquiladora zones farther north. And, time spent in the United States has provided many a migrant with first-hand accounts of the contradiction between the free movement of goods and the anxieties and hostilities associated with the transnational movement of labor. “Along with the NAFTA for goods and services,” states one government official, “we need a treaty for people” (State government official, Xalapa).

If migration can be viewed from one perspective, then, as a safety valve for rural disaffection, it can be seen from another as a process that sheds light on the ambiguities and the material inequities of neoliberal restructuring. The neoliberal subjectification of the Mexican rural countryside, in other words, might act to repoliticize neoliberal discourse and to thereby offer a nascent critique of its idealized form of economic subjectivity. This is particularly true of communities that have maintained a strong sense of indigenous identity and tradition and a more collective vision when it comes to questions of work and labor, on the one hand, and responsibility, on the other.

Managed Migration: Maintaining the Common

The tensions between traditional cultural values and the entrepreneurial individualism of neoliberal subjectivity are particularly evident among the indigenous communities of the Totonacapan. Today, even the most remote areas of the region have been exposed to recent forces of modernization, both culturally and materially:

If you go to any indigenous community . . . they have access to communication and are informed . . . and this is the panorama that young people will see when they get on the Internet . . . when they see things on television. . . . Some communities have seen the rapid arrival of services, roads, schools, health clinics. All of this acts to change the mentality of the younger generation . . . it changes their way of looking at things. (Government official, Papantla)

Migration can be seen as both cause and consequence of such changes, particularly among the younger generation, who face new pressures and opportunities, not only for material rewards but also personal growth. A local Totonac official explains the attitude:

You shouldn’t stay stuck here, because being rooted in tradition means poverty and low status in Mexican society. Remaining in your community isolated, forgotten, you aren’t a first-class Mexican . . . you are a third-class Mexican citizen because you’re from a rural area and you have to get out in order to realize that “other self.” (Totonac government official, Papantla)

In such a context, what is at issue in many communities is the extent to which, and the means by which, migration might be managed or channeled toward collective benefit, rather than becoming a disruptive force as in many neighboring towns.

During our interviews, respondents often described the cultural traditions of Totonac villages as more united, characterized by solidarity and a concern for collective well-being. As one resident of a nonindigenous community told us:

Something that distinguishes the indigenous areas is the integration of the communities. They are very unified—in religious questions, cultural and social issues, their values. In the way they help each other, their solidarity. To be
united, the cooperative spirit that occurs, this is different. (Clergyman, San Marcos, Papantla)

Another community leader, from a Totonac village where few still speak the language, contrasted his community with those of the highland sierra:

I would say that the difference between communities depends a lot on the kind of development they have. There are communities where a high proportion still speaks Totonaco . . . their customs are still basically preserved, like the way that they are organized for the well-being of their community . . . they have this hierarchy, this respect. There are communities where people still know these things, where they are more aware of them. (Agente municipal, Allende, Papantla)

In a number of communities, this more collective vision of the social appears to have helped to mitigate some of the more disruptive impacts of migration. We were told by community leaders in the highlands that fewer residents are engaged in migration, their trips tend to be shorter in length and duration, and those who are away are more likely to maintain closer contact with their families back home.

Many people go with the notion that they will earn money and then return. Couples remain in constant communication. Children may not see their father, but they are working to make sure that the nuclear family doesn’t disintegrate. (Agente municipal, Mecatitlán, Cuauhtitlán)

In addition, a portion of migrant remittances in indigenous communities is more likely to be channeled toward a collective project, such as the annual festival for the local patron saint or the building of a school or road. Collective labor—described by one interview respondent as “mutual aid”—has been a particularly important form of social organization and is mobilized for these kinds of community projects or for planting, harvesting, and housing construction. One interview respondent notes the contrast between his community and a Mestizo community with a high degree of migration:

Here we have progressed because if you’re going to do a project like a school, soccer fields, roads—all of the people agree, and go to work when called upon by the authorities. There, they don’t want to work, and if they do work, it’s for their own pocket! (Subagent municipal, La Campana, Papantla)

In such communities, then, migration is oriented differently with respect to neoliberalism. As in the more reactive communities, we can say that the decision to migrate has been a response to neoliberal policy changes and also facilitated by the networks and changing worldview brought about by neoliberal change. In the context of a wider community vision, however, migration has also become a means to mitigate some of the possible negative consequences of a fully neoliberalized community culture:

The village has not changed. Migration has not changed the young people—they leave, send money, and return. They only go to get money and then they come back to work the land. . . . I don’t think that we are going to lose our traditions. They will continue because there are many people who believe in their community . . . who like what we have, the customs, the traditions . . . the young people grow up, but they don’t change. No, there is always harmony with the culture of the community. (Totonac community leader, Ixtaltepec, Cuauhtitlan)

Such is not, we should point out, universally the case. Indeed, we heard stories of Totonac communities struggling with rapid social change, sparked in part by migration and resulting in high rates of alcoholism, health problems, and domestic violence. As another Totonac official said to us:

We worry . . . when the young people return to their communities, we can see that they know how to have their wild parties . . . the example is the city where they are doing such things. Because before, they didn’t fight, they didn’t know that much about drugs . . . so, when they return to the village it’s different. So, we are concerned. (Ixtaltepec, Cuauhtitlan)

In the context of such challenges, it is significant, we believe, that the collective vision associated with traditional Totonac cultural values has become for many, including nonindigenous residents of the region, a kind of idealized model of social organization. During many of our interviews, people spoke of indigenous communities as more unified, and then lamented the lack of unity characterizing their own community:

[Indigenous communities] are united and they have a high level of cooperation. This is something unique. Around here, for example . . . there is a desire to get things done, but it isn’t possible, because there is no sense of collective ownership. . . . Here, well, there is individualism—each one fights for himself. That is their right, but I feel that we need, ideally, some kind of effort to integrate ourselves. (Clergyman, San Marcos, Papantla)

We can see here the way that indigenous collective identity acts as an ideal against which to make sense of the inability of some local communities to manage the flux of change in the Totonacapan. Moreover, it
is neoliberal subjectivity itself that is at least in part to blame. In other words, the neoliberal culture of individualism, reinforced by migration as the most immediate response to rural decline, has not only failed to deliver a more entrepreneurial countryside but has also eroded the collective organizational capacity through which the consequences of rural restructuring might be managed. Once again, we can see how the cultural changes associated with migration have the potential to lay bare the impacts of neoliberalization, and to illuminate its incompatibility with long-term rural prosperity in Mexico.

Conclusion: For an Ethical Critique of Neoliberalism

To speak of neoliberalization in the Totonacapan is to invoke a complex set of social and economic changes, the specific outcomes of which are dependent on locally specific histories and geographies. As we have suggested, most of the region’s residents have experienced neoliberal change primarily in the form of decreased government assistance and greater price instability for their cash crops. There is a palpable sense that things have become more difficult and less secure than in years past, that modernity’s enduring promise of a brighter future, once backed by the Mexican state, is no longer guaranteed. In this context, a culture of U.S.-bound migration has become an indelible feature of daily life in the Totonacapan. Migrants—and their journeys and absences, their remittances and returns—are a widespread and taken-for-granted dimension of household strategies, community organization, and regional economic development in Veracruz. As in other regions of Mexico, widespread migration in the Totonacapan has proven at times to be profoundly disruptive but can also provide an economic lifeline in a time of diminishing opportunity.

Our consideration of these changes brings to the fore a number of issues that have implications for geographical understandings of neoliberalization, migration, and ethics. First, we have highlighted some of the ways in which neoliberalism is filtered through the material and cultural dimensions of everyday lived experience in Mexico. In giving voice to those who are affected by neoliberal policy shifts, and who are negotiating new ideas about citizenship and society, our work adds an empirical richness to discussions that can tend to be oriented around theoretical abstractions. In so doing, we hope to have also illustrated the value of regional ethnography in elucidating the local contours of neoliberal change.

Second, to contemporary considerations of neoliberalism’s path dependence and uneven regulatory development, we have added a focus on the uneven landscapes of economic subjectivity and ethics. In Mexico, neoliberalism has entailed not only a set of contested policy changes but also a refashioning of the national subject, as the collective ethos of Mexican revolutionary nationalism has given way to a neoliberal subjectivity emphasizing the individual and economic dimensions of citizenship and responsibility (Martin 2005). Brown’s depiction of neoliberal subjectivity aptly captures this:

Neo-liberalism normatively constructs and interpellates individuals as entrepreneurial actors in every sphere of life. It figures individuals as rational, calculating creatures whose moral autonomy is measured by their capacity for “self-care”—the ability to provide for their own needs and service their own ambitions. (W. Brown 2003, para. 15)

But it is here, third, that we can highlight the ways in which neoliberalism in Mexico intersects with transnational migration to produce a variegated landscape of change. If the new neoliberal subjectivity was supposed to turn peasants into self-made entrepreneurs, it has done so largely through the transnationalization of household and community strategies. It is through the “rational and calculating” decisions of the migrant that many households and communities are “servicing their ambitions” toward the future. This dynamic provides a good illustration of the way in which neoliberal change is not an imposition, but a negotiation, “always in conversation with other cultural formations . . . [and] articulated along with other political projects” (England and Ward 2007a, 255).

The results of this neoliberal conversation in Veracruz are locally variant. On the one hand, migration can be viewed as fully consistent with neoliberal forms of subjectivity. In many communities, it is a household matter, and few harbor expectations that remittances should be used for anything other than personal benefit. The stories and examples of successful journeys work to buttress this subject position, as returning migrants express their unique transnational personality: New styles of dress or music, the deliberate use of English, and an identifiable manner of comportment all attest to the entrepreneurial self-fashioning of migrant identity. Migrant performance in this way serves as a
kind of transmission belt for new forms of neoliberal subjectivity. Yet this fails to tell the whole story, for, as Bondi (2005) put it, “neoliberal subjectivity does not inevitably generate subjects oriented solely to the narcissistic gratification of individual desires via market opportunities” (499). In certain communities of the Totonacapan, neoliberalism is filtered through existing forms of community and reciprocity, leading to a more collective vision regarding the role of migration.

Finally, then, we would point to the ways in which the intersection of neoliberalism and migration is highlighting some of the contradictions of neoliberal discourse in Mexico and opening space for both critique and the development of possible alternatives. As Lawson (2000) has noted, “Migrancy creates especially fluid forms of subject position, a ‘state of between-ness’ in which migrants have a critical edge due to the very disruptions and perspectives that migration produces” (174). Neoliberalization and migration are leading to a recomposition of the social within the Totonacapan, as well as a redefinition of agency and responsibility. Although the outcomes of this process are yet to be determined, a strategic and optimistic reading would point toward a possible imminent critique of neoliberal policy arising from the cosmopolitan experience of migrant subjectivity. In the worst-case scenario, as we have recounted, migration has been largely unregulated and has led to widespread dissatisfaction with the laissez-faire ethics of individual responsibility. In the more hopeful scenario, a sensibility of solidarity or collective well-being has served to buffer communities from the worst excesses of neoliberalization. In either case, it seems to us, the phenomenon of migration has become the disruptive supplement to Mexico’s scripted entry into neoliberal modernity, the repressed trace of sociality returning to haunt the neoliberal dreams of market rationality and ethical individualism.

As it travels across borders and cultural contexts, neoliberalism is proving to be not only variant, but also unstable. For those who believe, as we do, that there are good reasons to oppose the narrow ethical worldview promulgated by neoliberal sensibilities, it is important to be attentive to the potential for ethical and political openings. As such, the negotiations of rural change taking place in areas such as the Totonacapan could have much to tell us about the now-obvious flaws of neoliberalism and about the prospects for developing alternatives based in a wider sense of collective responsibility.
