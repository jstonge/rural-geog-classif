Farm Size and Land Use Pattern Changes in Postsocialist Poland*

Peter Dannenberg
Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin

Tobias Kuemmerle
University of Wisconsin–Madison and Humboldt-Universität zu Berlin

The postsocialist transition resulted in widespread structural change in Polish agriculture, but fine-scale assessments of how these changes affected farming and rural landscapes are scarce. This article examines changes in farm sizes and land use patterns after the breakdown of socialism in Poland using micro-census data and satellite images. Our study area is Pyrzyce County. The analysis indicates a polarization of the farm size distribution (e.g., medium-sized farms disappeared) as well as marked changes in land use patterns (e.g., increasing field sizes and field aggregation). Interviews with farmers and stakeholders suggest that this shift can be explained by decreasing competitiveness of medium-sized farms in the emerging new agricultural value chain, which forced farmers to increase and intensify production or to downsize their farms, especially when the preference was for other occupations. Key Words: Central and Eastern Europe, Landsat, postsocialist transition, remote sensing, value chain.

The fall of the Iron Curtain in 1989 resulted in rapid and drastic changes in Central and Eastern Europe’s political, societal, and economic structures, which strongly
affected the region’s farming sectors (Lerman, Csaki, and Feder 2004; Müller et al. 2009). Poland is a particularly interesting case. Contrary to most other former socialist countries, Poland’s farming sector was never fully collectivized. Before 1989, only 18 percent of Poland’s agricultural land was state managed and most farmland was held by small, private farms with low labor productivity and poor returns (Chaplin, Davidova, and Gorton 2005). Poland’s farmers were embedded in a state-controlled food system, but most farmers also produced for themselves or for local markets. Overall, the majority of Polish rural areas were relatively undeveloped and agricultural production was highly fragmented (Adamowicz 1996; Munroe 2001; Dannenberg 2007).

The situation changed fundamentally after 1989. Prices were liberalized, state support diminished, and markets and trade agreements within the former socialist bloc disappeared. Changing economic conditions in the postsocialist period increasingly presented Poland’s farmers with new markets, internationalization of trade, and increased international competition. The liberalization also led to new organizational and governance structures of agricultural value chains and an increased demand for standardized large-scale production. This had a tremendous effect on the farming sector at the very beginning of these value chains, and overall triggered fundamental structural change (Munroe 2001; Dannenberg and Kulke 2005). To stay competitive under these new conditions, Polish farmers have tried to intensify land use or to increase farm sizes. Furthermore, farmers have tried to supplement their income with off-farm employment and job diversification (Chaplin, Davidova, and Gorton 2005). These developments likely affected farm numbers and sizes and led to land use pattern changes in Poland (Lerman, Csaki, and Feder 2004).

Postsocialist structural change in farming has potentially far-reaching social and environmental consequences. For example, structural change could result in increased rural unemployment and poverty, and indeed Poland’s agricultural workforce declined by 51 percent between 2000 and 2004 (Kaczmarek 2000; Minrol 2005). Likewise, land use pattern changes due to structural change (e.g., the aggregation of small fields into bigger ones) threaten Poland’s traditional cultural landscapes that are rich in biodiversity, have high aesthetic value, and bear considerable recreation potential (Palang et al. 2006). Despite these consequences, our understanding of structural change in Poland, and almost all of Central and Eastern Europe, is still relatively general.

One reason for this is that most studies to date relied on highly aggregated census data, often gathered at the national, regional (wojewodship), or county (powiat) level. Such data can give important insights into general trends in postsocialist structural change in Poland such as the privatization of the farming sector (Lerman, Csaki, and Feder 2004), the role of small-scale farming (Glebocki 2000), agricultural factor markets (Cimpoies and Baltag 2004), optimal farm sizes in Poland’s postsocialist agricultural sector (Munroe 2001; Sabates-Wheeler 2002; Dannenberg and Kulke 2005), and the fate of Poland’s rural landscapes during transition (Glebocki 2000). However, a detailed understanding of how changes in markets and agricultural value chains affected the livelihood of Polish farmers and the fate of rural landscapes in Poland requires fine-scale assessments of structural change.

Recently, multitemporal agricultural microcensus data have become available in Poland (Glowny Urzad Statystyczny [GUS] 2007); these data have the potential to offer new insights on structural change at the local level. A few studies used these data in country-wide assessments, generally finding a positive connection between farm income and farm size, and a tendency away from medium-sized farms (World Bank 2001; Csaki and Lerman 2002; Gorz and Uliszak 2002). However, analyzing the underlying causes of these trends and assessing their interrelationships requires in-depth studies at fine scales that link microcensus data and detailed field interviews. To our knowledge, no peer-reviewed study has done this, and the potential of the micro-census data to better show how farmers and their farms were affected by postsocialist structural change has so far not been fully explored.

Fine-scale agricultural census data are helpful to understand structural changes in the farming sector, but they lack the spatial dimension to assess how these changes manifested in landscapes. In Poland, full cadastre coverage exists, but about 40 percent of the land is not clearly registered (Prosterman and Rolfes
1999; Harvey 2006). Moreover, cadastre information might not reflect land tenure and land use patterns given that farmers make informal arrangements or manage multiple cadastral entities as a single field (Harvey 2006). An alternative is to map land use patterns using satellite images (Evans et al. 2002; Kuenmerle et al. 2008). Such an approach allows the inclusion of the spatial dimension that agricultural census data lack and thus potentially provides novel and interesting insights into the drivers and consequences of postsocialist structural change in Eastern Europe.

In sum, we were interested in studying postsocialist structural change in Poland using fine-scale statistical data, qualitative interviews, and remote sensing images. Based on a study area in northwest Poland (Pyrzyce County), we investigate the following research questions:

1. How did the structure of the farming sector change in the postsocialist period?
2. What were the effects of structural change on land use patterns in the study region?
3. How can these changes be explained against the background of developments in agricultural value chains after 1989?

Data and Methodology

Study Region
As a study region, we selected Pyrzyce County in northwest Poland (Figure 1), located about 50 km south of Szczecin.1

The area is characterized by mixed production, a domination of private family farms, and an agricultural workforce of 22.6 percent of all workers with social security (well above the Polish average of 17.3 percent; Statistical Office in Szczecin [SOS] 2004). The majority of farmers in Pyrzyce County are professionals (e.g., market-oriented commodity production); subsistence farming is not important, unlike in southern Poland (Glebocki 2000; SOS 2004). Livestock (especially pigs), dairy products, cereals (e.g., wheat, rye, barley, oats, and triticale), potatoes, and sugar beets are major agricultural commodities (SOS 2001).

Pyrzyce County is a typical rural landscape for those Polish areas that were a part of the German Reich before World War II and that were awarded to Poland as war compensation (Bingen 2001). After 1945, the majority of these areas were managed by large state-owned farms, but small-scale family farms persisted or emerged during socialism (Henkel 2004). This was different in those rural areas that were Polish before and after 1945, where state farms were absent during socialism. After 1989, state-owned land in Pyrzyce County was partly privatized. Some was sold to small-scale farmers but most former state lands were bought by a few large, often foreign, farms. The bulk of farms in Pyrzyce County, however, remained small and medium-sized (< 20 ha). This makes Pyrzyce County a particularly interesting case study for assessing postsocialist structural change because the effects on small-scale and large-scale farming can be studied at the same time.

Data Sets Used
To analyze structural change in Pyrzyce County, we acquired indicators from the country-wide micro-censuses of the Polish agricultural sector conducted by the Polish Central Statistical Office (GUS) in 1996 and 2002 (GUS 2007). The agricultural census covered private farms (> 0.1 ha), livestock farms even if they had less than 0.1 ha of agricultural land, and other agricultural holdings farmed by legal persons or units without legal personality (in total \( n = 3,098 \) farms in 2002; \( n = 3,297 \) farms in 1996). We relied on four indicators from the micro-census data: (1) the total number of farms, (2) the distribution of farm sizes according to different farm size groups (by share and number), (3) the number and share of professional full-time farms, and (4) the amount of private farmland and the average size of farmland per farm.

To better understand the underlying causes of farmers’ decisions leading to structural change, we conducted a survey on Pyrzyce County’s farming sector (Dannenberg 2007). The survey was carried out in October 2004 and included thirty-five interviews with local farmers and twenty interviews with local stakeholders (e.g., public and private agricultural services, suppliers, demanders, politicians) working together with farmers in Pyrzyce County or connected to local farmers via agricultural value chains (\( n = 55 \) interviews).2

Analysis of Structural Change in Pyrzyce County

We conducted four different analyses using the micro-census data. First, we analyzed the development of total farm numbers. Second, we assessed the distribution of farm sizes (by share and number). Third, we assessed changes in the number and share of farm households of professional full-time farmers. Last, we examined changes in the amount of total private farmland, average size of farmland per farm, and average farm sizes.

The micro-census data contains nine different size categories. Initial tests suggested that farm size categories can be grouped into the three clusters: small farms (1–7 ha), medium-sized farms (7–20 ha), and large farms (>20 ha). Because nonindividual farms had not been categorized in size groups by GUS in the 1996 data set, we only used the data of the individual farmers (in total \( n = 1,888 \) farms in 2002; \( n = 2,208 \) farms in 1996). We used statistical tests to assess whether differences in the total distributions of farm sizes in 1996 and 2002 as well as differences among the distributions per
farm size group (small, medium-sized, large) are significant.4

To assess land use pattern changes in Pyrzyce County based on our satellite images, we used image segmentation to map field size changes between 1991 and 2000. All nonfarmland was masked5 and we used an image segmentation routine to map individual fields6 for 1991 and 2000. Based on these area-wide land use pattern maps, we calculated the size of each farmland field, summarized field size distributions, and assessed differences between the distributions from 1991 and 2000.7

We used interview data to derive predominant trends in farmer and stakeholder responses. We used this to guide the interpretation of the micro-census and land use pattern changes and to identify the external causes and internal strategies associated with the structural changes. We did not calculate statistical parameters for the interview data due to the relatively small sample size of farmers (\( n = 35 \)) and stakeholders (\( n = 20 \)) interviewed.

Results

Changes in the Farming Sector

The agricultural micro-census data clearly indicated widespread structural changes in Pyrzyce County during the transition period. We found four main developments in the farming sector: (1) changes in the number of farms, (2) changes in the distribution of farm sizes, (3) changes in the number of professional farmers, and (4) changes in the area covered by farmland.

Our results showed that the number of farms declined substantially in Pyrzyce County between 1996 and 2002. Overall, the total number of farms (e.g., all registered agricultural holdings including private farms, livestock farms, and nonindividual farms) decreased from 3,297 to 3,098. The total number of individual farms decreased from 2,208 to 1,888. These trends resulted in marked changes in the farm-size distribution between 1996 and 2002 (Figure 2). In 1996 the majority of individual farms (1,175) were medium-sized (i.e., between 7 and 20 ha).

Conversely, the number of large and small private individual farms increased. In total, the share of farms larger than 20 ha increased from 13.6 percent in 1996 to 20.3 percent in 2002. The number of small farms (1–7 ha) grew from 731 in 1996 to 787 in 2002 and the share of small farms in the total number of individual farms increased from 33.2 percent to 41.7 percent (Figure 2). The decline in the number of medium-sized farms (−457 farms) exceeded the increase in the number of small and large farms (together +137) resulting in an overall decline in the number of farms in Pyrzyce County. The differences between the distributions of farm sizes from 1996 to 2002 were highly significant (chi-square test, \( p < 0.001 \)). Differences between the distributions of farm sizes per farm size group (small, medium-sized, and large) were also significant (Kolmogorov–Smirnov test, \( p < 0.001 \)).

The micro-census data also revealed that the number of professional full-time farmers declined substantially between 1996 and 2002. Both the number and the share of farms gaining income solely through agricultural activities declined markedly. In 1996, 1,495 of farm households gained their income solely via agricultural activities. In 2002, the number of such farms had declined to 1,065, as additional income sources like off-farm employment as well as pensions and retirement payments became more important.

The total cultivated area in Pyrzyce County increased between 1996 and 2002. Active farmland increased from 62,141 ha in 1996 to 66,789 ha in 2002. Due to this increase and the overall decline in farms (from 3,297 to 3,098) in Pyrzyce County, the average area of farmland managed by farms increased from 18.9 ha to 21.6 ha. In addition, the share of farmland managed by private farms increased substantially from 53.0 percent in 1996 (equaling 32,939 ha) to 87.2 percent (58,219 ha) in 2002.

Changes in Land Use Patterns

The land use pattern maps derived from the Landsat images from 1991 and 2000 showed a distinct dual field size distribution in Pyrzyce County (Figure 3). Clusters of small fields (<0.5 ha) coexisted next to areas of relatively large fields (>3.17 ha) in both years, whereas medium-sized fields were not very widespread. Clusters of small fields were found dispersed throughout the county. The satellite-based mapping also showed marked changes in land use patterns between 1991 and 2000. For example, many areas in the southeast of the study region dominated by small-scale farmland in 1991 were converted into large fields. The opposite trend, the subdivision of larger fields into smaller ones, was not frequent in Pyrzyce County and occurred mainly close to the city in the center of the study region. Moreover, field size increased considerably in areas that were already dominated by large fields in 1991 (Figure 3).

Overall, mean field size only changed moderately, as it was 5.01 ha (\( SD = 11.56 \) ha) in 1991 and 5.34 ha (\( SD = 14.16 \) ha) in 2000. Categorizing fields into field size classes revealed substantial changes in the areas covered by some classes between 1991 and 2000 (Figure 4, top). We found three major trends. First, classes with very small fields (<0.5 ha; i.e., household plots, gardens) increased strongly between 1991 and 2000 (by up to 65 percent; Figure 4, bottom), although the area covered by very small fields was almost negligible (0.45 km\(^2\)). Second, relative changes of field size classes between 0.5 ha and 50 ha were less pronounced (up to 30 percent). The area covered by such fields decreased slightly, equaling approximately 50 km\(^2\). Third, the two largest field size classes (50 ha–200 ha) showed considerable relative increase (by 69 percent and 230 percent, respectively) and the area covered by these two classes increased significantly. Fields larger than 50 ha covered more than twice the area in 2000 than in 1991 (93.89 km\(^2\) compared to 46.58 km\(^2\)). Changes in Farmers’ Strategies
Our interviews of farmers and stakeholders strongly suggested that farm strategies had shifted substantially during transition. All interviewed farmers stated that changes in agricultural demand chains, particularly the rise of large hypermarkets, led to fundamental economic challenges that often threatened the existence of their farms. According to our interviews, most farmers tried to enlarge their farms to realize economies of scale to remain competitive. Some farmers also increased mechanization or modernized their equipment to comply with new standards. Because such modernization is coupled with high investments, most farmers stated that such investments only pay off if farm output is high. Thus, modernization itself was often named as a major incentive for enlarging farm size. An alternative strategy appears to be downsizing of farms coupled with additional income sources such as off-farm employment,\textsuperscript{8}
job diversification, or pensions and retirement payments.

Most farmers who enlarged their farms achieved this by buying or leasing fields from retiring or downsizing farmers, often their immediate neighbors. Interestingly, some old farmers trying to keep farm size stable were well aware of the competitive disadvantage they faced. Such farmers often stated that their descendants were not interested in full-time farming due to the low profitability of their farms, and older farmers often expected that their descendants would sell at least most of their farmland in the near future. Conversely, among the farmers of small farms, many stated they had owned medium-sized farms until recently but could not afford modernization and enlargement. Most of these farmers sold parts of their farmland, sought additional income strategies, and kept some farmland for subsistence farming or traditional reasons. The stakeholder interviews generally confirmed the impression of the farmers that new agricultural value chains were the main reason for the challenges farmers, especially of medium-sized farms, faced after the breakdown of socialism.

Discussion

Changing Agricultural Value Chains and Structural Change in Pyrzyce County

Agricultural micro-census data and satellite-based land use pattern maps revealed widespread changes in Pyrzyce County during transition. Our two independent analyses clearly showed a polarization of both farm size (analysis of micro-census data) and field size (analysis of satellite images). Our interviews suggest that these trends were due to structural change, likely caused by a loss in competitiveness of medium-sized farms (7–20 ha) as a consequence of changes in agricultural value chains after the breakdown of socialism.

Agricultural value chains and the demand for agricultural products changed fundamentally during the second half of the 1990s, largely connected to Western retailers entering the Polish market (Munroe 2001; Deutsch Polnische Wirtschaftsförderungsgesellschaft AG [DWP] 2002a, 2002b). This was paralleled by a rapid increase in the number of large-scale hypermarkets (e.g., an increase from ten to 202 from 1995 to 2002; PricewaterhouseCoopers 2005). In contrast to agricultural value chains in which the Polish farmers were embedded prior to 1989,9 today large buyers apply strong pressure concerning price, quantity, quality, and standards (e.g., delivery, health, or safety; Pingali, Khwaja, and Meijer 2005). Typically, these new demand structures can only be met by large, capital-intensive, and highly specialized farms but not by small-scale family farmers (Pierce 1994; Munroe 2001; Veeck, Che, and Veeck 2006). In Pyrzyce County, this situation is exacerbated by the fact that the vast majority of small-scale farmers are not organized in cooperatives, which potentially could provide access to cheaper inputs, storage rooms, processing facilities, and marketing options.10 Our results clearly showed that especially large-scale farms (>20 ha), which were relatively well prepared for these new agricultural value chains, increased during transition in Pyrzyce County. Likewise, the area covered by large fields (an indicator of the realization of economies of scale) increased strongly after the system change, suggesting that larger farms were increasingly forced to enlarge and intensify land use (e.g., the aggregation of smaller fields into bigger ones; see Figure 4).

The expansion of large-scale farming in Pyrzyce County occurred mainly at the expense of medium-sized farms (7–20 ha). Moreover, our interviews suggest that structural change in Pyrzyce County was mainly driven by medium-sized farms reacting to their decreasing competitiveness under the new demand and value chain conditions. Most interviewed farmers suggested that medium-sized full-time farms can only survive by increasing farm size (and intensifying land use), focusing on additional income strategies, or if both options are unavailable, by withdrawing from farming. This also corresponds well with the findings of earlier studies (Munroe 2001; Soler 2005).

Although the growing number of large farms suggests that some medium-sized farms made the leap into more intensified production, the total number of medium-sized farms declined strongly (Figure 2). Certainly some of these farms closed down, but many also pursued additional income strategies or switched to part-time farming (Chaplin, Davidova, and Gorton 2005). Both options, however, almost always go along with farm downsizing. Indeed, we
found an increase in the number of small farms (<7 ha). Part-time farmers often also sell or lease some of their land to larger farms, which explains the expansion of large-scale farms and the more aggregated field size patterns in 2000. These farmers kept only a small amount for subsistence farming, which is reflected in the increase in the area covered by small-scale fields around settlements (Figure 3). Most of the farmland of medium-sized farmers withdrawing from agriculture was more or less absorbed by large farms—as a result, the total farm number declined and average farm size increased in Pyrzyce County in the 1990s (Figure 3). Our interviews confirm that this trend is especially common when farmers retire because their potential successors often prefer other jobs.

Additional Factors Contributing to Structural Change
Three factors other than changes in agricultural value chains might have contributed to structural change in Pyrzyce County. First, population decrease in Central and Eastern European landscapes, especially of younger population segments, has sometimes been linked to decreasing farm numbers (Dannenberg 2007). Yet, in Pyrzyce County this seems not to be the case, because total population declined by only 3.8 percent between 1995 and 2007 and young population segments increased markedly during that period (GUS 2007). Second, structural change could be a result of increased mechanization, but most Polish farmers lacked the funds to buy machines after 1989 (Gorz and Uliszak 2002); the amount of agricultural machinery (tractors, harvesters, etc.) in Pyrzyce County was 15 percent lower in 2002 than in 1996 (GUS 2007). Finally, the internationalization of trade might have affected Pyrzyce County, yet none of the interviewed farmers and experts deemed this factor important. In sum, although other factors might have contributed to the trends we observed, we suggest that decreasing competitiveness due to changes in agricultural value chains was the principal driver of postsocialist structural change in Pyrzyce County.

Implications of Structural Change
Although our study region is a representative rural area for those regions that had been German before World War II (about a third of Poland), we believe many of our findings also help understand structural change in the farming sector elsewhere in Poland. Some aspects of the structural change that were closely linked to small-scale farming in Pyrzyce County (i.e., farm downsizing or field aggregation) are likely to be found elsewhere in Eastern Europe, where restitution and privatization often created highly fragmented land use patterns (Sabates-Wheeler 2002). Moreover, our results fit well those of other nationwide studies (World Bank 2001; Csaki and Lerman 2002; Gorz and Uliszak 2002) that also found farm size polarization. Although structural change in Poland has clearly been atypical for Central and Eastern Europe, because of its rapid pace and the country’s low level of collectivization (Lerman, Csaki, and Feder 2004; Harvey 2006), we suggest the Polish case gives important insights into what is yet to come elsewhere in Central and Eastern Europe and in developing countries with similar economies (Pingali, Khwaja, and Meijer 2005; Harvey 2006).

The time period covered by the census data (1996–2002) was relatively short. Yet, it is important to note that we found similar trends in two independent data sets, the micro-census data, and the satellite-based land use pattern (1991–2000). Our 2004 interviews confirmed that many of the trends we observed are ongoing. Moreover, even if census data from the early 1990s were available, they would likely confirm the expansion of larger farms (as suggested at the voivodship level for 1990–1995; Glebocki 2000). Census data are currently being collected in Poland and can be used in the future to extend the time horizon of our analysis of postsocialist structural change.

The analyses of farm sizes based on micro-census data and the analyses of field size based on satellite images yielded remarkably similar overall patterns of polarization, suggesting that both data sets are able to capture major trends in postsocialist structural change in Pyrzyce County. Fine-scale micro-census data are unavailable in many regions worldwide and satellite-based indicators such as land use pattern changes might be a useful substitute to study structural change in such situations. Moreover, our analysis highlights the widespread environmental and socioeconomic consequences of structural change. For
example, land use pattern and field size changes affect biodiversity (Butler, Vickery, and Norris 2007; Rodriguez and Wiegand 2009), ecosystem services (e.g., recreation potential), and socioeconomic functions (e.g., settlement structure) of rural landscapes (Henkel 2004; Dannenberg 2007). Micro-census data are important to assess the magnitude of structural change, but mapping changes in land use patterns can complement such assessments by providing insights into how structural change manifests in landscapes and thus guide policymakers and landscape planners.

Conclusions

The postsocialist period was characterized by widespread structural change of the agricultural sector in Pyrzyce County, Poland. Our analysis of micro-census data revealed that the number of medium-sized farms declined in Pyrzyce County between 1996 and 2002. Our results suggest this was mainly due to declining numbers of medium-sized farms (7–20 ha), whereas the number of small-scale farms (1–7 ha) and large farms (>20 ha) has increased. Our satellite-based mapping of field sizes shows that structural change also had a profound effect on land use patterns in Pyrzyce County. Average field size increased between 1991 and 2000 and smaller fields tended to occur closer to cities or to become aggregated into larger fields. We suggest these changes can be explained by changing market systems after 1989 (e.g., emerging free-market, international agricultural value chain structures) that decreased the competitiveness of full-time, medium-sized farms. Our results suggest these farmers withdrew from farming, enlarged their farms, or switched to semiprofessional farming.

Our study also showed land use pattern maps and micro-census data were both able to capture the major trends in postsocialist structural change that occurred in Pyrzyce County. This suggests that satellite image analysis bears considerable potential to analyze structural change in the farming sector in Central and Eastern Europe and possibly elsewhere, particularly where fine-scale multitemporal census data are unavailable. Structural change resulted in a profound restructuring of farming in Pyrzyce County, but major changes still lie ahead given the recent accession of Poland to the European Union. Structural change might result in a more efficient, competitive, and stable agricultural sector in the long term; yet, in the short term, structural changes can amplify existing socioeconomic problems in Poland’s rural areas. Agricultural census data and remote-sensing-based assessments can help better understand consequences of structural change in Poland and elsewhere and can guide decision makers who strive to mitigate the negative impacts of structural change on rural populations and landscapes.
