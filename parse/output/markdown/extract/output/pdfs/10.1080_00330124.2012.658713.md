Ethnic Restructuring in Rural America: Migration and the Changing Faces of Rural Communities in the Great Plains*

Holly R. Barcus
Macalester College

Laura Simmons
University of North Carolina at Charlotte

Scholars have long documented widespread aging and depopulation of rural communities in the Great Plains. Paralleling these trends is the emergence, growth, and spatial dispersion of new and longtime non–white populations across the region. The dual processes of population loss in many counties combined with the growth of new, non–white population groups suggest that the ethnic structure of the population is changing. In this article we utilize choropleth maps, Hoover and Simpson indexes, and cluster analysis to assess whether the emergence and growth of ethnic minority groups in the Great Plains between 1970 and 2008 is a localized phenomenon or region–wide shift in the ethnic composition of the population. Results suggest that as depopulation is occurring in many counties of this region, the growth of non–white populations, both immigrant and native born, is changing or restructuring the ethnic composition of the Great Plains. Key Words: ethnic restructuring, Great Plains, internal migration, rural population, settlement patterns.

Many rural communities in America are undergoing a demographic transformation. A wide range of rural places struggle to maintain population while some high-amenity areas are experiencing significant population growth characterized by the in-migration of retirees and amenity migrants (Muller, Hall, and Keen 2004; Glasgow and Brown 2006). Other rural areas, including some in America’s traditional agricultural regions, are also changing demographically through the in-migration of foreign- and native-born ethnic minority populations (Millard and Chapa 2004; Kandel and Parrado 2006; Kirschner, Berry, and Glasgow 2006). Notably, the growing dispersion of these demographic groups away from gateway areas into rural places suggests that the ethnic and racial diversity common in urban America can increasingly be found in rural America.

Demographic structure includes the age, gender, and racial or ethnic composition of a population in a particular place and time, and demographic restructuring refers to substantive changes in these components. In much the same way that economic restructuring is identified by a shift from one economic state to another (such as from industrial to service-based), demographic restructuring is characterized by “a qualitative shift from one order to another” (Pandit and Withers 1999, 5). Two primary types of demographic restructuring include changes in age structure and ethnic composition. Because migration is a highly age-selective process, in- or out-migration of a particular age group results in a changing age profile of that region. Likewise, the movement of a minority group into or out of a region changes the balance between minority and nonminority populations. Ethnic restructuring, then, can be identified by a qualitative shift from a small representation of minority population to a much larger representation of that minority in a particular area, brought about by the dual processes of natural increase and migration (Pandit and Withers 1999).

The Great Plains is a region undergoing rural ethnic restructuring. Numerous case studies detail the rapidly changing population composition of counties and places within the region, documenting the emergence of new ethnic groups, including Latino, Bosnian, Chinese, Sudanese, Filipino, Korean, Vietnamese, and Indian populations (see, e.g., Carlo, Carranza, and Zamboanga 2002; Hansen 2003; Ravuri 2003; Dalla et al. 2004; Gaber et al. 2004; Willis and Fernald 2004; Xie, Xia, and Zhou 2004; Wahl, Breckenridge, and Gunkel 2007). These case studies suggest that changes in the ethnic composition of local populations are not isolated but rather reflect more widespread, regional-scale change. Alongside changes in ethnic composition, several studies have outlined aging and age-restructuring also occurring across the region (Archer and Lonsdale 2003; Ravuri 2003; Johnson and Rathge 2006; Wilson 2009).

In this article, we argue that rural areas in the Great Plains are undergoing a process of ethnic restructuring and that although many case studies document the emergence of new ethnic groups, it is a continuing process for the entire region, with some places experiencing much greater change than others. At the macro scale, three key forces influence this process: globalization, regional economic restructuring, and changes in immigration policy. We utilize four measures of change—choropleth maps, the Hoover and Simpson indexes, and cluster analyses—to provide evidence of widespread change in the ethnic composition of rural communities in this region.

Four additional sections follow this introduction. These include an overview of the study’s conceptual framework next. After that, we detail data and methods and provide a brief history of the Great Plains and then provide evidence of the changing ethnic composition of the region. Finally, we offer conclusions.

Factors Influencing Changing Settlement Patterns

Since 1990, Asian and Latino populations, inclusive of immigrants and long-term residents, have grown rapidly and dispersed away from gateway cities to new destinations throughout the United States (Kandel and Cromartie 2004). The processes of globalization, regional economic restructuring within the United States, and changes in U.S. immigration policy have facilitated the recent redistribution of these populations to new locations and, in the Great Plains, the emergence of these groups is stemming population loss and creating a more ethnically diverse population.
Globalization

Globalization is a process by which regional and national economies and societies are increasingly integrated into the international economy through trade, transportation and communication technologies, capital flows, and migration. It “include[s] fewer barriers and risks to trade and financial and investment capital flows and, thus, greater global economic integration, greater flexibility in labor markets, a compression of time and space” (Hytrek and Zentgraf 2008, 5). Integration occurs across various geographic scales, altering labor supply and demand, creating large and extensive international migration flows from the Global South to the Global North as well as between countries of the Global South (Castles and Miller 2009). Demand for low-skill, low-wage labor in the Global North coupled with population growth without sufficient corresponding economic growth in the Global South has created a surplus of workers, generating pressure for immigration to the Global North. Thus, through the process of globalization, labor demands and shortages create, through international migration, new populations within Global North countries. Since the 1960s, for example, the legal permanent resident flow to the United States quadrupled from 250,000 during the 1950s to just over 1 million between 2000 and 2007 (Jefferys and Monger 2008). The process of global economic integration and corresponding increase in migration from the Global South has prompted continued review of immigration policies by Global North countries that facilitate the migration of some groups and limit that of others (Castles and Miller 2009).

New immigrants initially settle in gateway cities, but for many this is not the final destination (Newbold 1999). Rather, because many of these cities have saturated labor and housing markets, migrants (either new immigrants or second- or third-generation residents) move to new destinations (Newbold 1999), a process that is facilitated by changing immigration policies (see later discussion of the Immigration Reform and Control Act) and new employment opportunities created by subnational regional economic restructuring.

Changing U.S. Immigration Policies

Immigration policy changes have historically influenced the magnitude of migrant flows to the United States. Although policy changes tend to be the most clear-cut and documented forces affecting migration, they are often the weakest in actual impact (Massey 1995). For instance, the cessation of mass European immigration in the 1930s is hailed as a resulting success of the “quota laws” of 1921 and 1924, which limited immigrants by national origin. These laws played a role in stemming the flow of immigrants, but World War I (which effectively halted immigration from Europe prior to the passage of the quota laws), the Great Depression (which occurred just as immigration from Europe began to recover in spite of the quota laws), and World War II (which further deterred international movement before the economy had a chance to recover from the Depression) also influenced this trend. Conversely, the lifting of the quotas through the 1965 amendments initiated new immigrant flows from Latin America and Asia.

Early policies increased labor migration to the United States. The Bracero Program (1942–1964), for example, was a labor recruitment program initiated to meet labor shortages for U.S. growers during World War II. The program recruited more than 4.6 million Mexican agricultural laborers during this period. Although it ended in 1964, the flow of employment-seeking migrants continued, penetrating beyond the Southwest and into the Plains (Durand, Massey, and Parrado 1999).

Policies during the 1980s and 1990s also played a role in determining the destinations of immigrant flows and initiating new settlement geographies (Vernez and Ronfeldt 1991; Durand, Massey, and Charvet 2000). The most significant was the 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA), which legalized nearly 3 million undocumented immigrants between 1987 and 1990 (Durand, Massey, and Charvet 2000). Although the IRCA was intended to discourage further immigration, its greatest impact was increasing geographic mobility among immigrants already living in the United States, resulting in a wider array of settlement choices across the country. Newly legalized immigrants, many of whom initially moved to a traditional gateway city and did not leave because of their undocumented status, could now leave the crowded labor and housing markets of the gateways and follow economic opportunity to cities and rural areas of the South and Midwest. Patterns of dispersal among both
foreign- and native-born Latinos and Asians, but especially for native-born Latinos, were identifiable as early as 1990 (Frey and Liaw 1999; Gober 1999) and were confirmed by the 2000 Census.

Regional Economic Restructuring

New rural economies have ushered in new rural migrants from very diverse racial, ethnic, and economic backgrounds: “Economic restructuring creates new jobs, altering regional and local labor demands and creating new employment opportunities for different segments of the population which may in turn alter the demographic and ethnic composition of the population in a given area” (Barcus 2007, 300, summarized from Pandit and Withers 1999). Regions with historically small immigrant populations as well as rural and suburban locations are increasingly included among these new destinations (Kandel and Cromartie 2004; Barcus 2006; Smith and Furuseth 2006). Expanding job markets create new destinations for migrants, immigrant and nonimmigrant, thereby changing the settlement patterns of social, economic, and ethnic groups.

Economic restructuring in the Great Plains includes agricultural restructuring and the expansion of manufacturing. In agriculture, technology continues to increase productivity per acre and decrease labor demands, a long-standing process that has been increasing the average farm size and decreasing the number of farms and farm families beginning in the 1930s and continuing since the 1950s (Johnson and Rathge 2006). By the 1990s, agribusiness replaced the family farm as the primary unit of agricultural production, and off-farm employment opportunities increased (Hudson 2003). Demographically, agricultural restructuring increased out-migration from the region, particularly of young people; aging and declining populations are common in many counties (Rathge and Highman 1998; Archer and Lonsdale 2003; Hart 2003; Hudson 2003).

Unlike agriculture, the manufacturing sector has expanded since 1970, growing twice as fast as manufacturing in other U.S. rural areas in the 1970s and 1980s (McGranahan 1998). The largest manufacturing growth occurred in meat packing (McGranahan 1998). This industry began restructuring in the 1950s, moving slaughter facilities from urban stockyard districts into cattle-producing areas, increasing plant sizes, and consolidating packing steps into one facility (Broadway and Stull 2006). Economies of scale within this model reduced the number of employees needed between 1974 and 1997, but low wages and high injury rates resulted in nearly 200 percent labor force turnover per year and created a reliance on immigrant labor, initiating ethnic restructuring in numerous communities throughout the Great Plains and Midwest (see, e.g., Millard and Chapa 2004; Broadway and Stull 2006).

In summary, although these three factors do not cause ethnic restructuring, they create conditions under which internal migration to rural areas, such as in the Great Plains, becomes feasible and desirable for new and long-term minority populations. The process of globalization creates a mechanism by which labor shortages in the Global North catalyze migration of labor surplus from the Global South. This is mediated by national-scale immigration policies that limit or expand legal migration opportunities for particular populations. On arrival in the Global North, subnational regional economic restructuring facilitates, through job creation, the dispersal of migrants from gateway cities to nontraditional destinations. We argue that these three processes create opportunities for migration and settlement in new and nontraditional destinations—specifically rural counties in the Great Plains.

Data and Methods

Data for the following analyses come from the 1970, 1980, 1990, and 2000 U.S. Decennial Census, the 2008 Population Estimates Program, the 2002 Census of Agriculture (U.S. Department of Agriculture 2002), and the Regional Economic Information System for 1969, 1979, 1989, and 2000 (Bureau of Economic Analysis [BEA] 2002). We focus our analyses on five demographic groups (Latino, black, Asian, Native American, and white populations) within the Great Plains. Although numerous definitions of the Great Plains region exist, we utilize boundaries inclusive of the major regional delimitations (Adamchak et al. 1999; Archer and Lonsdale 2003; Wilson 2009). To evaluate the extent to which ethnic restructuring is occurring in this region, we employ choropleth maps, Hoover and Simpson indexes, and cluster analysis.
The Hoover Index measures the concentration of a population in the study area. First introduced by Hoover in 1941, it remains a viable tool for understanding trends in population concentration and deconcentration at multiple scales, including the county, state, and region (Long and Nucci 1997; Otterstrom and Shumway 2003). Index values range from 0 to 100—the higher the value, the more concentrated the population; the lower the value, the more dispersed the population; a value of 0 indicates a population that is equally distributed (Plane and Rogerson 1994). This index allows us to assess whether overall minority population growth in the region is simply continued growth in the same places or an increase in the number of localities experiencing growth. From a restructuring perspective, a lower value, indicating more dispersed populations over time, would suggest that a particular minority population is present in a greater number of counties.

In the preceding equation, \(a_i\) is a “fraction of study region’s land area” and \(p_i\) is a “fraction of entire region’s population” (Plane and Rogerson 1994, 28).

Although the Hoover Index provides an overview of concentration levels, it does not indicate where concentration is occurring. The Simpson Index measures the diversity of each county, which can be compared to the index value of every other county in the region. Index values range between 0 and 1, indicating the probability that any two persons selected at random will come from different population groups, with 0 indicating no diversity and a value approaching 1 indicating greater diversity\( ^1 \) (Plane and Rogerson 1994). Thus, the more counties with an increased index value, the greater the evidence for ethnic restructuring.

Cluster analysis is a data reduction technique that identifies similarities across observations with the purpose of minimizing within-group variation and maximizing between-group variation (Rogerson 2001). This technique is frequently used to group geographic areas, such as block groups or census tracts, based on demographic similarities of their residents (Plane and Rogerson 1994). Unlike the previous two indexes, which simply indicate whether change is occurring, cluster analysis provides insight into the characteristics of places experiencing demographic change. A nonhierarchical cluster analysis with four clusters was specified with clusters based on six categories of variables: economic structure (including the percentage of county employment in manufacturing, agriculture, construction, wholesale-retail trade and service sectors; BEA 2002), percentage Asian (U.S. Bureau of the Census 2000b), percentage Latino (U.S. Bureau of the Census 2000b), proximity to interstates,\(^2\) and presence of universities or colleges and reservations (U.S. Bureau of the Census 2000a). Variables were standardized using z scores, resulting in 64 cases (counties) in cluster 1 (college towns), 224 in cluster 2 (interstate agricultural), 57 in cluster 3 (manufacturing destination), and 216 in cluster 4 (agriculture). SPSS (2008; version 17.0) was used for statistical analysis and ArcGIS (2010; version 9.3.1) for mapping.

Historical Context

The Great Plains region occupies the central United States, encompassing parts of eleven states and extending south to north from Mexico to Canada (see Figure 1). The history of the Great Plains is marked by the dual processes of economic restructuring and corresponding population expansion and contraction.

Archer and Lonsdale (2003) identified three distinct periods of migration and population change over the last 100-plus years: “net immigration in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, net emigration in the mid-20th century, and net immigration in the last three decades of the 20th century” (51). Although these periods reflect overall population growth since 1900, this growth has been much slower than for the United States overall (see Figure 2).

During the first period of migration, the Great Plains was still being settled, as land for farming and farm families increased. Fueled by the extension of railways across the region, many areas experienced population gains due to in-migration. Oil field development in Oklahoma and Texas extended the region’s

The Latino Population

The Latino population is the largest minority group, at approximately 4.3 million (U.S. Bureau of the Census 2008). The majority of Latino immigrants come from Mexico, and their presence is the product of the political and economic climates on both sides of the border. In the late nineteenth and early twentieth century, businesses and industries looking for cheap labor recruited Mexicans to work on farms, ranches, and the railroad (Davis 2001). The 1942 Bracero Program increased immigration to the plains, and the 1986 IRCA continued growth of existing Latino communities (Durand, Massey, and Parrado 1999).

The largest Latino populations are found in Texas, New Mexico, and Colorado. Figure 5 clearly illustrates the increasing dispersion of this population from a core area in the Southwest in 1970 to residence in nearly every county by 2008, albeit at varying levels of concentration. Latino population growth is widespread throughout the rural Great Plains.

The Native American Population
The Native American population increased to 447,748 by 2008 (U.S. Bureau of the Census 2008). The largest populations are found in Oklahoma and on or near reservations in the northern plains (see Figure 8). Although settlement patterns expanded outside reservation areas since 1970, it is notable that in the northern subregion, most of this growth occurred in counties adjacent to reservation lands, whereas in the southern subregion, population growth increased in some rural counties distant from reservation lands. Although a portion of this increase is attributable to increasing rates of self-identification and changes in census enumeration procedures (Gonzales 2003), it is unclear what role migration might also play in this redistribution.

The White Population
Finally, maps of the white population dramatically illustrate ethnic change in the Great Plains (see Figure 9). In 1970, minority populations are visible in the southern subregion and in the reservation lands, but the remaining areas are nearly 100 percent white. By 2008, it is clear that the primarily white ethnic structure of some parts of the region has changed, with many areas in the southern subregion comprised of populations that are less than 50 percent white and with noticeably fewer counties

in the overall region where the population is more than 50 percent white. The southern and central subregions are experiencing the greatest change, whereas some of the most remote counties in Kansas, Nebraska, North Dakota, and Montana remain the least diverse.

These maps help us visualize the substantive change in size and spatial distribution of minority populations in the Great Plains. Maps, however, are limited by the manner in which data are classified. To assess ethnic restructuring in the region more fully, the following section utilizes the Hoover and Simpson indexes to measure changes in the distribution of each group and diversity within each county.

Hoover Index values reflect a pattern of concentration–dispersal–concentration. Between 1970 and 1990, the concentration of all groups and the population overall increased, reflecting the long-term rural-to-urban migration trends and growth of the urban nodes previously discussed. Increased concentration indicates that within the nonmetro counties, there are particular places in which growth is occurring more rapidly and substantively than in others. Then, between 1990 and 2000, there was a brief reversal of this trend, with black, Asian, and Latino populations becoming more dispersed among nonmetro counties, suggesting that the overall trends of out-migration from rural areas might have been offset by the in-migration of minority groups. This also indicates greater ethnic diversity in a greater number of places—not just in growth nodes. Between 2000 and 2008, however, the trend toward concentration resumed, suggesting that the 1990 to 2000 dispersion might have been a brief departure from long-standing trends. This volatility at the regional scale is consistent with findings from Long and Nucci (1997) and Otterstrom and Shumway (2003), who found that a long-term trend toward concentration might be interrupted by short-term periods of deconcentration and that this volatility is reflected in the Hoover Index when it is applied to different spatial scales. Although the direct causes of increasing concentration between 2000 and 2008 are unclear, it is likely that economic expansion and prosperity in the 1990s benefited employment and population growth in nonmetro areas, whereas the economic crises

of the 2000s resulted in employment contraction, particularly in rural communities, leading to a corresponding reduction of population dispersal.

Within-County Diversity
The Simpson Index provides a measure of population diversity for each county (see Figures 10–12). Holding the index categories constant, the spatial extent of increasing diversity has clearly expanded over the nearly forty-year period. Whereas the southern Great Plains has historically been quite diverse, and pockets of diversity exist in the northern Great Plains around reservation lands, the central Great Plains has consistently been more homogenous.

Importantly, these maps depict increasing diversity in places with the greatest population loss. New residents are moving into rural Great Plains counties, offsetting overall population loss and increasing local ethnic diversity. Of the 306 counties in the Great Plains that lost population between 1990 and 2000 (the last full decade), the Simpson Index values for 292 (95.4 percent) of them increased, providing evidence that despite overall population loss, ethnic minority populations are expanding, either through natural increase or in-migration. Further, despite a slight overall renewal of concentration between 2000 and 2008, as illustrated by the Hoover Index results, the number of counties experiencing increased diversity continues to grow. Between 1990 and 2000 (and 2000 and 2008), Simpson Index values increased in 95.6 percent (95.8 percent) of counties (see Figures 13 and 14). The

twenty-five counties that declined in diversity during the 1990s (twenty-four between 2000 and 2008) are randomly distributed across the study area and only a few (three counties in Texas) that decreased in diversity in the first period experienced further declines in diversity in the second period (see Figures 13 and 14).

We use Sheridan County as an example of a county experiencing population decline while simultaneously witnessing an increase in ethnic diversity. Located in northwestern Nebraska, Sheridan County had a 2000 population of 5,461. Between 1970 and 2000, the total population declined by 14.9 percent. Although the white population declined by 22 percent, the minority population increased by 87 percent, reducing overall population loss and increasing ethnic diversity. This is significant because although counties such as Sheridan County have low or no population growth, ethnic diversity is still increasing. Unlike counties that have had significant population growth with corresponding changes in ethnic diversity because of the opening of a manufacturing plant, for example, ethnic restructuring in Sheridan County occurred slowly from 1970 to 2000 through natural decrease and out-migration of the white population and natural increase and slow in-migration of other minority groups. Although the numbers are small, the Latino, Native American, and black populations all almost doubled in size between 1970 and 2000, whereas the white population in 2000 was only 78 percent of its 1970 size.

Counties in the college towns cluster are characterized by a low proportion of employment in agriculture and higher levels of employment in service and retail jobs. Few of these counties have reservations. The two most important characteristics of these counties are the presence of universities and proportionally high Asian populations, reflecting the importance of universities in attracting Asian migrants.

Interstate agriculture counties are located in close proximity to the interstates, allowing greater accessibility to urban and rural areas (see Figure 15). In these counties, agriculture is an important component of the local economy, and they attract Latino migrants but fewer Asian migrants. These counties have no reservations or colleges.

Agriculture counties are the most remote counties in the region and are found primarily in the central plains. These counties appear to be much less desirable destinations for Asians and Latinos.

Counties in the last cluster, manufacturing destinations, do not have colleges, but ten are close to interstates and twenty-two contain reservations. These counties are attractive to Asian migrants and have a large manufacturing employment base. Finney, Ford, and Seward counties in western Kansas are good examples of this type of county, as exemplified by Broadway and Stull’s (2006) examination of Garden City, Kansas.

Summary and Conclusions

In this article, we demonstrate that the Great Plains has undergone marked ethnic restructuring since 1970. First, the three processes of globalization, immigration policy change, and regional economic restructuring have created conditions conducive to the migration of non-white, sometimes immigrant, populations to rural places that previously had few ethnic minorities, such as the Great Plains. At the regional level, Hoover Index analysis indicates a rural population that has become increasingly geographically concentrated, while, at a finer scale, a wide range of rural counties, not just growth nodes, increased in ethnic diversity, as illustrated by the Simpson Index analysis. Cluster analysis further revealed that the location and economic characteristics of rural counties have contributed to varying levels of diversity, especially the proportions of Latinos and Asians: Counties with large manufacturing operations appeal to Asians, heavily agricultural counties close to interstate highways attract more Latinos but fewer Asians, counties with a college or university and little agricultural activity are highly attractive to Asians and Latinos, and counties that are the most reliant on agriculture and are in the most remote locations are less attractive to both groups.

In the Great Plains, the in-migration of new populations holds hope for changing the trajectory of depopulation. Numerous case studies (see previous discussion) and this analysis reveal that many new residents are ethnic minorities of working age moving for employment opportunities. One outcome is that in 2007, “the Great Plains had a younger overall age structure than the United States as a whole” (Wilson 2009, 16). The influx of new, working-age residents and their potential future offspring could lessen, if not potentially halt, negative population trends in some places in the Great Plains. Natural increase plus in-migration could mean growth for this region and a resulting increase in potential business owners, employees, young families, and voters.

When viewed at the regional scale, the process of ethnic restructuring in the rural Great Plains warrants continued observation. Release of the 2010 Census will help clarify whether the trend toward increasing ethnic diversity has continued or whether the recent economic crisis has interfered. As demonstrated by the cluster analysis, not all communities will be affected by ethnic restructuring, especially the most remote and agriculturally dependent counties. But as the choropleth maps depict, the vast majority of communities have already begun to experience a shift in the ethnic composition of their population. Many questions remain unanswered: Will these trends continue? Which counties will be the biggest winners with population and economic gain, and which will lose? Are remote, dominantly agricultural counties destined to continue losing population? Finally, what will the Great Plains look like demographically in twenty, thirty, or fifty years?
