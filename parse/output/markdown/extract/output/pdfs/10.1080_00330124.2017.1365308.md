Inverted Two-Step Floating Catchment Area Method for Measuring Facility Crowdedness

Fahui Wang
Louisiana State University

The popular two-step floating catchment area (2SFCA) method has been widely used in the literature to measure spatial accessibility of residents for a service. The 2SFCA method accounts for the ratio between the supply capacity and demand amount of the service as well as the complex spatial interaction between them. This article introduces an inverted two-step floating catchment area (i2SFCA) method that is derived from an extension of the classic Huff model and used to capture the “crowdedness” (scarcity of resource or intensity of competition) for facilities. The method is illustrated and validated by a case study of evaluating hospital inpatient services in Florida. Several possible uses of the measure are also discussed. Key Words: distance decay, Huff model, inverted two-step floating catchment area (i2SFCA) method, two-step floating catchment area (2SFCA) method.

Since its inception (Luo and Wang 2003), the two-step floating catchment area (2SFCA) method has become a popular measure of spatial accessibility, particularly in its application to health care access (McGrail 2012). Spatial access emphasizes the importance of spatial separation between supply (i.e., facilities) and demand (i.e., population) and how they are connected in space (Joseph and Phillips 1984). The 2SFCA method captures two important aspects of spatial accessibility valued by people: proximity to service providers and availability of service while its scarcity matters. Many subsequent improvements to the original 2SFCA method have focused on different ways of conceptualizing the distance decay behavior and can be integrated into a generalized 2SFCA framework, where the distance decay function takes a continuous form, a discrete variable or a hybrid between them (Wang 2012). In essence, the method yields an accessibility score that is the ratio of supply and demand, each of which interacts with the other in distance decay. It measures the convenience of residents at a location reaching and obtaining a service that might be provided by multiple facilities.

From the preceding review, the 2SFCA is resident (or demand) oriented and assesses residents’ relative ease of getting a service from a residential location. By turning the regular 2SFCA upside down, this article proposes a method for examining an equally important property on the supply side. In other words, by switching the supply and demand terms in the formulation of 2SFCA, we have an inverted 2SFCA (i2SFCA) to measure, for lack of a better term, the “crowdedness” (or busyness, saturation, or stress level) for a service provided at a facility. To my knowledge, there has been no work reported in the literature on such an approach.

Related questions for the i2SFCA method include the following:

1. Is there any theoretical foundation for this approach?
2. What is the connection between the regular and inverted 2SFCA methods?
3. Can we validate the method in an empirical setting?
4. How can the method help us enhance our research and practice in real applications (e.g., health care)?
The remainder of the article attempts to answer these questions. The first section review the regular 2SFCA and derive the i2SFCA method from a Huff-based model to solidify its theoretical foundation. The next section illustrates the implementation of i2SFCA in a case study of measuring crowdedness of hospitals in Florida. Based on the case study, the next section compares the results from 2SFCA and i2SFCA and examines the connection between them. After that, I provide some validation of the i2SFCA results and discuss possible follow-up applications. The article concludes with a brief summary.

Review of Regular 2SFCA and Derivation of Inverted 2SFCA

Review of Regular 2SFCA
The review begins with the formulation of classic 2SFCA (Luo and Wang 2003). The 2SFCA repeats the process of “floating catchment area” twice (once on supply locations and once on demand locations):

1. For each supply location \( j \), search all demand locations \( k \) that are within a threshold distance (\( d_0 \)) from location \( j \) (i.e., catchment area \( \hat{j} \)), and compute the supply to demand ratio \( R_j \) within the catchment area:

\[
R_j = \frac{S_j}{\sum_{k \in \{d_{kj} \leq d_0\}} D_k},
\]

where \( d_{kj} \) is the distance between \( k \) and \( j \), \( D_k \) is the demand at location \( k \) that falls within the catchment (i.e., \( d_{kj} \leq d_0 \)), and \( S_j \) is the capacity of supply at location \( j \). The process repeats (floats) across all supply locations to obtain the ratios \( R_j \) (\( j = 1,\ 2,\ \ldots,\ n \)).

2. For each demand location \( i \), search all supply locations \( j \) that are within the threshold distance (\( d_0 \)) from location \( i \) (i.e., catchment area \( \hat{i} \)), and sum up the supply to demand ratios \( R_j \) at those locations to obtain the accessibility \( A_i \) at demand location \( i \):

\[
A_i = \sum_{j \in \{d_{ij} \leq d_0\}} R_j = \sum_{j \in \{d_{ij} \leq d_0\}} \left( \frac{S_j}{\sum_{k \in \{d_{kj} \leq d_0\}} D_k} \right),
\]

where \( d_{ij} \) is the distance between \( i \) and \( j \), and \( R_j \) is the supply-to-demand ratio at supply location \( j \) that falls within the catchment centered at \( i \) (i.e., \( d_{ij} \leq d_0 \)). Again, the process repeats (floats) across all demand locations to obtain the accessibility scores \( A_i \) (\( i = 1,\ 2,\ \ldots,\ m \)).

The first step assigns an initial ratio to each service area centered at a supply location as a preliminary measure of supply availability. The second step sums up the initial ratios in the overlapped service areas to measure accessibility for a demand location, where residents have access to multiple supply locations. Equation 1 is basically the ratio of supply to demand with only selected supplies and demands entering the numerator and the denominator, and the selections are based on a threshold distance within which supplies and demands interact. A larger value of \( A_i \) indicates a better accessibility at a location.

One major limitation of the 2SFCA presented here is its dichotomous approach that defines a supply facility being accessible or inaccessible by a cutoff (threshold) distance. The generalized 2SFCA incorporates a distance decay effect as a function \( f(d) \) in the supply–demand interaction (Wang 2012). It applies to both the numerator and denominator in Equation 1, written as

\[
A_i = \sum_{j=1}^n \left[ S_j f(d_{ij}) / \sum_{k=1}^m (D_k f(d_{ik})) \right].
\]

The distance decay function \( f(d) \) can be (1) a continuous function such as a power function (e.g., Joseph and Bantock 1982), an exponential function (e.g., Weisbrod, Parcells, and Kern 1984), a Gaussian function (e.g., Shi et al. 2012), or a log-logistic function (e.g., Delamater et al. 2013); (2) a discrete variable such as binary in the aforementioned classic 2SFCA or multiple weights in E2SFCA (Luo and Qi 2009); or (3) a hybrid of the two such as a kernel function with a cutoff distance range (Dai and Wang 2011) or a three-zone hybrid approach (McGrail and Humphreys 2009). Choosing which function and using what parameter values to use should be based on analyzing the actual travel behavior in a study area (Jia, Wang, and Xierali 2017b). Many researchers do not have access to such a data set, however, and often conduct sensitivity analysis by experimenting with different functions and employing a set of parameter values (e.g., Bauer and Groneberg 2016).

Hereafter, the preceding formulation of generalized 2SFCA is simply referred to as 2SFCA, as the classic 2SFCA is just a special case (when the distance decay function takes a binary variable with a cutoff distance). In contrast to the inverted 2SFCA to be introduced later, it is termed regular 2SFCA for differentiation.

Deriving Inverted 2SFCA (i2SFCA)
The conceptualization of the Huff (1963) model might be linked to the multichoice logistic model. The probability that consumers at location \( i \) choose a particular
facility \( j \) among a set of alternatives \( l (=1, 2, \ldots, n) \) is proportional to the perceived utility (\( U \)) of each alternative. That is,

\[
Prob_{ij} = U_j / \sum_{l=1}^n U_l .
\]

In the classic Huff model, the utility of a facility is measured as a gravity kernel, which is positively related to a facility’s attraction (e.g., its size \( S \)) and inversely related to its distance (\( d \)) from consumers in a power function. That is,

\[
Prob_{ij} = S_j d_{ij}^{-\beta} / \sum_{l=1}^n (S_l d_{il}^{-\beta}),
\]

where \( \beta > 0 \) is the distance friction coefficient. As discussed previously, the distance decay behavior can be generalized as a function \( f(d) \). By doing so, the general Huff model (Jia, Wang, and Xierali 2017b) becomes

\[
Prob_{ij} = S_j f(d_{ij}) / \sum_{l=1}^n (S_l f(d_{il})).
\]

Given the demand (population size) \( D_i \) at \( i \) with probability of selecting a facility \( j \) as \( Prob_{ij} \), the amount of consumer flow from residential location \( i \) to facility \( j \), denoted by \( F_{ij} \), is projected as

\[
F_{ij} = D_i Prob_{ij} = D_i S_j f(d_{ij}) / \sum_{l=1}^n (S_l f(d_{il})).
\]

Therefore, the total number of consumers attracted by facility \( j \), denoted by \( V_j \), is

\[
V_j = \sum_{i=1}^m F_{ij} = \sum_{i=1}^m \left[ D_i S_j f(d_{ij}) / \sum_{l=1}^n (S_l f(d_{il})) \right].
\]

Finally, the ratio of population served versus the supply capacity at facility \( j \) is projected as

\[
C_j = V_j / S_j = \sum_{i=1}^m \left[ D_i f(d_{ij}) / \sum_{l=1}^n (S_l f(d_{il})) \right],
\]

where the term \( S_j \) is eliminated in both the numerator and denominator for simplification, and the ratio \( C_j \) can be interpreted as “projected or potential crowdedness” (e.g., patients per bed in a hospital, students per teacher in a school, clients per clerk, etc.). A higher \( C_j \) value indicates a service facility being more crowded (saturated, stressed, or busy).

Comparing 2SFCA and i2SFCA

Symmetry is evident when comparing Equation 2 for the regular 2SFCA and Equation 3 for the newly derived measure by switching supply \( S \) and demand \( D \) in the formulations. Therefore, Equation 3 is termed *inverted 2SFCA* and, similarly, it can be implemented in two steps.

One important property of 2SFCA is that the weighted mean of accessibility (using the demand amount as weight) is equal to the ratio of total supply (\( S \)) to total demand (\( D \)) in a study area (Shen 1998). Similarly, we can prove that the weighted mean of crowdedness measured by the i2SFCA (using the supply capacity as weight) is equal to the ratio of total demand to total supply in a study area, such as

\[
\overline{C} = \sum_{j=1}^n \left( \frac{S_j}{S} \right) C_j = D/S.
\]

Table 1 is compiled to summarize the differences between the regular and inverted 2SFCA. In essence, Step 1 of 2SFCA captures preliminary availability of service at each supply location, and its Step 2 sums up accessible supplies around each demand location to yield accessibility there. In contrast, Step 1 of i2SFCA measures preliminary competition intensity for service at each demand location, and its Step 2 sums up reachable demands around each supply location to obtain crowdedness there. In implementing the i2SFCA method, when using the ArcGIS toolkit developed by Zhu and Wang (2015), one could simply switch the demand layer and associated items with those for the supply layer.

The derivation process shown in the previous section demonstrates that the i2SFCA method for projecting potential crowdedness for facilities has its theoretical footing in the Huff model. To some extent, this further justifies the rationale for the regular 2SFCA to measure accessibility for residents. This is worth noting as the regular 2SFCA was proposed without similar reasoning. Furthermore, barring circumstances that merit special consideration, it is unnecessary to alter the formulation of 2SFCA or i2SFCA with additional complications such as “3SFCA” (e.g., Wan, Zou, and Sternberg 2012; Chu et al. 2016).

Connection between 2SFCA and i2SFCA
How are the measures from 2SFCA and i2SFCA related to each other? One obstacle for examining the relationship between the results of 2SFCA and i2SFCA is the discrepancy in the scales: 2SFCA yields the resident-based accessibility in ZIP code areas, and i2SFCA outputs the facility-oriented crowdedness in hospitals. One way to unify the scales is to use the floating catchment area (FCA) approach to calibrate the average (and thus smoothed) value of one measure around each of the data points in another unit (Wang, Antipova,

and Porta 2011). Considering a much larger sample of ZIP code areas (983) than hospitals (268), it is more reliable to compute average accessibility around each hospital and examine its relationship with the crowdedness values there.

Note that rural ZIP code areas are much larger in area size than urban ZIP code areas. If a fixed distance range is used in implementing the FCA, the number of ZIP code areas participating in the calibration of average accessibility around each hospital would vary a great deal and raise the issue of comparability and reliability. Here the concept of “spatially adaptive filter” (Tiwari and Rushton 2005) was used to mitigate the problem. Specifically, a fixed number of nearest ZIP code areas around each hospital was used to define an adjustable window, and averaged accessibility values within the window were then paired with the crowdedness values across hospitals for comparison.

Figure 5 shows that the average accessibility score in ZIP code areas around each hospital declines with increasing hospital crowdedness, and the trend is nonlinear (\( \hat{R}^2 = 0.33 \) when fitted by an exponential function and a similar \( R^2 = 0.31 \) when fitted by a power function as shown in the graph).

It is understandable that less crowded hospitals were generally considered an amenity and contributed to better accessibility for residents. Due to the complex

Here the Urban–Rural Classification Scheme for counties by the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS 2006) was adopted to examine the variability across urbanization levels. Figure 6 shows the average accessibility scores and average crowdedness values across the six categories, respectively. The results from 2SFCA and i2SFCA were consistent in revealing the rural–urban disparities in hospital resource allocation between the most urbanized large central metro and the least urbanized noncore but differed to some extent in areas in the middle range of urbanization levels. The rural disadvantage in health care is a constant observation in numerous studies (Stanford eCampus Rural Health 2010) and is likely to worsen in the United States as an increasing number of rural hospitals are closed (Cecil G. Validation and Discussion

From the derivation of i2SFCA earlier, the crowdedness for a facility is simply the ratio of projected population served versus the supply capacity at the facility. In our case study, it is potential patients per bed. Based on Equation 3, estimated total patients in a hospital \( j \), \( V_j \), is written as \( V_j = C_j S_j \), which is the product of crowdedness and hospital capacity in bed size, termed total crowdedness.

How good is this estimate? Figure 7 shows that the total crowdedness values correlated with actual numbers of patients discharged by hospitals with \( R^2 = 0.65 \). This validates the i2SFCA method as a good measure of potential saturation or stress level of service supply at individual facilities.

The i2SFCA method yields a direct measure for the saturation or stress level for facilities and offers some guidance on public policy that targets facilities. Take the hospital care as an example. One might examine its possible linkage to stress level of staff and waiting time of patients in hospitals and whether these indicators lead to certain satisfaction levels rated by patients. The relative value of potential crowdedness indicates possible understaffing or surplus in staff and suggests adjustment to mitigate the problem. For instance, one might formulate a planning problem with an objective of minimizing the variability of crowdedness across hospitals in allocating available new resources in health care planning.

Conclusion

This article introduces the i2SFCA method in contrast to the popular 2SFCA method. Whereas the 2SFCA is resident oriented and assesses accessibility of a service, the i2SFCA method measures potential crowdedness for facilities providing the service (or scarcity of resource or intensity of competition). The formulation and implementation of i2SFCA is to simply switch the demands and associated items with those for the supplies. The derivation of i2SFCA from an extension of the classic Huff model further solidifies its rationale and, in essence, it is the ratio of estimated demand served versus the supply capacity at a facility. The derivation further justifies the rationale for the regular 2SFCA to measure accessibility for residents.

The i2SFCA method is illustrated and validated by a case study of evaluating hospital inpatient services in Florida. One important property is that the weighted means of 2SFCA-derived accessibility and i2SFCA-derived crowdedness in the study area are the reciprocal of each other. The study indicates that areas with better accessibility by resident tend to have hospitals of less crowdedness nearby. The relationship is hardly monotonic, however, and the two methods have distinctive emphases for different purposes, usually in different scales. Some might use the i2SFCA method alone to assess the disparity of resource allocation from the perspective of service providers, and others might use it concurrently with the 2SFCA method to have the dual perspectives of service providers and receivers. The i2SFCA method provides a direct measure of potential crowdedness for facilities and might guide public policy that targets facilities (e.g., adjusting their capacities) to achieve a fair allocation of public resources.
