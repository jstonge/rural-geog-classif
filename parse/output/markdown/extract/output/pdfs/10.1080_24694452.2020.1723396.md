This qualitative study, based on fifty-two focus groups, interviews, and participant observation within a 10-km buffer around Sariska Tiger Reserve in Rajasthan, India, builds on Monica Ogra’s foundational work bringing together feminist political ecology and human–wildlife conflict studies. Specifically, it exposes gender-based violence as a hidden cost of the socioenvironmental network of the tiger reserve landscape. This study asks these questions: How do gendered geographies in and around a protected area influence tiger reintroduction, and how do tiger reintroductions influence gendered geographies? What is the nature of the relationships between women’s economic and gender roles and attitudes toward tigers (original and reintroduced), and what are the main factors influencing this relationship? This research finds that (1) gender-based violence is a hidden cost of women working in and around Sariska and the reintroduced tigers, a hidden cost of human–wildlife conflict otherwise unnoted in the literature, (2) this hidden cost is not solely the product of human–wildlife encounters but in large part a consequence of the highly patriarchal society that dictates gendered human–environmental relations. The results and presented framework seek to inform developing debates and theory around just conservation, gender-based violence in relation to environmental change, human dimensions of apex predator conservation, and sustainable rural livelihoods in and adjacent to protected areas. Key Words: conservation, feminist political ecology, gender-based violence, well-being.

In 2005, Sariska Tiger Reserve (Sariska) in Rajasthan represented the lowest point in India’s history of tiger conservation. Despite the Indian government spending millions of dollars to protect the endangered species (*Panthera tigris tigris*), Sariska’s entire resident Bengal tiger population was extirpated by 2005. As recently as 2001, Project Tiger, the national tiger conservation program reported twenty-two and deemed Sariska well protected. The official tiger census of 2001–2002 indicated at least twenty-six tigers in Sariska, but the last sighting was in November 2004, after which no signs of tigers were noted. This lack of tiger activity prompted the Ministry of Environment and Forests to mandate an “intensive search.” In addition, the Indian prime minister and the chief minister of Rajasthan both called on nongovernmental organizations like the Wildlife Institute of India and the World Wildlife Fund to conduct their own field studies. They concluded that Sariska was undisputably tigerless as a result of poaching, local retaliatory killings, and poor park management (Wildlife Institute of India 2013).

Sariska’s tiger extirpation led then-Prime Minister Manmohan Singh to create the Tiger Task Force. What had been Project Tiger was reinvigorated, given more authority, and renamed the National Tiger Conservation Authority and the Wildlife Crime Control Bureau in 2006. On the ground, the Indian government went into immediate action in 2008 and 2009 by reintroducing tigers to Sariska; this was the first tiger reintroduction in the world. Thus, Sariska’s temporary tigerless status activated major changes to Indian and international tiger conservation. First, the loss motivated changes to Indian policy toward rapid assessment, resource allocation, and strengthened tiger conservation. Second, reintroduction became a ready tool for tiger conservation in India. In 2017, the National Tiger Conservation Authority released a statement of their intent for reintroductions in four other national parks—Buxa in West Bengal, Mukundra Hills and Rajaji National Park in Rajasthan, and Satkosia in Odisha (Pandey 2017). Third, the reintroduction set a new global precedent because there are proposals for reintroductions in several tiger range areas (e.g., Cambodia [Gray et al. 2017], Central Asia [Cheslin et al. 2017], south China [Qin et al. 2015], and Southeast Asia [Lynam 2010]).

As reintroduction becomes integral to tiger conservation, it is critical to note that more than 70 percent of India’s tiger habitat is in multiple-use forests, where human settlement and activity is a conspicuous landscape element (Ahearn et al. 2001). In this context, the social consequences of tiger reintroduction are as important as the ecological considerations for long-term reintroduction success. Apprehensions about the social consequences of conservation are rooted in historic injustice and ongoing negative social impacts to communities living in and adjacent to protected areas (e.g., Goldman 2009; Greiner 2012; Shahabuddin and Bhamidipati 2014; Chhatre, Lakhanpal, and Prasanna 2017). Conservation of biodiversity and protected areas can also provide benefits to human communities, such as livelihood provision through tourism, financial gains, sustainable management, participatory governance, and the safeguarding of heritage sites and cultural resources (e.g., Timko and Satterfield 2008; Andam et al. 2010). Within the scientific literature on the varied social impacts of conservation (e.g., Rastogi et al. 2012; Ahmadi et al. 2015; Bennett et al. 2017) and, more specifically, scholarship on well-being and conservation (Dhakal, Nelson, and Smith 2011; Leisher et al. 2013; Lovell et al. 2015), this article provides an analysis of what Ogra (2008) termed the *hidden costs* of conservation in the case of human–tiger entanglements in Sariska. Attention to these hidden costs reveals the fraught links among gender, labor, patriarchy, and large carnivores in a conservation landscape. Specifically, this study argues that women might suffer domestic abuse as a result of complex relationships between patriarchal society (Dutt 2019), gendered labor roles, and tiger reintroduction. These complex links necessitate a feminist political ecology (FPE) analysis of the
larger socioenvironmental network within which human–wildlife conflict takes place.

Ogra (2008) defined hidden costs as “those characterized by one or more of the following traits: (a) uncompensated, (b) temporally delayed, or (c) psychological or social in nature” (1410); these illuminate the indirect costs of human–wildlife conflict outside of purely economic loss. Although geographers are increasingly highlighting hidden costs of human–wildlife conflict (e.g., Holmes and Brockington 2012; Jadhav and Barua 2012; Barua, Bhagwat, and Jadhav 2013), by definition, “they remain largely overlooked” (Bond and Mkutu 2017, 2). The question of well-being and, especially, the well-being of women living under restrictive patriarchal societies within flagship endangered species conservation priority areas (e.g., tiger reserves) has been drastically understudied, with a few exceptions (see, e.g., Chowdhury et al. 2008). To this end, I ask these questions: How do gendered geographies in and around a protected area influence tiger reintroduction, and how do tiger reintroductions influence gendered geographies? What is the nature of the relationship between women’s economic and gender roles and attitudes toward tigers (original and reintroduced), and what are the main factors influencing this relationship?

To answer these questions, I employ an FPE framework conceived on the lines described in Rocheleau, Thomas-Slayter, and Wangari (1996) and demonstrated by Nightingale’s (2011) study of the ecological materiality of space in producing gendered subjectivities. I argue that hidden costs of tiger reintroduction in Sariska are unevenly distributed along the lines of gender, as a result of a broader context of patriarchal hierarchies and expectations of women and of a gendered division of labor. The FPE framework facilitates the focus on gendered everyday practices and micro-politics of well-being, security, and people–park relations with consequences for tiger conservation that are key to uncovering these hidden costs. Looking within the household, this research finds gender-based violence (GBV) against women2 derived from gendered divisions of labor as a yet-identified psychological or social hidden cost of conservation (Ogra 2008). These costs materialize in many ways, with the most prominent being a higher risk of domestic abuse3 highlighted by twenty women-only focus groups (WOGFs). Specifically, this study examines women’s embodied consequences of living in households controlled by patriarchal power relations where female family members are socialized “to accept hierarchical relations expressed in an unequal division of labor between the sexes” (Mathur 2004, 97) and within a conservation landscape dedicated to protecting endangered tigers.

Literature Review

Geographers have made significant contributions to the study of human–wildlife conflict through (1) developing frameworks and methods to measure conflict frequency and temporality; (2) detailing social, political, and cultural challenges to mitigating conflict; and (3) emphasizing direct causes, impacts, and economic costs of conflict (e.g., Brandon et al. 1998; Terborgh et al. 2002; Woodroffe et al. 2005; Ogra 2008). Yet, qualitative assessment of the multifaceted perceptions, sensitivity to multiple factors of influence, and costs of often hidden consequences of living alongside wildlife, especially for women, is still an emerging area of research. Research of this kind within the context of large carnivore reintroduction is even sparser. This study contributes to initial efforts to identify these hidden costs of conservation to women in developing countries living within high-profile landscapes of apex predator reintroductions.

Feminist researchers have made clear the linkages between household dynamics and differential control over resources (e.g., Boserup 1970; Sen 1981; Agarwal 1987; Folbre 1988; Nightingale 2006; Elmhirst 2011), as well as the struggles over gendered divisions of labor and livelihood security (Sultana 2011; Truelove 2011; Radel 2012; Mollett and Faria 2013). FPE scholars have furthered this work by reconceptualizing the physical environment in women’s lives as part of the household (e.g., Leach 1992; Carney 1996; Rocheleau, Thomas-Slayter, and Wangari 1996; Pearson and Jackson 1998), “casting the ‘environment’ as at once a source of physical asset as well as cultural, economic, and even domestic spaces” (Ogra 2008, 1409). Gender is a critical variable in shaping resource control and access that interacts with age, family structure, economic exchange, and other differences that affect processes of ecological change (Rocheleau, Thomas-Slayter, and Wangari 1996). Gender–nature–society relationships are not only suspended between material needs and access to natural resources but these relations are formed by “particular regimes of cultural meaning that in turn shape social relations” (Mollett and Faria 2013, 117) and substantially affect the
landscape. This research combines these feminist endeavors to address the lack of “deeper more meaningful analysis of how power relations operate to stimulate women and men in different forested landscapes; why certain individuals and groups are or remain, marginalized; and what role research can play in promoting gender and social justice” through an FPE lens of women’s socioenvironmental experience (Colfer, Sijapati Basnett, and Ihlainen 2018, 42). In doing so, this research furthers our knowledge on how women’s socioenvironmental experience can “contribute to unique experiences of oppression and privilege” (Association for Women’s Rights in Development 2004, 3) in conservation landscapes.

Further, patriarchy “shap[e]s and inform[s] women’s subordination and oppression” (Chua, Bhavnani, and Foran 2000, 823), and more comprehensive analyses are needed that take seriously the impacts of patriarchal power relations in both the methods and the findings of FPE research and in contexts relevant for conservation. My approach in this article contributes to remedying this shortcoming in its focus on women’s complex role in a patriarchal society and their existence within conservation networks. By attending to embodied experiences, I consider how women experience risk, labor, and cohabitation with tigers within the unique context of tiger reintroduction to address the need for political ecological analyses that are attentive to these patriarchal entanglements.

Methods and Study Area

As part of India’s second expansion of Project Tiger in the late 1970s, Sariska Wildlife Reserve was upgraded to the status of Sariska Tiger Reserve. In 1982, 274 km\(^2\) of the reserve also became a national park. Based on the National Tiger Conservation Authority’s (2010) specifications that viable tiger populations require at least an area of 800 to 1,000 km\(^2\) with limited to no human activity, the Government of Rajasthan (GOR) delineated 881.11 km\(^2\) of Sariska as “critical tiger habitat” in 2007. This included the Reserve Forest (602.97 km\(^2\)) and the Protected Forest (276.14 km\(^2\)) in Sariska (GOR 2004). In 2016, the Forest Department added an area of 332 km\(^2\) as a buffer zone, bringing the total area to 1,213 km\(^2\) (Bhardwaj 2016). Twenty-six villages are located inside Sariska’s critical tiger habitat (Joseph 2017), and forty more adjoin Sariska’s boundaries (Jain and Sajjad 2016). Thus, as most Indian tiger reserves, Sariska maintains a high human presence inside and adjacent to its boundaries.

Local residents’ dependence on the forest complicates implementation of the park’s conservation policies. Year-round grazing of the thousands of livestock in the reserve results in significant ecological stresses (Jain and Sajjad 2016). The immense biomass extraction from villagers living inside and along Sariska’s periphery greatly threatens the integrity of the ecosystem (GOR 2004) and tiger reintroduction success. With these stresses and regionally increasing human population,\(^4\) milk production is a livelihood at odds with the health and sustainability of Sariska’s ecosystem.\(^5\)

To reduce villagers’ biotic pressures on the landscape, the Forest Department has employed restrictions on grazing, new construction, producing mawa (which requires ample firewood), and agriculture (Jain and Sajjad 2016). These restrictions have, of course, led to people–park conflict. Conflict between local communities and protected area management personnel is common and often escalates when laws are implemented to limit or prohibit access to natural resources inside protected areas (Keleman, Goodale, and Dooley 2010; Shahabuddin 2014). Within this fraught conservation context, women’s intimate relationship with Sariska as a place of work, resources, and risk has been generally overlooked.

This research, in part, seeks to be exemplary in finding the questions we need to ask to avoid failure from underestimated social impacts. To do so, a variety of qualitative research methods were undertaken. Fifty-two focus groups (FGs) over the course of three fieldwork stages provided a variety of individual perceptions from diverse stakeholders, with various livelihoods and differing proximities to Sariska and tiger territories. FGs conducted in 2014 and 2015 were mixed-gender FGs (MGFGs), yet answers and discussion primarily came from the male participants. This gender imbalance, along with new areas of participant-driven concerns specific to women’s experiences, reflected the influence of patriarchy on women’s lives and on my research and necessitated the implementation of WOFGs, which I carried out in 2016 and 2017 (see Doubleday and Adams [2019] for details on the application of grounded theory, allowing for unexpected linkages to be prioritized in the research). Women in these groups, then, felt safe and comfortable disclosing their views on topics of
tiger conservation, livelihood, risk, and household dynamics. Indeed, for women to tell stories safely, patriarchal social structures must be addressed (Mannell, Ahmad, and Ahmad 2018)—in methods, in theory, and in our empirical work. Because the lead translator and myself are married women, a question we were always asked, this also shaped our engagement by gaining respectability in that experience. Yet, my ability to understand married life in India and the expectations placed on married women is limited by the vast difference in lived experience specifically affecting what marriage means and implies in the United States versus in India, as well as more general cultural differences. I lean on Indian scholars and the words of 160 women who participated in the FGs to guide my understanding.

Through criterion and opportunistic sampling, a total of 256 people participated in the thirty-two MGFGs (2014, 2015), and 160 women participated in the twenty WOFGs (2016, 2017), for a total of 416 FG participants. The data for this study were collected in a 10-km buffer around Sariska (Figure 1). Of the FGs, 75 percent (n = 39) were conducted with communities that live 5 km or less from Sariska; 55 percent (n = 11) were either inside Sariska, on the boundary, or within 1 km of Sariska’s boundary. Age of participants was the heterogeneous element used to stratify the FGs, because age is presumed to be a factor in attitudes toward tigers. Heterogeneous age then allowed for generational disagreement and insight into how tigers, conservation, and Sariska are perceived over time and by different generations to highlight “untidy geographies” or “contextual differences … [in] experiences across time and space” (Bosco and Herman 2010, 199)—all topics seldom discussed among themselves.6 In addition, the study area was divided into four quadrants with thirteen FGs per quadrant to capture the varied forms of human–tiger encounters and histories across the plane of spatially inconsistent removal, reintroduction, and tiger dispersal. Translators with linguistic, locational, and contextual sensitivities were employed throughout this research project. Pseudonyms are used for all participants in this study.

Interviews, participant observation, and walking tours were also conducted. Forty interviews were conducted with one or two individuals at varying lengths. These interviews took place after logistical meetings for future FG discussions, after FG discussions, or during participant observation of human–wildlife conflict resolution. These observations took place while volunteering more than 300 hours with self-described “wildlife rescuers” in and around Sariska, primarily relocating snakes. Families and neighbors would often talk with us at length about wildlife and Sariska during and after these removals.

Findings

Kaplan-Hallam and Bennet (2017) provided a conceptual representation of human well-being for social impact assessments in environmental management. The framework has five domains of “human well-being,” conceptualized as an individual’s or communities’ values, goals, and experiences (Breslow et al. 2016): governance, economic, health, social, and cultural. Within each of these domains are several sub-domains. In Sariska, each of these domains for women’s well-being is affected by (1) milk production as the primary livelihood, (2) patriarchal society, and (3) Sariska’s management for conservation. All three of these preconditions interlink to greatly affect women’s well-being, experiences, and attitudes (Figure 2).

These connections (Figure 2) were revealed through grounded theory–guided qualitative research applying the lens and framework of FPE. Together this FPE research shows how women’s well-being must be considered as a significant facet of long-term tiger conservation in human–tiger landscapes.

The following sections present empirical findings of FGs in narrative form, concentrating on the hidden costs of tiger reintroduction. The quotes that follow are all from WOFG participants’ descriptions of their experiences within this socioenvironmental landscape. Two major findings follow: (1) Hidden costs outside the home include heightened risk of tiger encounter for women (and buffalo), which increases fear, risk of punishment from the government, and sacrifices to their well-being to maintain their status as “good Indian wives,” and (2) hidden costs inside the home materialize as GBV against women if they do not live up to the ideal of a “good Indian wife,” due to injury or predation decreasing their economic productivity. Thus, human–tiger cohabitation within this patriarchal society coupled with women’s gendered labor roles results in the hidden cost of GBV. An FPE framework that takes seriously these factors can offer a more nuanced understanding of just conservation debates (Pooley and Redpath 2018; Vucetich et al. 2018) and human–wildlife conflict going forward.

Cultural Norms and the “Good Indian Wife”: Expectations, Risk, and Tiger Reintroduction

The findings detailed here are contextual to two major cultural factors of the landscape: the need to uphold the expectations of “good Indian women” combined with their low position within a patrilineal, patrilocal hierarchy of households (Doubleday and Adams 2019). Haq (2013) stated that “good Indian women” are brought up to accept “that suffering in this life is bearable by hoping for a better life after death, only if they are patient, pious and persistent in graciously bearing the burden of this life through self-sacrifice” (174). Although there might be caste-based differences of performativity of the ideal “good Indian wife,” this standard is in large part sustained by a masculine identity that “cuts through distinctions of caste and class” in much of India (Vindhya 2007, 349; see also Lau [2014] for analysis of the “good Indian woman” trope in Indian women’s writings and Gold [2015] for discussion on the related ideal of women as pativrata [a husband-devotee]). The risk that women incur by venturing into tiger territory is for the welfare of their families, which, Ogra (2008) pointed out (in the case of Indian women at risk from elephants), supports the views that exposing oneself to animal-related risk can be part of the expected role of a “good Indian woman”—an ideal enforced by relatives, community, the state, and religion.

The household unit in the Sariska landscape is defined by a patriarchal hierarchy, with variations from household to household depending on the number of children. For this research, the perspective of the daughter-in-law (a category most women embody for the majority of their lives) is central. Within the patrilineal, patrilocal household,7 all resident women are “outsiders,” with new wives, of course, feeling this position most starkly. A daughter-in-law occupies the precarious position at the bottom of the household. Her in-laws are at the top, her husband is next, and then her sons; she and her daughters are at the bottom.8 Because of their position in this hierarchy and their typical youth, compared to their mothers-in-law, daughters-in-law take over the difficult tasks that require the most time and physical exertion inside tiger territories (forests). Any fluctuation in women’s roles as providers is a great danger and a substantial risk to their family and to their children’s health, to their social status, and to their own well-being. These risks manifest as hidden costs both outside and inside the home, shaping women’s lives and perceptions of conservation.

Finding 1: Hidden Costs Outside the Home

In addition to the “good Indian woman” expectations and patriarchal household setting, women’s distinctly gendered labor roles became evident during WOFGs. Specifically, in the Sariska landscape, women are responsible for feeding their families, raising children, housekeeping, farming and gathering fuelwood, and tending to livestock. Local residents are highly dependent on Sariska for their livestock-based livelihoods. This dependency is multifaceted but dominated by the need for green fodder to produce domestic Murrah water buffalo (Bubalus bubalis) milk for consumption and sale, which involves a gendered division of labor and gendered expectations regarding the allocation of household capital. Buffalo milk is the primary source of income inside (Jain and Sajjad 2016) and within Sariska’s periphery (Shahabuddin, Kumar, and Shrivastava 2007), and this work is overwhelmingly performed by women.9 According to the WOFGs, 75 percent of respondents considered buffalo milk a primary livelihood and the most reliable source of cash: “milk is main source of income, we sell milk then we buy food and clothes for our kids” (~6 km from Sariska). This is in contrast with most men’s intermittent work, which often includes farming or chopping wood (for the women to collect and bring back), working in nearby towns selling produce, working in cities,10 mining, or working as laborers11; this work is often sporadic or seasonal.

To feed a half-ton buffalo, women spend several hours a day collecting fodder from Sariska. Although it is not uncommon for women to shepherd their buffalo into Sariska, many leave the buffalo at home because “in the jungle [tigers] will kill them, then what will we do?” Instead, the women spend three to four hours every day collecting fodder from the jungle for their buffalo; “it’s not worth risking the buffalo’s lives”—but it is necessary to risk their own lives (~4 km from Sariska). Averaging the hours reported by women from all twenty WOFGs, four hours are spent inside Sariska or the buffer zone daily collecting fodder (primarily), wood, and other forest products. Older women often talked about the difficulty of collecting fodder, because it requires extensive walking in Sariska’s rocky terrain. When discussing the time spent in the forests, a fifty-year-
old woman said, “Cannot bring [fodder and wood] in one go, sometimes two or three times we need to go to the jungle … we could be attacked [by predators]” (~6 km from Sariska). The group added their agreement and noted that each trip requires them to walk 5 to 6 km each way: “We need to go so far. The fear of tigers is there. Sometimes it may eat one of us” (~6 km from Sariska). Across FGs, women reported walking just over 11 km per trip for fodder or wood, with the majority of women citing two trips a day as average. We asked, “Going this far, do the forest guards ever stop you?” They said, “Yes, they stop us. But we tell them if we will not go there, how would we eat?” The women said it was necessary because they were not able to save much money, and their husbands only earned Rs 100 to Rs 200 per day for labor work, compared with Rs 270 or more per day from selling milk.

Three daughters-in-law summarized their fear, and the rest of the WOFG agreed wholeheartedly:

Geeta: We are scared, but we cannot keep our buffalo starving.

Neera: Yes, if we do not give them fodder, how would we get milk? And how would we earn money?

Priya: We run our house with that money only. This is how our children survive. (~2 km from Sariska)

I asked the last woman in this exchange to explain what she meant by “we run our house with that money only.” She explained her husband’s pattern of on-and-off labor employment, which other women echoed; his jobs do not provide a consistent income for daily needs. Women in all twenty WOFGs described similar circumstances, agreeing that men’s work does not consistently provide to guarantee daily needs—milk is the means of daily survival for most.

Usha, one of the older mothers-in-law in this group, pointed to her daughter-in-law and said, “She milks the cow, sells it, and earns the money. … When [the husband] gets the money, they are happy with their wives.” Roopa, the daughter-in-law, replied, “Women are responsible for this, to get the grass, take care of the buffalo, half our life is spent on this. It’s our duty” (~6 km from Sariska). This weighty responsibility means that expectations are high and disruption of milk sales could incur mistreatment—the major form of disruption in Sariska is a tiger, leopard, or Forest Department guards restricting grazing and punishing women for fodder collection, punishment that women are well aware is connected to tiger conservation.

Women are at higher risk of repercussions from the Forest Department, because their responsibilities for resource collection are illegal. Even though there are restrictions on cattle grazing, the Forest Department is reportedly stricter with wood collection. Because women are primarily responsible for grazing and wood collection, they are at greater risk of official punishment that could also lead to family displeasure or sacrifice (children not being able to eat). When difficulties of Forest Department guard encounters emerged, we asked how women avoided detection. One of the young daughters-in-law in the group named Chetna said:

Chetna: We send them [buffalo] at night.

Translator: Okay, and if the tiger eat your buffalo?

Chetna: We go with them.

Translator: But, even in the night?

Chetna: We take a stick with us. (~2 km from Sariska)

Women and buffalo, then, can be exposed to even more risk during the dry season when those who cannot afford to buy fodder resort to taking their buffalo into Sariska during late evenings and nights—when tigers are active (6–9 p.m. and midnight–3 a.m.; Modal et al. 2012). The risks posed to women (and buffalos) outside the home create a surrounding landscape of fear and contribute to the factors of sacrifice that women must make to embody the “good Indian woman.”

Finding 2: Hidden Costs Inside the Home

The web of dangers to which women are subjected in providing for their families extends beyond risks from predators and the Forest Department to encompass risk from their families. Many studies have traced domestic violence against Indian women to “alleged failure of the wife to fulfill household responsibilities and obligations of a ‘good wife’” (Vindhya 2007, 349; see also Dhawan et al. 1999; Sriram and Mukherjee 2001). This scenario manifests in Sariska if women do not live up to the ideal of a “good Indian wife,” due to injury or predation decreasing their economic productivity, and they suffer a hidden cost of GBV. Any GBV a daughter-in-law suffers from not meeting expectations is considered the daughter-in-law’s fault
(Kaushik 2003). Therefore, women’s, and particularly daughters-in-law’s, subjectivities are rooted in their marginalized status within everyday life, Sariska, the network, and their labor responsibilities. Their spatiality (contextual factors) emerges from their performances as working mothers, providers, livestock feeders, and, obligatorily, tiger challengers. Underlining this embodiment is the need to replicate traditional Hindu standards of a “good Indian wife.” Although women’s larger exposure to risk from human–wildlife conflict has been recognized by others, of primary interest here is the revelation of human–tiger cohabitation within the rural Indian patriarchal society combines with women’s gender roles to result in the hidden cost of GBV.12

In Sariska, “good Indian wives” must enter precarious areas to secure their family’s survival and meet family expectations (only sometimes the same). Many women’s ability to meet financial expectations has been affected, however, by the extirpation and reintroduction of tigers. During the tigerless period (2004–2008, with limited presence before and after this time frame depending on tiger mobilities), fodder collection and grazing were “better” and thus the milk production, at times, was higher. Women feel that their families hold them to these past expectations when milk production was easier. WOFGs often turned to men’s lack of appreciation for how tiger reintroduction has made all the “pressures more heavy” (~10 km from Sariska). The tigers’ return ushered in a new boundary wall, tall enough to keep livestock out of the park (where it is erected) but not tall enough to keep in wild ungulates or predators. The wall’s primary function, then, is to restrict fodder collection as part of the broader enforcement of conservation laws. These restrictions and their enforcement vary across the study area, but there is a consensus from women that it is more difficult to provide enough fodder for their livestock than before the tigers’ reintroduction.

When a WOFG turned to difficulties of collecting enough fodder, including fear of sighting tigers, we asked, “So do the men come and ask why the supply has reduced?”

Radha: Yes, they do fight us about this.

Translator: Really?

Aparna: When the men return from the fields, they do ask.

Shruti: Yes, they do ask if there is money … why the production has reduced?

Chhaya: Yes, they are just concerned about the milk production … how and when they are not bothered about. (~6 km from Sariska)

The women went on to explain that daily milk production is very sensitive. In all sixteen WOFGs where buffalo milk is a primary livelihood, women described a typical pattern of not going to the forest for two or three days after a tiger sighting to protect themselves and livestock (e.g., temporally delayed hidden cost). The delay comes with another substantial hidden cost: “It is that day … they [buffalo] will not produce. It is a challenge,” a woman said of her buffalo’s inability to graze after a tiger sighting. Women in all of these WOFGs stressed the immediate drop in milk production when buffalos are fed substandard fodder, significantly reducing cash flow: “That is the problem, we are not able to meet expenses, and we have a big problem in the home” (~3 km from Sariska). Consequently, during these two or three days of waiting, a woman described her anxiety at home: “Without cash [milk sales], we do not want to stay home” (~6 km from Sariska). The translator asked for clarification on “not [wanting] to stay home,” to which Chhaya, a daughter-in-law, said, “beatings,” as her neighbor sitting next to her nodded and met my eye.

During this part of WOFGs, mothers-in-law repeatedly verbalized their understanding of the situation; it is not their daughters-in-law’s fault for lower milk production as a result of truncated fodder collection and grazing after tiger sightings. Yet, they said that their voice did not reach the ears of their husbands and sons: Men had consistently high expectations. The reintroduction has not uniformly affected male family members’ expectations (for lower production); thus, women’s risk of GBV from not meeting expectations has increased because milk sales are expected to stay constant.

A difficult conversation followed, adding more detail: “[Men] are just concerned about milk production … not bothered” about “how” the milk is produced. The women said that men’s concern for production supersedes their worry for the women. Women discussed verbal abuse and physical abuse in the moments “cash is discussed … beatings—it is all happening here.” Radha, a thirty-one-year-old woman, added, “A tiger does not matter to [my husband]” when the milk quantity does not meet his expectations. Shruti added, “We are living with our [in-laws], and it is expected … what we see [tigers]
does not matter” (~6 km from Sariska). In thirteen of the sixteen WOFGs citing buffalo milk as a primary livelihood, participants described physical abuse (over the last four years) as a consequence of not meeting milk production expectations as a result of tiger presence (i.e., a temporally delayed hidden cost), which subsequently materialized into a psychological or social cost (Ogra 2008) that has otherwise gone unnoticed as a hidden cost of human–wildlife conflict.

In all twenty WOFGs, domestic violence was verbally confirmed as a regular occurrence by at least one WOFG participant, to varying degrees. Verbal abuse was also regarded as frequent and touching every family. These repeated experiences “reinforce gendered and classed social differences materially shaping and constraining physical hardships and life opportunities” (Truelove 2011, 147). This is not to say that men do not likewise have precarious work. Rather, it is to say that men’s impermeable position at the top of the family hierarchy affords them little fear of repercussion or risk (e.g., GBV) of not meeting expectations. Meanwhile, women’s overall well-being is affected by the many socioenvironmental conditions of their lives. Prerana, a fifty-year-old woman, ended a discussion on working inside Sariska by saying:

We are really very unhappy, very, very unhappy. In the city, there is peace and peace, but here it’s only trouble and trouble. … I leave home at four in the morning to go to forest, just leaving the house as is … return by eleven and just start preparing food for lunch and then do the other house chores. That’s just our daily routine … and to prepare the food for the children also. Then again in the evening we go to forest. Suffering. No happiness at all.

To this older woman’s ominous portrait of life, a younger woman added, “This is Rajasthan, and everyone over here is distressed” (~6 km from Sariska). Although this was the only statement referencing the whole of Rajasthan, nine WOFGs included a similar ending, with the women making a generalized statement about their condition of unhappiness—and a general lack of well-being.

Mathur (2007) synthesized why violence against women is so ingrained in everyday life in Rajasthan as follows: “The woman’s body is continuously made to fit and mold to societal expectations with a severe denial of rights, her bodily integrity constantly violated” (3). Expectations are linked to obedience in this cultural context (Bradley 2010), which often is generalized to obedience in gendered divisions of labor.

Scholars widely credit patriarchy as “responsible for creating and maintaining a system that leaves women vulnerable to male control, which is often violent” (Bradley 2010, 364). Embedded in this patriarchal context is the figure of the “good Indian wife” that involves specific expectations. Women shouldering the burden of an unpredictable and susceptible economic activity (milk production) means that women face GBV when incomes fall short—and their status at the bottom of the hierarchy is compounded.

Discussion

Compounded Risk Conceptual Framework

From these findings, I argue that there is a causal link to GBV because of the (a) cultural and social-context, (b) tiger presence and conflict, (c) access-and management of natural resources, and (d) gendereddivisions of labor and livestock-based livelihoods factors of women’s lives in Sariska (Figure 3). Women in the WOFGs perceive tigers to be a disproportionately weighty factor in their experiences of GBV. These perceived links influence attitudes, which have cascading adverse effects on long-term tiger conservation.

Approached through an FPE framework, this study provides insight into women’s position in society and the home, which results in their spatiality inside Sariska, producing a life of high emotional and physical risk (Figure 3). Male family members’ expectations and repercussions enact place-based divisions of labor that put women in danger outside and inside the home, creating an overlapping and ever-present risk geography. The aim of FPE in this case is to uncover not only the proximate but less obvious coupled lives (human, nonhuman) and context (landscape, culture) of gendered risk within the social–ecological network (humans, environment, culture, and nonhumans) of interlinking subjectivities, described in the previous sections. These data reveal women’s fear and well-being; cohabitation with tigers is not solely dependent on fear of lethal tiger encounters but intensified by a preexisting social–ecological network of subjectivities. Thus, an FPE approach and attention to hidden costs of human–wildlife conflict contribute to the notion that women’s vulnerability is not only gender-based but shaped by the confluence of traditional Indian family hierarchy, religion, environmental

positionality, and other factors (Agrawal and Gibson 2001). As Figure 2 and Figure 3 illustrate, the materialization of this risk is a result of a complex socio-environmental network. Also evident is that tiger reintroduction alone does not create risk but several that converging factors produce the hidden cost of GBV. These theoretical contributions bring together diverse bodies of literature and elicit new ways of thinking about policy as well.

Applied to Ogra’s Hidden Costs Model

The findings previously outlined provide further evidence that the three primary hidden costs of human–wildlife conflict (Ogra 2008) are not isolated but that (1) uncompensated costs and (2) temporally delayed costs can lead to (3) psychological or social costs to women, as a result of the socioenvironmental network in which human–wildlife conflict is embedded. Women have influence and contribute to intrahousehold and extrahousehold (Agarwal 1997) decisions and attitudes. Intrahousehold decisions revolve around livelihood, and family welfare and extrahousehold decisions influence community ideas and policies. As the principal caregivers, women can communicate and strengthen knowledge and positive attitudes in their children, which has enduring effects for conservation (Carter and Allendorf 2016). As Carter and Allendorf (2016) pointed out, women have power at the community level to help ensure that tigers are conserved and laws are followed (Doss 2013). They argued that the more knowledgeable and positive women are about tigers, the safer tigers will be. Understanding and then addressing hidden costs of human–tiger cohabitation have to be precursors to that aim (Figure 4).

Tiger presence and the sometimes consequential attack on people (women of concern here) and cattle (women’s responsibility) are represented by (b); access to natural resources and park management are represented in (c). Moreover, the livestock-based livelihood of milk sales, which is highly gender based, is represented in (d). All of these internal factors shape the devaluation of women and girls and the larger human–environmental relations (attitudes, management, etc.) that affect each other and shape women’s risk of GBV. For instance, in attempts to alleviate the burden of negative side effects of conservation and mitigate future conflict, compensation for loss of cattle or human life is often standard policy. Damage compensation aims to reduce the economic burden and financial risk for those living alongside carnivores (Loveridge et al. 2010). Much of the discussion on predator conservation in India centers on reimbursements for livestock predation or postdeath compensation. Although this is an important component of tiger conservation, many scholars advocate strategies that target attitudes, arguing that compensation does little in the way of changing public perception of tigers. This study demonstrates new thinking, in that not only is the financial value of livestock at risk but so is the value of women for whom the well-being of livestock is essential for stabilizing household relations. This is important to conservation because economic losses from predation result in considerable local animosity toward tigers and support for activities that undercut tiger conservation (e.g., forest fires, hunting, poisoning wildlife, facilitating poachers, etc.; Johnsingh and Madhusudan 2009). This research highlights the hidden costs of current gendered divisions of labor that negatively influence women’s well-being in many ways outside of economics. These
negative experiences affect how women perceive tigers, which percolates to their children (Figure 4). The hidden costs described here add significant emphasis to the necessity of adapting livelihood practices to conserve endangered tigers and benefit a local community’s stability (as others have also argued; e.g., Nyhus and Tilson 2004; Chowdhury et al. 2008). The compound risk conceptual framework (Figure 2) provides guidance for the analysis of other complex human–environmental landscapes.

Implications for Conservation

Women are noted as major actors in Sariska’s degradation through grazing and resource collection (GOR 2004). Women are easily blamed as a result, overlooking the household dynamics that place them in this position. This research shows that the household and broader social systems reinforce gendered divisions of labor that support livelihood practices that transfer and complicate accountability.

GBV and risk of injury or death via human–wildlife conflict is dependent on silence. The Rajasthan Forest Department (and by extension the policies and forest guards who enforce them) silence women’s risk. The fact that grazing inside and outside of Sariska is illegal, in addition to wood and fodder gathering, which is primarily a woman’s role and more often punished by the Forest Department, means that women must be silent while working in Sariska. I hypothesize that because women, by a vast majority, undertake this risk and are socially less vocal, there is hesitancy in demand for livelihood adaptation. The current push for better compensation and village relocations result in visible, measurable outcomes toward the Forest Department’s goals for tiger conservation but, in reality, maintain women’s precarious role in supporting families in and adjacent to Sariska. In contrast, changing husbandry practices and familial expectations is slower and more difficult but might provide more powerful benefits to tiger conservation and women’s well-being. The insights here further demonstrate that changes to livestock husbandry must be conceptualized through women’s experiences if they are to be successful.

The Forest Department has put forth ambitious socioenvironmental plans to improve Sariska’s habitat and suitability for forty to fifty tigers. Sariska’s Tiger Conservation Plan (GOR 2004) includes ambitious goals for halting grazing in the park through multiple initiatives: village relocation, expanding periphery walls, creating alternate grazing areas, improving milk yields through breed improvement, promoting stall feeding,13 encouraging farmers to grow fodder crops rather than cash crops, “promoting non-cattle-based livelihood options,” and stringent law enforcement (GOR 2004, 147). It is encouraging to see official documentation outlining these goals. Implementation, however, will continue to be very difficult due to politics, tradition, and logistics. This research specifically supports the Forest Department’s proposal and would argue for the need to subsidize and intensify a system of stall-feeding buffalo, which would lessen women’s risk and benefit habitat restoration. According to this research, focusing limited conservation funds into materials and education shifting from free-ranging to stall-fed buffalo husbandry would be cost and socially effective.

Conclusion

In Sariska, women’s personal stories of GBV in relation to tigers create a landscape of fear across home and work. This human landscape is in place because of women’s historical invisibility and consequential low regard of their work. In the context of family dynamics, their roles in tiger territory are downgraded to “necessary” and “not any more difficult.” This devaluing of women’s labor is part of the system that creates higher rates of resentment toward the forest guards, Sariska as a protected area, and the reintroduced tigers (Figure 4). Although women have long been undervalued (Haq 2013), this does not mean that they are not aware of this inequality of work, of risk, and of the disproportionate burden. This is compounded by women’s responsibility to maintain the household, and it materializes in their prioritizing of other family members’ welfare at the cost of their own, which Ogra (2008) hypothesized might lead to greater resentment of carnivores, as this study substantiates (Figures 2–4).

As Chowdhury et al. (2008) argued, to have a practical conservation policy an understanding of the undercurrent of a “negative ecological cycle” operative in tiger landscapes is needed, including insight into the eco-psychiatric aspect (ecological influence on mental health) of conservation: the aftermath and hidden costs of human–wildlife conflicts. In line with the argument of Chowdhury et al. (2008), this research highlights the effect of human–tiger
cohabitation on GBV, which influences eco-psychiatric consequences to women. This more complex understanding of women’s experiences provides a guide for further analysis of the socioenvironmental network. Second, this study’s findings might also enable local and regional women’s groups and nongovernmental organizations to form pertinent narratives to improve ongoing campaigns for more equitable distribution of labor roles and less risky stall feeding.

Feminist scholars have articulated the political struggles involved in making women’s work and daily realities more socially visible and economically viable (Wright 2004). The economic process that devalues women’s labor often comes as the result of the female subject as socially obscure, “as a subject who does not count in history, in the public sphere, in cultural practices, or even in conceptualizations of some place” (Wright 2004, 372). Feminist geographers have illustrated the powerful compounding of this issue based on discourse associated with spatial practices (McDowell 1997; Lawson 1999) that create “sociospatial circuits through which cultural and personal stories are circulated, legitimated, and given meaning” by way of producing space and places (Pratt 1999, 218). “So let us try not to fragment people and their lives,” as Bhasin (2012) implored, but consider “hierarchies, such as those of class, caste, race, and the north–south divide, majority versus minority … try[ing] to understand their interconnection” (2–3). This study seeks to provide an example of this type of nonfragmenting scholarship that considers hierarchies to understand the interworkings of a socioenvironmental network and how they can produce injustice.

In conclusion, research and conservation practices that involve gender-sensitive approaches contribute to more equal and inclusive strategies that strengthen conservation (e.g., Sodhi, Davidar, and Rao 2010). By illuminating why coexistence with carnivores is difficult for women who spend time in protected areas and collect forest products, we can better identify the drivers of negative perceptions and find ways to improve coexistence. This research is part of the larger body of literature redressing the preoccupation of wildlife conservation with masculinist perspectives and promoting the aim of more just conservation (Hunter, Hitchcock, and Wyckoff-Baird 1990; Carter and Linnell 2016). Not only do conservation practices need to be adapted, but pursuits highlighting and protecting women’s rights and well-being must also be promoted. As scholars call for more mitigation in developing countries around human–wildlife conflict and better preparations for reintroductions, do they truly want to “address” the deeply culturally gendered disparities that are the “conflict potential” (Linnell, Nilsen, and Lande 2005, 171) within conservation zones for carnivores? If so, women’s well-being within societies in conservation landscapes must be prioritized to ensure equity in conservation landscapes that do not despairingly disadvantage women based on culturally gendered labor and norms. This study provides analyses, highlighting how an FPE framework of the socioenvironmental can link disparate theory and literatures to illuminate new perspectives of women’s complex role in a patriarchal society and their existence within conservation networks, providing a more nuanced understanding of just conservation and human–wildlife conflict.
