School, Community, and Spatial Equity: An Empirical Investigation of Access to Elementary Schools in West Virginia

Emily Talen
Department of Urban and Regional Planning, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign

To date, only a handful of studies have empirically investigated the relationship between school locations and their constituencies—i.e., the accessibility of schools. This article makes a contribution by presenting an in-depth case study of school accessibility in three counties in West Virginia. The study utilizes an extensive dataset of distances between students and eighty-four elementary school locations. The study examines whether or not the distribution of travel cost between resident locations (blocks) and schools is equitable on the basis of the density of resident populations and the socioeconomic status (SES) of resident populations. The article also carries the analysis one step further, investigating the effect of access on student achievement.

The findings for the first hypothesis indicated that spatial inequities in access to school were substantial and varied by county and school zone. In terms of the relationship between SES and access to schools, results were inconclusive and could be described in the familiar “unpatterned inequality” paradigm supported by a great deal of accessibility research. The analysis involving student achievement showed that distance to school had a significant and inversely related effect on third-grade test scores.

The article concludes by arguing that these exploratory initial findings should be expanded upon in future geography-based studies. First, the finding that significant clusters of poor accessibility indeed exist should be investigated vis-à-vis the implicit assumption in school consolidation planning that school catchment areas conform to some kind of higher rational principle. Second, the findings point to a need for more research regarding the import of socioeconomic status in school accessibility. Finally, the results indicate that access is negatively associated with achievement, but that relationship needs to be scrutinized in greater detail. Key Words: accessibility, community, rural, schools.

The last several decades have witnessed a momentous change in the geographic dynamics of schools. In particular, school consolidation has resulted in substantial alteration of the geography of schools. However, the geographic implications of consolidation have been largely neglected. Given the profound centrality of schools in social, political, and economic life, access to them is far from inconsequential: school locations and the boundaries that create their constituencies dramatically affect the spatial interaction between home, school, and community. To date, only a handful of studies have empirically investigated the relationship between school locations and their constituencies—i.e., the accessibility of schools. This article makes a contribution by presenting an in-depth case study of school accessibility in three counties in West Virginia. The study utilizes an extensive dataset of distances between students and eighty-four elementary school locations. Since the effect of consolidation is especially severe for rural areas, the study focuses on a predominantly rural section of the country.

In general, research by geographers on the issue of schools falls into three categories: multiobjective programming models used to optimize school locations (Church and Murray 1993; November, Cromley, and Cromley 1996); school population forecasting and planning (Taylor, Code, and Goodchild 1986; Rushton, Armstrong, and Lolonis 1995; Parsons, Chalkley, and Jones 1996); and analysis of school outcomes and their determinants (Bradford 1990, 1991; Coombes and Raybould 1997). While accessibility may be one component of these types of predictive inquiries (e.g., access as one variable to optimize), little attention has been focused on revealing the existing equity of access to schools.

In terms of the literature on access, scant research is devoted to school locations. While differential access to various types of services and facilities has been widely studied in geographic research, little effort has been focused specifically on evaluating the spatial patterning of schools. To some extent, this may be due to a lack of geographic data on schools. This article presents the results of an intensive empirical investigation of elementary school access (which involved digitizing the
attendance zone boundaries of eighty-four schools) in an effort to shed light on the role, meaning, and predominance of spatial equity in elementary school access. Given the fact that school access is under-studied at a time when consolidation is increasingly imposed as a remedy for financial burdens, the spatial equity implications of school locations and the changing boundaries that determine school populations need to be investigated.

The study of school accessibility is important for at least three reasons, each of which is explored in this article. First, access is important in terms of how it affects basic questions of fairness—i.e., the degree of spatial inequity that results when some children can walk to school and others endure long bus rides. The question of fairness is investigated by revealing the spatial pattern of school accessibility. Secondly, the accessibility of schools has important implications in terms of social equity, since it may be the case that students of lower socioeconomic status (SES) have less access to schools. This is explored by analyzing the relationship between access and SES. Finally, school access may potentially be related to student performance, and this relationship is also investigated.

In the first part of this article, I lay the groundwork for the significance of the problem of school accessibility, in particular the importance of maintaining a healthy proximity between home and school and between school and community. Having established the proper context, I then address the following main research problems:

1. Is the spatial pattern of elementary schools in rural areas fair; i.e., does it conform to any nonrandom, logical spatial pattern?
2. Does the spatial pattern of elementary schools in rural areas put lower-socioeconomic students at a disadvantage in terms of accessibility?
3. Does the spatial pattern of elementary schools in rural areas have any effect on student achievement?

Background

Consolidation: Bigger Schools and Longer Bus Rides

School consolidation is usually an unwelcome but persistent phenomenon in rural areas. West Virginia, one of the most rural states in the U.S., has been particularly hard hit by consolidation: between 1987 and 1994, 26 percent of West Virginia schools were closed (Howley 1996). The immediate impacts of this include longer distances between home and school, longer bus rides, bigger schools, and separation of school and community—outcomes that are largely unsought by rural families. Although some recent studies have documented the negative effects of long bus rides, research on busing in general has been virtually nonexistent since the early 1970s, and no one knows what the magnitude of the current problem is (Zars 1998). What is known is that, in most rural states, transportation costs now far exceed other expenditures, such as the cost of feeding school children (Rural Challenge Policy Program 1999b). Also, the more rural the state, the larger the impact of consolidation: when measured as a percentage of total expenditure on education, West Virginia spent more than any other state on school transportation in 1995–1996 (6.4 percent; Rural Challenge Policy Program 1999b).

The negative impact of consolidation, longer bus rides, and bigger schools has been well documented in various qualitative studies (Zars 1998; Rural Challenge 1999; Scott 1999). Grassroots organizations have been working against school consolidation by arguing that busing children long distances to consolidated schools is essentially a form of discrimination that denies students equal access to education. Organizations such as the Rural Challenge exist solely for the purpose of helping rural communities challenge education policies such as consolidation. Correspondingly, the main charge of the Rural Challenge (1999) is to improve rural education by “strengthening relationships between rural schools and communities.”

Some supporting research on the negative effect of long bus rides has been descriptive, providing powerful testimonies about the real consequences of educational policy (see, for example, Scott 1999). Other studies have been more analytical. One particularly insightful study (Witham 1997) addressed the underlying meaning of increased time spent traveling to and from school: Does it really matter that, as schools are closed, students have to travel further to get to school? The question boils down to an assessment of the worth of children’s time. Calculating the hours of “lost opportunity time” children spend on the bus, and dividing this time by anticipated savings from school closure, Witham concluded that long bus rides represent a serious exploitation of children’s time. A related study elucidated the effect of time lost by riding on the bus. The research concluded that increased time on the bus correlated with lower grades, poorer levels of fitness, loss of sleep time, poor study habits, and loss of social activity (Fox 1996). Related to this, a few studies have attempted to directly link consolidation with student achievement via the variable school size (Friedkin and Necochea 1988; Fowler 1992; Howley 1994). This is discussed in more detail below.
Legal Challenges

One of the most important legal battles over consolidation and increased distance between home and school surfaced in West Virginia a few years ago. In 1997, the West Virginia Fourth District Court ruled in favor of parents attempting to block the consolidation of two rural high schools. The case involved the merger of Circleville High School, a school listed on the National Register of Historic Places consisting of 130 students in grades 7–12, and Franklin High School, a school of 500, also spanning grades 7–12, about seventeen miles from Circleville.

The fundamental problem with the consolidation plan lay in the decrease in access to the school for Circleville students. The heart of the issue, parents argued, was that students from Circleville would have to spend sixty to one hundred more minutes on the bus after consolidation, and three-fourths of the students would have to be on the bus more than one and a half hours each day. Some students would have to be on the bus as much as two and a half hours each day, beginning at 6:30 a.m. (Rural Challenge Policy Program 1999a).

The court’s ruling in favor of the parents and against consolidation essentially hinged on the idea that diminished accessibility to school had an adverse effect on the students of Circleville. The court gave strong weight to the fact that Circleville High School had a high level of parental and community involvement in the school, and that closing the school would cause “significant damage to the educational opportunities and prospects of Circleville students,” as a result of “the longer commuting times, the lower levels of participation, the diminished parental involvement, the diminished support, and the relative socioeconomic disadvantage of Circleville High School students” (Rural Challenge Policy Program 1999a).

One year later, the West Virginia Supreme Court reversed the lower court’s ruling. Interestingly, it did not repudiate the logic of that court’s argument. Instead, the court stated that insufficient evidence existed that children forced into consolidated schools had suffered “overall disparities and discriminatory adverse effects” (Rural Challenge Policy Program 1999a). It also sided with the state’s School Building Authority (SBA), an entity whose policies strongly favor the funding of large, consolidated schools, citing funding limitations as a compelling state interest. The impartiality of the West Virginia SBA has been repeatedly called into question, given that, as of 1996, five of the nine SBA members were connected with the construction or real estate industries and none were professional educators (Bartlett 1997; Bradburn 1997).

Consolidation and Student Achievement

Socioeconomic status is by far the most important determinant of student achievement (Howley 1996). However, anticonsolidation activists realize that they must establish, not only that longer distances to school negatively impact children as a function of lost opportunity time, but also that consolidation negatively effects student achievement. Toward this goal, some researchers have been able to establish that when school and district size interact with socioeconomic factors, they do have an impact on achievement. The effect is indirect: it has been shown that large schools compound the negative effect of poverty on student achievement. Specifically, larger schools tend to have some benefit for students of higher socioeconomic status, but a significant negative effect for students with lower socioeconomic means (Fowler 1992; Howley 1994). A widely cited study by Friedkin and Necochea (1988), which was later replicated by Howley (1996), postulated the “interaction hypothesis” that socioeconomic status regulates the effect of school and district size on student achievement. While size itself has no correlation with achievement, the interaction between size and SES (measured as the product of the two) as an explanatory variable has been found to be significant in studies in Alaska, California, and West Virginia (Rural Challenge 1999). The use of this interaction term has confirmed that smaller schools have positive effects for impoverished students, a phenomenon that may in part be due to the fact that smaller schools have been linked to higher levels of participation in extracurricular activities (Fowler 1992; Cotton 1996), higher attendance rates (Fowler and Walberg 1991; Fowler 1992) and fewer behavioral problems (Stockard and Mayberry 1992). Based on these findings, the closing of small schools in poor rural areas would seem to be ill-advised and counterproductive, and in fact would probably compound the problem of delivering quality education to poor students (Walberg and Fowler 1987; Huang and Howley 1993).

Within the context of the analysis presented in this article, three important points must be made about the activism, legal activity, and research discussed above surrounding school consolidation in rural areas. First, the two primary problems with consolidation from the perspective of many rural constituents—longer bus rides and bigger schools—have been investigated via completely different methodological approaches. Analysis of the former has been largely descriptive and anecdotal (e.g., testimonies about the impacts of long distances to school on people’s lives), whereas analysis of the latter has been much more quantitative. Second, the quantita-
tive studies about the negative affect of bigger schools have shown that the effect of size on achievement, via its interaction with SES, is less important at the elementary school grade levels. Howley (1996) postulated that this is due to a cumulative effect on size, consistent with conventional wisdom that the achievement gap widens as students progress through school. Finally, Howley’s (1996) study showed that the effect of size was more pronounced at the district level than the school level. To the extent that district size and distance to school are intercorrelated, this may have some bearing on the effect of distance on school achievement. Yet no studies have looked at distance. The basis of this article is that, while the central role played by geography—i.e., distance—in these debates is obvious, it has not been studied. This deficiency motivates the need for an empirical investigation of access to schools.

School and Community

How can the assumption that access (i.e., proximity) is beneficial and lack of access is detrimental be supported from a theoretical viewpoint? One answer lies in the theory that the relationship between a school and its surrounding neighborhood and community is of vital importance. In the sociology of education, the role of schools moves well beyond instrumental educational objectives. Highly symbolic interrelationships between school and community extend to the realm of communal identity and religion, for example (Wheaton 1985). Janowitz and Suttles (1978, 89) refer to the local school as a “natural repository of sacred involvement.” Even in rural areas such as West Virginia, the need for school-community interpenetration has been a highly salient, emotional issue (Howley 1997). This is particularly true because schools are the primary civic institutions in unincorporated (i.e., rural) areas.

Historical views of the optimal design of neighborhoods concentrate on the central focus of the elementary school. Starting with Perry’s (1929) neighborhood unit concept and continuing in the design of new towns such as Columbia, Maryland, schools act as an organizing device for neighborhoods and the community at large. Recent developments in planning point to a renewed interest in the pivotal role of elementary schools in their surrounding context (see Ehrenhalt 1996; Ewing 1996). The trend is promulgated by new urbanist developers (New Urban News 1998), who extol the traditional neighborhood unit concept and the centrality of elementary schools. The importance of localism in school location has led some districts to replace race-based busing with attendance rules based on geographic proximity (Hendrie 1996). While these developments are primarily associated with neighborhood schools, and thus may lose some applicability within rural areas, the sentiment of strengthening the external role of schools and giving weight to their geographic situation is broadly applicable within a wide variety of community contexts.

The theory extends to ongoing developments in school-community collaboration literature that focus on finding ways to improve the relationship between schools and public service agencies (see Smithmeir 1996). Such research probes schools in a locational context by evaluating linkages between schools and the needs of surrounding populations. Schools are seen as playing a strong role in building service coordination networks and fostering interagency collaboration (Capper 1994; Crowson and Boyd 1995). Indeed, the notion of promoting the intimate bond between school and community and assigning schools the role of delivering external services has moved well beyond the identification of this role toward the development of specific service delivery models (see Adler and Gardner 1994; Smithmeir 1996).

In each of these areas—the importance of the non-instrumental role of schools, the historical traditions of defining neighborhood form in terms of schools, and the growth of school-community collaboration research—the role of proximity to schools is paramount. While specific accessibility rules would be difficult to legitimize, each of these debates attaches prominence to the issue of schools in their geographic context; accessibility between school and constituency defines the issue. Emphasis on school accessibility elevates the importance of schools as places with distinct and profound social meaning, as opposed to emphasizing the importance of schooling, which focuses on the significance of instruction of predetermined bodies of knowledge (DeYoung and Howley 1992). In this way, the role of access to schools is interconnected with the debate about the central purpose of schools. For districts experiencing financial contraction, the resultant widening of school catchment areas has a direct bearing on accessibility, which is, in turn, a major factor in the interrelationship between school and community.

Accessibility Research

Current Trends

Geographical and political research on access to public services has proliferated during the past two decades (see Knox 1978; Pinch 1984; Smith 1994; Hay 1995). In “classic” accessibility studies, geographers have exam-
ined patterns of accessibility to certain services and the spatial relationship between service deprivation and area deprivation (Knox 1978; Pacione 1989). Geographers have explored regional and local variations in access to recreational amenities (Millward 1996), the geographic distribution of public subsidies (Cox 1973; Kirby, Knox, and Pinch 1984; Hodge 1988; Pacione 1989), access to secondary education (Pacione 1989), and public playgrounds (Talen and Anselin 1998). The issue of environmental justice, defined as the spatial relationship between locations of disadvantaged groups and environmental hazards, has also been a relevant topic for geographers (Bowen et al. 1995).

In addition to these classical approaches, geographers have also been concerned with more multidimensional views of access. The notion of how accessibility must be redefined in the information age, in which transactions take place in virtual space, as opposed to physical space, in or some hybrid of the two, has been a recent topic of inquiry (NCGIA 1998). Other researchers stress the role of social barriers in the definition of access, in which case spatial equity must be investigated with an entirely different logic. For example, access to childcare can be conditioned by how childcare is specifically defined and who needs it, which is, in turn, dependent on how the notion of social justice is conceptualized (Holloway 1998). In the case of public park space, accessibility can be examined on the basis of racial and gender restrictions (Mitchell 1996; Ruddick 1996) or the role of “citizenship” as a determining factor (Staeheli and Thompson 1997).

In addition to exposing differentials in accessibility and uncovering the multidimensional meanings of access, there is the quest to discover why certain distributional patterns exist. Factors implicated include urban form (Hodge and Gatrell 1976; McLafferty 1982), organizational rules (Lineberry 1977; Rich 1982), citizen contacts (Mladenka 1980, 1989), politics (Meier, Stewart, and England 1991; Miranda and Tunyavong 1994), and race (Mladenka and Hill 1977; Cingranelli 1981; Cingranelli and Bolotin 1983). Until recently, spatial inequity has predominantly been explained by the notion of “unpatterned inequality” (Mladenka and Hill 1977; Mladenka 1980; Cingranelli 1981). This is the idea that, although there is inequality in people’s access to services and facilities, no evidence exists that there is a clear discriminatory pattern to it. In the absence of patterned inequality, some argue, it is difficult to attach blame to those responsible for the existing distributional pattern. Current critiques of this theory (Meier, Stewart, and England 1991; Miranda and Tunyavong 1994) focus on the failure to take the political process properly into account and the problem of variable definition.

The Unique Case of School Access

The approach used in this article constitutes a fairly straightforward assessment of school accessibility: it presents the results of an empirical investigation of the distances between school constituencies and school locations for eighty-four schools in three school districts. While this scale of data is unique, the format of the study follows in the tradition of a handful of empirical studies of school access that currently exist, notably studies of inequality in preschool and childcare provision (Pinch 1984, 1987; Truelove 1993) and differential access to secondary schools (Pacione 1989). In each of these studies, the investigation intended to reveal the spatial pattern of access to schools and to investigate how this pattern differs according to socioeconomic criteria—i.e., to uncover the equity implications.

The accessibility of schools represents a unique case of spatial equity that warrants a straightforward evaluation in the classic tradition. Several reasons can be cited. First, in the majority of cases, access is not undermined by the mobility of students because of the uniform provision of public transportation.1 In West Virginia, as in most school districts, the public has assumed responsibility for travel to and from elementary school, except in locations where students live in the immediate vicinity of the school. This has a substantially different effect on access in comparison to other public facilities where no transportation provision mandates exist.

Second, even if transportation were not provided, the fact that students are required to attend school presents a unique situation in terms of accessibility. Such a requirement means that the frictional effects of distance and other impediments have less of a bearing on predicting patterns of use, since students are required to travel to school no matter what the effect of distance might be. Psychological and physical impediments such as distance are more likely to be relevant in the case of voluntary travel.

Third, elementary schools are less susceptible to the problem of social barriers to access. Although found to be relevant in situations dealing with access to public areas such as parks, such issues are arguably less significant at the level of a public elementary school. Also, while studies of urban access to education at the high school level have revealed barriers to access to special programs such as after-school activities (Meier, Stewart, and England 1991), this is less applicable in the context of elementary school attendance.

Finally, the concept of need, often cited as an element to be factored in when looking at access equity, is not particularly relevant in the case of elementary schools.
How does one child have less need for access to elementary school than another? In the case of parks, it could be argued that residents of single-family homes on large suburban lots have less need for public green space than inner city apartment dwellers. However, in the case of schools, such straightforward arguments are not easily made.

Equity as Equality

The notion of equity is paramount in research that focuses on determining what factors account for, or are correlated with, territorial variation in service delivery. Accessibility can be used as a tool to discover whether or not equity, variously defined, has been achieved. Taken together, these two concepts are the primary building blocks used to assess the spatial distribution or spatial pattern of public services such as schools.

Many different definitions of equity can be operationalized in defining locational or spatial equity. A lot of these definitions are incompatible: for example, social equity conflicts with territorial justice (Pinch 1985), and equity in risk distribution conflicts with equity in access to services (Humphreys 1988). A number of taxonomies have been developed (see Lucy 1981; Crompton and Wicks 1988; Truelove 1993; Marsh and Schilling 1994), and at least four separate categories of equity are distinguishable. First, equity can be defined as equality, in which everyone receives the same public benefit, regardless of socioeconomic status, willingness to pay, or other criteria. Residents receive either equal input or equal benefits, regardless of need. Second, equity in the distribution of public benefit can be predicated on the basis of need, termed compensatory equity by Crompton and Wicks (1988). Lucy (449) refers to this as “unequal treatment of unequals,” based on indicators such as poverty and race and ideally factoring in the nature of the service being provided. Third, the equitable distribution of services or facilities can be based on demand. Here, active participation in the distributive decisionmaking process is “rewarded” by increased user benefit. Demand may be conceptualized as economic (demonstrated use) or political (vociferous advocacy; see Crompton and Lue 1992). Finally, equity can be based on market considerations, where cost of service is a key factor in distribution. In particular, willingness to pay may be operationalized, in which the equitable distribution of services reflects the degree to which people use (and therefore pay for) a particular service.

Given the arguments cited above, in which school access is deemed to be less subject to various conditioning factors, the definition of spatial equity utilized in this study is based on equality. The lack of variation in mobility, need, willingness to pay, or other factors results in a more even playing field in the question of school access, which in turn legitimizes the view that equity in access should be equated with equality of benefit (benefit, in this case, defined as proximity to school). While there are a whole set of questions related to school equity—quality of inputs (teachers, facilities) and quality of outcomes (student achievement scores)—equity in access to schools is based on proximity alone.

Defined in this way, the spatial equity of school access forms the basis of a social response to the existence of inequity in school locations. Admittedly, this tends to elevate the importance of school proximity beyond its status as one objective in a multiobjective framework. For example, proximity to schools is often viewed as one factor among many others in school location planning and analysis. In fact, distance-minimizing objectives often compete with objectives related to maximizing efficiency or maintaining socioeconomic integration. These approaches have had the effect of minimizing the importance of distance to schools as the defining variable in school planning. Thus, school redistricting and consolidation has relied on downplaying the importance of school proximity, so that financial efficiency is thought to compensate for the inconvenience of travel distance.

A reasoned response to spatial inequity is particularly important in view of the fact that school districts continue to consolidate and that the geographic repercussions of consolidation are almost always in the direction of worsening accessibility. School administrators argue that bigger, consolidated districts result in better quality schools (a point much contested), but the geographic implications are often not assessed.

The vast majority of West Virginia’s school children attend public schools. Approximately 91.3 percent of all children in the three counties (44,457 out of 48,696 students) were in public schools in the 1996–1997 school year.2 Schools are funded primarily by the state, rather than through property taxes. In most counties, school selection is determined by geographic boundary, i.e., by whatever school catchment zone a student lives in. This is the case for the three counties in this study. The boundaries of individual schools are referred to in the remainder of this article as zones or catchment areas. This distinguishes these areas from school districts, which, in West Virginia, are synonymous with counties.

The counties used in this study were selected on the basis of their ability to represent different parts of the state: rural and affluent, rural and poor, and rural/urban mix. In addition, the counties were selected on the basis of data availability, especially the degree to which the boundaries of school areas could be precisely identified. In West Virginia, data on school locations and their constituent boundaries is not readily available. The data was obtained through direct contact with each school district, countywide, as well as follow-up investigation in which individual schools were contacted. The resulting dataset is significant in that having distances between schools and student populations for such a large number of schools, covering multiple school districts in a rural area, is fairly unique.

Since socioeconomic and locational data for individual students were not available at this level of analysis (i.e., for a multiple-county analysis involving eighty-four schools), block-level data was obtained from the 1990 U.S. Census. Of course, the finer geographic detail of the census block had to be weighed against the loss of data detail available for larger census units, such as tracts. In particular, school population at the elementary level had to be approximated using the variable “population under 18.” The appropriateness of using this variable was tested by correlating population under eighteen with population aged five to eleven (elementary school age) at the block group level. As expected, the correlations were extremely high: 0.989 for Berkeley County (51 block groups), 0.987 for Kanawha County (218 block groups), and 0.985 for Jackson County (26 block groups). Thus, the assumption could be made that the variable “population under 18” captures the degree of variation among blocks in terms of the number of elementary-school-aged children they contain.

For the block-level analysis, two census block (SES) variables were used to assess socioeconomic status: median housing value and percent occupied housing units. Again, the use of the census block unit, necessary to obtain population data on as fine a geographic level as possible, limits the number of variables available. The two SES variables do not capture the full dimension of SES; however, as with the population variable, housing value and income were found to be strongly correlated at the block group level. Correlations between average household income and average housing value by block group resulted in the following statistics: 0.578 for Jackson County, 0.760 for Kanawha County, and 0.739 for Berkeley County.

The boundaries that define constituencies were digitized for each of the eighty-four schools based on 1996–1997 school-year data. In almost all cases, school boundaries were drawn in or prior to 1990, and thus did not change between the years 1989–1990 and 1996–1997. This is crucial, since it renders the use of 1990 population data by census block entirely appropriate.

Access as a Variable. There has been a recent surge of interest in defining, computing, interpreting and visualizing accessibility in the literature on spatial analysis and geographic information systems (e.g., Arentze, Borgers, and Timmermans 1994; Geertman and Ritsema Van Eck 1995; Talen 1997; Talen and Anselin 1998; Kwan 1999). This article follows in the tradition of visualization of accessibility in a GIS format by investigating the spatial patterns of distances between census block centroids (locations of population) and school locations. Specifically, access is defined as the total distance or “travel cost” along a street network between block centroids and elementary schools. Measures of access that involve counts of facilities within an areal unit, or potential measures (based on the gravity model), are not relevant in the context of measuring access to elementary schools. Instead, access becomes a variable for each census block, calculated on the basis of the street network (i.e., shortest path) distance between each centroid located within a given school zone and the point location of the school. The analysis involving travel cost and student achievement (hypothesis number three below) uses a weighted aggregation of this measure.

Hypotheses

This study examines whether or not the distribution of travel cost between resident locations (blocks) and schools is equitable on the basis of (1) the density of resident populations and (2) the socioeconomic status of resident populations. A third issue carries the analysis one step further to investigate the effect of access on student achievement. These three associated hypotheses are discussed below.

1. Equality of School Access. There are many reasons why schools are located where they are, and why zone boundaries are drawn as they are. The investigation of access equality asks the question: do school locations and catchment boundaries make geographic sense? In other words, are school locations and the boundaries that define their constituencies drawn in such a way that travel distances are minimized? A working hypothesis is that, within a given school zone, access to school is related to the density of school population (i.e., population under eighteen per areal unit). Hypothetically, access should be directly proportional to population density: blocks with a lower density of school-age population should have lower access, and blocks with a higher density of school-age population should have higher access. While access between blocks and schools will vary widely due to block area differentials and low and varied population densities, it can nevertheless be expected that some logical pattern will be found.

2. Equity Based on Socioeconomic Patterns. Conventional wisdom about the relationship between accessibility and SES ranges from the view that, while inequalities exist, they are not patterned based on SES (i.e., unpatterned inequality) to the notion that patterned inequality does exist given the correct measurement of variables. In the case of schools, there are structural reasons to predict that access will be higher for areas with higher SES and lower for areas with lower SES. Consolidation has meant that poor rural schools merge with larger schools in more urbanized areas. Since SES is positively correlated with density in West Virginia (i.e., more rural populations have lower SES), areas with lower SES will be expected to have lower accessibility. Since areas that are the target of consolidation (i.e., the targets of school closure) are more rural, it can be hypothesized that less affluent students will have lower accessibility to schools than more affluent student populations. This hypothesis does not conflict with the first hypothesis, since the variables used are percent occupied housing units and median housing value and thus do not factor in population size.

It should be noted that a distance-based measure of accessibility—one that does not include behavioral assumptions or social constraints—will tend to result in a nonrandom spatial pattern by design, since the use of distance to facilities as a metric will yield similar values for access in neighboring locations (such loca-
tions will be roughly the same distance away from facilities that are not in their immediate vicinity). The question to be resolved is whether or not such a nonrandom spatial pattern corresponds to the spatial pattern of SES.

3. Student Achievement. To continue the analysis one step further, the relationship between school access and measures of student achievement is also investigated. This analysis is carried out within the context of existing studies on the relationship between school and district size and measures of student achievement discussed previously. Based on research that indicates that size influences achievement through its interaction with socioeconomic status, the study tests this model, as well as an approximation of the model using weighted travel cost in place of district size. It is hypothesized that weighted travel cost will have some explanatory effect on student achievement. This effect should also be present with the addition of other explanatory variables that characterize the elementary school learning environment: student/teacher ratio, class size, years of teacher experience, and enrollment. To test whether or not there has been any change in these relationships over time, two sets of data are used, from 1989–1990 and 1996–1997. Achievement is measured as the third-grade mean percentile score of West Virginia’s Comprehensive Test of Basic Skills (West Virginia Department of Education 1989–1990, 1996–1997). Socioeconomic status is assessed using two variables: percent participation in the free and reduced meal subsidy program (for the two time periods), and 1990 median housing value for all census blocks within a particular school catchment zone (1990 is used for both school years).

Statistical Methods

Familiar methods are used to analyze the accessibility patterns. For the first hypothesis, I use zero-order correlations to measure the strength of linear association between the population density variable (population under eighteen per unit of area) and access (per block). This is extended using an analysis of inequality in access values, weighted by population under eighteen, for each district as a whole. For the second hypothesis, I use a two-sample Wilcoxon rank-sum test (Mann-Whitney two-sample statistic) to test the hypothesis that access and SES are from populations with the same distribution. Finally, after an investigation of spatial effects in the data, I use linear regression analysis (ordinary least squares) to estimate the strength of travel cost (i.e., access) and other variables as predictors of student achievement.

Hypothesis 1: Equality of School Access

The analysis of spatial equity begins by evaluating the distribution of access for each county. Figure 2 shows the distribution of access (miles per block) for each county, including only those blocks that had some population under eighteen in 1990.5 The distribution of access varies widely among counties, and within each county, the histogram indicates that there is wide variation in access to schools. Jackson County's distribution is marked by a higher variance overall (sample variance = 13.74), while access in Kanawha County is more skewed and has a lower variance (sample variance = 5.60). This parallels the fact that Jackson County is much more rural than Kanawha, and thus many blocks have a much higher distance to school. Overall travel cost in Berkeley County is higher than Kanawha and lower than Jackson, but its distribution is more even (i.e., it has the lowest sample variance, 4.56). In sum, there is wide variation in access: some students live within easy commute, while many others travel five to ten miles. In the case of Jackson, a significant number of children travel more than ten miles to school.

One method involves comparing perfect equality in distribution—such that there is an even distribution of all values—against the actual distribution of access in the three counties. Figures 3, 4, and 5 show the Lorenz curve for the three distributions, which is based on the distance between block and school weighted by the population under eighteen within that block. The corresponding Gini coefficient is also listed. The Gini coefficient calculates the ratio of the area between the Lorenz curve and the straight diagonal and the area in the triangle below the diagonal. Thus, the higher the coefficient, the greater the size of the area between the curve and the diagonal, and the greater the inequality in the distribution. In all cases, the index is high—the distribution of weighted access (labeled “cost”) is largely unequal.

Inequality in access is expected, but to what degree?

Theoretically, schools that are centrally located within a relatively compact zone will probably conform to standard location-allocation rules, minimize distance for as many students as possible, have low Gini coefficients, and therefore make “geographic sense.” In more rural school zones, or in zones with mixes of urban and rural housing developments, finding the geographic center of a zone is more difficult. Nevertheless, it is reasonable to expect that, within each school zone, there would be some correlation between mean distance (travel distance to the centroid of a block) and population density. In other words, less densely populated blocks should be expected to travel further overall than more densely populated blocks. While there might be some smoothing effect for the district in general—i.e., an evening out of distance disparities at the county level—the distance a student population will need to travel to school is determined at the level of the individual school zone. For each school zone, it would be reasonable to hope for some indication of a relationship between distance and population density.

To make this assessment, correlation coefficients between mean distance and density of population under eighteen were computed for each school zone. Hypothesis 2: Equity Based on Socioeconomic Patterns

Due to consolidation effects, which disproportionately impact rural (and usually poorer) areas, it was hypothesized that access to schools would be inversely correlated with SES. Two variables, median housing value and percent occupied housing units, were used to analyze the social equity of access, serving as proxies of SES. Both would be expected to be inversely correlated with travel cost. Poorer areas are more rural, and rural areas, it was originally hypothesized, have less accessibility by definition.

It should be stated at the outset that the relationship between density (i.e., the degree to which a given block is “rural”), poverty, and access is complicated by the findings reported thus far: that is, that access does not appear to be consistently related to the spatial location of population under eighteen. If there were a relationship between access and population, whereby schools were located in such a way that distance was minimized for the greatest number of children, then the hypothesized relationship between access and SES would be more likely to hold. As it turns out, it is not straightforward to implicate density in the attempt to explain the pattern of access and its relationship to SES.

Hypothesis 3: Student Achievement and School Quality

Regression analysis was used to test the strength of travel cost as a predictor of school achievement. The specific strategy for analyzing this key question involved the use of four models: (1) a model incorporating an interaction term between SES and school size; (2) a model incorporating travel cost as a substitute for school size in the interaction term; (3) a “full” model using additional explanatory variables related to school quality; and (4) a best-fit model, derived from the results of the first three models. Before explaining these results, I first review the results of an analysis of spatial effects.

Spatial Effects. It was important to determine whether or not it made sense to include all three counties in the regression analysis (n = 79), or simply use Kanawha County (n = 59). Specifically, it was important to find out the extent to which the relationships among variables were constant in all three subregions. The tests described below were performed on the best-fit model generated using all three counties (n = 79). All of the analyses listed here were carried out using SpaceStat software (Anselin 1992).

The best-fit model, which is explained in the section below, was first estimated using a test for spatial homogeneity called the spatial Chow test (Anselin 1990). In this test, coefficients are allowed to vary by subregion, and thus can be tested for spatial homogeneity. The results of the spatial Chow test were highly significant, and thus the null hypothesis of homogeneity was rejected.6 In subregion 3 (Kanawha County) the coefficients of the best-fit model were highly significant; the coefficients in the other subregions were not. Given the small number of observations in subregion 2 (n = 7) and subregion 3 (n = 13), the coefficients from these two spatial regimes are not very reliable. Therefore, in the interest of avoiding the introduction of additional noise in the analysis, only Kanawha County was used.

For all regressions carried out for Kanawha County, tests for spatial autocorrelation were performed as standard diagnostics. These tests, using both a distance-based weights matrix and a four-nearest-neighbor weights matrix, did not reveal any form of residual spatial autocorrelation.7 Finally, a test for heteroskedasticity was performed as well, and indicated that the dataset did not have a problem with nonconstant variance in the error term.8

Regression Results. Although all models were originally tested on the full dataset (n = 79), the regression results described here were performed for Kanawha County (n = 59), for the reasons discussed above.

I began the analysis by attempting to fit the Kanawha County dataset to the standard model used to relate school size (enrollment) to student achievement, via an interaction term (SES multiplied by size). Two variables were used to measure socioeconomic status: median housing value for all census blocks included in each school catchment area, and the percentage of students per school who receive free and reduced-price meals (National Center for Education Statistics 1996).

The basic form of the model was specified by Friedkin and Necochea (1988) and later applied to West Virginia by Howley (1996):

\[
\text{ACHIEVEMENT} = f[\text{SIZE}, \text{SES}, (\text{SIZE} \times \text{SES})]
\] (1)

Modifying it for the West Virginia dataset, the following model was used:

\[
A_i = \alpha + \beta_1 S_i + \beta_2 H_i + \beta_3 SH_i + E
\] (2)

where \( A_i \) = the third grade test scores for each elementary school; \( \beta \) = the associated regression coefficients; \( S_i \) = school size, measured by total enrollment; \( H_i \) = one of two SES indicators (median housing value or meal subsidy); \( SH_i \) = school size multiplied by median hous-
ing value/meal-subsidy, a proxy for the interaction term; and E = a stochastic error term. Also, in order to test the dynamic nature of variable relationships, two sets of data were used: 1989–1990 and 1996–1997 Kanawha County school data. The only variables common to both datasets were 1990 median housing value and weighted travel cost.

In the second model, travel cost was substituted for size in the original model, in an effort to identify the feasibility of using distance as an alternative to size as an explanatory variable. For the fifty-nine zones included (two were eliminated because they lacked test scores), a weighted average cost per population under eighteen, per school zone, was used as the travel cost variable.

A third model broadened the number of explanatory variables, for the same two time periods. In addition to school size, SES, and distance, three variables were added that characterize school educational quality and thus could potentially have an explanatory effect on student achievement: student-teacher ratio,9 average class size, and average number of years of teacher experience.10 An additional variable, student test scores in 1989, was added as an indicator of the pre-existing achievement level of each school for the 1996–1997 dataset. Based on a combination of all of these variables, a best-fit model was constructed.

As a useful starting point, a correlation matrix of all variables used in the multivariate analysis of student achievement was constructed. The matrix, shown in Table 6, indicates the following bivariate relationships:

1. SES (measured by meal-subsidy rate) is strongly and negatively correlated with enrollment, class size, student-teacher ratio, years of teacher experience, and achievement score.

2. Achievement levels are positively correlated with class size, student/teacher ratio and teacher experience levels. The fact that student/teacher ratio has a positive relationship with student achievement can be partially explained by the fact that the student/teacher ratios are fairly low for the fifty-nine districts overall, ranging from a low of 12.4 students per classroom teacher to a high of 24 students per classroom teacher. The median classroom size is 21.2.

3. Distance to school is inversely correlated with student achievement.

Multivariate analysis is needed to better understand these relationships, especially with respect to student achievement. The results of the first two models described above, using the interaction term proposed by previous studies, are not significant. In both models, neither school size (i.e., enrollment) nor travel cost nor the associated interaction terms (using either meal-subsidy rate or housing value) are shown to have any effect on student achievement. These findings do not support those of Friedkin and Necochea (1988) and Howley (1996). However, as previously pointed out, the effect of size on student achievement, via an interaction term, has been found to be least pronounced for elementary-school-aged children. Furthermore, the results support the conclusion that school size and distance to school are very different components of student achievement. Distance to school, it appears, cannot be substituted for school size. It should also be pointed out that the limited size of the sample may have some bearing on the statistical significance found for this second model, and that a larger sample could in fact result in a different conclusion.

The 1996–1997 data present a different picture. As the regression results in Table 8 show, weighted travel cost does have some explanatory effect when 1989–1990 basic skills and meal-subsidy rate are controlled for. Thus, in addition to SES, and controlling for previous achievement levels, travel cost has some significance as a predictive variable. The three explanatory variables had a combined R-squared of .537. As expected, the relationship between test scores and travel cost was an inverse one, such that higher test scores were related to lower travel cost.

Two important observations can be made about these results. First, it is significant that the only variables that appeared to be related to achievement were SES (always the strongest determinant), prior test scores, and distance. School size, class size, student/teacher ratio, teacher experience, and the interaction term between SES and school size did not have any explanatory effect on student achievement. This conclusion warrants the need for further research, since it is possible that distance, not school size, is the more important variable in attempting to predict student achievement, at least in the case of rural elementary schools. This reasoning forms the underlying basis of this article, that distance to school is a significant factor that must be addressed when looking at the issue of school equity.

Second, it is significant that an inverse relationship between travel cost and student achievement did not appear in 1989–1990. By including prior test scores, the model tests the relationship between travel cost and change in test scores. As stated earlier, the school catchment areas used in this particular analysis were, for the most part, established prior to or around 1990, and held stable at least until 1996–1997. One conclusion to be drawn, then, is that between the years 1989–1990 and 1996–1997 the effect of school accessibility on student achievement increased from no significant effect to a slightly significant negative effect. Possibly, schools with less accessibility (e.g., more distant blocks) did not improve as much as schools with greater accessibility (e.g., fewer distant blocks). Ultimately, it is possible to associate improvement in skills with schools with greater accessibility, and associate lower levels of improvement in skills with schools with less accessibility.

Conclusions

This article has investigated the spatial equity of school locations. The findings are important, not only
because they confirm the existence of spatial inequity, but also because they highlight the need for additional research in particular substantive areas. In this section, I will briefly summarize the main empirical findings, suggest future research needs, and speculate on how the geographic aspects of schools could be of use in a political context.

The results of the analysis of hypothesis one indicated that spatial inequities in access to school are substantial, and vary by county and school zone. In terms of the relationship between SES and access to schools (hypothesis two), results were inconclusive and could be described in the familiar unpatterned-inequality paradigm supported by a great deal of accessibility research. It is likely that this lack of pattern is complicated by the fact that access is not correlated with population-under-eighteen density, thus blurring the traditional relationship between density and poverty in rural West Virginia.

As an interaction term, SES did not appear to play a role in the relationship between travel cost and student skills (hypothesis three). For 1996–1997 scores, travel cost emerged as a significant, inversely related, explanatory variable when prior achievement and SES were controlled for. The fact that this relationship was not seen for 1989–1990 could indicate a worsening situation in which students with less access are starting to be linked to schools with lower test scores.

These exploratory initial findings should be expanded upon in future geography-based studies. First, the finding that many blocks have poor accessibility should be investigated vis-à-vis the implicit assumption in school consolidation planning that school catchment areas conform to some kind of higher rational principle. How is the (usually) financially based rationality of school consolidation to be reconciled with the basic inequity of accessibility? In light of the increasing role of community in the life of schools, how can the geographic context of schools be dismissed? Can the spatial context of schools be subordinated as merely one factor among many others, or does it weigh more heavily? These issues need to be considered thoroughly. Second, the findings point to a need for more research regarding the import of socioeconomic status in school accessibility. Would more refined measures of SES yield a stronger relationship between access and SES? A more definitive look at this issue is warranted, given the implications of such a finding: being both poor and far from school is a predicament that should not be ignored. Finally, the effect of poor accessibility on student achievement should be investigated more thoroughly. The findings give an indication that access is negatively associated with achievement, but the relationship needs to be scrutinized in greater detail. For example, the long-term implication of long bus rides and inequity in access should be considered by way of more detailed, perhaps longitudinal analysis.

No one can dispute that school locations will necessarily result in differential accessibility patterns, and that these patterns will have the effect of creating inequities. The question to be resolved is: what degree of spatial inequity is acceptable, and what should the appropriate response be? There are three possible reactions to the kind of spatial inequity uncovered here: (1) unfairly burden some students with longer travel costs; (2) restructure school attendance zones to insure the best possible accessibility pattern, such that there is a negative association between travel cost and student population; or (3) implement some sort of compensatory factors which serve to offset accessibility differentials. The first option fails under the weight of basic rules of fairness, particularly if the link between school and community is deemed important. The second option may become more feasible if the geographic implications of school locations are deemed important, particularly in the political arena. Better access to data, particularly in a GIS format, will play a critical role here. Compensation or some kind of flexibility in the delivery of public education, the third option, could be feasible, although much work remains to be done on this option.

The investigation of school accessibility presented here has omitted any consideration of causality. This is because, in the case of schools, the reasons behind the existence of access inequities are not difficult to surmise. There are a wide variety of factors involved in the determination of school location, including budgeting issues, assumed benefits of scale, and efficiency of transportation routes. There is ample anecdotal evidence to suggest that school locations and their resultant accessibility patterns are based on bureaucratic rules and the financial perspectives of educational administrators, driven by school board politics and emotionally charged debate, not on geographic principles. While such decisions are made under the guise of adhering to rational (financial, pedagogical) criteria, the reality is that many students suffer under the burden of long rides to school. In rural areas in particular, school location inequities are caused by consolidation rules that are established without benefit of evaluation of spatial patterns of accessibility.

The decision environment that gives rise to spatial inequities in school access is a subject of debate. For example, some have charged that decisions about school locations are not based on the desire to maximize learning opportunities for children. DeYoung and Howley (1992) argue that decisions about school closure and consolidation are closely tied to the changing political economy of the U.S., in which the state legitimizes its goals related to the promotion of national and international economic
development. Even from a financial accounting perspective, consolidation remains a wide-open debate (Wiles 1994). While one study conducted from an economic point of view found that school closings were not based on rational economic factors (Phipps and Anglin 1993), the results reported in this article point to the fact that school locations are not based on rational geographic factors either.

If the discovery of access differentials is illuminated, perhaps the addition of knowledge about the geographic context within which schools function will elevate the school location debate beyond emotional criteria toward rational spatial equity criteria. As it stands, closure and consolidation decisions are based on concepts that lack sufficient empirical or even theoretical backing. If the goal of schools-related research is to strengthen the information base needed to guide decision makers, geographers should insure that this information base includes a rigorous accounting of school accessibility.
