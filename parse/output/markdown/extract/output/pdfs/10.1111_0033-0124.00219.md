Health Seeking Behavior: Q-Structures of Rural and Urban Women in India with Sexually Transmitted Diseases and Reproductive Tract Infections*

Suprabha (Sue) Tripathi
Department of Social Services, Denver, CO

I use a gender framework to examine why women need to utilize social networks to discover and resolve problems related to reproductive tract infections (RTI) and sexually transmitted diseases (STDs). In India, women's health is linked with social status and perception of their health needs. This paper provides insight on how social networks are gender-, location-, and context-specific and how they are linked to STD issues. This paper examines why social networks are integral to women. The health-seeking behavior of rural and urban casual sex workers is examined via Q-analysis, a language of mapping relationships. Results from Q-analysis reveal women's use of four systems—kinship, belief, traditional medical, and western medical services. Finally, I conclude with important implications for research on gender relations and social networks. Key Words: STD, social networks, gender relations, rural-urban, Q-analysis, India.

Introduction

Sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) have a major impact on people's health, especially for women and neonates. In many developing countries, reproductive tract infections (RTIs) and STDs are among the five most common health problems for which people seek treatment (Gittelsohn et al. 1994). In countries such as India, where rural to urban migration among males is high, the likelihood of rural females being infected with STDs is growing since male migrants may get infected and transmit such diseases to rural females (ICMR 1990). So far three million STD cases have been reported in India (The Statesman 1996), with an estimated 2.8 million HIV-infected people and 100,000 AIDS cases (Times of India 1995). This problem is important since 70% of India's population live in rural areas and estimates project that 8–10 million people in India will be infected with STDs, HIV, and AIDS by the year 2010 (Health Care Media Center India and WHO 1992; WHO 1992; Kakar 1995).

The situation is most serious for women, especially rural women since they are affected at a
younger age and are frequently blamed and stigmatized for having STDs. While incidences of STDs are reported in India, people are often reluctant to seek treatment out of a sense of shame. Delayed treatment often results in infections that are painful, distressing, and sometimes life-threatening. STDs have a gender dimension in that their spread is influenced by the status of women. It is not sufficient to focus on high risk groups or behaviors, although these are certainly important, because STDs spread through the general population and are linked to risk behaviors and the status of women in Indian society. While this paper includes high risk groups, casual sex workers, the focus is on rural women. Generally, women in rural India have limited access to information about diseases and are ignorant about sexual practices and preventive measures (Gittelson et al. 1994). They are socially conditioned to accept their husband’s dominant role, predisposing themselves to infection in cases in which the husband is promiscuous and infected. Unlike their counterparts in the Western world who have more access to information and medical services, rural women have insufficient knowledge about STDs. This suggests that perceptions of risk and knowledge and skills to interpret prevention methods into sexual behavior among women are inadequate. Thus, knowledge of women’s health problems and how they are resolved is important.

Literature Review
So far, research on STDs has focused primarily on the clinical and epidemiological aspects (Bang et al. 1989; Bang and Bang 1991). Reproductive health issues on women primarily encompass studies confined to maternal and child health, family planning, and nutritional studies. While some knowledge can be gained by a systematic review of existing literature, research on health-seeking behavior of rural women in India is needed for providing information in areas where there are information gaps (Gesler 1984; Klovadahl 1985; Gaitley and Seed 1989; Krishnaraj and Chanana 1989; Basu 1990; Elias 1991; Basu 1992; George 1993; Grover et al. 1993; Khan 1993; Ford 1994, 1997; Singh et al. 1998). This paper focuses on the utilization of the social networks of women, which is a key information system for poor rural women who do not have access to media sources that are common to men. These social (information) networks are defined by the social and cultural order specific to each geographic location. Such information networks would help in intervention strategies but the key in this study is to understand the choices that women make.

Objectives
An understanding of women’s health problems is important if programs designed to curb further spread of STDs is to be effective. The research presented here uses a gender framework to examine why women need to utilize social networks to discover and resolve problems related to STDs. The purpose is to examine the health-seeking behavior of rural women in India infected with such diseases. The focus is on two key issues:

• Why are social networks integral to resolve women’s health problems? Related research questions are: why women rely on social networks to seek treatment; are health services available to women; what constraints, if any, do rural women have in accessing services?
• What are the geographic dimensions of social networks; that is, are they specific to context, location, and gender? What patterns of interpersonal communication emerge as a result of using social networks for women with STDs, HIV, and AIDS? Can social networks be viewed as both a preventive and intervening tool for educating and treating women with STDs, HIV, and AIDS?

Based on these research questions, I argue that due to societal values placed on STDs, women are forced to utilize an intricate social network to express their health problems.

Gender and STDS: Social Networks and Health Status of Women in Rural India
The health status of women is the outcome of ideologies, particularly the ideology of patriarchy. Patriarchy here includes gender relations and, in the context of India, class, caste, and ethnicity. This paper focuses attention on the gender division of production as well as access and control over resources and benefits. In In-
dia, division of labor depends on gender, status, and power, which determines social networks. Interpersonal networks of women with STDs are circumscribed by the larger socio-economic order. The socialization of males and females is organized on the basis of position-status and the roles of men and women in the family and society (Stacheli and Lawson 1994). The socialization process is extremely significant in explaining the general problem of inequality and gender relations in the context of STDs, HIV, and AIDS. Women are tied to the private sphere by doing labor-intensive tasks, while men perform tasks that generate financial reward (Bruyn 1992; Hanson 1992; McDowell 1996, 1997). In their early socialization, which is reinforced by the prevailing norms of adulthood, women learn that they are subordinate to men and must respect their wishes. Even if they dare suggest avoiding risky sexual acts or using condoms, they may often encounter male refusal, and accusations of adultery or promiscuity. As in many other countries, men in India raise numerous objections to condom use; they are unmanly, unnatural, decrease sexual pleasure, or represent a plot to keep the population down (Jeffrey 1991). Condom use is also associated with prostitution and the associated stigmas form a powerful deterrent to use.

Women in rural India are particularly vulnerable to STDs because of their subordinate social and economic status. Important family members, mostly female, often dictate their healthcare needs. In general, women do not discuss problems, especially sexual problems, initially with male members of the family but with female members of the same age, caste, or religious group, create a separate gender-based geographic space. Personal discussions between the sexes about STDs are discouraged and when they occur, women find it very difficult or embarrassing to broach the subject. As in other spheres of social activity, an unspoken understanding exists between men and women, where decisions on child or personal care that affect the prevention of the disease or its cure are the domain of women. Processes of decision-making on health and nutrition are largely traditional or rule bound rather than “informed” by Western medicine. Moreover, men are more likely to get special medical attention and care. Sexual disease symptoms among them can be detected at an early stage.

In contrast, STDs among women are a critical issue because symptoms such as chronic vaginal candidiasis, papilloma, severe and chronic pelvic inflammatory disease, cervical dysplasia, and cervical carcinoma appear late, and women often find it difficult to identify the problem (Kelly 1992; Novello and Torres 1992; Bloom and Godwin 1997).

In India, where open discussion of sex is taboo, rural women are not expected initially to share STD problems with their predominately male physicians. Historically, Indian traditional systems of medicine have been male-oriented and male-dominated, largely ignoring women’s diseases (Leslie 1976). In addition, class and caste, shame, and seclusion denied women access to them. While such rigid boundaries are less formalized in current rural India, economic dependency, physical and social accessibility and, most significantly, existing norms toward STDs make any permanent behavioral change difficult. Women are viewed as primary caregivers and health guardians of their family, while their health-related problems are not emphasized. Although 275 STD clinics are located in India at the district level, stigma associated with STDs prevent many women from accessing these facilities.

The Geographic Dimension of Social Networks

To understand how women utilize social networks, the locational and contextual information about the patient, practitioner, and institution is of great significance (Kearns 1993). The use of social networks depends on the degree of illness, degree of awareness of an individual towards the presence of health care services, the perception of what these services provide, and beliefs and customs specific to each location. Cultural norms include processes through which a woman perceives herself to be ill and chooses and implements strategies to facilitate the restoration of her health. In general, there are four systems available to women: kinship, belief, traditional medical, and Western medical. Of these, kinship and belief are most significant since women's illnesses are often viewed as spiritual in origin and treated initially as such (Bhardwaj 1975). Methodology

This study collected ethnographic information and implement Q-analysis (a language of mapping relationships) via group discussions, interviews, and questionnaires of 172 rural and 37 urban females infected with STDs in specific study areas of India. Data was analyzed using STARPACK, a program of Q-analysis. Q-analysis was applied to map the actual social networks used by women in their search for “treatment.” A detailed discussion (with examples) of Q-analysis is provided later. For purpose of brevity, all tables are not presented in this paper but, rather, key findings from Q-analysis are discussed.

Limitation
Since state policies did not permit me to conduct research in the villages in Pune, urban women had to be included in the study. The study therefore is atypical in that approximately half of the women visiting STD centers were casual sex workers or CSWs (Pune, total = 36, CSWs = 20; North Arcot, total = 38, CSWs = 17) or prostitutes, residing in urban centers. Their social networks do not reflect the networks of women in rural or urban India. This study, therefore, presents an interesting mix of two diverse social networks, CSWs and women belonging to other occupational groups within a specific social setting. Although this paper provides a better understanding of women and STDs, it does not aim to provide a comprehensive coverage of research needs for health problems of all poor rural women in India.

Quantitative Analysis: Q-analysis

Q-analysis was used to map the relationship between women for all diseases and the key members of their social network. In brief, Q-analysis involves mapping of relations between individuals to identify how individuals structure their world through relationships (Gould et al. 1984). It is based upon the rigorous definition of sets, a collection of objects or elements, and the explicit specification of relationships between sets. Sets can have hierarchical arrangements. A relationship allows a description between elements of sets in an open, unconstrained manner, as the data are actually observed, representing real life situations. In order to have any relationship between two sets, there must be a connection. Women employed language, graphs, and symbols to describe such relations. These relationships represented a woman’s social network. For this study, members within the social network of women included the use of four sets or systems: kinship, belief, traditional practitioner, and Western medical practitioner (Fig. 3).

While these systems are typically available all over India, including the four districts in this study, the use of each system varied between districts depending on factors such as financial and physical accessibility, intensity, and type of disease. For example, women in Haora did not use the STD center due to distance and stigma associated with the center. Similarly, in the remaining three districts, women who did not actively seek Western medical services but were diagnosed with HIV/AIDS were initially identified as spouses of husbands with HIV/AIDS. Thus, whom women interacted with within each system was of utmost importance. Each system represented an action set, where women belonged to at least one set and interacted with

The interconnections between women (based on events, i.e., occurrence and intensity of disease) are the mapping of relationships between and within sets. For instance, consider the terms that might be used to describe the kinship group below:

N + 2   {Kin Group}
N + 1   {Family, Friend, Caste Member}
N       {Mother, Sister, Daughter, Mother-in-law, Sister-in-law}

The set {kinship group} included multiple elements {family, friend, and caste member}. Hence, the set kin group is a cover set at the N + 2 level for {Family}, {Friend}, and {Caste Member} at the N+1 level. Similarly {Family} is a cover set for {Mother, Sister, Daughter, Mother-in-law, Sister-in-law} at the N level. But in this hierarchical scheme, an element at one level, for example, N, need not belong exclusively to a single set at the next higher level, N + 1. For instance, sister-in-law belongs to family, friend, and kin member. The idea of a cover set therefore implies that words/terms at one level are covers for words/terms lower down the hierarchy. Thus Q-analysis allows mapping observations into higher order algebraic structures with minimum information loss. For instance, let W be a set of women with diseases [white discharge, syphilis, gonorrhea, HIV, AIDS], C a set of communication with elements {Friend, Family, Social Worker, Block Nurse, Caste Member}, and M a set of medical services with elements [primary health center, government hospital, private clinic, STD clinic, and AIDS clinic] then all sets W, C, and M are at the same hierarchical level. By choosing a slicing parameter, \( \theta \), to define the binary relation of a particular element, the structure of communication will change according to the set of slicing parameters chosen. For example, women with gonorrhea may initially communicate with members within set C (kinship system) although with increase in the severity of disease, they may interact with members belonging to different systems (sets), such as set M (Western medical system). Thus the first stage of the social network is most crucial since it is at this stage that a woman confides in her friend, relative, or same-caste member about her problem. The first stage therefore represents a high level of interaction and confidentiality between women while the last stage of the social network represents a low level of interaction and confidentiality (Fig. 4).

The people or elements in the first stage, kinship members, are selected because of their specific role in society; that is, their gender. Ironically women, because of their gender, generally avoid members in the last stage of the social network, male practitioners of Western medicine. The interlinkage between the interpersonal network of women to a structure of communication is thus spatially defined by the nature of the event and social relation, that is, the role of the individual member in a specific system. Depending on the type and intensity of the disease, a woman may choose to use all members or as few as one member within her social network.

The structure of communication depends on the sample size within each system, and the sample size varied for each system. In each district, women used more than one member within each system. For this study, a slicing parameter of \( \theta = 1 \) was most suited since the sample size was relatively small in each region as compared to the total population of the area. When \( \theta = 1 \), then all elements with a value greater than or equal to 1 were converted to 1 while the rest were set at 0. Similarly, when a very high slicing parameter, say \( \theta = 3 \) was chosen, a highly fragmented, much less connected structure of communication emerged.

Q-structures of Women in Faridabad, Haryana
The Q-structures of women in Faridabad included all members used in the social network from each system, although not all women used every member from each system. While the total sample in Faridabad is 86 (78% Hindu, 22% Muslim), some women communicated more than once with members within each system (Table 4).

Thus, there could be many women who spoke with friend and/or family member more than once. While the sample size in the kin system is large (109) compared to the total sample, it is much smaller in the belief system (21). This reflects the importance of a particular system over another. Also, since women did not use all the systems in each region, an equal comparison of each system (kinship, belief, traditional, and Western medical) in every region is not possible. Data is thus represented by region, not by system. Results from Q-structure vectors indicate that women in the kinship system who had:

i. White discharge, a common RTI, communicated with all kin members.
ii. White discharge and chlamydia, spoke with all kin members excluding sister.
iii. White discharge, chlamydia, gonorrhea, and vulvovaginitis, confided in husband, friend, mother-in-law, and sister-in-law.
iv. White discharge, chlamydia, gonorrhea, and vulvovaginitis, articulated their problem to husband, friend, and mother-in-law while women with genital sores and viral hepatitis communicated with husband and friend only.
v. White discharge, chlamydia, gonorrhea, vulvovaginitis, viral hepatitis, genital sores, HIV+, syphilis, and chancroid, confided in husband and friend. Thus, as the number of diseases increased, women spoke with selective members within the system. Q-structures indicated that for all diseases, husband and friend were the two most important nodes in the kinship system, while mother-in-law was the least favorite confidante for most women.

In the belief system, women who had:

i. White discharge consulted with the dai (midwife), nurse, priest, and social worker.
ii. White discharge, gonorrhea, and syphilis sought advice from the dai and nurse and only from nurse for genital sores. Nurses were instrumental nodes in the social network of women in rural Faridabad since they were involved in health related family programs such as immunization.

Q-structures of Women in Haora, West Bengal

The traditional medical and Western medical system was combined as one system since only one node, government hospital, was used by women in their social network. As in Faridabad, the number of times women communicate with members is more than once. So the sample size within each system is larger than the total sample size: 49 (27% Hindu, 73% Muslim) in Haora. Q-structures of Women in North Arcot, Tamil Nadu

The Q-structures of women in North Arcot were divided into two categories, women who were CSWs (n = 17) and all other women (n = 21, total sample size = 38; 68% Hindu, 6% Muslim, 26% Christian). Separate Q structures were analyzed by occupation group (Table 6).

For both groups, two systems were collapsed to form one Q-structure since only one member was used within a certain system (Table 6). Data from the kinship and belief systems were grouped into one table, while data from the traditional and Western medical system formed a separate table. The following Q-structures provide insight on the health-seeking behavior of both groups.

Within the kinship and belief system, all women excluding CSWs:

i. Spoke with their friend, husband, and social workers about RTIs such as white discharge. Social workers were consulted for white discharge problems and were used as referral sources by women.
ii. Who were HIV+ and had white discharge, conferred with all kinship members, including mother.
iii. Who were HIV+, had white discharge, genital ulcers, chancreoid, and syphilis consulted with husband, friend, and social worker.
iv. Communicated with their husband when they were diagnosed with white discharge, genital ulcers, chancreoid, syphilis, and HIV+.

Within the traditional and Western medical systems all women excluding CSWs who were:

i. HIV+ were evaluated at the STD center, CMC, private registered and unregistered practitioner, and/or by the touring medical representative.
ii. HIV+ and had white discharge, genital ulcers, chancreoid, and syphilis visited the local STD center, an extremely significant node in the social networks of women. Although private registered practitioners evaluated HIV+ women, women were tested at the STD center or referred to CMC. In addition, due to the educational efforts of local medical and non-medical personnel, women understood the importance of being treated and tested by medical practitioners rather than visiting healers and “quacks” and had some level of AIDS education. However, visits to these centers did not mean that women did not have traditional beliefs, although visiting STD centers reduced their average waiting period to treatment notably.

Within the kinship and belief system, all CSWs who were:

i. HIV+ included friend, social worker, and their agents to communicate their problems. This reflected the social world of these workers, where members of the kin system were not available to them. CSWs had comparatively limited nodes in their social network. Their networks showed the differences between rural women and women who had more members within their kin and belief system.

Conclusion

In conclusion, beliefs, economic, social and physical accessibility, and perception of STDs either prohibited or sanctioned women to use their social networks. For instance, religious beliefs inhibited women to visit traditional practitioners such as the bakim in Haora, while increasing level of STD/AIDS education allowed women access medical services in North Arcot and Pune. Access to external information sources other than kin members influenced how women created and used social networks in each region. In all the districts, access to such sources was associated with seclusion, asymmetrical power relations, and work attended by women. It is clear from the above results that:

i. Women depended on each other for social and health problems, particularly for STDs.
ii. Women preferred to discuss their health problems with other women of the same age, caste, and religious group. They also relied on their spouses for help.
iii. Women were less informed than men about the availability of health services and, in general, about diseases. However, between the four districts, women in North Arcot and Pune were more informed and had greater access to resources and services.
iv. Women categorized diseases by increasing frequency and severity. As the intensity of the disease increased, the number of people used within the social network extended beyond the “micro” space, that is, village to encompass a much wider “macro” space that included visits outside the village to medical centers. In Faridabad and Haora, for common RTIs such as white discharge, treatment was sought within a smaller space, the home. Home remedies and different traditional forms of medicine were used for all diseases, including STDs. The services of traditional and Western medical practitioners were availed upon only after home remedies did not help in curing the disease. In contrast, women in North Arcot and Pune women availed themselves of western types of health services for all STDs outside the village. Physical and social distance played an important role in the attendance of such services.
v. The average waiting period of women in North Arcot and Pune was comparatively less as compared to women in Faridabad and Haora. This can be attributed to the three key factors: 1) perceived threat of disease, 2) awareness and knowledge of the disease, and 3) informal kinship structures which help in accelerating the “cure” process.
vi. Women sought external help, that is, help from members outside their immediate family, for problems related to STDs more often in North Arcot and Pune than in Faridabad and Haora.
vii. The role of traditional and western practitioners in the health-seeking behavior in the four districts varied. Traditional medicine was preferred over Western medicine in Faridabad and Haora, while the latter was sought more often in North Arcot and Pune.

Analysis reveals that women need to utilize an intricate interpersonal network to address personal problems. Women create geographic spaces via the use of social networks that mirror the belief and customs of each location. In the four districts, results from Q-analysis show how social networks are gender-, context-, and location-specific and how they are linked to STD issues. Specifically, social networks are sources of information, where members within the network provide advice, services, and access
to social contacts to women. Thus women with particular networks may be more successful in obtaining medical care, although the type of medical care sought is a function of cultural beliefs and social norms. In this paper, social network has important implications for STD risk reductions since advice from, or influence of, specific network members may produce either positive or negative changes in health seeking behavior. In Faridabad and Haora, members within the kin and belief system are dominant while traditional and western medical practitioners are more influential nodes in the social networks of women in North Arcot and Pune. Social networks thus reflect gender relations. In rural India, these relations can be in the social positions of Indian women and men and their respective location in the social structure. As a result, women’s access to many of the rewards societies offer is indirect. The structural differences between the positions of women and men are related to their social status, and therefore their health status.
