Spatial Patterns of Migration and Income Change in the Mountain West: The Dominance of Service-Based, Amenity-Rich Counties

J. Matthew Shumway and Samuel M. Otterstrom
Brigham Young University

One of the most recognizable and important changes occurring in the West is rapid population growth. This article intends to address questions associated with whether patterns of population growth and income migration are associated with “new” and “old” West economies. Rural restructuring in the U.S. has created a group of counties with service-based economies. In the Mountain West, a number of counties with service-based economies are located in areas with high levels of environmental or natural amenities, creating what has been termed the “New West.” Migration to the rural parts of the Mountain West, and the income transfers associated with migration, are increasingly concentrated within these New West counties. Rapid population growth, the changing characteristics of in-migrants, and their spatial concentration in New West counties provide a basis for conflicts over what the rural West is becoming.

Key Words: environmental amenities, income migration, New West.

Like most places, the western United States defies easy description, categorization, or understanding. The western U.S. (hereafter the “West”) is both a place and a process. It is a place populated by real people who live, graze cattle, write software, irrigate crops, mountain bike, ski, drive cars, migrate into and out of the region and debate what the West is. The West is also a process or phase in American history—the embodiment of the nation’s last frontier, or the image of American manifest destiny. Donald Snow (1998, 1) captures the juxtaposition of place and process: “[the West is] a region that seems always to be becoming but never finished, a place in perpetual motion, always traveling, never arriving.”

One of the most important discussions now occurring in the West concerns one such transformation: the evolution of the rural economy from one oriented around extractive industries (ranching, farming, mining, logging) to one based on preservation of environmental amenities (tourism, retirement, and second homes and the services that go along with these economic activities). A correlate of the current transformation is population change. Population change represents more than a simple redistribution of people; it is an indicator and, in many instances, an instigator of a wide range of economic, social, cultural, political/policy, and environmental transformations.

Recent research, including that by Rudzitis (1998), Nelson and Beyers (1998), Vias (1999), and Shumway and Davis (1996), has addressed the shifting regional economy and population change in the West. These studies have focused on the dichotomy in rural areas between what has been labeled the Old West and the New West. The Old West is characterized by extractive activities, as well as expenditures by the federal government (on dams, power plants, military facilities, and so on). In contrast, the New West is characterized by environmental amenities, recreation-based economies, and retirement communities. This reported shift has had an effect on the perception of what the West was and is (Rudzitis 1996, 1998; Riebsame 1997). The identity of the West, particularly its rural parts, and the notion of that rural West as a place with a particular identity—whether fictional or not—are shifting along with economic, land-use, sociocultural, and demographic characteristics.

The purpose of this article is twofold. First, we want to determine what spatial patterns stem from the restructuring of the rural West economy from old to new and find out if rapid population growth is associated with the suggested economic transformations—that is, to what extent are patterns of population change associated with New West economies? Second, we want to examine how recent migration pat-
terns into the rural West are changing patterns of income concentration and distribution.

Rural Restructuring and the Environmental Model of Development

According to Galston and Bachler (1995), during the 1980s and into the 1990s, increased international competition in both natural resource commodities and manufactured goods lowered the real prices of natural resources, decreased the importance of primary goods within the national economy, and decreased the amount and/or limited the spread of rural-based manufacturing in the U.S. Such restructuring has affected types of employment opportunities for rural residents. Brown and Deavers (1988) and Summers, Horton, and Gringeri (1990) document a decline in employment in extractive industries producing primary goods in rural areas, some highly selective growth in manufacturing, and enormous growth in services. McGranahan and colleagues (1986) categorize most of this growth in service sector employment as “household-oriented” services, including health care, education, retail trade, personal services, entertainment, and recreation. Beyers and Nelson (Beyers 1991; Nelson and Beyers 1998; Beyers and Nelson 2000) has found that export-oriented producer services and professional services are also important employment growth categories for rural areas. As a result of rural restructuring, employment in many rural areas has shifted from employment in extractive industries to primarily service-based employment, both household and producer services.

Power (1994, 1996) and Rasker (1993, 1995) have argued that these shifts do not simply reflect period effects—unique events that have coalesced at this specific time—but rather indicate a fundamental structural transformation of the rural West’s economy. According to these writers, the basis for future economic growth and development lies, not with the exploitation of natural resources, but with the exploitation of the natural environment as a place. The principal driving force in this new economy is environmental quality. Environmental quality is encapsulated in such items as an unspoiled natural environment, recreational opportunities, cultural richness, reasonable costs of living, safe communities, and quality public services. Such amenities attract highly skilled labor and entrepreneurs (Rudzitis 1998). They also attract retirees and tourists, creating demands for additional services. These processes then create additional economic activity, which is cumulative and which provides the fuel to perpetuate continued economic viability.

Research from Rudzitis and his associates reinforces this environmental model of economic growth. Using primary data from surveys, they have found that recent migrants to the region increasingly indicate that social and natural amenities and overall quality of life factors are more important determinants of their moves to rural areas in the West than more direct economic factors such as employment and income (Rudzitis 1989, 1991, 1993; von Reichart and Rudzitis 1992, 1994). Such amenity factors are also important in keeping people in these places, dampening out-migration tendencies and rates (Rudzitis and Streatfield 1993) and thus increasing local population growth. Vias (1999) found that population growth is driving employment in rural counties in the West, not the other way around. In other words, jobs are following people, rather than people following jobs. Lorah (2000) found that counties with designated wilderness areas, national parks, and so forth had significantly higher levels of population growth than those in counties that did not have any of these natural amenities. Environmental amenities and quality of life factors appear to be important determinants of population change and employment growth in some rural western communities.

We intend to pursue three main questions. First, which counties in the rural Mountain West are New West in nature? Second, is net migration into the rural Mountain West increasing or decreasing geographical concentration within high-amenity counties? Finally, does the environmental model of development selectively draw higher-income individuals, to the extent that this model can sustain rural development in rural western communities?

Population and Income Concentration in the Rural West

If, as has been argued, the new rural economy of the Mountain West follows the environmental model of development, population growth
in rural counties should be concentrating in those counties with growing amenity economies, rather than those in traditional Old West, extractive-based economies. Before this comparison can be made, the New West must first be identified. In order to determine this we used a data-clustering technique to show which counties are closely related to each other in terms of their economic and other geographic characteristics.

Defining the New West
In this case, we defined the rural West as those counties in the U.S. Mountain Census Division designated as rural (nonmetropolitan) as of 1993 based on the rural/urban codes developed by Butler and Beale (1994). Using these codes, we selected a total of 246 rural counties. Once counties were designated as rural, we used the Economic Research Service (ERS) county typology codes as a framework for examining county level economic structure (Cook 1994). These codes include six mutually exclusive economic types—farming, mining, manufacturing, government, services, and nonspecialized—as well as a number of overlapping policy types, including a retirement designation. We also used the ERS’s natural amenity index, which includes factors measuring aspects of climate, topography, and water (McGranahan 1999). We also added a set of measures developed by Beale and Johnson (1998) to determine recreation-based economies.

Using continuous versions of the typology variables along with the natural amenities index and the recreation measures as our clustering criteria, we performed a cluster analysis using SPSS k-means clustering procedure. After creating the clusters, we compared and contrasted them along demographic and locational dimensions. Each cluster is labeled by its dominant economic characteristics. That is, rural counties the economies of which are dominated by farming income and employment are clustered together within the farming cluster. Population Concentration

The clustering procedure makes clear that a significant number of rural Mountain West counties have economic structures along the lines of Power’s (1994, 1996) environmental model of development. These New West counties rank high on the ERS amenity scale, are retirement and recreation destinations, and are characterized by service-based economies. Panel B in Table 1 presents the results from the clustering strategy, along with some demographic and locational characteristics of each cluster. New West counties have the largest populations, the largest average population growth (about six times the amount of in-migration as the next largest category) and the highest median income. Even though all groups of counties have had some population growth due to net in-migration, population growth appears to be concentrated within New West counties.

In order to test this further, we calculated a Hoover index, which measures spatial focus (see Long and Nucci 1997a), for 1950, 1960, 1970, 1980, 1990, and 1999 for the entire continuously rural Mountain West. We then calculated the index for each category type over the same time periods. The Hoover index is

\[
Ht = 0.5 \sum 100|pit - ai|
\]

where \( Ht \) is the Hoover index at time \( t \), \( pit \) is the proportion of population in area \( i \) at time \( t \), and \( ai \) is the proportion of land in area \( i \). The index would be 0 if each area had the same proportion of land and population; it would be 100 if everyone lived in a single area. Essentially, the index tells us the percentage of the total population that would have to move in order to achieve equal densities in each area. Population deconcentration occurs when there is a decline in the index over time. Deconcentration results from either below-average growth in units of analysis—counties, in this case—with higher-than-average density or above-average growth in counties with lower-than-average densities (Long and Nucci 1997a). Population concentration happens when the opposite scenario occurs. The principal use of the index is to identify whether population trends exhibit tendencies toward more uniform distributions or whether they serve to focus population in a more concentrated pattern (Plane and Rogerson 1994). Knowing that current and incoming population is not evenly distributed among all rural counties, we want to see how it varies within our typology.

Panel A of Table 2 shows the Hoover index for all continuously rural counties and the
changes in the index between 1950 and 1999. Examination of the Hoover index over time indicates that the rural Mountain West population is becoming increasingly concentrated. As of 1999, almost 42 percent of the population residing in rural counties in the Mountain West would have to move in order to have equal population densities. As would be expected, the smallest level of concentration occurred in the 1970s, during the population turnaround or rural renaissance. The 1980s saw the largest increase in population concentration, which corresponds with the trends found in other regions of the U.S. (Long and Nucci 1997a). However, during the 1990s, population concentration continued at a rather quick pace. In fact, this decade saw the second largest increase in population concentration since 1950. This is surprising, given the “rural rebound” in the U.S. during the 1990s (Fuguitt and Beale 1996; Long and Nucci 1997b).

Panel B of Table 2 shows the Hoover index disaggregated by type of county group. The New West cluster is the only group that has Hoover values over 10 as well as decadal increases in concentration of at least 0.50. In other words, New West counties have the largest degree of population concentration among all the county groups, as well as being the only group of counties to have relatively large and continued population concentration. For the other county groups, the small amount of changes in the Hoover values over the nearly fifty years studied underlines both the relatively low populations of these counties and the fairly stable nature of their populations. The very low Hoover values across all of the county groups also indicate a dispersed or evenly distributed population among each group of counties. This means that the population densities within each of the groups are more uniform than they are when all of the counties are analyzed together as the rural Mountain West (see Table 2, Panel B). The aggregated region exhibits much higher Hoover values (more concentration) over time than the individual grouped types. This stems from the increasing concentration occurring in New West counties in comparison with the other county groups, and it indicates that the clustering technique used to differentiate the county groups succeeded in grouping counties of similar economic base and population density.

One of the difficulties with exploring this issue—and, indeed, any question that deals with migration—lies in finding adequate data. All of the population change and migration data used in this article comes from estimates prepared by the U.S. Census Bureau. Because most of the growth in the rural West has occurred since 1990, the decennial census does not provide sufficient information to model migration patterns post-1990, much less examine the impacts of such migration. To overcome this problem, following Cromartie and Nord (1997) and Plane (1999), we use the recently released IRS/Census migration files distributed by the Internal Revenue Service (U.S. IRS). In order to avoid problems associated with using only a single year of data, we average the data over a three-year period using 1994–1995, 1995–1996, and 1996–1997 data files (see Cromartie and Nord 1997 and Plane 1999 for a detailed discussion of this data set).

It is not just the total number of individuals and families migrating into the rural West and where they are concentrating that are important in understanding the effect of in-migration on local changes; it is also the characteristics of the migrants. The selectivity of who moves where and how such selective characteristics compare to out-migrants and nonmigrants have at least as much impact as the total numbers of in-, out-, and nonmigrants. Cromartie and Nord (1997) suggest that migrants moving into high-amenity rural areas have higher incomes than either out-migrants from these same counties or nonmigrants. Plane (1999) shows the important role that retirement migration plays in the growth of aggregate and per capita income by region in the U.S.

Are these New West counties in which population is concentrating becoming richer due to the selectivity of migration? Migration can change the relative income among these counties in two ways. First, if more people move to a specific county than leave it, there will be a net transfer of income, assuming that the average income of both in- and out-migrants is equal. Second, the per capita incomes of in- and out-migrants may be different. If in-migrants have higher per capita incomes than out-migrants, there will also be a net transfer of income. We expect that migrants moving to New West counties will have higher per capita incomes than out-migrants, and therefore New West counties will gain income from both the total number and income characteristics of in-migrants. In order to test this expectation, we use the income efficiency and decomposition measures on the IRS/Census data used by Plane (1999).

The first two columns represent total aggregate income (in thousands of dollars) for in- and out-migrants. For each county group, the figure in the in-migrant income column represents a “gain” to the county group, the figure in the out-migrant income column represents a “loss” to other counties, and the figure in the net migration income column represents the net gain or loss of aggregate income by the county group due to migration. All county groups—although not all counties within each category—experienced a net gain in aggregate income. Rural counties in the Mountain West had an average aggregate income transfer of 3.7 million dollars per county—a total transfer of almost one billion dollars. Approximately 87 percent of the gain in total aggregate income over the three-year time period was concentrated in New West counties. The net aggregate income gain was approximately ten million dollars per county for New West counties, versus slightly less than one million per county for the next highest group (mining/manufacturing).

The last column of Table 4 is an income-migration effectiveness measure. This measure controls for the different population sizes of the county groups, and is thus a better measure of the impact of income transfers than is net aggregate income. Income-migration effectiveness is defined as the percentage ratio of the net to the total amount of aggregate income accruing to in-and out-migrants (see Plane 1999). As can be seen in the last column of Table 4, New West counties have the most highly effective income gains, with almost twice the increase of the next highest county group (nonspecialized).

In examining the Hoover index and the various measures of income change, we have seen that, in the Mountain West region, population growth and income are increasingly spatially concentrated and that such concentration is focusing on what we have called New West counties. Table 6 presents information on the relative population sizes of each county grouping (average size over the 1994–1997 time period), the relative number of in-migrants to each county group over the three-year time period, and the relative aggregate income gain. Of the approximately 4.5 million people who lived in rural areas in the Mountain West during these years, 45 percent were found in New West counties—twice as many as in the next largest county grouping. Eighty-seven percent of the almost one billion dollars of aggregate income gain the rural West experienced during this time period came to New West counties.

The current population distribution is primarily in New West places. If current trends continue, in-migration will continue to reinforce this pattern. Of perhaps equal importance, immigrants are bringing with them significantly more income, due to the ratio of in-migrants to out-migrants and because the in-migrants to New West counties have higher incomes. If this pattern persists, the rural Mountain West will find itself increasingly divided between high-amenity and high-income locations and other, less favored locations.

The Changing West

In this article, we aimed to try to delineate where the New West is, to associate patterns of population growth through migration with our typology, and to see what the impact of in-migration to the region was based on local levels of changing per capita and aggregate income. We were able to successfully identify clusters of counties based on their economic structure and, using the classification scheme, associate increasing population growth with New West counties. These counties are attracting the greatest amount of new in-migrants of all the county groups in the Mountain West region, and this has resulted in higher levels of population concentration in the rural West. The New West has also attracted a disproportionate share of income associated with net migration, thus providing the basis for potential sustainability of the service-based economies in the New West counties.

Many of the rural counties in the West must cope with shifting employment bases, rapid population growth, and the changing demographic makeup of their populations. The metamorphosis of parts of the rural West from Old to New—which includes changes in employment, population size, and demographic composition—has affected these areas in a number of ways, including engendering conflicts over what the West is or what it ought to be.

They come from all over, modern-day migrants fleeing freeways, smog and crime, yearning for their own little piece of the West. And with each new arrival, there is that much less of the wide open space they all crave. The American West is
torn between two visions of one place. Although many cling desperately to the Old West ethos of a hardworking people who came to tame the land and tap its wealth, other are just as determined to bring on a New West, where nature is no longer ravaged, but restored. Yet, before the West can choose between Old and New, this stream of new settlers could doom them both.... Amid all the clamor over mining and grazing, grizzlies and wolves, [there is] something more troubling.... The single most dramatic resource issue we face, and I mean really immediately, is people. (Diringer 1994, 1)

Like Snow (1997), Robbins (1994) suggests that the West's principal identifying characteristic is its continuity of change. He identifies three driving forces that have caused and continue to cause change in the West: interaction among (1) the West's "unique geography," (2) investment in and exploitation of its natural resources by external capital interests, and (3) the imposition of federal policy. Robbins argues that continuous change stems from the rural West's dependent relations with external actors and institutions that represent the increasing linkage of economic actors in rural areas to distant, powerful financial and market forces that determine the nature of land use and economic activity from outside the region. Thus, rural change is a spatially variable condition that results from the interaction of place characteristics with the processes that continually (re)create such places. Such continuity of change affects employment patterns, population movements, and demographic characteristics of places. For the rural West, this has meant that places have shifted from being areas of production to becoming areas of consumption. This results in a New West in which the importance of mineral, cattle, and lumber production is diminished by an economy based on a new paradigm of the amenity region, which creates increased demands for amenity space, residential and recreational property, second homes, and environmental protection. One result of both changing employment and production/consumption patterns is a changing pattern of population concentration, population deconcentration, and demographic differences related to selective patterns of in- and out-migration. These processes continue to reshape and reinforce settlement patterns, rural western landscapes, and the rural West as a place.
