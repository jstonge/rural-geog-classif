Business Services, Productivity, and Wages: Metropolitan and Nonmetropolitan Michigan Counties, 1977–1997*

Jay D. Gatrell
Indiana State University

This article investigates the growth of business services (SIC73) in Michigan and models the relationship between the industry and local economic conditions in metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties. The research makes four conclusions. First, SIC73 measures explain variance in manufacturing productivity. Second, SIC73 indicators explain variation in manufacturing and overall wages. Third, these relationships vary between metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties. Despite growth in rural regions, SIC73 indicators more effectively predict wages and productivity in metropolitan counties. Hence, the localized benefits associated with SIC73 industries are more strongly associated with metropolitan economies. Fourth, the interdependencies that have developed between SIC73 industries and the manufacturing sector decrease over time in both metropolitan and nonmetropolitan contexts. The article concludes with suggestions for future research. Key Words: business services, economic development, productivity, wages.

Over the past few decades, specialized service industries have experienced rapid growth across rural and urban America (Hansen 1990, 1993; Lindahl and Beyers 1999). This article investigates the growth of these services in Michigan and models the relationship between the industry and local economic conditions in metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties. By focusing on a single collection of specialized services, business services (Standard Industrial Classification [SIC] 73), the article considers whether an expanded division of labor has resulted in two divergent economic geographies—one metropolitan, the other not.

To test this hypothesis, the article develops three models. These models investigate the relationship between the business-service industries and three local economic indicators: overall average wages, manufacturing wages, and manufacturing productivity. In concert, the results are used to determine if the trajectories of Michigan’s metropolitan and nonmetropolitan economies vary. In the following pages, the article: (1) defines business services, (2) examines the related literature, (3) investigates the geography of business services in Michigan’s counties, and (4) concludes with a discussion of trends observed in metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties.

Business Services:
Definition and Location

In 1985, Daniels’ Service Industries: A Geographical Appraisal sketched a changing industrial geography. Instead of investigating traditional industrial agglomerations, this work charted a new research agenda for economic geography that emphasized the exploration of emerging corporate complexes. Today, a new post-Fordist industrial geography has emerged that is characterized by a complex network of specialized service providers and manufacturers, narrowed economies of scope within and beyond the service sector, and an intensification of knowledge-producing industries (Storper 1997). In concert, these factors have blurred the boundary between the service and manufacturing sectors and have prompted researchers to focus on the dynamics of producer services to understand the changing structure of developed economies (McCrackin 1985; Tschetter 1987; Sass 1990).

Conceptually, the following review accomplishes several tasks. First, it defines business
services. Second, it positions business services within the theoretical context of industrial restructuring, the spatial division of labor, and contemporary industrialization. Finally, it provides an overview of selected theoretical and empirical research concerning the geography of business services.

Defining Business Services

The definition of business services is imprecise. Investigators face the uncertain task of discriminating between the end-users of services—firms, or individual consumers. While this consumption-based definition appears straightforward enough, the reality is anything but simple. This dilemma is known as the “‘mixed market’ problem” (Wernerheim and Sharpe 1999). That is, some services are targeted at both types of end-users (Goe and Shanahan 1990). For this reason, efforts to define business services result in a range of conceptual and practical concerns.

Conceptually, the notion that business versus consumer services can be clearly delineated is suspect, especially as the distinction between these two markets is blurred by technology (and other forces) that continue to redefine the absolute and relative boundaries of places, firms, and industries. As a result, no universally accepted definition of producer services has evolved. Nevertheless, efforts to define business services have continued. In practice, the data requirements of producer-service research have prompted researchers to reach a consensus concerning a few select industrial classifications. The most frequent classification of producer services corresponds to the two-digit SIC code 73, “Business Services” (O’hUallachain and Reid 1991, 1992). SIC73 includes advertising, direct-mail marketing, equipment leasing, computer programming, building maintenance, and many other services. Additional expanded definitions of producer services have also included the FIRE industries (SIC60—finance, insurance, and real estate), legal services (SIC81), management and engineering services (SIC87), and miscellaneous services (SIC89) (Harrington and Lombard 1989; O’hUallachain and Reid 1991). However, the spatial distribution of these activities tends to heavily favor urban areas, resulting in a variety of data limitations (to be discussed later). This research uses the two-digit SIC73 definition of producer services.

Importance

With the growth of information-intensive industries, the business-services sector has been considered a barometer for the changing structure of the economy. The sector has been linked to ongoing specialization, the segmentation of the spatial division of labor, increased productivity, and wage growth (Hansen 1990, 476; Hansen 1993, 255; Tickell 1999).1 For this reason, a considerable producer-services literature has developed within geography and across the social sciences to understand the dynamics of this rapidly changing industry.

Throughout the many interrelated literatures, one theme is ubiquitous: firms must obtain access to specialized skills and new knowledges. However, firms do not necessarily have internal capacities to meet the demands of rapidly changing markets and technologies. Because of this, advanced service industries have emerged to meet their needs. In the advanced services, the skill-sets of firms often represent the state of the art in an industry and are necessary to maintain and upgrade existing products and processes, particularly in the manufacturing sector (Calzonetti, Allison, and Gatrell 1999). For these reasons, the role of producer services is a central component of contemporary industrialization in rural and urban places (Illeris 1996). Consequently, the once-“peripheral” functions associated with service firms now lie at the heart of contemporary industrialization and industrial geography (Amirahmadi and Wallace 1995; Wood 1996).

In an attempt to increase the overall efficiency and flexibility of firm performance, businesses develop a range of service-provision strategies. The specific strategy a firm employs depends upon the local labor market, internal capacity and organizational structure, technological change, and interfirm linkages. Accordingly, firms can increase flexibility in four contexts:

1. Numerical (layoffs/rehires);
2. Functional (multitasking);
3. Temporal (part-time/overtime); and
4. Tenurial (subcontracting/temporary) (Coffey and Bailly 1991)2
In each case, intra- and interfirm flexibility mandates access to and increased demand for a variety of producer services, such as employment agencies and computer programming. As Coffey and Bailly’s strategies suggest, firms can develop a variety of flexible service-delivery plans to supplement, expand, and increase internal capacity and improve the overall competitiveness of their firm and the local economy.

Location: Metropolitan versus Nonmetropolitan Space Economies

In 1995, a special “Focus” section of The Professional Geographer (vol. 47, no. 1) was dedicated to producer-services research. The special section identified three determinants of service-firm location: (1) proximity to customers, (2) access to highly skilled labor, and (3) the information intensity of “front” versus “back” office functions (Bailly 1995; Coffey 1995; Daniels 1995; Harrington 1995). Besides these three factors, the special section also recognized the potential for enabling information technologies (IT) to liberate firms from the spatial limits of everyday life and the degree to which technologies influence the development of the business-services sector (see also Beyers 1993).

The initial emphasis on the locational determinants of business-service firms within the literature can be linked to the locational characteristics of industrial restructuring and spatial characteristics of vertical disintegration experienced throughout the 1980s (Harrington and Lombard 1989; Goe 1991). Because much of the early research focused on vertical disintegration and the emergence of postindustrial corporate-service complexes in existing urban centers, the geography of business services has essentially been an urban geography.

With almost nine out of ten producer-service jobs located in metropolitan areas, the industry—and the literature about it—does have an inherent urban bias (Beyers 1992, 1993; Gatrell 1999). Within the context of academic research, these empirical realities translate into a predominately metropolitan research agenda that focuses on the dynamics of highly localized production systems and corporate complexes. These localized industries benefit from the pooling of highly skilled labor, intangible information flows, spillovers, and the material inputs of flexible supplier networks (Lyons 1995; Reid 1995). Additionally, the institutional benefits of contemporary industrialization—including research universities, tax incentives, and venture capital—are more readily available in urban areas (Feldman 1994; DeVol 1999).

Despite the industry’s urban orientation, the geography of producer services in peripheral regions and the diffusion of industry activities across space should be more fully understood. The growth of business services in rural regions has been widely discussed in the literature and throughout the policy community (Harrington 1995; Beyers and Lindahl 1996a, 1996b). Scott’s (1988) model of the decentralization helps to understand the dynamics of the industry’s growth. Scott’s model—a variation on the product-cycle model—combines an internal and external hierarchy that acknowledges the strong centripetal forces of existing economic centers. Internally, Scott’s model demonstrates the outward movement of economic activity from central business districts to the suburbs. Once activities enter the suburbs, a second downward phase occurs when firms seek external locations within the urban hierarchy. Finally, the decentralization of economic activities moves across space from existing metropolitan centers to peripheral regions.

In general terms, this model reflects the locational behavior of the business-service sector. That is, the initial outward movement of business services from the city-center to the suburbs has been well documented (O’hUallachain and Reid 1992; Coffey, Drolet, and Polese 1996). The outward phase, characterized by an intrametropolitan diffusion of the industry, coincides with the deskilling of labor, the development of supporting telecommunications infrastructures, and the separation of “front office” and “back office” activities within firms, as well as the suburbanization of clients. For each of these reasons, the industry has decentralized.

However, the decentralization of services industries has been inherently uneven. Urban and nonurban service geographies can be readily differentiated based on the relative concentration of high-paid and highly skilled jobs in urban areas and low-wage and low-skilled jobs in rural regions (Glaseimer and Howland 1994). Because of this generalized spatial division of labor, the producer-services literature
tends to focus almost exclusively on information- and transaction-intensive “higher-order” business services in urban areas (for example, O’hUallachain and Reid 1991; Coffey and Shearmur 1997; Graves 1997).

In Michigan, the industry’s urban trajectory is evident. In 1997, the state’s twenty-five metropolitan counties—all located along the I-94 (Detroit to Benton Harbor), I-96 (Detroit to Grand Rapids) or I-75 (Detroit to Saginaw) industrial corridors—accounted for 95 and 86 percent of statewide employment in the business-services and manufacturing sectors, respectively. In addition to the concentration of total business-service employment within the industrial corridors, the average concentration of SIC73 workers in MSA counties—approximately 8.3 percent—was also higher than statewide concentrations in 1997 (see Table 1). Collectively, these counties—especially the Detroit-Ann Arbor-Saginaw-Lansing agglomeration—have comprised the center of the state’s automobile manufacturers and suppliers.

Because of this urban bias, the producer-services literature assumes that low-skill standardized service jobs will migrate from primary centers to more peripheral locations as telecommunications infrastructures are developed and skill sets standardized (Gatrell 1999). In the process, the literature assumes that the decentralization of business services alters the structure of the sectoral interdependencies and benefits observed in urban areas. In short, service-led development in nonmetropolitan regions has been conceptually limited to low-skill and low-wage jobs with few localized benefits. Unfortunately, this conceptual limitation has been reinforced by the regional inequalities that have been documented with respect to the urban geography of higher-paying advanced business services (Bailly 1995). Despite rapid growth throughout the sector and around the nation, comparative analyses between metropolitan and nonmetropolitan regions to determine the trajectory of the localized benefits do not figure prominently in the literature.

While it has been assumed that peripheral locations would house more generic service providers, the exact diffusion of advanced business services is not so clear. However, in practical terms, this basic conclusion makes sense. That is, local markets do not exist to maintain and support the entry of specialized service providers into peripheral regions. For this reason, successful entrepreneurs and firms in nonurban areas must balance the “local” demand for a wide-ranging collection of more basic services with the competitive realities of delivering highly specialized services to clients beyond the local market (Lindahl and Beyers 1999, 4).

Because of this inherent barrier, the availability of specialized services in peripheral locations has been limited. As such, firms seeking more advanced assistance must obtain services from outside the local area—often from existing centers (MacPherson 1997).

Yet exceptions to this general rule have been documented, as rural regions experience rapid growth throughout the service sector, including the business services (Beyers and Lindahl 1996a). To illustrate the growth and changing nature of service-related development in rural regions, researchers have documented a trend among highly specialized professionals to locate well beyond the boundaries of traditional service centers to take advantage of place-specific quality of life benefits (Beyers and Lindahl 1996a). This phenomenon has been documented around the nation (Atchison 1993; Kotkin 1998) and in northern Michigan regional service centers, such as Traverse City and Petoskey, as professionals abandon cities to head “up north” (Newman 2000; B. Echlin, business reporter, Traverse City Record-Eagle, personal communication via email, 2001).

Known as “lone eagles” (sole proprietors) or “high fliers” (small businesses), these rural service providers maintain key linkages with existing service centers (i.e., major metropolitan areas). On northern Michigan’s high-tech Traverse City “coast,” firms maintain linkages throughout the region, as clients, investors, and potential workers are located in urban centers such as Chicago, Detroit, and Ann Arbor (Echlin 2001). These linkages are essential as service providers seek to identify new opportunities and respond to changing markets (Beyers and Lindahl 1996). In nearly all cases, information technologies are a primary means of communicating with clients and maintaining necessary collaborations with other professionals. However, these professionals are not confined to virtual workplaces. Peripheral service providers recognize that they must meet face-to-face with clients at their home base to maintain stable working relationships (Beyers and Lindahl 1996). For this reason, highly specialized service providers must strategically target markets with the potential to overcome the added costs of maintaining complex transactions over great distances.

In addition to newcomers, indigenous Michigan firms in nonmetropolitan counties—particularly regional service centers—have also become competitive “high fliers” in a variety of niche high-tech service markets (Echlin 2001 and personal communication). In 1997, regional centers in Grand Traverse and Emmet counties had relatively high levels of SIC73 employment—5.6 percent and 4.1 percent, respectively—compared to other nonmetropolitan counties. The concentration of SIC73 workers in these two counties is comparable to those in other rural regional service centers, but much higher than those in most nonmetropolitan counties (Table 2). In Michigan, the concentrations of SIC73 workers are higher in regional service centers located in the Lower Peninsula than in those in the Upper Peninsula.

First, spatial proximity to larger urban markets and current agglomerations are necessary in transaction-intensive industries and cannot be entirely replaced by the “virtual” possibilities of IT (O’hUallachain 1989). Likewise, the structure of some markets will not enable providers to overcome the added costs of distance. Second, nonmetropolitan counties are handicapped by more limited labor markets than are present in metropolitan regions (Glasmier and Howland 1994). For this reason, wage differentials can be observed between metropolitan and nonmetropolitan labor markets insofar as the technical, literacy, and mathematical skills required to perform tasks are not readily available in the latter (Fuguitt, Brown, and Beale 1989). Similarly, a study of computer usage and skills in the workplace demonstrated that the skill level of metropolitan workers is greater than that of nonmetropolitan workers (Kusmin 1996). In concert, these factors result in and reinforce an observed agglomeration within the industry and a tendency for firms to locate in urban areas.

In summary, the subjects of the bulk of research performed by geographers can be subdivided into two primary categories: (1) the locational determinants and spatial diffusion of business-service firms (intra- and intermetropolitan); and (2) the numerous strategies developed by firms to assure delivery of key services and/or of the strategies developed by firms to deliver services to industry. In the first case, research has focused primarily on two interrelated units of analysis—MSAs and urban counties—and has utilized secondary data sources. In the second case, research has typically used the firm as the unit of analysis and has often employed an array of survey techniques and in-depth interviews. A smaller collection of literature has examined the contribution of producer-services industries to the local economies of metropolitan areas.

In contrast to earlier studies that focused almost exclusively on metropolitan areas, this study investigates the contribution of a growing business-services industry to the local economies of both metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties. In particular, the article examines the relationship between the business-services sector and the manufacturing sector. By focusing on manufacturing, the article considers the pivotal role of the business-services industry in deepening the spatial division of labor, improving the competitiveness of firms, and the ability of local economies to reap the benefits of increased specialization (i.e., increased productivity and wages) (Hansen 1990, 1993; Bailly 1995; MacPherson 1997; Calzonetti, Allison, and Gatrell 1999). The study also tests whether business-service variables can statistically account for average observed wages across all sectors. These are important questions to explore as the rapid growth of the service sector in rural regions continues to be of interest to researchers and policymakers alike.

The interaction of variables using this approach has an added significance to geographers. That is, the method focuses on how the interaction between these variables changes not only over time, but also over space. For example, Jones and Hanham (1995) suggest the expansion method contextualizes empirical analysis. The approach enables researchers to pursue “creative” research and aid in the development of models that explore the many and diverse sets of local contingencies that shape sociospatial relationships (Casetti 1972, 81; Jones and Hanham 1995). Within the context of this analysis, the interdependencies between the business-services and manufacturing sectors may change over time. Similarly, the use of the interaction variable may demonstrate how the relationship interacts differently in metropolitan and nonmetropolitan contexts.

Limitations
While “County Business Patterns” and “Census of Manufactures” data are readily available, these resources have important limitations. First, data are suppressed in those cases where only one firm exists and/or firm specific data can be determined. For example, Michigan’s Midland County manufacturing data are excluded because of the prominence of a single firm—Dow Chemicals. Second, the U.S. Census Bureau does not report sole proprietorships or independent enterprises. This policy severely handicaps researchers interested in business-service activities in nonurban areas, because many of these service providers are individual entrepreneurs. As such, this study is unable to reasonably assess the role of the entrepreneur—the “lone eagle”—in either urban or nonurban contexts. Third, the process of combining the two data sets—“Census of Manufactures” and “County Business Patterns”—results in the reduction of total cases, as missing or suppressed data by county does not necessarily correspond. Finally, the SIC system is unable to account for the primary and secondary economic activity of firm or facility. For this reason, differentiating between the types of activities performed in a location or the spatial organization of firms (i.e., front versus back office) is impossible (Goe and Shanahan 1990).

In concert, these important limitations have handicapped research focused on nonurban regions. These issues have limited research into service-led development in rural regions as researchers try to unlock the contribution of service industries to local economies. To minimize these limitations, this research focuses on a single category of producer services—business services (SIC73)—that has a well-established data infrastructure in both urban and rural counties.

Models
The following analysis uses a pooled time-series econometric approach. The data are pooled in the sense that data were collected for all eighty-three Michigan counties at five-year intervals, 1977–1997. By pooling the data, a total possible number of 415 observations are used to test the study models presented below. Each of these regression models has been created to investigate the relationship between the business-service sector, wages, and productivity. The models also explore how these variables may or may not perform differently in metropolitan and nonmetropolitan regions.

The first model examines whether the concentration of business-services employment and metropolitan designation (the independent variables) can statistically explain variance in the dependent variable, manufacturing productivity. This model has been created to explore the general implications of increased specialization within and between firms and to observe how, if at all, specialized producer and business services may statistically explain observed variance in productivity at the county level. Using Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression, the following model tests the relationship between business services and productivity:

\[
P = a + BS + MA + T \tag{Model 1}
\]

where P represents productivity, a is a constant, BS represents the relative concentration of workforce employed by business-service firms,
MA represents metropolitan area designation, and T is the interaction between time and BS.

Using a similar rationale, two wage models were created to address the assertion within the producer-services literature that a deepened spatial division of labor should result in increased specialization within county labor markets and higher wage jobs (Tschetter 1987; Hansen 1990, 1993; O’hUallachain and Reid 1991). The wage models presented below were tested using OLS:

\[
AW = a + BS + MA + BSW + TW + T
\]
(Model 2)

where AW represents average wage for the entire county workforce, a is a constant, BS represents the relative concentration of workforce employed by business-service firms, MA represents metropolitan area designation, BSW represents the average wage of employees in the business-service sector, TW is the interaction between BSW and time, and T is the interaction between time and BS.

\[
AMW = a + BS + MA + BSW + TW + T
\]
(Model 3)

where AMW represents average wage of production workers, a is a constant, BS represents the relative concentration of workforce employed by business-service firms, MA represents metropolitan area designation, BSW represents the average wage of employees in the business-service sector, TW is the interaction between BSW and time, and T is the interaction between time and BS.

The above models were initially tested using OLS. The stepwise approach was then applied to those models identified as statistically significant using the OLS method. The stepwise regression results were calculated with an alpha threshold of 0.125. The estimated equations presented in Tables 4–6 are optimized versions of the models presented above. The probabilities for each obtained r-square are provided, and only statistically significant (\( \alpha = 0.01 \)) results presented.

Michigan, 1977–1997

The case study examines the historical development of the business-service industry in Michigan over twenty years. Over the three decades of the analysis, Michigan’s economy—like those of most of the Great Lakes States—changed dramatically as a result of industrial restructuring. In the following analysis, concentrations of business-service employment at the county level are compared to statewide changes. In global terms, the concentration of business-service employers and employment in Michigan favors urban areas. As Table 1 illustrates, this trend is true in terms of the concentrations of both employment and observed wages. In the following analysis, the divergent geographies of metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties are more closely examined to understand the dynamics of the business-services sector and to demonstrate how the industry is spatially expressed across Michigan’s eighty-three economic landscapes.

Statewide

The performance of the models was most effective when applied to all counties between 1977–1997. All three models were statistically significant; Table 4 presents the results of the stepwise analysis. The least effective model related business-services activities to overall observed wages (r-square = 3.5 percent). The most effective models directly tested the observed covariance between the selected manufacturing sector and SIC73 variables. Measures of business-services activity statistically explain observed variation in both productivity (r-square = 30.5 percent) and manufacturing wages (r-square = 38.8 percent).

In the productivity and manufacturing wage models, the observed levels of productivity were higher in metropolitan areas. In all three models, the predictor MA is positive. In both wage models, the MA predictor demonstrates a clear distinction between metropolitan and nonmetropolitan labor markets, and wage differentials are observed. More importantly, they demonstrate the real-world consequences of the spatial division of labor within and between urban and nonurban labor markets.

Second, the performance of the models illustrates that business-service activities can explain—in part—observed measures of the manufacturing sector. In the productivity model, the measures BS and T are both statistically significant predictors of manufacturing productivity. In addition to productivity, the SIC73 measures (BSW and T) explain variance in manufacturing wages. In each of these cases, the relationship between the business-services and manufacturing indicators is positive.

Third, the results indicate a rather interesting and important possibility. Specifically, the expansion of business-service industry and associated benefits (increased productivity) may be reaping diminished returns over time. This observation is based on the performance of the interaction variables. If the t-values of “time” interactions were positive, the results would indicate that an observed relationship might be accelerating over time. Alternatively, a negative t-value suggests a slowing of the relationship—in this case, the observed relationship between selected SIC73 measures and the productivity variable. For example, the interaction variable T is negatively related to productivity. Hence, the ability of an expanding business-services industry to explain observed productivity growth decreases over time. The same general trend can be observed with respect to overall average wages (i.e., TW). This is an important finding, as it applies to both urban and nonurban counties.

While all three models are statistically significant, the variable MA “entered” the stepwise analysis of the wage models before any of the SIC73 indicators. Consequently, the metropolitan designation of counties was a better predictor of observed wages than were measures of business-service activity. While the business-service measures aided the overall predictive capacity of the models, the metropolitan designation of counties was clearly the more effective predictor. Nevertheless, the performance of the wage models is entirely consistent with earlier research linking business services—particularly advanced-order ones—to metropolitan areas, innovative industrial clusters, and traditional agglomeration economies (Tornqvist 1977; O’hUallachain and Reid 1991). For this reason, this result merely rearticulates the basic principle that population (or size) matters (O’hUallachain 1999). Likewise, the wage results underscore the importance of agglomeration, the industry’s inherently metropolitan home base, and the concentration of advanced services in existing centers (O’hUallachain 1989). For this reason, the wage models are instructive. Indeed, the strength of MA indicates that the role of the business-services sector as a determinant of wages at the county-level should continue to be investigated to better understand the two geographies, urban and rural, of the industry.

In summary, the results of the analysis are consistent with the research expectations and literature. The analysis demonstrates that a statistical relationship between the manufacturing and business-services sectors exists. Likewise, the models demonstrate that metropolitan and nonmetropolitan differences can be observed. The statewide analysis bears out the widely held belief that the service sector in nonmetropolitan regions does “not mirror the developmental experience commonly associated with services in the nation’s cities” (Glasmeier and Howland 1994, 198). This study confirms not only that there are lower concentrations of advanced service jobs in rural regions; but also that the contribution of the industry—high- and low-paying SIC73 jobs combined—to local economies differs between metropolitan and nonmetropolitan locales.

As such, the analysis may enable economic geographers to more effectively model wages and productivity in the future.

In metropolitan cases, only the productivity and average-manufacturing-wage models were significant. In both models, the availability of business services contributes to a deepened division of labor and promotes increased efficiencies through specialization. Hence, the ability of the SIC73 indicators to predict observed productivity in metropolitan counties (r-square = 35.1 percent) is slightly greater than the statewide results (r-square = 30.5 percent). In more exact terms, observed manufacturing productivity and wages are positively associated with the deepening of the spatial division of labor in Michigan. The results indicate that the expansion of business services and the inherent specialization associated with these activities promote increased efficiencies and productivity, as well as contributing to increased manufacturing wages. However, like the statewide analysis, the metropolitan productivity model may suggest a trend towards diminished returns with respect to the interdependencies between the sectors has developed over time (T).

On a related note, the clustering of manufacturing and business-service workers in MSAs that has been observed between 1977 and 1997 indicates that industry has not decentralized. Based on both the statewide and the metropolitan county analysis, the overall scale and scope of the relationship that has developed between the sectors in metropolitan counties is statistically different from nonmetropolitan counties in terms of productivity and manufacturing wages. Hence, the benefits of increased specialization and a deepening of the spatial division of labor favor urban areas.

In nonmetropolitan counties, business-service activity can statistically explain observed manufacturing productivity. Like the statewide and metropolitan results, the concept of “diminished returns” can also be observed. Unlike the case with urban counties, the SIC73 variables do not appear to explain manufacturing wages in nonmetropolitan counties, as model 2 is insignificant. However, measures of SIC73 activity do partially explain observed variance in average county wages (r-square = 17.4 percent). However, the relationship is complex. Interestingly, the interaction variable TW enters the equation first, and both the variables TW and T are positively related to the average county wage. Despite a slightly inverse relationship between business-services wages and average wages, the totality of the model demonstrates that TW and T positively covary with observed increases in average wages.

On balance, the relationship between the nonmetropolitan business-service industries and average county wage is positive. This finding indicates that business-service industries are collectively “pulling up” wages over time (TW and T) in nonmetropolitan counties. At the same time, based on the slightly negative relationship between business-services wages and average county wages, the model also indicates that wage differences between urban and rural labor markets still exist. Glasmeier and Howland (1994, 1995) have emphasized (in admittedly broad strokes) the lower pay associated with service employment compared to other sectors in rural counties.

It would be tempting to try to explain why the performance of the wage models varied between the two subgroups, but the results do not allow this. However, the analyses indicate that differences persist despite rapid growth throughout the service sector in rural counties. Because of the clear metropolitan and nonmetropolitan distinctions, further research is needed to more fully understand how the wage and skill structure of metropolitan and nonmetropolitan labor markets vary and how, if at all, service-led growth in rural regions—including advanced business services—may change the relative geographies of each.

Conclusion

While no single regression model or collection thereof can entirely explain the complex realities and interactions that characterize contemporary industrialization, the findings indicate that business services are a key component of the economic landscape. These results echo earlier findings (Hansen 1990, 1993) that an expanding service industry—particularly specialized services such as business services—can induce local economic development. Unlike previous studies, this article used secondary data to document that the expansion of business services and the deepening of the spatial division of labor contribute to increased productivity and wages in both metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties. This is an important task, as the local contribution of business-service firms must be investigated if the dynamics of service-led development in nonurban regions is to be more fully understood. For this reason, this research represents an expansion of the existing body of empirical research beyond metropolitan regions with an emphasis on the relationship between the business services and the manufacturing sector.

By extending the frame beyond metropolitan regions, the research begins to shed light on key questions concerning the challenges and context of service-led development in nonmetropolitan spaces. The statewide analysis indicates that the trajectory of urban and rural counties in Michigan differ empirically. The findings support the conclusion that urban areas accrue more localized benefits (increased productivity and wages) from the producer services sector relative to nonurban counties. While it is certainly the case that not all regions can be equally engaged in all economic activities, the findings are important because they indicate that the expanded and deepened spatial division of labor may be widening the gap between metropolitan (core) and nonmetropolitan (periphery) counties. Despite the promise of new technologies, lone eagles, and high fliers and the tendency of industries to decentralize over time, the business-services industry in urban areas continues to benefit from the centripetal forces of agglomeration.

In addition to observed metropolitan and nonmetropolitan trajectories, the study found that the relationship between the manufacturing sector and business-service industries is changing over time. Consequently, the localized benefits that have developed between the industries may continue to lessen. This tendency towards diminished returns can be observed in both metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties.

Future Research

Based on the results, three questions emerge. First, why do the benefits of localization and sectoral interdependencies continue to accrue more readily in urban areas? Second, how and why are these interdependencies changing over time and space? Third, how are these changes experienced differently in urban and rural places? While no answers exist, geographers should consider the utility of developing and performing comparative studies to understand the nation’s many economic landscapes. In the process, they will inevitably revisit, rethink, challenge, and confirm widely held positions within the literature.
