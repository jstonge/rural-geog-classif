ECONOMIC-DEVELOPMENT STAKEHOLDER PERSPECTIVES ON BOOMTOWN DYNAMICS IN THE EAGLE FORD SHALE, TEXAS*

TREY MURPHY, CHRISTIAN BRANNSTROM, MATTHEW FRY and MICHAEL EWERS

ABSTRACT. Unconventional oil and gas production in the United States reversed a decades-old trend of rising oil imports, provided an argument for lifting the U.S. crude oil export ban and motivated the development of domestic natural gas export facilities. But the most visible impact of unconventional-hydrocarbon extraction is the creation of boomtowns in rural regions. Despite widespread media coverage, scholarly analysis of boomtowns is restricted to regional econometric studies with little attention to how economic stakeholders understand and respond to booming economies. Here we analyze interviews with key economic stakeholders in the Eagle Ford Shale in Texas. Respondents consider their community’s economic success relative to the price of oil and indicate concerns about the deterioration of roads, high housing demand, and skyrocketing wages. We also re-examine John Gilmore’s foundational work on boomtowns in the 1970s in the context of contemporary unconventional extraction. Keywords: hydraulic fracturing, problem triangle, resource materiality, social disruption.

INTRODUCTION

Unconventional-hydrocarbon extraction, which requires relatively large capital investments and energy inputs (Chew 2014), produces numerous economic and political changes, such as new forms of municipal regulations (Fry, Brannstrom, and Murphy 2015) and the December 2015 reversal of a multidecadal ban on U.S. exports of crude oil. However, the effects of unconventional oil and gas activities on the boomtowns near sites of extraction are not well understood. Here we analyze views of economic-development officials on boomtown issues in the Eagle Ford Shale, focusing on roads, wages, and housing. By modeling how lives are disrupted through the “problem triangle,” John Gilmore, in 1976, provided one of the first modern scientific accounts of boomtowns. Gilmore’s findings are relevant today, but his theory requires updates to cope with the materiality of unconventional hydrocarbons.

In the south Texas Eagle Ford Shale—a major oil, gas, and condensate supplier—the first boomtowns emerged when Petrohawk Energy, a subsidiary of BHP Billiton since 2011, drilled two exploration wells in October 2008 (Figure 1). That first year, the Eagle Ford produced 600 barrels of oil per day. By May 2016, production reached approximately 1.3 million barrels per day, 13 percent of total U.S. production (EIA 2016). Over 16,000 wells have been completed on the Eagle Ford, with another 3,700 permitted as of May 2016 (Railroad Commission of Texas 2016). Hydrocarbon activity in the Eagle Ford provides an estimated $87 billion in economic impact to twenty-one counties and supports 115,000 workers (Tunstall and others 2014).

Boomtowns are isolated semiurban centers near sites of high-demand resource commodities that provide revenue windfalls for local communities, but may strain local infrastructure, social structures, and governance (Gilmore 1976; Haslam McKenzie 2013). Prior studies identified numerous deleterious local impacts of boomtown development, including high levels of individual stress, diminished social cohesiveness, rising social tensions from rapid population growth, and high labor turnover (Kassover 1981). Boomtowns respond to economic circumstances based on site-specific characteristics, including “commodity base, location and socio-demographic structure” (Lawrie, Tonts, and Plummer 2011, 160). However, the boomtown literature fails to capture these local, place-based development characteristics and the profound transformations towns undergo from commodity-driven growth (Barnes and Hayter 2005;
Lawrie, Tonts, and Plummer 2011). One of the few exceptions is Trevor Barnes, Roger Hayter, and Elizabeth Hay’s (2001) work that uses the metaphorical destruction of a community by a meteorological cyclone to describe the entry of external firms in a single industry, resource towns, and the negative impact of their subsequent flight after resource depletion.

In the Eagle Ford Shale, news media reported the sprawling landscape of “man camps,” large production royalty checks, and new grocery stores, breathing economic life into declining rural communities. Chain hotels, built rapidly to accommodate oil-field workers, offered 3 a.m. breakfasts and charged $325 per night at the height of the boom in July 2014. Some towns specialized in building hotel capacity for the Eagle Ford Shale (Malewitz 2014; Buchele 2015).

We describe and analyze economic-development stakeholder views of boomtown processes using concepts from Gilmore’s 1976 model of boomtown development. We extend and amplify Gilmore’s model to consider features of unconventional drilling (for example, long-distance commuting, recovery processes and time) while aligning it with recent literature on boomtown development. We find that industry-specific characteristics of the unconventional oil and gas sector reproduce a set of well-documented boomtown processes, but also generate new development dynamics not previously identified by other boomtown studies. Moreover, these revisions create testable predictions that may be applied to other boomtowns overwhelmed by unconventional-hydrocarbon production. Therefore, we are fulfilling Jeffrey Jacquet and David Kay’s recommended research agenda: “Focusing on the differences [between the boomtown literature of the 1970s and today] may be even more fruitful when it comes to generating data, analysis, and contemporary insights that can aid energy impacted communities” (2014, 15).

Certain regions of the Eagle Ford Shale (EFS) had experienced hydrocarbon exploration between the late 1800s and early 1980s (Pirtle 2011). Prior to the 2008 boom, the western and central areas of the EFS focused on cattle ranching and hunting leases, while the eastern areas concentrated on farming, ranching, and poultry production. Most municipalities had less than 5,000 people, with stable or decreasing populations. As recently as 2011, some EFS counties experienced poverty rates above 30 percent, double the U.S. 2011 average of 15 percent (Tunstall 2015). Thomas Tunstall also noted that EFS municipalities experienced difficulty deploying windfalls to diversify their economies, prepare for the eventual downturn in oil prices, and maintain public infrastructure in the midst of the boom.

We obtained data using a semistructured framework to interview fifteen respondents, selected for their role in the economic development of their cities or counties, in eleven unique face-to-face interview sessions between August 2014 and March 2015 (TAMU-Institutional Review Board #2013-0157). Our fieldwork period included the recent peak in global crude oil prices, and captured the steady decline in the price of oil, causing firms to halt investments and
reduce operations (Aaronson and Malewitz 2015). Petroleum prices plummeted from nearly $100 per barrel in June 2014 to $26 per barrel in January 2016. Between fall 2014 and winter 2015, the U.S. oil and gas labor force decreased by over 100,000, according to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (Gallucci 2016). Therefore, we conducted follow up interviews with four respondents and new interviews with seven economic stakeholders in June 2016. All quotations refer to 2014–2015 interviews unless noted specifically as “June 2016.”

Respondents represented four organizations responsible for boomtown policymaking: chambers of commerce that advocate for probusiness policies; local development corporations, funded by tax revenue, responsible for attracting new business and lobbying for business-friendly regulations; Texas county governments, led by elected judges and commissioners; and municipal policymakers, who provide economic development and impact perspectives. Nearly all EFS hydrocarbon production occurs under county jurisdiction, where firms must comply with state protocols, floodplain regulations, and any infrastructure-related county guidelines.

The views of economic-development actors, who regularly interact with firms, are highly relevant because they indicate how local residents, businesses, and petroleum firms experience rapid economic growth. Each interview lasted thirty minutes to three hours. Similar to Peter Kedron and Sharmistha Bagchi-Sen’s investigation of the Iowan ethanol industry, we sampled respondents purposively, aiming to define a spatially diverse group of respondents from major drilling areas (2011). We transcribed then coded the interviews in Atlas.ti, using a grounded theory approach to identify emergent themes, which we use to structure our findings (Creswell 2012).

Boomtown Geographies in the Unconventional Hydrocarbon Era

Unconventional hydrocarbons require heavy investment in capital and energy to extract oil and gas from difficult-to-access reservoirs (Chew 2014). Unconventional oil and gas from shale in the United States boomed after the early 2000s, corresponding with hydraulic fracturing’s improved economic viability combined with lax environmental regulations—especially in Texas, where oil and gas extraction is embedded into the state’s political culture (Rahm 2011). Scholars have reported public health, environmental, and environmental justice concerns arising from the fracking boom (Johnston, Werder, and Sebastian 2016).

Boomtowns in the unconventional-hydrocarbon era are sites of interactions among diverse hydrocarbon firms, ranging from multinational corporations to specialized drilling services contractors. Despite centralizing knowledge-intensive business services at their headquarters in global energy centers, such as Houston, hydrocarbon production still requires a physical presence of firms and their employees.

Although fracking spurred boomtowns, knowledge of town dynamics has lagged behind environmental, social perceptions, and econometric studies. In
the New Albany Shale, survey respondents were concerned about decreasing population and a depressed regional economy that could deter companies moving to the region (Crowe and others 2015). Kathryn Brasier and her colleagues argued that the perceived benefits and consequences associated with fracking are spatially diverse across the Marcellus Shale, as a result of population size, proximity to metropolitan centers, the amount of preexisting infrastructure development, and the history of extraction (2011). In the EFS, scholars have studied road damage and how communities can economically diversify (Rahm, Fields, and Farmer 2015; Tunstall 2015). In the North Dakotan Bakken Shale, Bret Weber, Julia Geigle, and Carenlee Barkdull examined the performance of social services during the boom (2014). In the Barnett Shale, residents and government officials are aware of energy extraction’s social and environmental costs and yet still believe that it provides economic-development potential (Anderson and Theodori 2009; Theodori 2012).

Theoretical framing of boomtown dynamics allows for comparisons and predictions across unconventional-hydrocarbon sites. Gilmore provided one of the foundational social scientific accounts of boomtowns, tracing the development of the hypothetical town of Pistol Shot. Based on 400 structured interviews through a twenty-one-page questionnaire and two focus groups, Gilmore noted that most boomtowns experience four phases of development: enthusiasm for the possibilities of economic growth; uncertainty as the resource extraction descends on the city; panic as citizens find themselves unable to cope with the changes; and adaptation to the major consequences of resource extraction (Gilmore and Duff 1975; Gilmore 1976). Many scholars adapted and reinterpreted these four stages of development (for example, Ennis, Tofa, and Finlayson 2014; Cope and others 2015).

Gilmore used a “problem triangle” to illustrate how the challenges that boomtowns face can spiral into ruin as the town is convoluted by economic and societal shocks that result in social disruption (Figure 2). Impact was greatest when local business (e.g., grocery stores, housing) and government (e.g., schools, police, hospitals) services were insufficient for the boom. Diminished private industry efficiency and local government services prompted existing firms, long-term residents, and newcomers to consider leaving and discouraged new firms from locating in the area. This disappearance of nonextractive industries resulted in degraded quality of life that would then potentially compel other residents and businesses to leave. This feedback loop, Gilmore contended, would endure until a stasis or reduction of resource extraction was achieved.

According to Gilmore, the solution to the problem triangle was through growth-management policies that align local, state, and federal government actions, while coordinating with the media, energy resource industry, and broader public. As such, Gilmore yearned for alliances between all levels of government, extractive industries, and other stakeholders to manage resource development (Schmidt 1981). The problem triangle has received insufficient attention from boomtown scholars, even as they debated Gilmore’s related claims. Kenneth Wilkinson and others suggested that resource extraction could be positive for communities (1982). Ignoring the problem triangle, he provided a critique of Gilmore’s social-disruption hypothesis, arguing that quantitative research is more appropriate to examine the boomtown consequences of resource development. Recently, social scientists have referred to the problem triangle in broad examinations of boomtowns (Jacquet 2009, 2014), while discussing the importance of updating theoretical models (like the problem triangle) for unconventional-hydrocarbon extraction (Jacquet and Kay 2014). Scholars also assert that resource extraction leading to boomtown development disrupts municipal services, as indicated through the problem triangle (see England and Albrecht 1984; Weber, Geigle, and Barkdull 2014). Although boomtowns can recover or even improve over pre-boom conditions (Brown, Dorins, and Krannich 2005), officials must take action to mitigate the consequences of these negative economic shocks (Besser, Recker, and Agnitsch 2008).

Human capital acquisition in boomtowns follows a similar pattern to resource economies, such as rapid labor market growth with initial development, specialization around resource extraction, cost cutting as exploration turns into production, and ultimately, unemployment and out-migration unless
new deposits are found (Lucas 1971). More recently, these patterns have become more pronounced with the reliance on service industries to operate extraction activities, which allows for rapid responses to market activity, and through long-distance commuting (LDC) arrangements—such as fly-in, fly-out or drive-in, drive-out—which only temporarily places workers in resource regions (Tonts 2010; Keough 2015). However, LDC arrangements limit worker-community integration and paralyze sustainable municipal development (Storey 2001).

STAKEHOLDER PERSPECTIVES ON BOOMTOWN DYNAMICS

INTERPRETING ECONOMIC DEVELOPMENT: “GOD, PLEASE SEND US ONE MORE OIL BOOM”

EFS economic-development officials are cognizant of the potential for fluctuations in drilling activity, yet hopeful that drilling will persist, even as they realize that development is dependent on the price of oil, the nature of the organizations, and the subsurface geology. As a county commissioner claimed, “I think they’re going to keep on drilling. It’s the price. The price is going to determine what’s going to happen” (EFS011). This respondent compared the current boom to the late 1970–1980s Austin Chalk saying, “It’s not over. This isn’t like the first boom… This one here is going to go on for I’d say twenty years.” Similarly, a chamber of commerce director exclaimed, “I see this town as being an oil town, every bit of twenty years!” (EFS009). EFS011 recalled a bumper sticker from that era: “God, please send us one more oil boom and I promise I won’t piss this one off.” He told us, “I remember that sticker well,” suggesting that Texas petroleum extraction has an entrenched, cyclical history between booms and busts. Economic-development outcomes are linked to a commodity known for its drastic swings in price, as reflected in comments such as, “It all depends on the price of oil!” (EFS004).

Respondents embraced the idea of their local governments having persistent economic ties to the oil industry and the revenues it generates. As the city manager of a boomtown said, “The reserves are here; it’s mainly a matter related to the price, as soon the price adjusts itself to whatever level they want, then the drilling will commence full speed ahead again” (EFS006). Another city manager of a boomtown near significant hydrocarbon production was unconcerned by the drop in oil prices, acknowledging that “there is some diminishing on the exploration part [of drilling],” but also noting that “the production side of it is still very active, no matter where the price of oil in the global markets is” (EFS007). Overall, respondents associated economic development with oil prices. The price of oil, obviously outside the control of respondents, is considered as essential as any other policy instrument for delivering economic growth.

At the same time, some economic-development stakeholders attempted to insulate their jurisdictions from oil-price volatility. A chamber of commerce executive director admitted, “In the [1980s] first boom, we missed a lot of things… And so, as the second [current] boom comes along, they [local
policymakers] have been a lot more cognizant of what do we need to do to make sure these companies . . . stay longer” (EFS010).

Economic criticism of hydrocarbon firms was modest. For example, one respondent complained that large hydrocarbon firms “think they’re going to come down here and pull the wool over these people [local residents and officials]... It’s been a hard scrabble for years and years, trying to make ends meet. Before this [oil and gas boom] happened, we were one of the twenty poorest counties in the whole U.S.” (EFS003). Another respondent urged caution in using mineral wealth to expand public-sector employment, suggesting the need to consider the inevitability of nonrenewable resource extraction: “mineral taxes are cyclical, and so none of them are permanent. The value of the well today first drilled is as high as it will ever be. It will only go down from there and eventually die to zero” (EFS015).

ROADS: “A BUNCH OF STRAIGHT RUNNING IDIOTS”

Degradation of transport infrastructure from increased traffic and heavy loads over weight-limited sections of roadway was a major concern among economic-development stakeholders (Fig. 3). Approximately 1,272 truck trips are required to drill and frack a gas well on the Barnett Shale with an additional 88 truck trips required per year for the life of the well to maintain the well and withdraw saltwater and flowback fluids; refracking requires nearly 1,000 additional trucks (Quiroga, Fernando, and Oh 2012). Moreover, wells are often located in areas serviced by roads that quickly deteriorate under the unprecedented heavy loads, encouraging the emergence of dangerous potholes, damaged culverts, and weakened road edges (Rahm and others 2015). Approximately $133,000 per well is required to upgrade and maintain county road systems that enable both energy industry and general uses (Naismith Engineering 2015).

For example, a chamber of commerce director from a boomtown near intense drilling noted how roads were the “biggest headache of this whole thing” for local officials. “In these small cities and small counties,” road degradation is the issue that “gets the citizens most excited” (EFS002). A county commissioner said, “That’s the one thing that the oil industry has brought here is getting our roads slapped up, and we only have ‘x’ number of dollars to put into our roads” (EFS014). Although the state government had allocated funds to its transportation department (TXDOT) to repair roads, several respondents were highly skeptical, calling the state agency “a bunch of straight-running idiots” (EFS003)—a theme highlighted by other stakeholders who reported tension between state and local governments related to road funding.

The 2013 Texas legislative session produced a bill that disbursed $225 million to county governments to alleviate traffic concerns through County Energy Transportation Reinvestment Zones (CETRZ) managed by TXDOT (Batheja 2013). Another problem noted by respondents was the lag between hydrocarbon drilling and arrival of tax receipts to counties where drilling is taking place. Policymakers indicated that they had an electoral and legal imperative to not raise taxes; under state regulations, raising the effective property tax rate by more than 8 percent exposes the budget to a potential rollback election—a voter-petitioned referendum on the increased tax rate. The lag between the start of production and the arrival of tax dollars further complicates the issue: “Even if [a well] is completed in December 2015, then it’s January-December 2016 that the evaluation is calculated upon. In Texas, counties cannot assess road-usage impact fees. However, several respondents approached oil and gas companies about making donations to the county to help mitigate damage costs. The requested donation is relatively small, around a few thousand dollars per well. As one respondent indicated, “The oil companies have been very good about working with the county for [donating] some [road] material. We have come up with an option for the oil companies that all oil companies... have taken up. Basically, they make an $8,000 donation towards the roads per well. It ain’t much” (EFS015).
Road deterioration became so significant that state legislators revised a longstanding legal opinion withholding royalties from mineral interests under county roads and rights-of-way, and conveyed the wealth to the state’s budget, rather than the county jurisdiction (Blanchard 2014). During the 2015 legislative session, EFS county government leaders successfully lobbied the Texas legislature to return county-road mineral royalties to counties starting in 2017 (EFS015; EFS017).

Finally, respondents noted problems with the quality of truck drivers. One respondent lamented how high demand for licensed truck drivers influenced road safety: “We have a bunch of trucking companies, and they will hire you, get your [commercial driver’s license], and then you have a guy who has been driving an eighteen-wheeler for three months, who thinks he’s been driving it for thirty years. And he’s going ninety miles per hour down the highway with a full load” (EFS009). Respondents noted that these drivers are frequently overworked. Driver fatigue presents its own challenges, especially on county roads with significant traffic, which is exacerbated by the twenty-four-hour nature of the oil and gas industry: the drilling, then fracking, of a well rarely stops until completion in rural areas.

HOUSING: “THERE IS NOWHERE TO HOUSE THE FAMILY HERE”

Respondents mentioned housing as a major inhibitor of future growth in their communities. Each respondent discussed housing unprompted, referring to the lack of long-term housing or the overabundance of temporary accommodations. Respondents cited possible tax benefits of allowing hotel-motel accommodations in the city, and the secondary businesses such as restaurants and shopping centers that hydrocarbon workers support.

Long-distance commuting (LDC) appears to be a popular form of workforce management on the EFS. In the LDC mode of labor production, personnel work in the EFS for a period of days or weeks, and then return to their homes, which are outside the production area. No data exist on the spatial origin on EFS workers, but it is likely that they drive from large Texas metropolitan centers. Regional development driven by LDC does not have the same positive economic impact as traditional development (Storey 2001). One respondent articulated the arrival of LDC petroleum workers reflecting that “you would walk into a restaurant and know everybody. And now you walk and you don’t know anybody!” (EFS005).

According to respondents, EFS energy workers face insufficient housing in towns that were unprepared for the arrival of workers to the region. Initially, workers found accommodations in short and extended-stay housing, but these were quickly exhausted. Respondents noted the strength of the housing demand and how their cities attempted to cope with that demand: “Now, we spend more time answering questions probably for people in the oil industry about anything—about buildings and about motels, and about housing” (EFS002).
To meet demand, some municipalities advocated for the rapid construction of hotels and some urban redevelopment. For example, the number of hotels in Cotulla, a town of 3,000 people southwest of San Antonio, increased from three in 2008 to twenty-six. Many towns have been vying for recreational-vehicle (RV) parks and other unconventional accommodations that can decrease the pressure on the local housing markets. These self-described “man camps” are popular in boomtowns and create dramatic news headlines. Man camps appear in several forms, ranging from RV parks, which have little infrastructure (Figure 4), to modular, quasi-permanent structures offering amenities (Figure 5). Finally, loft apartments were built in a converted movie theater in downtown Kenedy, a town of 3,000 people southeast of San Antonio (Figure 6). In other municipalities, officials discussed construction of new apartments: “These oilfield guys, they get tired of living in those mobile units. They want something little more permanent. So, the apartment complexes are important to them too” (EFS008).

Economic-development stakeholders interpreted the housing demand as a problem for quality of life. One respondent noted, “The reason why these people aren’t bringing their families here is because there is nowhere to house the family here. Or it is so expensive” (EFS005). Another indicated that “[I]f you try to recruit people from outside the area, the biggest complaint is that, ‘We can’t afford to live there,’” and went on to focus on rent: “You could have rented a house here for $250, $350 a month six or seven years ago. Furthermore, officials have attempted to entice permanent housing developers to the region, often unsuccessfully, because the “initial costs of land are high so it drives the square-footage costs to build and then [developers have to] import materials and import the labor” (EFS003). Communities benefited from the short and extended-stay boom in housing through occupancy taxes shared between town governments and municipally based economic-development agencies. “The business [the energy industry has] brought to town just in motel-hotel taxes, which [the chamber of commerce] gets a portion of... Hotel-motel taxes have gone from $100,000 to over a million dollars!” (EFS008). Depending on the regulations in place to disburse the tax revenue, municipalities and the economic-development organizations can use funds to improve services, enhance the visibility of economic-development opportunities, or improve quality of life. For example, in a meeting in August 2014, a group of civic leaders on the western portion of the EFS discussed using this money to build a new electronic billboard to attract interstate travelers to downtown, construct a municipal golf course, improve landscaping, expand internet connection, or attract new events (for example, rodeo, firearms shows, motocross). Other cities used the burgeoning tax revenues to build new park facilities, airports, hospitals, and maintain convention centers (EFS006; EF007 June 2016; EFS013 June 2016; EFS014). Counties used the money to rebuild worn roads and to expand or renovate county courthouses.

Many officials emphasized how the increased sales tax from accommodations influenced economic development. Local government officials stressed that workers bring opportunities for increased sales-tax revenue. This revenue is not restricted to the municipalities: “In the past [the county officials] were
lucky to have sales-tax returns of $40,000 or $45,000 a month. Well, now they are over $500,000” (EFS006). Another respondent noted, “We also get a very small percentage of sales tax—so the more people that you have in here that are dealing with oil and gas activities, the more things they’re buying, the more groceries, the more gas, and the more of everything else” (EFS014).

Not all officials were enthusiastic about construction of hotel and motels. One respondent articulated a fear, expressed by other officials, of overbuilding, stating that “We have also been reluctant to overbuild because … it’s great while it lasts, but when they all pull out and there’s no end, you don’t know where the end date is going to be” (EFS010). Indeed, development actors knew that without permanent housing, workers were more likely to leave the region once drilling slowed. Respondents believed that if there was more permanent housing, then workers may bring their families and become permanent residents—even if there was a cutback in exploration: “They’re [oil and gas well maintenance workers] going be our long-term families! And again, it goes back to the housing” (EFS003).

WORKFORCE AND WAGES: “RAISE YOUR PAY TO KEEP YOUR HELP”

EFS respondents also discussed the disruption of the traditional wage profile of the region. In 2013 alone, the energy boom generated over $2.7 billion in wages for oil and gas employees working on the EFS, and $1.5 billion in wages for employees indirectly associated with oil and gas (Tunstall and others 2014). With labor in high demand during the height of the boom, many companies raised wages to levels beyond the regional norms, especially when contextualized relative to the high levels of regional poverty leading into the boom (Tunstall 2015). As the boom overtook the region, wages rose as labor demand increased: “Everybody had to raise their pay, because if not [employees] were working for the oilfield. You had to raise your pay to keep your help” (EFS022 June 2016). Several respondents noted that the minimum wage had naturally risen in their regions to over $12 per hour, with many firms still struggling to find enough workers: “The biggest impact probably is that obviously the businesses we have in town all have the same complaint: They can’t get enough employees” (EFS002). Normative wage disruption negatively affects local government agencies in particular, which depend on an adequate workforce but have budgetary constraints on salaries.

High demand for employment forced the oil and gas industry to seek labor from beyond the EFS. Often, work is organized through LDC arrangements. Respondents believed that energy companies hired from a variety of locations: “[Oil and gas firms] are hiring a lot of the locals—giving locals jobs—but as well is a lot of this oil-field companies are not just located in [the respondent’s municipality]. So they’re having the people that work in their main offices come down, train the people maybe from throughout here in the area, and then they’ll go back and they’ll have it under control here” (EFS005).
Even with workers coming from outside the region, many respondents suggested that labor was not an important driver of economic development. One respondent likened the oil and gas industry to a military base, where the workers live independently of the host municipality (EFS013). Another interviewee noted that in his city, “There’s 8,500 [people] before the Eagle Ford [boom] and they all had jobs” (EFS009). In the EFS, however, energy firms were eager to take advantage of the resource, even if that required introducing workers from beyond the region.

Adding to the complex nature of the labor on the EFS is high mobility. Three respondents said petroleum workers would “jump [leave a firm] for fifty cents [per hour pay increase].” Economic officials complained about their inability to retain labor: “we lost three or four deputies [in the county police force] to the oil industry because it paid so much better. And so, when you only have twelve deputies, that’s 25 percent of your law enforcement workforce” (EFS015). Many stakeholders lacked the financial means to attract labor away from the oil and gas industry, resulting in shortages of police, road workers, border guards, teachers, and coaches.

POST-BOOM: “I’D RATHER HAVE A PLOW HORSE THAN A RACEHORSE”

Despite a boom-bust cycle awareness, the intensity and speed of the bust surprised local officials: “There were very few people that expected it to… be as drastic as it did and go all the way down to the $20s,” said an economic-development official (EFS015 June 2016). Some stakeholders used the bust to diversify their economies: “We’ll keep reaching out to other industries, and when oil comes back, it will come back, and we’ll still be here. [The shale] ain’t going nowhere” (EFS015 June 2016). As one city manager stated, “We…sat down and decided to create a strategy whereby we diversified our local economy. Yeah, we have some oil-field businesses, and we have fewer oil-field businesses than we did a year ago and even fewer still than two years ago. But we put our emphasis into making improvements to our community” (EFS020 June 2016). To emphasize his point, this respondent noted that he would “much rather have a plow horse than a racehorse, because that old plow horse is just going to be steady,” equating petroleum-based industries to racehorses and his city’s traditional agricultural production to plow horses. An economic-development coordinator used decreased local oil production to convince policymakers to diversify the local economy away from the petroleum industry (EFS013, June 2016).

Roads were no longer critically damaged. One county judge noted that his government has “been able to catch up on some of our infrastructure needs” (EFS015 June 2016), although two county judges (EFS015 June 2016; EFS017 June 2016) were fearful that the 2017 state legislative session might further limit the amount counties could raise taxes to maintain roads (Ramsey 2016).

Regarding housing, “The [hotels and motels] that came in late, I bet they’re struggling,” said one chamber of commerce director (EFS022 June 2016). The
executive director of that chamber added, “A lot of the apartment complexes too are struggling” (EFS021 June 2016). A city manager echoed this sentiment: “We’ve had one hotel that closed its doors … one of the last hotels that came in. It didn’t do well” (EFS007 June 2016).

Wages had stabilized across the region by mid-2016. “We’re no longer competing with McDonald’s for employees. At one point, McDonald’s paid entry-level employees more than we did,” explained a city manager (EFS020 June 2016). A county judge commented that petroleum workers were “back in town, flipping burgers, back to the minimum-wage ranks,” (EFS019 June 2016) while waiting for the boom to return.

A “Problem Triangle” for Unconventional-Hydrocarbon Boomtowns
Our findings support an overhaul of the Gilmore problem triangle for unconventional-hydrocarbon boomtowns. The materiality of shale oil and gas is key to our revisions. Over 100 workers are needed for six weeks to drill an unconventional well, followed by a smaller workforce to monitor and maintain the well. This differs from Gilmore’s hard-rock—mostly coal and soda ash—mining, which requires a moderate workforce over a longer period. New wells must be constantly drilled to maintain production levels, because of the steep production-rate decline (Ambrose and others 2011); workers also frequently move between production regions and firms for higher wages. Each fracked well requires massive sand and water inputs, thus requiring thousands of trucks on county roads. Wells are drilled in diverse subregions across the EFS, which forces policymakers to disburse transportation funds to nearly every road that is proximate to drilling. This differs from more centralized hard-rock mining, as policymakers can restrict access to public roads if necessary. However, once the drilling of new wells slows, the region will witness a major exodus of workers and resources from the EFS. Thus, the materiality of unconventional hydrocarbons makes the shock of drilling-resource mobilization more dramatic.

In our model, optimized for unconventional-hydrocarbon extraction, we note an immediate strain on the local labor market as the boom begins to materialize (Figure 7). Locals involved in nonenergy industries transfer to oil and gas companies that offer higher wages, illustrated by the claim that “[oil and gas firms] are hiring a lot of the locals—giving locals jobs” (EFS005). This leads to declining nonenergy productivity. The high wages in the energy sector also raise regional labor costs, straining local, nonenergy industry and government, creating, for example, situations where local government officials have to compete for workers with fast food restaurants (EFS020 June 2016). With the local labor market engaged with the energy industry, there is a rise in LDC as demonstrated through the Cotulla, Texas, hotel industry. Simultaneously, because of Texas property tax law, there is a lag between the demand for public services and the deposits of tax revenues for public coffers. As services such as

Conclusion

In the EFS, economic stakeholders grapple with chaotic hydrocarbon production in relation to oil prices, degraded infrastructure, mismatches among desired and existing housing, and high wages that compromise local governance capacities. Resource extraction forces residents to contend with decreasing quality of life, which is only ameliorated through slowed resource development and hypothesized institutional change. Boomtown economic policymakers attempt to mitigate the negative externalities associated with explosive development through policy interventions, while simultaneously navigating diverse outcomes for their communities.

A revised Gilmore problem triangle can aid policymakers and social scientists in evaluating and responding to current and future booms. Namely, we maintain that the materiality of unconventional hydrocarbons requires an updated model that better conforms to the realities of modern drilling. This model considers how LDC, the ephemeral nature of booms, policymaker leadership, time, and how local and state governmental relationships affect Gilmore’s problem triangle.

The transformed conceptual framework is applicable to the EFS, but we encourage other scholars to use the model in shale-production regions elsewhere in the United States and globally. Evidence from other investigations demonstrates the model’s potential applicability to areas such as the Marcellus or the New Albany shales (see Anderson and Theodori 2009; Braiser and others 2011; Jacquet and Kay 2014). Furthermore, this investigation and its study site should be reexamined over time as resource prices fluctuate and policymakers manage irregular shale production.
Despite the production slowdown, the EFS is far from finished. In June 2016 oil prices recovered to over $50 per barrel—viewed by many stakeholders as the economic break-even point for exploration in south Texas. Moreover, the EFS maintains importance as firms can now export crude oil with the December 2015 lifting of the export ban. Framing boomtown processes within a revised conceptual framework allows policymakers to understand and predict how people and places will manage new demands associated with the materiality of shale and unconventional hydrocarbons.
