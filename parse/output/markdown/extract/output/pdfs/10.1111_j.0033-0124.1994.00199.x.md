Spatial Accessibility to Health Care Facilities in Suhum District, Ghana

Joseph R. Oppong
University of North Texas

M. John Hodgson
University of Alberta

To improve geographical accessibility to health facilities in rural Ghana, it has been recommended that additional health facilities be built. Resource constraints make this recommendation infeasible. Using location-allocation models, this paper demonstrates that in the Suhum District of Ghana substantial improvements in accessibility can be achieved with better locational choices and without additional facilities. Also, additional facilities will produce little improvement in accessibility if location is not explicitly considered. The paper demonstrates the utility of location-allocation models for improving accessibility to services in the Third World. Key Words: Ghana, geographical accessibility, location-allocation models, rural health, Third World.

Introduction

Developing countries face the problem of improving their health care delivery systems but lack the necessary resources to do this. Health facilities are often geographically inaccessible to the majority of the rural population (Freund 1986; Lasker 1981; McEvers 1980; Stock 1985). Women and children, in particular, experience difficulties in reaching a source of care (Phillips 1990), but the explosion of health care costs makes it increasingly difficult to improve geographical accessibility to services. Attempts to extend care are frequently hampered by financial and human resource shortages and other barriers (Phillips 1990).

Given this problem, better spatial organization of existing health care delivery systems is a logical strategy to follow. Rushton (1984, 1988) has argued strongly for the role location-allocation models might play in using limited resources more efficiently to provide access for those currently without access. In their work in Nigeria, Ayeni et al. (1987) show that accessibility to maternal and child health clinics could be improved substantially through careful location of new facilities. Yet, critics such as Phillips (1990) question the usefulness of location-allocation models in the Third World. Phillips (1990, 145), citing the problem of data availability and reliability, states that "optimally designed spatial health delivery systems are not practical in the Third World." In response to such criticism, Rietveld (1990) has correctly noted that critics tend to exaggerate the data requirements of location-allocation models.

This paper contributes to this debate by applying a simple location-allocation model using readily available local data to evaluate a recent public health policy recommendation for improving accessibility to health facilities in rural Ghana. The results indicate that simple applications of location-allocation models provide important information for general spatial decision making and data limitations do not preclude their application in the Third World. Furthermore, location-allocation models may be used to improve spatial accessibility without increasing the number of facilities and with minimum disruption to the existing system of facilities.

The paper is organized in the following manner. First, there is a brief description of the geographic inaccessibility problem in Ghana and the rationale for the latest policy recommendation. This is followed by a brief description of the study area and the modelling approach used. The analysis begins by evaluating, using the \( p \)-median criterion, the accessibility provided by the system of facilities in place. It demonstrates how accessibility might be improved, and evaluates the potential for improving accessibility by providing additional facilities. This is followed by an evaluation, using the maximum coverage criterion, of the ability of mid-level facilities to supervise low-level facilities. The paper considers the potential for
enhancing supervisory capability by adding more facilities and the costs that would be involved in relocating facilities. It concludes with a discussion of how location-allocation modeling might be included in primary health care planning in Ghana.

The Geography of Health Services in Ghana

Historically, the Ghana Ministry of Health has grappled with an "unacceptable disparity between service coverage and quality in urban areas compared with rural areas" (Adjei et al. 1984). In 1972, 149 of 298 government doctors worked in the Accra-Tema metropolitan area which contained about 8% of the country's population (Sharpston 1972). In 1978, an estimated 70% of the population were unable to use the official health system (Ministry of Health 1978) because of prohibitive distances to rural facilities.

The dilemma of the Ghana Ministry of Health has been how to provide an efficient and equitable system of service throughout the country with a highly constrained budget. To accomplish this, Ghana adopted a Primary Health Care (PHC) policy in 1978 with a stated goal to "maximize the total health of the Ghanaian people" (Ministry of Health 1978, 9). Details of Ghana's PHC are presented in Morrow (1983). In brief, the system is based on a three-level hierarchical structure (Ministry of Health 1978) of A-level, B-level, and C-level facilities. Each A-level facility is usually staffed with a minimally trained health worker. This level provides first level curative measures, pregnancy management, child health promotion, environmental protection, and mobilization for health-related community projects. B-level facilities are usually staffed with a community health nurse and a trained midwife. They provide technical supervision of A-level workers, routine immunizations, and care of patients including those referred from A-level facilities. C-level facilities are based on the district hospital, have at least one doctor, and provide mostly curative services including in-patient care and some preventive services. Within each district, a District Health Management Team (DHMT) is responsible for identifying locations to host PHC facilities and overall health care programming in the district.

Ghana's PHC policy has three aims (Ministry of Health 1978, 10): (1) to improve the accessibility (coverage) of health services by setting up a village-based system of health care; the aim will be for everyone to obtain essential basic services rather than for a few to receive sophisticated care; (2) to improve the quality of PHC at the point where it is most needed in the village and peri-urban areas . . . to meet priority health problems of all the people; and (3) to improve and strengthen the management capacity to support the PHC system at all levels; the aim will be to make more effective use of existing resources.

Between 1978 and 1988 it appears that Ghana's PHC policy had little effect on the spatial imbalance in accessibility to services. Only 20% of doctors served the rural population (80%) in 1982 (Ministry of Health 1982). In 1984 more than 605 of the 818 government doctors in Ghana were found in the Greater Accra and Ashanti regions (Fig. 1). Furthermore, 54% of the 262 private practitioners in the country were in the Greater Accra region. Two of 11 districts surveyed by the Ministry of Health had no physicians. The population per doctor ranged from 20,944 to 113,322 in districts with doctors (Adjei et al. 1984).

In 1986, Dr. Moses Adibo, Director of Health Services, observed that "of the 1031 physicians in Ghana, one-third were at Korle Bu Teaching Hospital, another one-third were in Kumasi and Sekondi, the remaining one-third in the other communities. An analysis of the distribution of some other health professional groups would reveal a similar pattern" (Adibo 1987, 154). Similarly, in 1987 only 186 (28%) of the 665 government physicians served the rural population. The two teaching hospitals accounted for 47% of the total number of doctors (Adjei et al. 1988).

Ten years after the adoption of the PHC program, spatial accessibility to health facilities was still a major problem, especially in the rural areas. A PHC Review Committee within the Ministry of Health recommended that many more facilities be set up with consideration given to "the geographical coverage and population of the country" (Adjei et al. 1988, 15). This paper refers to this approach as the "add-more" strategy. It was assumed that establishing additional facilities in rural areas would increase both accessibility and coverage. There are two major problems with this recommen-
dation. First, the resources available to the government are limited and preclude locating a health facility, or even a health worker, in every village. Even if this were possible, the additional cost of equipping, staffing, and supplying these additional facilities would still be a large obstacle. Second, additional facilities may not provide the greatest potential increase in coverage and accessibility if location is not explicitly considered. Furthermore, evidence from the locational analysis literature questions such an approach. For example, in their work in Nigeria, Ayeni et al. (1987, 1093) show that "the proliferation of locations of the health service delivery facilities of maternity and child welfare centers cannot eliminate the inefficiencies of location."

This study examines the "add-more" prescription to determine if it is based on a correct evaluation of the accessibility problem. The rationale is that if the diagnosis is wrong the prescription will also be wrong and is unlikely to fix the problem. It is essential to determine the extent to which the spatial inaccessibility problem is a result of an inadequate number of health facilities or of deficient locations. To accomplish this objective, the study will evaluate the "add-more" recommendation in the Suhum District of Ghana (Fig. 1) and explore other alternatives to improving accessibility. In effect, the study uses location-allocation models as tools for measuring spatial accessibility.

Study Area

The Suhum District is in the Eastern Region of Ghana (Fig. 1). It is one of 10 pioneering districts which the government of Ghana selected to implement the PHC policy in the country. Consequently, it is the focus of ongoing research into the planning and delivery of health services in Ghana. In fact, at the time of fieldwork for this research, the district was involved in a World Health Organization assisted project to delimit the catchment areas of health facilities and identify gaps in service delivery.

According to the latest available (1984) census, Suhum has a population of 146,100 and encompasses about 877.5 sq km (District Health Management Team 1987). The district is located on the edge of Ghana's forest belt. The district capital, Suhum, has a population of 19,298 and is 60 km from Accra, the national capital. Suhum has several central place functions, including education, administration, commerce, and health. Besides Suhum, only Nankese has a population exceeding 3,000 people (3,414), and just 14 communities exceed 1,000. The remainder of the population is located predominantly in small rural farming villages (Fig. 2). Because of this skewed population distribution, Suhum is the hub of most activities in the district.

The district has 30 government health facilities altogether: the C-level facility at Suhum, six B-level facilities, and 23 A-level facilities. The system is successively inclusive; higher-level facilities offer all the services provided at lower-level facilities in addition to services that are not provided at lower levels. Thus there are 30 facilities providing A-level services, seven of which provide B-level services, and Suhum hospital provides all three levels of service. Accessibility and the Goals of Ghana’s PHC Strategy

In location-allocation modelling, geographical accessibility may be conceptualized in two main ways—proximity and coverage. When defined in terms of proximity, accessibility has a distance minimization connotation and may be measured using the average travel distance. As a locational goal, proximity translates into the simple \( p \)-median problem which selects facility locations to minimize average travel distance for users. Previous research has found distance to be a major determinant of facility use (Stock 1982). Accessibility may also be expressed using a coverage distance. If a facility is within the specified coverage distance from a person, it is considered accessible to the person and the person is said to be covered. Those people beyond this distance are not covered. The measure of accessibility in this context is the proportion of people covered. As a locational goal, this approach translates into the maximal covering location problem (MCLP).

The first goal of the Ghana government, improved accessibility, may thus be seen as reducing the distance between health facilities and users. The goal can also be viewed in coverage terms, i.e., improving the proportion of
people who are within a specified distance of a health care facility. The second goal, improving the quality of PHC, is not as easy to interpret or deal with because location-allocation models do not explicitly address quality issues. Therefore, this goal is not directly addressed in this study. It could be argued, as has the Ghana government, that by improving other aspects of the system, supervision for example, the quality of service would be improved.

The third goal emphasizes management and interfacility relationships including referral. There are many dimensions to management, but the government has isolated supervision of low-level facilities for special attention. The government's aim is to ensure that all facilities work effectively, and it has stressed that supervision of rural health workers is absolutely critical (Adjei et al. 1988). To facilitate this, there must be a B-level facility within eight km of every A-level facility. Location-allocation models can address this problem effectively.

From the government's goals, therefore, two central locational problems emerge: accessibility of facility locations to users (involving distance minimization and coverage) and accessibility for suppliers (coverage considerations for supervision, supplies, and referral). This paper uses the \( p \)-median and maximum coverage models to measure the level of accessibility provided by the existing system of facilities and to determine if this level of accessibility can be provided without considerable additional resources. Using these results, the paper evaluates the "add more" recommendation and shows how location-allocation models could help to improve accessibility.

Data Requirements and Modelling Approach

Essential data for this study included population and locational information for each community. Population data were obtained from the 1984 population census. Because Ghana does not have a digital spatial database, locations were digitized as x and y coordinates from the Ghana 1:50,000 map series obtained from the Ghana Geologic Survey in Accra. There were no major problems with the maps, but the population data of some smaller communities had been aggregated with larger centers close by. Another minor problem was communities with different locations but similar names. These were carefully organized to avoid assigning the wrong population figure to any location. A simple distance generating algorithm was used to generate Euclidean distances between locations.

The analysis involves 150 population centers in the Suhum district as demand points and the population at each center as the proxy for demand. Euclidean distances are used because walking is the main mode of travel in Suhum district. This avoids the problem of seasonal inaccessibility along different types of roads. The Tietz and Bart (1968) heuristic algorithm was used. The modelling approach treats the three levels of service independently and locates an optimal system of 30 facilities in keeping with the successively inclusive nature of the system. Seven of these will provide B-level services, and one of these seven will provide C-level services.

p-Median Accessibility to Higher-Level Services

Next, the provision of higher-level services beginning with B-level is considered. Because it is successively inclusive, seven of the 30 facilities in the actual system provide B-level services. In order to conform to this hierarchical structure, the optimal model must select seven facilities from the 30 providing A-level services to maximize proximity to users. The results are presented in Table 2. Hodgson (1984) demonstrated that such a bottom-up approach can result in suboptimal higher-order facilities. This effect is evaluated by selecting seven facilities freely from the open system of 150 demand points. This system, however, differs from the bottom-up solution by less than 1% (Table 2). The optimal p=7 system would provide an AWD of 3.28 compared to 3.75 in the actual system. The actual B-level system is thus not as accessible as it could be, since it involves 15% more travel distance than the p-median system. In fact, six optimally located facilities would provide an AWD of 3.6 km and involve less travel than the actual system (Table 1).

Finally, the location of C-level services is considered. Only one facility, Suhum, provides C-level services. To conform with this structure, one facility to provide C-level services will be selected from the seven providing B-level services. The goal is to verify whether it is possible to improve accessibility to C-level services. In this analysis, the bottom-up approach identified the same location, Suhum, as the best location for C-level services. When one facility is selected freely from the 150 demand points, however, only a slight improvement in efficiency (less than 1%) could be achieved.

To model the recommendation that additional facilities be established to improve accessibility requires the retention of all 30 actual facilities and establishing additional facilities. The result of this approach is presented in Table 3. It shows that, even if they were optimally located, five additional facilities would be required to provide the same level of proximity that 30 optimally located facilities would provide (AWD=1.09). If additional facilities were not located optimally, many more might be required.

Supervision Coverage

Coverage models are based on the simple notion that there is a distance (travel time or cost) within which patrons are served (covered) and beyond which they are not (Church and Re-Velle 1974). The amount of coverage provided by a system of facilities depends critically on the coverage distance. A small coverage distance requires many facilities whereas a larger distance requires few. The Ghana government has not explicitly stipulated a coverage criterion for A-level facilities, but desires a B-level facility within eight km of every A-level facility. This would ensure that B-level workers supervise and hold clinics in A-level facilities much more easily. In turn, A-level workers could visit B-level facilities for consultation when necessary with little effort.

The maximum coverage model is used to deal with the problem of covering the 30 existing facilities. The objective is to determine what proportion of existing A-level facilities are within eight km of a B-level facility. Also, if facilities were located optimally, how many facilities would provide complete coverage for existing A-level facilities? This analysis involves choosing only from the 30 existing facility locations. Coverage distances ranging between four and 10 km will be considered.

The existing B-level facility system (p=7) provides 95.4% coverage within eight km for A-level facilities. Yet four optimally located facilities would provide 98.6% coverage, and five would provide complete coverage within eight km (Table 4). The latter solution would entail the relocation of four B-level facilities, possibly a politically contentious move. Furthermore, the actual B-level system provides complete coverage at nine km, but this could be provided with only four optimally located facilities. If the relocation of B-level facilities was not possible, the alternative would be the provision of additional B-level facilities. The analysis indicates that two additional facilities would raise coverage to 100%. Thus, building upon the present system would require that nine facilities be provided whereas complete coverage could be provided with only five facilities if four were relocated.

Frequently, decision makers require help in formulating their location goals. When they see the locational outcome of specified decision criteria, they may want to revise the original location goals. For example, the government has specified a coverage distance of eight km for B-level facilities without knowing the exact number of facilities that would be necessary to achieve that goal. It is likely that if they knew the costs involved in achieving this goal, they might want to choose a different goal. This situation is where modelling is most useful and supports Rushton's (1989) contention that location-allocation models can be used as spatial decision support systems.
Table 4 illustrates the trade-offs of different coverage distances with the number of facilities required to achieve total coverage of the existing system of 30 facilities. To provide 100% coverage within eight km would require five facilities. Only four facilities would be necessary to provide total coverage within a nine km coverage distance. To provide total coverage with a four km coverage distance, 17 facilities would be required. If resources were restricted to only 10 facilities and the coverage distance was four km, only 87.8% of total demand could be covered. Through this type of analysis, location-allocation models can be useful for identifying decision alternatives, trading off resource inputs against alternative locational outcomes, and even in defining or refining the locational objectives.

Conclusions

A systematic evaluation of proximity to health facilities in Suhum district using the \( p \)-median and coverage models as the bench mark consistently shows that accessibility could be improved without additional facilities. Providing additional facilities is not the only way to improve accessibility to services in Suhum district. Depending on the relative costs of relocation, greater accessibility can be provided with the same amount of resources currently employed. The relocation costs of A-level facilities would probably be minimal, while the gains in accessibility could be substantial.

Regardless of what action the Ghana government takes, these results confirm that data limitations do not preclude the use of simple location-allocation analysis to support spatial decision making in the Third World. Rather, performing simple analysis using readily available data can provide invaluable inputs for making difficult spatial decisions.

The results also show the importance of location-allocation models as objective tools for measuring spatial accessibility. Without satisfactory measurement tools, it is difficult to evaluate policy options effectively. Moreover, there is no way of telling when adequate accessibility has been established. A large number of facilities does not necessarily imply greater accessibility. Rather, as Rushton (1984) has argued, the selection of optimal locations might be an important, but neglected, key in the effort to improve accessibility to health facilities in the Third World. Spatial efficiency at the district level could be the key to an efficient national system of facilities.

One of the difficulties of improving accessibility to health facilities in the Third World is that "health care delivery systems have often grown up in a fragmented manner, provided by public, private, charitable and aid sources and they have frequently been non-complementary or even competitive in their functions and locations" (Phillips 1990, 145). This problem is at the root of the redundancy and present day maldistribution of facilities, especially where government facilities have not sought to maintain a spatial balance. The decentralization of decision making being promoted under Ghana's PHC program provides an excellent opportunity to address and solve this problem.

Under Ghana's PHC program, the District Health Management Team (DHMT) is responsible for all health planning and programming within the district. A team member, trained in the use of simple optimization models, could work with the DHMT to identify an "optimal" and desirable set of facility locations for the district. This could be the basis of a health facility location plan for the district, and the selected locations could be prioritized for development. District resources for additional facilities could be allocated based on these priorities. In addition, any organization seeking to develop a health facility within the district could be encouraged to select one of the priority locations. Improved accessibility and efficiency at the district level will surely be the basis of a more accessible and efficient national health care system.
