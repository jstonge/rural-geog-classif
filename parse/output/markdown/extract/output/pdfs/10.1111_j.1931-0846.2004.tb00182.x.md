IMPACTS OF TRANSPORTATION CHANGES ON THE WOODWORKING INDUSTRY OF MEXICO’S PURÉPECHA REGION

PAUL MARR and CHRISTOPHER SUTTON

ABSTRACT. The Purépecha region of Michoacán State, in west-central Mexico, has a strong tradition of craft production, especially wooden items. Transportation improvements in the region since the mid–twentieth century have led to a higher level of integration of the regional economy, yet access to the broader Mexican market remains limited. Towns throughout the region have experienced substantial changes in the types of crafts produced, the extent of their local and external markets, and the location of craft production. Transportation improvements have, in part, led to agglomeration economies and a resultant contraction in the number of different types and an increase in the volume of the crafts produced. Market changes have led to the proliferation of craft storefronts in towns with higher levels of accessibility. Conversely, remote towns still rely heavily on distributors and have not been able to expand their markets. As a result, the region has seen the development of a two-tiered system whereby towns with increased accessibility have viable craft industries and towns with limited accessibility struggle with stagnant craft industries. Keywords: Michoacán, Mexico, Purépecha region, road network, transportation, wooden crafts.

During the 1940s and 1950s a series of cultural geographical studies examined village life in the Purépecha region of northwestern Michoacán, a part of Mexico that was geographically remote and both culturally and economically insular (Brand 1943; Beals 1946; Foster 1948; West 1948; Stanislavski 1950). Movement in the region was difficult, hampered by the rugged topography and lack of infrastructure. In the late 1940s, as those studies were being conducted, the situation began to change—first with the completion of the highway connecting Guadalajara and Mexico City and then, shortly thereafter, by the construction of an extension highway running through the Purépecha region to the Pacific coast. By the 1970s most of the larger settlements had been connected by two-lane blacktop roads, and the more remote settlements were linked by improved dirt roads. Bus service was established in the region between the larger towns (for example, Uruapan and Zamora) and the major urban centers of Guadalajara, Morelia, and Mexico City and was eventually extended to the smaller settlements. These transportation improvements have led to a higher level of integration of the regional economy with the broader Mexican economy, but integration has come at a price.

The Purépecha region has a strong tradition of craft production, and much of what was locally produced was locally consumed. As connectivity to the rest of Michoacán increased, less expensive and/or higher-quality products manufactured outside the region were substituted for locally produced crafts. In response to this influx of external goods, towns throughout the region experienced substantial
changes in the types of crafts produced, the size of market-area, and the location of resident craft production. Changes in the woodworking industry in particular in this region reflect the transportation improvements and the larger issues of increasing accessibility of underdeveloped rural areas. Researchers have noted that infrastructural improvements in rural areas decrease transportation costs, thereby allowing rural populations greater access to urban markets (Boserup 1981; Njenga and Davis 2003), and vice versa (Kilkenny 1998a). Within this scenario, places such as the Purépecha region experience significant economic changes as they become better connected, both within the region and externally. Trade linkages are often realigned, and differentials in accessibility can be magnified, resulting in uneven development and economic opportunities. Thus, towns in the region must contend with shifts in the economic landscape that are well beyond their control and limited resources.

This study focuses on seven towns located in the Meseta Purépecha of west-central Michoacán (Figure 1). The communities range in population from fewer than 3,000 to more than 15,000 (Table I) (INEGI 2002). The seven towns were chosen because they are representative of the various levels of accessibility within the region and have some form of woodworking as their single, dominant craft industry. Field research was conducted in 2001 and 2004 to examine how current economic specialization (craft production) has changed relative to improvements in the transportation infrastructure. Fifty-four craft producers in the study towns (approximately eight per town) were interviewed. The interviews provided information about production methods, sources of raw materials, and markets for the products. In addition, every street in each of the seven study towns was surveyed during peak business hours (10:00 A.M. to 2:00 P.M.), both in 2001 and in 2004, to determine craft sale locations.

REGIONAL BACKGROUND

The Meseta Purépecha is in the volcanic uplands west of Lake Pátzcuaro (Figure 2). Dotted with hundreds of small cinder cones, the region lies between mountains to the north and the Balsas escarpment to the south. Numerous alluvium- and ash-filled basins have long supported agriculture, but recent abandonment of agricultural activities has led to increased shrub and forest stands (Works and Hadley 2000). The porous volcanic rock results in a lack of perennial streams, but springs are present on the lower slopes of the volcanoes. Near those springs the native Purépechan people built their settlements.1 Prior to the Spanish conquest of Michoacán, the Purépecha region comprised many more settlements than it does now, especially the smaller estancias or rancherías (small agricultural settlements) that were scattered throughout the meseta (West 1948; Warren 1985). The Meseta Purépecha is one of the poorest regions in Michoacán, which itself is one of Mexico’s poorest states. As such, the region has played a minor role in Mexico’s larger economic development trends (Belshaw 1967; Foster 1967; Acheson 1972a, 1972b, 1982; Kemper 1981).

Craft specialization was particularly strong in this region of Mexico, and trade among neighboring villages and estancias was frequent. Production of wooden crafts was supported by forests in the upper slopes of the region, which experience cooler temperatures and increased rainfall. The historical trade routes used by the Purépechan people generally followed the most direct line of travel between villages and were well suited to the use of draft animals carrying modest loads. With the onset of Spanish control, new settlements were plotted based on the “Laws of the Indies” pattern—grids aligned to the cardinal directions with streets intersecting at 90° angles. A central plaza surrounded by important religious, educational, and administrative buildings was the focus of the city (Stanislavski 1947). Toward the end of the sixteenth century, Spanish edicts required that many of the hillside villages be moved into the valleys, because the lower locations facilitated efficient instruction of the indigenous population in Christianity (Ricard 1966), collection of tributes, and increased agricultural production. The few valley settlements that predated the Spanish conquest had the grid street pattern superimposed on a previously existing organic pattern (Stanislavski 1950).

Although Spanish town plans established a principal thoroughfare and a commercial focal point, shops in these locations were owned primarily by mestizos. This situation probably had more to do with the manner in which the two groups approached business (Acheson 1972a) than with an attempt by the mestizo shop owners to monopolize prime business locations. Craft production was the primary source of income for mestizos, so they had an incentive to locate their businesses where traffic would be greatest.

In Purépechan towns craft production was scattered, because the native population viewed this activity as secondary to agriculture, so it took place almost exclusively in the home and at more peripheral locations (Stanislavski 1950). As a result, towns with large mestizo populations tended to have a greater degree of development along their principal thoroughfares. Purépechan towns that had the grid pattern superimposed by the Spanish rarely displayed any significant development along their planned principal thoroughfares, and over time these roads lost their significance.

The study towns and villages fall into two categories based on the current significance of the principal thoroughfare. The first consists of those towns in which the principal thoroughfare continues to act as the focus of commercial activity. Three of the seven study sites fall into this category: Pichátaro, Capácuaro, and Paracho. As a general rule these towns are located on routes that have higher levels of through
traffic.2 The second group comprises those towns in which the principal thoroughfare has little or no commercial activity. Turúcuaro, Comachuén, Arantepacua, and Quinceo fall into this category. These towns are much more remote and have little in the way of through traffic. The towns in these two categories have responded very differently to regional transportation improvements.

PICHÁTARO, CAPÁCUARO, AND PARACHO

Pichátaro is situated in the mountains 10 kilometers west of Lake Pátzcuaro. Even though the town’s remote mountain location kept its lands from being appropriated into haciendas, it was never isolated and once formed a vital link between the lake region and the Meseta Purépecha (Stanislawski 1950). Well-used trails connected Pichátaro with Comachuén, nestled high on a mountain slope 10 kilometers to the west, and with the lakeside settlement of Erongaricuaro about 9 kilometers to the east (Figure 1). Pichátaro predates the arrival of the Spanish and has always been more Indian than mestizo in character. Even so, its original street pattern was realigned to a grid. Surrounded by ample farmland, the town historically produced enough agricultural surplus to support fairly diverse craft production. In the late 1940s and early 1950s Pichátaro produced several items in quantity, including cabinetry, palm-leaf hats, woven-reed sleeping mats, wooden bowls, broom handles, shawls, belts, and blankets (Table I). Craft production was not concentrated, nor did it tend to locate along the main street or near the plaza (West 1948; Stanislawski 1950). The paved road through Pichátaro was not completed until 1950, and, although the trail from Erongaricuaro to Nahuatzen was considered a “through route,” its importance to the craft workers was minimal.3

Today Pichátaro is bisected by a well-maintained, two-lane paved road that connects Pátzcuaro to Cherán. Completion of the paved road has realigned the movement of goods through this part of the region and has severed the historical link connecting the mountain villages to Lake Pátzcuaro. Pichátaro no longer has a direct connection to either Comachuén or Erongaricuaro. Sevina, Nahuatzen, Cherán, and Pátzcuaro, all of which are located on the paved route running through Pichátaro, have seen increases in population over the last several decades (INEGI 1994, 2002; Marr and Sutton 2004). The combination of population growth and availability of automobile and bus transportation has increased the flow of people through Pichátaro.

Currently, Pichátaro’s single most important industry is furniture production. The furniture tends to be of ornately carved pine, with dining tables, chairs, mirror frames, and headboards being the most common items (Figure 3). A significant amount of mechanization is involved in the contemporary production of furniture: Saws, planers, and jointers, as well as a variety of power hand tools, are common, but the detailed final carving work is typically done by hand. Although the hand carving is time-consuming, the level of mechanization has resulted in a substantial amount of furniture being produced.

In response to the increase in traffic along Pichátaro’s principal road, furniture makers have set up storefronts to display their finished pieces to passing motorists,
although many small shops without storefronts are scattered throughout the town (Figure 4). In 2004, sixty furniture storefronts were located along the main road. In spite of the number of storefronts, little distinguishes the furniture of one maker from that of another. Throughout the town the furniture is remarkable in its similarity, in terms of both style and price. Even though furniture is sold locally to passing motorists, a significant amount is sold at regional markets, particularly in Pátzcuaro.

A similar situation exists in the town of Capácuaro, located about 15 kilometers north of Uruapan along mx-37, a four-lane, north–south highway. Capácuaro lies in one of the larger valleys in the Meseta Purépecha, and the route through this low point in the mountains historically connected the highlands with the tierra caliente (the hot, tropical zone typically found along coastal plains) to the south. Unlike Pichátaro, the completion of mx-37 during the 1940s reinforced and strengthened the previously existing trade linkages among the towns along this route.

The historical distribution of craft production in Capácuaro is uncertain. No studies were conducted detailing the mid-twentieth-century distribution of Capácuaro’s craft industries, but some inferences can be made based on Dan Stanislawski’s research (1947, 1950). In the Purépechan towns he studied, craft production was in peripheral locations, away from the plaza and main arterials, particularly in towns with few or no mestizos. Craft production in Capácuaro, a predominantly indigenous town,4 is likely to have been similarly diffuse.

The ephemeral nature of craft production in Purépechan settlements has been noted by others (for example, Beals 1946; West 1948; Dinerman 1972), and Capácuaro is no exception. During the mid-1940s Capácuaro produced cotton belts and wooden broom handles (West 1948), but by the 1960s folding chairs, headboards, and free-standing cupboards were being produced for sale outside the village (Rees 1971). The volume of furniture production noted by John Rees was quite high, with an average weekly production of forty-eight chairs per woodworker, whereas in the village of San Lorenzo, located just a few kilometers west of Capácuaro and away from the main road, furniture production was nearly absent.

Capácuaro’s current craft industry is still dominated by furniture making, although folding chairs have given way to dining chairs. The furniture makers of Capácuaro have responded to the increase in accessibility in the same manner as those of Pichátaro, by concentrating sales along the principal thoroughfare (Figure 5). In 2004, furniture was produced throughout the town, but storefront sales were limited to the principal road. As with Pichátaro, the furniture makers in Capácuaro display their goods outside the storefronts, plainly visible from the road. Along the entire length of the main road shops are occupied by craftsmen, which makes an immediate impression on anyone passing through town.

One is struck by how similar the types of furniture made in Capácuaro and Pichátaro are. Both towns produce pine dining tables, chairs, cupboards, and headboards, but with significant differences. In comparing Pichátaro and Capácuaro, it is important to note the differences in furniture quality relative to the accessibility of the two towns. Although Pichátaro is served by a well-maintained, paved road, it has only moderate through traffic, because much of the traffic from Pátzcuaro to Uruapan was diverted following the completion of \( \text{mx-14} \), a four-lane toll highway connecting Morelia and Uruapan that passes just south of Pátzcuaro. Pichátaro, though considered moderately accessible, may become increasingly isolated from tourist traffic as people passing through the region use the \( \text{mx-14} \) toll road and \( \text{mx-37} \). The craftsmen of Pichátaro have been able to attract sales to the town by offering higher-quality, more ornate furniture, and several interviewees stated that Pichátaro’s style of furniture was unique in the region.\(^5\) Capácuaro, on the other hand, has been experiencing an increase in traffic as automobiles become more widely available and more people use \( \text{mx-37} \). These craftsmen produce simple, less expensive furniture, but because they are located on a route used by regional traffic they can rely on higher-volume sales. The impact of the highways running through these towns has resulted in increased in situ sales proportional to the accessibility of the town. Field observations suggest that the amount of traffic through Capácuaro is sufficient to maintain local furniture sales, although some of the production is transported to regional markets. The volume of ornate and expensive furniture produced in Pichátaro more than satisfies local demand, but the volume of traffic through the town is insufficient to rely solely on in situ sales. Craftsmen must sell their furniture at regional markets.

Paracho, like Capácuaro, is in the large valley that connects the highlands with the tierra caliente. Located approximately 30 kilometers north of Uruapan on \( \text{mx-37} \), Paracho is land poor. Situated near the northern flank of the Cerro Paracho and surrounded by cinder cones and lava-flow badlands, the town has always had to rely more heavily on its craft industries than have other meseta towns (Kaplan 1965). But it differs significantly from the other woodworking towns in the region in that its single most important industry—musical instruments, especially guitars—is well known both within Mexico and abroad. The economy of the town is overwhelmingly geared toward the production of luxury items, such as musical instruments and chess and other game pieces, and, of all of the towns in this study, Paracho is by far the most involved in extraregional export at the individual producer level.

Transportation improvements in Paracho have been somewhat different from those in either Pichátaro or Capácuaro, but the response to increased accessibility has been remarkably similar. The highway skirts the western side of Paracho, and the building setback from the road is relatively large. Automobile-service businesses proliferate here, along with a few inexpensive motels and restaurants. The old principal thoroughfare, Calle Independencia, branches off from the modern highway north of town, reconnecting with the highway to the south. The commercial district
along this business route does not extend to the highway, and nothing visually entices people to turn off from mx-37. Yet Calle Independencia is the focal point of Paracho’s guitar-making industry, with more than fifty storefronts devoted to production and sales (Figures 7 and 8). Even though the level of through traffic is less than that in either Pichátaro or Capácuaro, it is still a busy street. Paracho is also a much larger town than is either Pichátaro or Capácuaro, and Calle Independencia is well known to Mexican musicians and many foreign tourists. The unusual nature of Paracho’s craft production, as well as its reputation, acts to draw both tourists and passing motorists to the town.

Unlike either Capácuaro or Pichátaro, instrument making in Paracho has been extremely stable. Although the town has seen a contraction in the number of different types of crafts, musical instruments have been made here since the late 1700s (West 1948) and quite possibly longer (García López 1998). Turned items such as chess pieces and wooden cups can still be found in the local shops, but musical instrument production dominates the town, and people from throughout Mexico come to purchase instruments. Some instrument makers have been successful enough to open shops in larger urban areas, specifically Uruapan, Morelia, and Mexico City. Several guitar makers have gained international recognition for their instruments, and many individual shops export their instruments to resellers in the United States, Europe, and Japan. The labor pool of instrument makers is fairly stable, and several small guitar factories have opened in Paracho.

Turícuaro, Comachuén, Arantepacua, and Quinceo

In contrast to Pichátaro, Capácuaro, and Paracho, the mountain villages of Turícuaro, Comachuén, Arantepacua, and Quinceo are both remote and isolated. They have little in the way of services, aside from a few small, poorly stocked stores. Of the four villages, Turícuaro is the most isolated, still accessible only by dirt roads in 2001.6 Comachuén, Arantepacua, and Quinceo are all accessible via good two-lane paved roads, but these are very lightly traveled. During the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries Comachuén, Arantepacua, and Quinceo produced wooden objects, principally saddle frames and boat oars, and Turícuaro produced stone grinders.

By the mid-1940s three of the four towns had abandoned their older crafts (West 1948). Comachuén was producing wooden bowls and woolen blankets; Arantepacua produced brooms and palm-leaf rain capes; and Quinceo produced woolen blankets, cotton belts, and rain capes (Table I). Saddle-frame making, which was so common among the mountain villages, declined as the use of horses and burros for transport declined. The loss of boat-oar making in Comachuén and Arantepacua was the result of three factors: the decline of the fishing industry on Lake Pátzcuaro because of overfishing and fluctuating lake levels; the adoption of boat motors by fishermen and general lake transportation; and the realignment of the trading networks brought on by the construction of paved roads, which reoriented the economies of the two villages away from the lake. Today craft production in all four towns has shifted to simply constructed chairs made from locally harvested pine. The style and manner of construction of these chairs is similar to those being produced in Capácuaro (Figure 9). Yet, unlike Capácuaro’s diverse production, chairs are the only furniture items being produced in any quantity in the four mountain villages. All four towns are well away from the major arterials, and through traffic is infrequent. Given that no economic force attracts chair production to any particular place, the distribution of chair manufacturing in all four towns is diffuse, with no apparent locational preference. Rather than producing and displaying chairs along the main thoroughfare, most production is relatively small in scale and occurs at home.

A fair amount of mechanization is used in the construction of the chairs. Rough lumber is cut into smaller planks that are then stacked and air-dried, typically on roofs of houses or sheds to take advantage of the sun and wind. Table saws or band saws are employed to cut the planks to the correct dimensions, and chair legs are turned using electric lathes. Each piece of the chair is standardized, and craftsmen typically have dozens of pieces ready for assembly. Given the level of mechanization, chair production is well beyond the amount necessary to satisfy local demand. On average a craftsman produces approximately 100 chairs per month or up to four chairs per day, a level actually below what could be produced.
The rate of production of chairs in these towns is limited by a variety of factors. Because the chairs are relatively inexpensive, the economic return precludes purchase of large volumes of raw material; that is, pine. The combined annual production by the four villages of thousands of chairs further depresses economic return, so individual producers tend not to produce beyond what can be sold immediately. Producers must also incorporate the cost of transporting the finished product to the market. A producer can generally afford to export only one truckload of chairs at a time, because most exporting involves a loosely structured network of independent haulers throughout the region (Harner 2002). Time spent by craft producers traveling to regional markets is time not spent on either producing crafts or tending the fields. Given the small profit margins of most crafts, in this type of production environment the independent haulers and distributors have the upper hand (Carruthers 2001). Haulers and distributors take full advantage of this situation, charging higher rates for transporting and distributing the goods. The furniture producers in these town find themselves in a situation in which the local market for their product is limited, and access to the larger national and international export market is beyond their means or control.

TRANSPORTATION CHANGES AND THE TRANSFORMATION OF FURNITURE PRODUCTION

Craft production in Purépechan villages during the mid–twentieth century was typified by a wide variety of crafts (Beals 1946; Kaplan 1953; Belshaw 1967; Dinerman 1972; Acheson 1982), and most of the goods produced were common, utilitarian items with little adornment. Cotton and woolen goods, especially blankets, belts, and shawls, were produced in most towns (West 1948). A few towns produced metal, leather, or clay goods. Wooden products such as furniture, spoons, bowls, saddle frames, and boat oars were commonly produced in most of the mountain settlements and in a number of valley settlements. The technology of production was simple and labor intensive, so the volume of production was relatively low. The number of different types of goods produced was roughly associated with the size of the town, with larger towns generally producing a wider variety of crafts. Because production was, in most cases, a supplement to agricultural production, crafts were typically produced based on the immediate needs of the family (Dinerman 1972).

Craft trade at this time was still dependent on the centuries-old network of trade routes (Figure 1) that connected mountain settlements with those of the lake region and the tierra fría (the cooler, high-altitude zone) with the tierra caliente (Stanislawski 1950). The mountain–lake, or west–east, trade routes were important internal connections, whereas the tierra fría–tierra caliente, or north–south, routes formed important external connections. Although some locally produced goods were sold over an area extending far beyond the Meseta Purépecha, the majority of the simple, everyday items were sold within the region.

By the end of the twentieth century a network of paved roads had been constructed throughout the meseta, and craft production went through a significant
contraction in scope (Table I). Each of the seven study area towns experienced a decline in the total number of crafts produced, with all but one of the towns producing only one item. Paracho made two types of products, musical instruments and turned objects. This reduction in the diversity of craft production was the result of several forces. The construction of highways resulted in greater interaction between the meseta and the larger population centers elsewhere in Mexico. The increased connectivity allowed for the introduction of less expensive items produced outside the region, which were able to compete and ultimately displace locally produced crafts. Throughout the meseta, less competitive crafts were abandoned as less expensive, more durable goods were made available (for example, ceramics and plastics replaced indigenous pottery and wooden bowls, and mattresses and bedding replaced woven sleeping mats). Ultimately, towns exploited their comparative advantage and abandoned less competitive, utilitarian crafts in favor of higher-order items like furniture. Conversely, the increased connectivity allowed local craft producers some measure of access to a much larger market area.

The construction of highways created greater connectivity among the villages not only with regard to personal travel but also for commercial hauling. In short order, trucks replaced burros as the mode of transportation by which raw materials and finished goods were exchanged throughout the Meseta Purépecha. Trucks served three significant roles in the context of craft production. They allowed greater mechanization among woodworkers as the large, heavy tools needed for furniture making (lathes, jointers, planers, and the like) could be brought into the region. In turn, increased mechanization enabled increased production. Trucks also facilitated export to market centers by carrying larger objects and greater quantities of goods and by moving products more quickly. Trips that used to take three days by burro could now be completed in an hour or two. Finally, trucks enabled craftsmen to import raw materials from greater distances as deforestation and degradation reduced the availability of local wood, particularly the larger pine trees in the areas west of Lake Pátzcuaro (Foster 1979; Klooster 2000; Works and Hadley 2004).

In some communities the construction of the highways resulted in another important change—the shift of shop locations to cater to through travelers. Though not the case in all of the meseta villages, highway traffic has resulted in the clustering of woodworking shops along the main road, with wares placed outside to be visible to people passing through the village. Shops display not only the expected sampling of small items that can easily be purchased by casual consumers but also large pieces (armoires and tables, for example). Woodworkers achieve several goals with this practice. Along the major thoroughfares, shop after shop has furniture displayed at the curbside, creating a powerful visual enticement. The large items serve as advertising to passing motorists who may not notice smaller items like wooden bowls and toys. As these towns have become known for their furniture production, woodworkers can capture return customers who may not have had a vehicle large enough to transport the piece when originally passing through the town. Finally, and perhaps most important, because through travelers haul pur-
chased pieces away themselves, the woodworkers are able to maximize profit by avoiding transportation costs.

CRAFT STABILITY AND CHANGE

Woodworking in the region has been geographically stable over time. Towns that were reported to have some level of woodworking consistently occurred in a band stretching from west to east along the old mountain–lake trade corridor (West 1948; Stanislawski 1947, 1950). This region of the Meseta Purépecha was thickly forested at higher elevations, and villagers relied heavily on this local resource. Historically, the mountain towns engaged in woodworking for a much more diverse pool of products than they do today. Within the last half-century the range of items produced has diminished markedly, although the volume of production has increased significantly.

The decrease in the range of items produced may be explained in part by substitution or reduced demand, reflecting the conversion from a local, folk economy to a national, capitalist economy. Although saddle frames are still used, locally produced wooden frames have been replaced with leather saddles made outside the region. Metal and plastic spoons have replaced the traditional wooden spoons. As newer, inexpensive items replaced locally produced items, more families began to exploit the local comparative advantage and adopted the remaining viable crafts. The increased demand for rustic, traditionally made furniture in the last few decades has been opportune. Furniture production has long been an important component of regional craft production, and rustic furniture can be made with a modest capital investment in tools. Furniture making is more time intensive than producing simple, utilitarian items, but furniture commands higher prices at the market.

It is also important to note that much of what has been lost in terms of types of crafts produced has been those items that do not use wood as a raw material (Table I). Wood has remained the primary raw material for craft production in the study towns, and woodworking has emerged as the principal craft industry. Although many of the modern woodworking techniques and stylistic details are the result of recent programs that target rural artisans (for example, the Mexican government’s National Foundation for the Development of Folk Art [FONART] and the UNESCO’s Regional Center for Adult Education and Functional Literacy in Latin America [CREPAL], woodworking in the region exhibits a high level of historical tradition as skills are passed from one generation to the next. This helps to explain the persistence of woodworking in spite of diminishing local wood sources and the added expense of importing wood from other regions and thus to partially explain the contraction in scope and expansion in scale of woodworking.

The decrease in the friction of distance through improvements to the road network and the greater availability of automobiles has allowed producers to tap into new regional markets, but access to the broader Mexican market remains more limited. Although items are sold to passing motorists, all of the craft producers interviewed stated that they sold their products to distributor agents and at regional markets (see also Harner 2002), most commonly those in Uruapan and Pátzcuaro.
Ina Dinerman (1972) suggested that craft communities operate in an "open" rather than a "free" market; that is, the means of production are available to all potential participants. Production occurs solely within the family unit, and other families are viewed as potential economic rivals. Innovations in production are rare, and they are adopted only if they are shown—typically through the success of others—to be viable options. In the Meseta Purépecha, the dramatic contraction in the range of items produced and the increase in the volume of single-craft production in the study villages cannot be explained by innovation diffusion and economic rivalry among families alone. Agglomeration economies and the region's comparative advantage are a more likely explanation; for families to continue to invest both time and resources in producing a craft, a reasonable expectation of economic returns must exist. When families were geographically restricted to only a few local market options the potential for sales would similarly be restricted, with the most likely result being persistent conservatism (Foster 1948). Under this scenario craft producers would view change as risky. Production levels would be low, and sales would be based on immediate need, resulting in limited cooperation.

The improvements in transportation infrastructure and the increased availability of automobiles has encouraged craft producers to expand beyond their local markets to regional markets in Pátzcuaro, Uruapan, and elsewhere. Concurrently, these transportation improvements have brought more people to the region, either as tourists or as passing motorists, expanding markets beyond the region. Although the amount of tourism in the Purépecha region cannot be compared with the high level in Oaxaca, it has a direct impact on the scale of craft production and the type of crafts produced (Popelka and Littrell 1991). This windfall in market potential not only reduced the level of production conservatism but also enticed many more families to produce crafts. The success of one producer generates interest among other families, and under this scenario successful innovations can spread quite rapidly through a community, sometimes even to the point of market saturation (Acheson 1972a). If the capital costs associated with the new product or technology are worth the risk, the innovation can diffuse at the rate at which families accumulate the necessary capital. As more families adopt the new innovation and the level of production increases, prices drop and/or markets have to expand.

Because Purépechan villagers do not have complete access to all markets, falling prices lead to an inability to reaccumulate capital. Villages can see a rapid adoption of a craft innovation, but only if entry into the new business requires little initial investment. This dynamic nature of craft production in the region has been noted by several researchers, as has the subsequent stagnation of these craft industries as production levels exceed sales (West 1948; Belshaw 1967; Acheson 1972b). When the ability of the producers to geographically extend their market has reached its limit, the only response open is an attempt to augment craft sales by focusing on consumers who enter the market area from outside—passing motorists and tourists. The proliferation of craft storefronts on the main routes through the meseta towns is the visible expression of the producer's understanding of the continued existence

of geographical market limitations and of the few options available to increase, or at least maintain, their existing sales (Figure 10).

Transportation improvements can either lead economic development (Gauthier 1968) or be led by economic development (Wright, Meyer, and Walker 1983). They lead economic development by reducing transfer costs and the geographical limits these costs impose on manufacturing, thereby promoting economic development. Conversely, when economic development strains the existing transportation infrastructure, the resulting infrastructural improvements are led by economic development. In the Brazilian studies cited above, economic development was due in large measure to the influence of regional urban centers (São Paulo, in Gauthier [1968]; Curitiba, southwest of São Paulo, in Wright, Meyer, and Walker [1983]). Whether transportation was leading or being led by economic development, that development was an extension or projection of the urban center’s influence into the hinterlands. William Fox and Sanelia Porca (2001) have shown that the economic effects of transportation infrastructure improvements is “modest” in peripheral rural areas, and Paul Krugman (1991, 1996) suggests that such improvements may actually have negative impacts on these areas.8

Morelia and Uruapan, the regional urban centers in the Purépecha region, have not projected their influence beyond their immediate surroundings, nor are they likely to in the foreseeable future. Morelia, capital of the state of Michoacán, is approximately 80 kilometers east of Cherán and had a population of about 550,000 in
2000. In that year the population of Uruapan, approximately 40 kilometers south of Cherán, was about 265,000. Both cities have a typical urban economic base with large-scale and small-scale manufacturing and well-developed commercial and service activities. Since the mx-14 toll road between Morelia and Uruapan was opened, most commercial traffic has bypassed the smaller towns in the study area.

So, in the case of the Meseta Purépecha, transportation improvements appear to have led to economic development. Those locations that have seen increased accessibility are now linked to the broader economy directly through the distributors and haulers who move products to the markets and indirectly through increased in situ sales. Places where accessibility has seen little change can take advantage of the same direct link to the broader economy but have no access to the now-important indirect links.

Given that the woodworking economies of the study towns remain distinct but dependent on the larger urban centers, and that market extension for these towns remains largely out of their control and in the hands of distributors, resellers, and tourists, the response of the craftsmen is not surprising. The visible agglomeration of producers along the main thoroughfares and the curbside displays perform the dual function of encouraging people to stop and creating an image for the towns—they become known and acquire a reputation for furniture production as people see, buy, and spread the word. The craftsmen have taken advantage of the opportunity opened up by the improved roads running through their towns and have increased their market in the one obvious manner open to them. Furniture production in the mountain towns may be equally as large, but, because they are not on a principal thoroughfare, curbside displays and agglomeration are not warranted. These towns are not known for their chair production, although they certainly could be if this were determined by production volume alone. Market extension in these towns must be accomplished by physically moving the goods to distant locations.

Accessibility and Craft Production

Throughout much of its history, craft production in the Meseta Purépecha of Michoacán, Mexico was diverse and locally sustainable. The construction of highways through the region beginning in the 1950s led to important differential changes in accessibility among the towns within the region, in terms of both the towns’ reach beyond the region and the through traffic of travelers from outside the region. One of the most obvious changes was a reorientation of trade from the historical mountain–lake (west–east) flow pattern to a tierra fria–tierra caliente (north–south) orientation as trucks replaced burros as the primary mode by which products were transported. Towns responded by reorienting their craft production to a concentration in wooden furniture, expanding their market area, and centralizing the location of resident production and sales along the main thoroughfares.

All of the study villages and towns experienced a contraction in the scope of crafts produced and an increase in the scale of production of the ones that remain. Production in all settlements except Paracho ultimately shifted completely to wood-
working because most other previous production could not compete with imported inexpensive, higher-quality, low-order products like fabrics, plastics, and ceramics. The availability of abundant local or imported wood and the lack of significantly less expensive competition enable these towns to survive in the “niche market” of woodworking. Production has responded to changes in accessibility in such a way that the towns located along the highways produce higher-order items such as cabinetry, while the remote villages have shifted production to focus exclusively on the lower-order production of chairs. But intravillage competition, overproduction, and price gouging on the part of independent haulers have kept profits low.

Although overall accessibility increased throughout the region, the growth was not uniform. Some settlements saw dramatic increases; others remained isolated. The uneven nature of this process has led to significant differences in craft production among the study-area settlements. Towns that saw moderate to high increases in accessibility responded with growing clusters of furniture production and stores along the main through routes. Craft producers in these towns have been able to expand their markets, which has kept craft production viable. In towns with little or no increase in accessibility, craft production has remained diffuse, and products are sold exclusively in markets of the larger nearby market centers, such as Pátzcuaro. The markets available to these remote towns are much more limited, and overproduction has led to sales stagnation. Consequently, these processes have resulted in a two-tiered system in which towns with increased accessibility have viable—albeit shifting—craft industries, but towns with limited accessibility have not only much-reduced but also stagnating craft industries.
