BORDERLAND LIFEWAYS IN THE AMERICAN EXCLAVE OF POINT ROBERTS, WASHINGTON

MARK BJELLAND

ABSTRACT. Point Roberts, Washington is a geographical anomaly that opens a window into the lived experience of borders. Created by the imposition of an antecedent geometric boundary on an undulating coastline, Point Roberts has transitioned from a resource extraction frontier to a summer tourist destination for Canadians to a transnational borderland that defies notions of the bounded state as the container for society. The community is defined by the international border, which both connects and isolates it. Point Roberts relies on a mixture of services from both countries and fills a unique transnational economic niche, reflecting the complementarity between rural and urban and the United States and Canada. Point Roberts is a hybrid borderland: a privileged exurb for U.S. citizens working in Vancouver, a U.S. service center for Canadians, and a seaside retirement community with pockets of isolation and neglect. Keywords: borders, political geography, Vancouver.

In Point Roberts, Washington, the dictionary definition of an exclave—"a portion of a . . . state, which is separated from the main part and surrounded by alien territory"—is an everyday lived reality (Clark 1998, 138–139). Point Roberts is a geographical anomaly created by the clash between an undulating coastline and the unyielding geometry of the 49th parallel. Once a physiographic island, Point Roberts is now a political island. It is a U.S. exclave at the tip of a Canadian peninsula, dominated by the presence of an international border and the Canadian population at its doorstep. While most scholarship on exclaves has been statist in its orientation (van Schendel 2002), this paper focuses on the lived experience of borders. Like other borderlands, the exclave of Point Roberts has important implications for how we think about the nation-state, international borders, and transnationalism in an era of simultaneous economic globalization and heightened border security following 9/11. Beginning in the 1890s, Point Roberts grew from a borderland frontier into a hybrid transnational place with economic functions rooted in symbiotic relationships between the United States and Canada, and between its rural landscape and the adjacent Vancouver metropolitan region. It is an unincorporated U.S. community where gasoline prices are advertised in liters and the grocery store cash registers have separate drawers for U.S. and Canadian currency. Point Roberts is both a highly connected borderland region shaped by international flows of goods and people, and a place isolated by an increasingly hardened international boundary. Everyday life in the exclave of Point Roberts mirrors the contemporary world’s dynamic of boundedness and unboundedness as it entails both regular border crossings and frequent reminders of the powerful hold of the territorial state.

A Bordered World

Julian Minghi’s seminal review asserted that political boundaries are “the most palpable political geographic phenomena” and are central to the study of political geography (1963, 407). After half a century of geopolitical change and the addition of numerous new borders, Alexander Diener and Joshua Hagen affirmed that borders remain “the most obvious political geographic entity in the lives of human beings” (2010, 1). The precisely surveyed and demarcated borders of the contemporary world are central features of geographic space, reflecting the ideals of the modernist territorial state (Diener and Hagen 2012; Murphy 2013).

Political geographic scholarship, however, has shifted from an earlier focus on demarcating, classifying, and evaluating international boundaries to critical work exploring the border as a site where social-spatial differences are enacted and represented (Van Houtum 2005). This critical turn in political geography rejects narrowly statist conceptions of social and political life. Much of sociopolitical thought, argues John Agnew, falls into the “territorial trap” of conceptualizing rigidly bounded states as the containers for societies (1994). This conception of the earth’s surface divided into discrete territorial units retains a remarkable ideological hold on our spatial imaginations (Murphy 2013). Critical geopolitics, by contrast, does not treat the identity of the sovereign state as a given, but as emerging through practices and discourses of bordering, sovereignty, and security (Coleman 2007; Kuus 2010). Borders function as key spaces where the nature of states and citizenship are actively negotiated and operationalized (Van Houtum and Van Naerssen 2002). Border management policies reflect the tensions between conflicting state commitments to globalization and a borderless world alongside rebordering tendencies that reinforce socio-spatial identities of “us” and “them” (Newman and Paasi 1998; Flint 2002). In short, borders are powerful social constructions that function as both gateways and barriers, and zones of cooperation and conflict (Newman 2006).

While borderlines often demarcate abrupt administrative and cultural transitions, permeable boundaries can create borderland regions of interaction, exchange, and hybridity (Rumley and Minghi 1991; Popescu 2012). Everyday life in some borderlands may be highly integrated with shopping, work, and socialization taking place across state boundaries (Wastl-Walter 2009). Reflecting the shift in scholarly focus from the boundary to the borderland region, scholars have explored the formation of cultures and identities along the U.S.–Mexico border, finding simultaneous processes of polarization and hybridization (Heynman 2010). The borderlands along the U.S.–Canada border have developed their unique character due to the border’s function as both barrier and link (Slowe 1991). The U.S.–Canada border has long been a site where U.S. desires for hegemony and Canadian nationalism have been contested and where more recently the contradictory liberalizing and exclusionary processes of neoliberalism are actively mediated (Nicol 2015). In the context of the North
American Free Trade Act, the U.S.–Canada border has seen enhanced cross-border movement of workers and goods while, on the other hand, in the post 9/11 era the border has become the site of increased exclusion with the war on terrorism. These contradictions have been resolved to benefit a privileged transnational business class, argues Matthew Sparke, by creating “spaces of exception” through the NEXUS expedited border-crossing program, which sits in stark contrast with the curtailed citizenship and expedited removal procedures of post-9/11 border security (2006).

Making Sense of Exclaves

Exclaves are, by definition, border regions characterized by an “unusually high degree of cross-boundary circulatory pressure” (Minghi 1963, 422). Exclaves are part of a broader class of territorial outliers that represent deviations from the compact, geographically contiguous form that is idealized for nation-states. They are almost always the product of contingent historic, even accidental, circumstances during the establishment of borders. Most exclaves are tiny and lack geopolitical significance, yet their persistence challenges those who predict the eventual disappearance of the nation-state (Ormae 1990; O’Brien 1992; Guéhenno 1995; Castells 1996). Exclaves are more than geographical oddities; they offer valuable insights into the contingent practices of statecraft and the social construction of borders (McConnell 2011).

A true exclave is a territorial outlier completely surrounded by a host country. Technically, Point Roberts is a pene-exclave, an exclave for all practical purposes. It is possible to reach Point Roberts by air or water from the contiguous United States without crossing into Canadian territory but few trips are made that way since it features neither an airport nor adequate moorage facilities for anything beyond recreational craft (Vinokurov 2007). Travelling by land to Point Roberts from the contiguous United States mainland involves two international border crossings and travel through 37 km of Canadian territory. Another pene-exclave of the United States, Minnesota’s Northwest Angle, is larger, jutting 69 km north of the 49th parallel into Lake of the Woods. However, the Northwest Angle is in a remote area of lakes and forests and home to just 179 people, while Point Roberts is located adjacent to Vancouver, British Columbia, Canada’s third largest metropolitan region with 2.5 million people (U.S. Bureau of the Census 2011; Statistics Canada 2013).

No definitive worldwide count of exclaves exists, although Honoré Marc Catudal asserts that there are more exclaves worldwide than nation-states (1979). Many exclaves are concentrated along the India-Bangladesh, Belgium-Netherlands, and Kyrgyzstan-Uzbekistan-Tajikistan borders. Some European exclaves have been unstable pawns in geopolitical disputes and have disappeared through incorporation into the host state, while others are stable fixtures of the geopolitical landscape (Catudal 1979). Exclaves that persist do so through a combination of acquiescence on the part of the host country and
nationalist allegiance to the home country. Susanne Nies notes that exclaves often face serious problems of passage through the host country, governance, economic isolation, and weak cultural identity (2003). The simultaneously bounded and unbounded character of life in an exclave shapes social life and cultural identities. For example, in the small exclaves along the India-Bangladesh border, transnational daily routines and a weak state administrative presence have led to fluid cultural identities and populations that are effectively stateless and nationless (van Schendel 2002).

Catudal proposes three economic alternatives for exclaves: integration into the home country’s economy, assimilation into the host country’s economy, and economic independence. The first option requires overcoming the friction of distance; the first and second involve reducing the impedance presented by the border; and the third, in Catudal’s judgment, is the most risky (1979). Some exclaves, such as the tourist-oriented exclave of Campione d’Italia, find their economic niche by providing unique borderland functions that reflect the complementarity between home and host country. Campione d’Italia is a 1.6 square kilometer piece of Italian territory surrounded by Switzerland, located 12 km inside the Swiss-Italian border (International Seminar of European Small Exclaves 2009). The community’s revenues are largely derived from a large municipal casino that takes advantage of relaxed gambling regulations relative to those in the host country of Switzerland. Campione d’Italia is highly integrated into Switzerland’s economic and administrative systems, using Swiss banks and vehicle registration, and receiving its telephone, hospital, and firefighting services from Switzerland. On the other hand, the exclave’s residents and businesses can use both Italian and Swiss postal codes, the Swiss Franc or Euro currency, and are subject to unique taxation rules (International Seminar of European Small Exclaves 2009). Campione d’Italia’s borderland economic niche and hybrid mix of services from both countries suggests a fourth model, with advantages over the three alternatives outlined by Catudal.

STUDY OBJECTIVES AND METHODS

This study explores the everyday lived experiences of this borderland through an examination of its historical geography, evolving economic functions, border crossings, land ownership, and residential population. It draws upon both quantitative analysis of public data sets, close personal observations dating back to 1991, and interviews with key informants and local residents. Data sets include the American Community Survey, United States and Canadian censuses, border crossing data from the Department of Homeland Security, and the 2014 Whatcom County property tax parcel geodatabase. Since 1991, I have periodically visited Point Roberts, Washington, first while living in Vancouver, Canada, and later while living in Point Roberts during the 2011–2012 academic year. During 2011–2012, I lived with my family at latitude 48.9933° North on Boundary Bay Road in Point Roberts, Washington, and conducted participant
observation by immersing myself in community life. Challenges of everyday life such as daily border crossings, connecting utilities, hiring contractors, shopping, and educating middle-school and high-school age children revealed a new face to everyday life in this borderland. Between 2011 and 2015, interviews were conducted with key informants: local business owners; Whatcom County planning, zoning, and environmental services personnel; and local utility providers, to develop a more complete picture of service provision in the shadow of an international boundary. Semistructured interviews were conducted with members from ten households, all with U.S. citizenship, who lived year-round or part-time in Point Roberts. Questions explored the choice to live in Point Roberts and perceptions of life in an exclave.

WHERE THE 49TH PARALLEL MEETS THE SEA

Point Roberts is the western terminus of the continental land boundary between the United States and Canada, occupying the southern portion of the Tsawwassen Peninsula. However, Point Roberts wasn’t always a peninsula. When the glaciers covering the region were beginning to recede 10,000 years ago, it was an island in a sea that stretched from present-day Vancouver, B.C., and Blaine, Washington, to the Canadian Gulf Islands. Deposition of large volumes of sediment at the mouth of the Fraser River caused the Fraser River delta to grow outward, eventually connecting Point Roberts to the mainland (Armstrong 1990). The extensive shoals in Boundary Bay on the eastern edge of the point are a legacy of alluvial delta construction. Spanish explorers mistook Point Roberts for an island, naming it Isla de Zepeda (Inglis 2008). One year later in 1792, British explorer George Vancouver determined that it was a peninsula, naming it in honor of his friend the naval captain Henry Roberts.

For 2,010 kilometers from Lake of the Woods to the Strait of Georgia, the 49th parallel defines the U.S.–Canada border. The Oregon Treaty, signed in London in 1846, set the border between the United States and British Canada at 49° North from the summit of the Rockies to the center of the channel that separates the continent from Vancouver Island. From the midpoint of that channel—the Strait of Georgia—the border was drawn south-southwest to the Juan de Fuca Strait and then west to the Pacific Ocean, assigning all of Vancouver Island to British Canada (International Border Commission 1921).

While conducting the border survey on the mainland, surveyors discovered that Canada’s Tsawwassen Peninsula dangled 3 km south of the 49th parallel, creating the pene-exclave of Point Roberts (Figure 1). The British foreign secretary Lord Clarendon assumed the tiny 12.7 square kilometer peninsula tip would be worthless to the Americans and suggested a transfer to British Canada (McCutcheon 1986). The United States was not persuaded. The conflicts over both Point Roberts and the location of the maritime border were eventually mediated by the emperor of Germany, who awarded Point Roberts

Rural Isolation and Resource Extraction

Prior to European settlement, the Tsawwassen band of Coast Salish maintained a permanent settlement on the lowland coast just north of Point Roberts (Harris 1992). During the spring and summer months, Straits Salish bands occupied the prime fishing grounds at the southeast tip of Point Roberts, now known as Lily Point (Doe 2001). There, they cast reef nets into the shallow waters to catch the salmon rounding the bend of Boundary Bay on their return trip to spawn in the Fraser River.

After it was awarded to the United States, Point Roberts was designated a military reserve, but remained undeveloped with the exception of a few traders and smugglers (Kirk and Alexander 1995). Lacking both a safe harbor for boats and a bridge crossing of the Fraser River to connect with Vancouver, Point Roberts remained isolated. The Alaska Packers Association (A.P.A.) salmon cannery opened on Lily Point in 1891, and the George and Barker cannery opened on the western shore in 1900 (Clark 1980). Beginning in 1893, Icelandic immigrant families began to settle on Point Roberts (Thor 2002). The Icelandic
immigrants worked in the canneries and, claiming squatter’s rights, built wood-frame houses, planted gardens, and raised dairy cattle. In 1908, they were granted homestead rights to the lands they had occupied (United States Congress 1908). The salmon canneries exploited the same strategic fishing location the Strait Salish bands had used, but their methods were more effective and ultimately destructive. They constructed fish traps across the entire shoal on the eastern shore of Point Roberts, capturing the migrating Fraser River salmon. By the late 1910s, the number of returning salmon was in marked decline; the A.P.A. cannery closed in 1917, the George and Barker in 1929, and fish traps were banned in 1934 (Clark 1980). Farming was poor and off-farm work was scarce. Thus, many farmsteads were abandoned in the ensuing decades and the community’s population declined (Figure 2).

Development in Point Roberts was hampered by its lack of connectivity with the contiguous United States. The shoals around Point Roberts were still a preferred fishing grounds in the mid-twentieth century, but the American commercial fishing fleet operated out of the harbor in Blaine, WA, 26 km east. In the 1960s the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACOE) evaluated the feasibility of constructing a harbor for the U.S. fishing fleet and recreational craft. The project was deemed economically viable, but died because there was no state or local institution willing and capable of partnering with the USACOE (USACOE 1966). Instead, change was to come from the other side of the 49th parallel.

The period of rural isolation drew to a gradual close as Point Roberts was pulled into the expanding urban field of Vancouver. In the middle of the twentieth century, the farmer-fisher population began selling land to Canadians looking for places to build vacation cottages, often without recording the sales at the county seat or obtaining building permits (Minghi 2010).

TRANSACTIONAL FUNCTIONS IN A BORDERLAND SPACE

Rural and relatively underdeveloped, Point Roberts exists symbiotically with the dense, urbane, and prosperous Vancouver metropolitan region. Businesses in Point Roberts have flourished by offering Canadians cheap waterfront land, low prices, low taxes, or items forbidden at home. In the 1950s, two changes deepened the connections between Point Roberts and the growing Vancouver metropolitan region: a Washington state law permitting Canadians to own property in the state, and the 1959 opening of the four-lane Massey tunnel under the Fraser River (Minghi 2010). The first change opened up the vacation-cottage market to the largest nearby group of potential buyers, and the second made Point Roberts accessible to Vancouver residents. The growing connections triggered dramatic growth in seasonal recreational use and triggered a slow reversal of decades of decline in the permanent population. By the late 1950s, 400,000 border crossings were recorded during the summer tourist season of June through August (Minghi 1962).

While Canadian tourists flocked to Point Roberts in the summer, permanent residents struggled. In 1971, the International Joint Commission on the border held a hearing in Point Roberts on problems facing residents of Point Roberts. The problems voiced by permanent residents were: customs regulations; limited employment opportunities and lack of access to Canadian labor markets; and complications in securing services including health care, electricity, telephone, and public safety (Minghi 2010).

Like other borderlands, less restrictive laws allowed certain businesses to flourish in Point Roberts (Minghi and Rumley 1972). In the 1970s and 1980s, Point Roberts developed a reputation as the Tijuana of Canada, and was the second-busiest border crossing between the Pacific and Detroit (Ellensburg Daily Record 1984). Until bans on Sunday alcohol sales in British Columbia were lifted for EXPO 86, two adjoining Point Roberts taverns did great business hosting concerts and serving cheap alcohol to Canadians. Together, they featured seating capacity for 1,300 customers—more than twice the permanent population of the community at the time (McCarthy 1986). Enhancing Point Roberts’s wild reputation, the Playtime Theatre drew border-crossing customers by showing hard-core pornographic films banned in British Columbia (Belter 1984). The pornographic theater and the larger tavern are long closed, and the most risqué illicit offerings in Point Roberts today are the medium rare hamburgers served in local restaurants—forbidden across the border by the B.C. Ministry of Health (Hopper 2012).
In addition to its affordable waterfront cottages, Point Roberts draws Canadians with its parcel-shipping services and inexpensive fluids: beer, wine, milk, and gasoline. Prior to September 11, 2001, the number of same-day Canadian visitors to Whatcom County was highly correlated with exchange rates; afterwards, border-security concerns weakened that relationship (Hodge 2007). Still, the traffic remains considerable: 20,000 pedestrians and 1.6 million personal vehicle occupants crossed into Point Roberts in 2014 (U.S. BTS 2015). For Canadians, the American enclave of Point Roberts is an ideal place for shipping and receiving packages to and from the United States, a function that has grown with online retailing. In addition to the U.S. post office, there are four private shipping companies that allow Canadians to register a U.S. shipping address. One boasts of over 2,000 lockers with a 24/7 automated package-retrieval system. While some businesses hesitated to release their exact number of registered customers, an email survey of the shipping and receiving businesses on Point Roberts indicates that the number of Canadian residents with a registered Point Roberts mailing address is approximately forty times Point Roberts’ permanent population. For Canadians, driving to Point Roberts, paying a fee to the shipping vendor, declaring their purchases at the border, and paying duty is cheaper and faster than having it shipped directly to Canada. Some Canadians avoid declaring items at the border by discarding the shipping boxes in the United States, creating such a problem that one shipping and receiving company charges a box-disposal fee. During the Christmas holiday season, the shipping activity generates vehicle queues of 1 km, blocking driveway entrances and access to the trusted-traveler lanes, and expanding the frustration. Because many of the shipping businesses are located close to the border, some people walk across, making Roberts the third-ranked northern border crossing for pedestrian traffic (U.S. BTS 2015).

The permanent population of Point Roberts could likely support a single gasoline station. But with gasoline priced 20–30 percent less than across the border, Canadian customers keep four gas stations in business. Many Delta, B.C., residents purchase all their gasoline in Point Roberts and others fill up portable containers as well. Alcohol and dairy products are substantially cheaper in the United States due to Canadian taxes and price supports. It is not uncommon at the Point Roberts grocery store to see shopping carts filled with beer, wine, or milk. Sometimes the bulk purchasers of milk are Hindus preparing to make religious offerings at one of the Vancouver area’s temples.

Tourist and real estate promoters point out that Point Roberts is part of the rain shadow of the Olympic Mountains, leading to 40 percent less annual rainfall than in Vancouver (Slaymaker and others 1992). For residents of the high-density, high-priced Vancouver metropolitan region, Point Roberts is an affordable place for a beach cottage. While the average house price in the Vancouver metropolitan area was Can$814,000, the average house price in Point Roberts was US$222,000 (Canadian Mortgage and Housing Corporation 2015;
U.S. Census Bureau 2014). With large inflows of global real estate investment, Vancouver’s housing market is the most unaffordable in North America (Ley 2010; Demographia 2015). Meanwhile, Point Roberts offers affordable ownership opportunities for detached single-family housing. Modest summer cottages on 0.10-acre lots at Maple Beach and South Beach fill with families while the most-expensive houses with ocean views line the southern and western waterfront of the point (Figure 3).

The properties owned by Canadians are dispersed across the peninsula with strong concentrations at Maple Beach and South Beach. The mean size of properties owned by Canadians is smaller (0.50 acres) than those owned by U.S. residents (1.1 acres). The typical cottage at Maple Beach or South Beach is a two or three-bedroom single story wood-sided structure with 800 square feet, built in the mid-twentieth Century. The smaller and more modest properties owned by Canadians reflects, in part, the fact that Canadians are typically limited to part-year residence on Point Roberts. Immigration rules forbid Canadians from staying in the United States for more than 180 consecutive days; those who pass a substantial presence test are subject to U.S. income taxes, and those who spend more than six months out of Canada can lose their British Columbia health-care benefits (Olson 2015).

Cultural Landscapes of Point Roberts
Upscale, well-manicured suburban subdivisions extend right to the Canadian side of the 49th parallel; south of the border the scene abruptly changes to a semirural landscape. The different stages in Point Roberts’ historical geography have left a disjointed cultural landscape. The majority of businesses serving Canadians are located near the border entry point. The area near the former George and Barker cannery was the town’s center in the early 1900s, and still has the post office, community center, and library. Point Roberts lacks a true center—at what should be the prime-value intersection, sit several abandoned, overgrown farmhouses (Figure 6). The early 1900s white wood-frame Lutheran Church and a dozen pre-WWII houses are testament to the early Icelandic immigrants. A few offshore wooden pilings, some rusting machinery, and a wharf are all that remains of the canneries.

The parks in the four corners of Point Roberts are the premier public spaces. Monument Park in the northwest features a granite obelisk that was installed in 1861 as Border Marker 1, the westernmost point on the United States’ northern mainland border (Figure 7). Lighthouse Park in the southwest corner is a popular place to picnic and watch Orca whales and sunsets. Lily

Dwelling in an Exclave

Life in Point Roberts follows seasonal rhythms. The population surges to over 5,000 in the summer when Canadians outnumber Americans almost three to one. When the school year starts in September, the population dwindles to the permanent population of 1,314. Two out of three houses are closed up and empty over the winter.

Most Point Roberts residents came from somewhere else: 85 percent were born outside Washington State and 41 percent were born outside the United States. Many of the permanent residents are Canadians who have become naturalized citizens of the United States. Since the vast majority of residents are newcomers, the natural question concerns the pull factor for migration to Point Roberts. The reasons people cite for choosing to live in Point Roberts are diverse. Many are retirees attracted to the scenic coastal location who can choose optimal times to cross the border when delays are minimal. Lower housing costs compared to Vancouver are a factor commonly cited by U.S. citizens who have relocated to the Vancouver region to take jobs under the North American Free Trade Act (NAFTA) professional worker visa program. Compared to Blaine, Point Roberts’ residents were more likely to work in construction, retail, arts, entertainment, and recreation, and less likely to work in manufacturing, transportation, or public administration. The differences in employment structure translate into a substantial earnings differential. In 2013, the average payroll per employee in Point Roberts was $28,900 a year compared to $65,700 a year in Blaine, Washington (U.S. Census Bureau 2013). The poverty rate for persons under eighteen years of age in Point Roberts was 29 percent, higher than the national rate of 22 percent (U.S. Census Bureau 2015). The mean travel time to work for Point Roberts residents is a mere twenty-four minutes (U.S. Census Bureau 2014). But such an average is meaningless to describe the bimodal reality of short commutes to service or construction jobs on Point Roberts, alongside long border-crossing commutes to better-paying jobs in Vancouver or the contiguous United States. The most common commute time is under ten minutes and a higher than average 9 percent of workers work from home. But 23 percent of workers have commutes greater than forty-five minutes, probably because 27 percent of workers commute to jobs outside of Washington—mostly in the Vancouver region (U.S. Census Bureau 2014). Some Point Roberts residents drive across the border and then take public transit to jobs in Vancouver, since a bus stop is located just 290 meters from the border.

Seasonal migrations between southern U.S. states and Point Roberts are a pattern for some retirees and self-employed business owners. One couple with a permanent address in southern California directs a binational nonprofit organization with its U.S. office in California and its Canadian office in British Columbia. They own year-round houses in California and Point Roberts, spending summers in Point Roberts while working out of the Canadian office. Another family with school-age children, are self-employed and migrate seasonally between California and Point Roberts.

Many of the businesses in Point Roberts are centered around the needs of weekend visitors from Canada rather than permanent residents. For example, several restaurants and the coffee shop are only open on weekends. There is an art gallery, and bike and paddleboard rentals, but no hospital, dentist, veterinarian, or government service center. These services are available in nearby Delta, but usually one’s medical and dental insurance don’t apply across the border. In small towns, the local schools are often the nucleus of community life. Thus, the closure of the school in Point Roberts and absorption into the Blaine School District in 1963 was a devastating blow to the community. The Blaine School District operates a K-3 primary school on Point Roberts, but beyond the third-grade students are bused through two international border crossings and 37 km of Canada to attend school in Blaine. Usually the school bus is given expedited passage through the border crossing, but students still spend about 2.5 hours a day on the bus. There is no activity bus and attending a child’s after-school event involves negotiating Vancouver’s rush-hour traffic
and four international border crossings. One family reported plans to relocate to Blaine, Washington, so their kids could participate in extracurricular high school activities. Working out an arrangement with the local Canadian public schools would seem to be an obvious solution. But nationalist commitment to a U.S. education had led to a commitment to the Blaine solution. Further, Canadian public schools are off-limits to individuals unless the parents can afford the nonresident tuition, which was Can$13,000 a year in 2015.

INFRASTRUCTURE CONNECTIONS

The infrastructure that makes contemporary urban lifestyles possible is often taken for granted. But on Point Roberts, those infrastructure details matter and they often reflect complex arrangements with most services provided by U.S. companies relying upon Canadian suppliers. Until 2008, a Canadian company provided telephone service. That was satisfactory for Canadian tourists, but it meant that a U.S. resident calling their children’s school in Blaine, Washington, had to pay international dialing rates. Reception from American cell phone providers is poor, but excellent from Canadian providers, forcing U.S. residents to pay international rates or pay extra for a dual-country plan. Puget Sound Energy, a Seattle-based firm, provides electrical service, but the actual electricity comes by contract from B.C. Hydro. Solid waste is hauled all the way to a landfill in north-central Oregon. A backup heat source and camping lanterns are almost a necessity for year-round residents. Extended power outages occur frequently when strong winter storms off the Pacific knock branches off the towering Douglas fir trees, downing power lines, and crews must travel from distant locations.

Water has been the greatest infrastructure challenge and short supplies nearly halted development. There are no permanent streams on Point Roberts. Hydrogeologically, Point Roberts is an island surrounded by salt water. Pumping too much groundwater leads to saltwater intrusion. The marine West Coast climate features wet winters and drier summers, resulting in aquifer recharge during the winter and falling groundwater levels during the summer. Thus, water demand and water supply peak at opposite times of the year. In the 1970s and 1980s, municipal wells were chronically inadequate, sometimes running dry or increasing in salinity when the summer tourist population swelled during the warmer and drier summer months. The Greater Vancouver Regional District (GVRD) has an abundant drinking-water supply from its mountain reservoir system. However, the GVRD’s policies forbade the export of water and during an intense drought during the summer of 1973 when Point Roberts’ wells went dry, the GVRD refused to help. Instead, water had to be trucked in from Blaine, Washington, using milk tankers (The Bellingham Herald 1973). After difficult negotiations, in 1988 the Point Roberts Water District began purchasing drinking water from the GVRD (Forest 2010). Some Point Roberts residents are still unhappy, complaining of the GVRD’s unfair pricing policies,
which are based on the maximum-allocated capacity rather than actual use. In a typical municipal water system, the peak daily flow is 150 to 180 percent of the average daily flow (Lindeburg 1989). However, in Point Roberts the peak daily flows during the Canada Day and British Columbia Day summer holidays reach as high as 390 percent of the average daily flow (Point Roberts Water District #4 2015). To meet water demands during the peak tourist season, the Point Roberts Water District contracts with the GVRD to purchase 3.2 million liters per day. Actual daily usage averaged 0.9 million liters per day in 2014. Thus, Point Roberts was required to pay for 3.5 times as much water as the community actually used just in order to have it available for Vancouver tourists during Canadian summer holidays. In response, some permanent residents have pressured the water utility to evaluate a submarine water pipeline from Blaine to Point Roberts.

Border Crossings

The international border is an ever-present reality in the exclave of Point Roberts. While often described as the world’s longest undefended border, the U.S.–Canada border’s permeability varies depending on one’s citizenship, one’s professional qualifications, and the category under which a good or service is classified. Away from the border crossing station, the border consists of a 6 meter clear zone, a grassy strip or roadway marked by intermittent orange posts. The backyards of Canadian houses come right to the edge of the border clear zone and some Canadians regularly step across the border for a stroll or even quick swim at Maple Beach. Still, the boundary is real. People moving to Point Roberts from elsewhere in the United States often discover the reality of the border when their moving crew is stopped at the Canadian border because one or more movers don’t have passports. Furniture and building supply shipments can be long delayed because the shippers couldn’t get through the Canadian border. Finding skilled tradespersons is a major challenge. For example, there is no plumber on Point Roberts, Canadian plumbers aren’t licensed and legally eligible to work in the United States, and plumbers from Blaine are often unable or unwilling to travel Point Roberts.

Heightened security at the border post-September 11th converted Point Roberts into what locals describe as a gated community guarded by the newly created U.S. Department of Homeland Security’s Customs and Border Patrol. Property theft is rare since escape would be difficult. It is rumored that Point Roberts is a haven for people on the witness protection program, as well as those who have obtained restraining orders against an abuser. Persons who might want to harm them can expect trouble getting through the border. On the other hand, for some residents of Point Roberts the international border renders the peninsula a 12.7 square kilometer prison since Canada has the right to deny entry to U.S. citizens with a criminal record, including any alcohol-related offense.
In response to complaints from frequent border crossers after border security was hardened and the PACE trusted-traveler program was dropped after the September 11, 2011, terrorist attacks, the U.S. Customs and Border Patrol and Canada Border Services Agency jointly developed the trusted-traveler NEXUS card program for the U.S.–Canada border (Sparke 2004). Point Roberts residents agree that a NEXUS card is almost a necessity for life on Point Roberts. The NEXUS program allows shorter border-entry crossing times, with expedited processing and designated lanes with little or no queue. At the Peace Arch crossing at Blaine, Washington, a 2014 study found that weekend border delays for NEXUS card users averaged 1.9 minutes versus 21.7 minutes for nonusers (Dingman and Edgel 2015). Many Point Roberts residents time their trips based on the ten-to-twelve daily open hours for the NEXUS lane. Predictability also improves for NEXUS cardholders who are less likely to experience extended delays that can result in missed appointments or late arrivals at work. To qualify for the NEXUS program, participants must be citizens or permanent residents of the United States or Canada, and pass criminal and law-enforcement background checks. Many Point Roberts residents complain of being denied a NEXUS card due to minor criminal infractions that occurred long ago or being denied without explanation (All Point Bulletin 2007). Others live in fear of having their NEXUS card revoked because of the program’s no-tolerance policy on violations of rules on restricted items, declaration of goods, and commercial use (U.S. House of Representatives 2008).

The complex and changing regulations on importing fruits and vegetables are emblematic of the challenges of living in a borderland space. The limited fruit and vegetable selection in the Point Roberts grocery store is typical of a small town. But just over the border in Canada there are several supermarkets and green grocers with expansive fresh fruit and vegetable selections. But the fresh foods from nearby Delta, B.C., are off limits, even if they were originally grown in the United States. To be caught at the border with illegal produce is to risk permanently losing one’s NEXUS card—one’s lifeline in the borderland space of Point Roberts.

CONCLUSIONS

Point Roberts may be a geographical anomaly, but it sheds light on the lived experience of borders. Once a literal island in the sea, this peninsula has grown evermore connected to the outside world, giving us a chance to consider the processes that connect us and the walls that divide. Over the past 125 years, Point Roberts has transitioned from a rural resource-extraction frontier to a summer tourist destination for Canadians to a transnational borderland that defies notions of the rigidly bounded state as the container for society. Like the Italian exclave of Campione d’Italia, it relies on a mixture of services from both countries and fills a unique transnational economic niche. Increasingly, Point Roberts functions as a U.S. service center for Canadians and a residential space
for U.S. citizens working in Canada. Future research should explore the differences in place perceptions and experiences between U.S. and Canadian citizens and between residents and visitors.

Surrounded by water on three sides and on the fourth by one of the world’s most livable cities, Point Roberts could be mistaken for paradise. Residents and vacationers benefit from a quiet rural retreat with relatively low housing prices, just across the border from the dense, bustling, cosmopolitan city of Vancouver, British Columbia. But while it promises the best of two worlds, the reality on Point Roberts is more complex, as attested by a landscape of both million-dollar waterfront homes and overgrown, abandoned houses.

The challenges of living in the exclave of Point Roberts as identified in the 1971 International Joint Commission hearing are still present. The presence of the border complicates daily life and hinders the provision of basic services. The problem of limited employment opportunities in Point Roberts has been solved for those university-credentialed professionals who qualify under NAFTA to work in Canada. Those lacking the technical skills and university degrees favored under NAFTA still face low wages and limited options. Point Roberts may be a “space of exception” for a small number of the privileged elite, but the tenuousness of access to the NEXUS trusted-traveler program casts a shadow of uncertainty over everyday life as it renders residents vulnerable to the vicissitudes of the border patrol. Many of the regular border-crossers are not a global elite, but those who must cross the border regularly to find work, to attend a child’s after-school function, or to receive basic services. At times, the border creates powerful experiences of isolation that resemble those of deeply rural locations, despite the nearby presence of a large metropolitan region. In short, Point Roberts is a hybrid borderland that embodies the simultaneously bounded and unbounded, connected and isolated character of contemporary life.
