PRICKLY PEAR MENACE IN EASTERN AUSTRALIA 1880–1940*

DONALD B. FREEMAN

ABSTRACT. Prickly pear cactus, introduced into eastern Australia by early settlers, became a major pest in the early twentieth century and caused abandonment of large tracts of rural land. Governmental strategies to halt the pest were ineffectual; some helped the cactus to spread. The discovery in the mid-1920s that the larvae of the Cactoblastis cactorum destroyed the pest was attributable more to luck than to cohesive policy.

THE campaign waged from 1880 to 1940 by Australian governmental officials, scientists, and private landowners against the spread of prickly pear cacti throughout Queensland and New South Wales has been called “one of the world’s most spectacular achievements in the history of economic entomology” (Johnston and Lloyd 1982, 216). The sheer speed with which the prickly pear invaded farming and grazing lands, the huge area it devastated, and its seeming invulnerability to conventional means of eradication had indeed presented a fearsome challenge. Scientific experts of the early twentieth century were all but at a loss for a solution (Steele 1923, 6). In the face of a looming, apparently irreversible ecological disaster, the sudden termination of the rampage by the prickly pear through inland eastern Australia appears nothing short of miraculous.

Government-sponsored researchers grappling with the crisis had realized quickly the extent to which geography was involved in both the problem and its potential solution. Both spread and eradication of the pest had global as well as regional ramifications and involved a combination of environmental, economic, and technological considerations. Recognition of this fact led the small band of scientists desperately searching for solutions eventually to vanquish the pest.

In retrospect, however, the assertion by a 1923 royal commission that a definite policy had been “consistently followed by successive governments” to combat the spread of prickly pear (Steele 1923, 4) and the notion that the spectacular elimination of pear infestation was a direct result of carefully planned and consistent governmental action are highly debatable. During most of the period in which prickly pear emerged as a grave threat to Australian rural development, governmental policies unwittingly promoted the spread of the pest rather than arrested it. GEOGRAPHY OF A PESTILENCE

Prickly pear is the common name for several species of the genus Opuntia (family Cactaceae) that are indigenous to the western hemisphere. All are hardy, drought-resistant, subtropical perennials comprising a profusion of fleshy, flattened, segmented stems studded with tufts of spines and covered with fine, nettlelike cilia. Some, like the common pest pear Opuntia inermis, which is native to the Greater Antilles and the coast of the Gulf of Mexico, and O. stricta, which is from Chile, grow in massive thickets between one-half and two and one-half meters high, but others, such as O. streptacantha and O. tomentosa, both native to central Mexico, are tree pears reaching heights of eight meters or more. Depending on the species, they may be propagated very rapidly by seeds or by broken stem segments, which are transported by animals, strong winds, and floodwaters. In their native environments, these cacti have rarely been accorded the status of agricultural pests; however, introduced into other parts of the world, notably Australia, Hawaii, India, Sri Lanka, Madagascar, and southern Africa, they have quickly gained notoriety as noxious weeds. In Australia, the prickly pear turned into a land-greedy menace that ruined many farmers and ranchers and threatened the rural economy of an entire continent.

Coastal and interior areas of eastern Australia proved to have climatic and edaphic characteristics very well suited to several species of prickly pear. The early, rapid dissemination of prickly pear species was a direct result of misguided experiments by European immigrants who had deliberately carried the plant and established it in areas of new settlement. Thereafter, very rapid diffusion of prickly pear was aided by accidental cartage by humans, consumption and dissemination by domestic animals and native fauna, and transport by wind and water.

Like many environmental disasters, the prickly pear problem began with good intentions. The plants initially arrived in Australia on the ships of the first fleet bringing European convicts and soldiers. Cuttings were taken on board at Rio de Janeiro on an impulse by the first governor, who believed, wrongly, that the plant might become the basis for a flourishing cochineal dye industry (Johnston and Tryon 1914, 127). Several different varieties of the prickly pear imported by later immigrants proved useful as a cheap, effective hedge plant that bore edible fruit. The hard-coated seeds of the prickly pear germinated readily in the scattered droppings of native birds and browsing cattle who ate its fruit. Even so, it was then generally regarded as a useful addition to the local flora and as a stock fodder of last resort during times of drought. Not until the 1870s was concern recorded about proliferation of the wild prickly pear.

The pest pears flourished in an area of sparse rural settlement, newly opened to grazing and wheat cultivation. Much of the land was held on lease from the colonial government or was purchased as freehold for a few pennies a hectare. Although in their native areas the main pest species occur mostly in coastal and subcoastal environments (Johnston and Tryon 1914, vii), for reasons that are still not clear dense infestations did not arise in coastal areas of Australia but thrived on the inland, tree-covered downs and plains west of the Great Divide (Fig. 2). This area is mostly subhumid, with an annual rainfall between 500 and 760 millimeters, with elevations ranging from 180 to 360 meters, and with subtropical to temperate temperatures varying from 21.4° C at Rockhampton to 16.8° C at Scone (Dodd 1940, 20–21). Though tolerant of different soil types, the main pest species favored bentonitic or black-cotton soils and were thickest in the Brigalow Belt, a broad tract named for the dominant vegetation of Acacia harpophylla or brigalow trees. They formed an open canopy above the thickets of prickly pears, which prefer such habitats to grasslands (Fig. 3).

Although a few indigenous diseases and predators attacked prickly pear, these had a negligible impact on its rapid spread. Domestic cattle, which browsed on the pears when more palatable food was unavailable, likewise made no headway against the pest, even in times of drought. It seemed that the extremes of weather characteristic of inland Australia, including both drought and flood, aided the diffusion of the pest. During the all-too-frequent droughts, wandering cattle scattered pear seeds and overgrazed competing grasses and other vegetation. Graziers were reluctant to destroy a plant that, though problematical, might keep their starving cattle alive until the rains came. Often the rainfall was torrential, and the resulting floodwaters carried broken segments of prickly pear far into the interior along westward-flowing rivers, which helped the pest to establish itself quickly over a wide area.

Data from contemporary surveys and estimates (Steele 1923, 1–2) show that the rate of spread was almost exponential in the first two decades of the twentieth century (Fig. 4). At the peak of the infestation, approximately 250,000 square kilometers, an area larger than Great Britain, were rendered useless. More than 80 percent of the affected area was in Queensland, with most of the remainder being in a contiguous section of inland northern New South Wales.

EARLY GOVERNMENTAL INDIFFERENCE

The prevailing notion that the proliferating prickly pear was worth tolerating as drought fodder for cattle lingered long enough for the pest to gain a formidable hold. When a member of the Queensland parliament voiced alarm in 1882 at the rate of spread of the prickly pear, fellow politicians scornfully laughed at him (Steele 1923, appendix 3:37). In the following decades, however, there was less to laugh about. Between 1883 and 1887, no fewer than twenty-two local authority boards declared the prickly pear a nuisance under the Divisional Boards Amendment Act of 1882 (Steele 1923, appendix 3:37; Johnston and Lloyd 1982, 216). In theory that action allowed them to seek governmental help in controlling the prickly pear pest; in fact, few of the boards actually received that assistance.

Although the alarm had been sounded, the governments of Queensland and New South Wales took no direct action against the pest. They were preoccupied at that time with other matters, such as the rapid opening of vast tracts of virgin land for settlement, unaware that the prickly pear literally followed in the footsteps of settlers and their cattle. If attention turned to pests at all, these were more likely to include the scourge of the European rabbit or the native dingo, or noxious weeds such as the Scotch thistle or Bathurst burr, which never became pests of the magnitude of the prickly pear. The prickly pear was not officially added to the list of noxious weeds until 1893 (Dodd 1940, 20).

The Crown Lands Act of 1895 became the first Queensland statute to mention prickly pear, but it merely noted that land covered by this pest on grazing leases was exempt from the area on which rent was payable (Steele 1923, 2) and that prickly pear eradication by crown-land tenants was to be considered “an improvement for which compensation could be paid on the termination of the lease otherwise than by forfeiture” (Steele 1923, appendix 3:37). The Queensland Land Act of 1897 defined a form of land tenure called scrub selection, a thirty-year leasehold on crown land that required tenants to clear scrub, which often meant prickly pear, during the term of the lease (Steele 1923, appendix 3:37).

In short, by the beginning of the twentieth century, the diffusion of the prickly pear was uncontrolled. Having profoundly underestimated the problem, the governments of Queensland and New South Wales persisted in the belief that private owners, tenants, or local governments were both responsible for and capable of clearing the prickly pear from the land, with no financial or technical help from the colonial governments. By 1901, when the colonies confederated to form the Commonwealth of Australia, an estimated four million hectares in eastern Australia were infested by prickly pear. In that same year, the parliament of Queensland passed the Prickly Pear Selection Act, designed to encourage destruction of the pest on heavily infested land. The act allowed for a ten-year lease during which prickly pear infestations were to be cleared by a tenant, for which improvement a bonus
would be paid and after which the tenant would be granted the deed to the land if it was found to be free of the prickly pear. The maximum land granted to each tenant was about 1,160 hectares. The program was a resounding failure. In the decade following passage of the act, only ten persons committed themselves to the program (Steele 1923, appendix 3:38).

After the great drought of 1902, when graziers gladly had their starving livestock feed on the prickly pear, the pest expanded its area at an ever faster rate. By 1913, it was estimated that 1,432,950 hectares were covered with dense pear infestation and 4,889,622 hectares with scattered infestation (Steele 1923, 2). During World War I, when governmental attention was again diverted to more pressing matters and rural labor was in short supply, diffusion of the pest accelerated. By 1923, the year in which a royal commission was convened to investigate the prickly pear problem, the area infested with dense pear was estimated, probably conservatively, at 4,167,862 hectares and with scattered pear at 5,504,021 hectares. The brief time, less than three years, required for scattered seedlings and segments to become dense infestations astounded and demoralized farmers (Steele 1923, 1).

By 1925–26, the total area infested was estimated to be 24,000,000 hectares, of which 12,000,000 hectares were said to be so densely infested as to be virtually useless (Dodd 1940, 2). That blighted area stretched from 20° S to 33° S, a north-south distance of more than 1,440 kilometers. The western limit of pear infestation varied from 320 kilometers inland in central Queensland to 640 kilometers at Charleville and 560 kilometers at the border between Queensland and New South Wales. At its peak in 1925–26, the rate of increase in the affected area was 1,000,000 hectares annually. Until the mid-1920s, governmental policies not only were totally ineffective in slowing the prickly pear infestation but also abetted its spread.

INDIRECT GOVERNMENTAL POLICIES 1900–1923

For the first two decades of the twentieth century, state-government policies in Queensland and New South Wales were uncoordinated and hap-hazard, as were efforts at eradication by shire councils and other local jurisdictions. Vigorous efforts to deal with the problem in one jurisdiction were often thwarted by indifference or inertia in neighboring administrative areas. Different levels and sectors of government frequently acted at cross-purposes, took only token measures, or ignored the problem altogether.

In general, governmental policies aimed at eradicating the prickly pear were either indirect, through measures such as land-tenure legislation and local governmental statutes, or direct, including fiscal policies related to land clearance, environmental policies controlling diffusion agents, and research on methods of eradicating the pest. In both the indirect and direct categories, a process of reactive escalation is evident, as governments reluctantly and sluggishly responded to the worsening crisis.
The indirect policies were the most problematical. They were aimed at the tenants on various types of governmental or crown land. Early colonial statutes, such as the Crown Land Act of 1895 and the Land Act of 1897, served as prototypes for later legislation that compelled tenants to clear the prickly pear off holdings under penalty of forfeiture. To these were added the Queensland local authorities acts of 1902 and 1920, in which the prickly pear was declared a noxious weed "and hence subject to all legislative enactments for the extirpation of such weeds" (Steele 1923, 2). Special clauses allowed local authorities to deal with the prickly pear on freehold land and on roads and reserves under their control. These clauses and, indeed, the laws themselves proved useless, because few local jurisdictions could afford the cost of eradication programs in their underdeveloped, often heavily infested territories.

The coercive regulations governing land tenure likewise failed for several reasons. First, there was no provision for keeping the prickly pear off unoccupied crown lands, which often became reservoirs for reinestation of nearby cleared leasehold or freehold lands. Tenants on infested crown lands, scrub selections, and prickly pear selections could not be forced to stay on the land once they realized that fighting the pest was a full-time job that left no opportunity to make a reasonable profit from farming. Even if farmers diligently cleared the prickly pear at the outset, near the expiry date of their leases they had no incentive to do so, and they often abandoned the land before the lease expired. If a state government insisted on forfeiture of neglected lands, they were usually completely overrun by the prickly pear in short order, because no legislation obliged any government to clear them. Infested lands almost invariably failed to attract new tenants (Steele 1923, 5). On freehold lands, farmers were at liberty to use whatever methods they pleased to clear the land, not necessarily the most effective or economical. In shires with lax administrations, the prickly pear often spread unchecked onto public roads and reserves as well as onto private lands.

There were additional weaknesses in the legislative approach adopted by the state governments. Landholders in uninfested areas were not alerted to the imminent danger of prickly pear encroachment. Concessions and compensations for prickly pear clearance were never sufficiently attractive to induce wholehearted cooperation by tenants on leasehold lands. Subsidies to help landholders clear the prickly pear were virtually nonexistent. There was no mechanism for obtaining information about the problem or about dealing with it. Meanwhile, tenants frequently complained that the state and local governments actually penalized conscientious tenants by raising crown-land rents and, thus, land taxes once the tenant had cleared the land (Steele 1923, 8). Tenants who cleared grazing land were vulnerable to a challenge by others who wanted the land subdivided for closer settlement, which sometimes meant that the diligent tenants lost all or part of their land and were not compensated for the hard work of clearing the property.
DIRECT POLICIES 1900–1923

Direct fiscal and legislative actions to deal with the prickly pear menace were likewise weak and ineffectual in the first two decades of the twentieth century, owing to failure to develop coordinated policies and to mobilize the states’ resources for a determined counterattack. Policies for direct action, which came later than the indirect measures mentioned previously, were based on a penny-pinching concern about costs. The policies lacked conceptions of an appropriate strategy and of the enormous future costs of failure.

The policies reveal a three-phase pattern of reactive escalation in response to the expanding threat. The first phase, beginning around 1900, involved legislative and fiscal measures to study the problem and to commence clearance of the prickly pear in selected areas by conventional, labor-intensive mechanical methods. The second phase, after 1912, consisted of arrangements to import and subsidize the distribution of poisons for chemical clearance of primary infestations. This approach was coupled with ruthless extermination of native species of birds, notably the emu, the crow, and the scrub magpie, and was based on advice from researchers who concluded that these species spread prickly pear seeds to both new and previously cleared areas. The third phase involved the federal and state governments and focused on finding effective biological agents to control prickly pear. The biological-control policy became the last hope for curbing the runaway diffusion of the pest, which was not an especially bright prospect: in the 1920s, few successful biological-control programs existed anywhere in the world.

In the first decade of the twentieth century, mechanical clearance of the prickly pear was the only known method of eradication. It involved the unpleasant, onerous task of hand-cutting plants, whose spines inflict pain and persistent irritation on contact with unprotected skin. Stacked atop platforms of dry wood, huge piles of prickly pear were then set afire. No plant roots or broken or unburned segments could be left lying in the fields, or reinfestation would be swift (Fig. 5). Secondary clearance would be needed for an indefinite period on each parcel of cleared land, because of the high probability of reseeding by birds and cattle. To clear land that was then generally worth between forty cents and two and one-half U.S. dollars a hectare, the minimum cost using conventional, labor-intensive methods was no less than ten times that amount. Not surprisingly, this method of clearance was quite ineffective.

The Queensland Department of Agriculture had been conducting experiments with poisons since 1899, and an experiment station was established in 1912 at Dulacca, on the Darling Downs, under the direction of a full-time scientist who continued trials of poisons to determine the most effective agent against the prickly pear (Johnston and Lloyd 1982, 216). Arsenic pentoxide became the poison of choice but was expensive. Because it had to be imported from Europe, it was in short supply until after World War I. Although at the time they could not have foreseen its eventual spectacular success, governmental scientists were urging the establishment of research into biological controls to combat the prickly pear in the first decade of the twentieth century. In 1911, advisers at the University of Queensland recommended a worldwide search for a biological agent to control the pest (Johnston and Tryon 1914, vi). That search was undertaken from 1912 to 1914 by the Queensland Prickly Pear Travelling Commission, a three-person team that scoured the globe to bring back specimens of insects that might attack the pest species in Australia. A combination of bad luck, marked by promising specimens not surviving translocation, and the outbreak of World War I frustrated the efforts of that dedicated group. A second attempt began in 1920 with the establishment of the Commonwealth Prickly Pear Board, located in a Brisbane suburb and funded largely with federal money. It sent entomologists to retrace the steps of the previous commissioners, and as a result fifty different insect species were sent to Australia for effectiveness trials. Meanwhile, the obvious inadequacy of existing policies had engendered a political crisis in the wake of the agricultural one. With scores of farms and grazing properties being abandoned every week, a rising crescendo of accusations that the governments were not doing enough mingled with plaintive appeals for help from farmers losing the battle against the prickly pear (Fig. 6). The low-profile scientific tests on a handful of insect species did not seem an adequate response to the now thoroughly alarmed politicians, perhaps an understandable attitude in view of the dearth of prior, truly successful biological-control programs. Members of the Queensland parliament now loudly demanded appointment of a royal commission to investigate the problem.

The report of a Queensland royal commission of 1923, known as the Steele Commission, contained a ringing indictment of preexistent strategies of the government: "unless existing methods of holding the pear in check are radically changed, there is little hope but that the spread will continue" (Steele 1923, 4). Among the policy failures outlined by the Steele Commission were a lack of coordination of legislation dealing with different forms of tenure, a lack of machinery to ensure that unoccupied crown lands were
kept clear, a lack of incentives for crown tenants to keep their leaseholds free of the prickly pear in the closing years of tenancy, a failure to impose clearance requirements on slothful and indifferent local authorities, and a powerlessness to act in freehold areas, in which one neglectful owner could undo all the pest-clearing measures taken on neighboring lands (Steele 1923, 7).

The commission recommended important policy changes to combat the prickly pear menace. These included discarding previous failed policies, recognizing that the problem was countrywide and required not simply state or regional strategies, replacing revenue generation with prickly pear clearance and farm rehabilitation as the chief goals of land policy, and encouraging settlers to stay on their land and to fight the prickly pear. The last involved replacing previous arrangements with new, more secure and more attractive leases of longer duration, at least thirty years, and adjustment of taxes to reward efforts to keep land clear. On the prospect of a biological solution to the prickly pear problem the commission was pessimistic. "The prospect of . . . insects acting quickly enough to deal with the very scattered pear," asserted the report, "is so remote that it may be left out of consideration and effort concentrated on other methods of dealing with this class of infestation" (Steele 1923, 24).

THE MIRACLE BUG

For the four years after 1923, little evidence can be found to suggest that the commission's recommendations had any significant positive effect. The Prickly Pear Land Commission was created to cover an area almost the size of Great Britain. The sale and transportation of arsenic pentoxide continued to be heavily subsidized by the Queensland government. The first notable successes with biological agents became apparent in 1924, when a "wild" cochineal insect (Dactylopius near confusus) was proved to be effective when released into infestations of some tree pears and the tiger pear (O. aurantiaca), albeit not against the main pest species. In the face of this limited success, the Queensland government withheld separate action on biological-control experiments, arguing that this strategy was already undertaken by the federal government. The diffusion of the pests went on unabated, and tenants continued openly to defy legislation forcing them to clear leasehold lands at the end of their tenure period (Queensland Legislative Assembly 1927, 336).

Then, in 1927 came an unpredicted breakthrough. An insect, Cactoblastis cactorum, that had previously failed its acclimatization test had been reintroduced from Argentina and Uruguay in 1925. The second time, the eggs hatched and the larvae were raised to provide a population of breeding adult moths. They passed the standard starvation tests on commercially useful plant species, showed a single-minded preference only for prickly pear species, and bred prolifically. By September 1927, the first batch of 100,000 eggs was ready for liberation in pear-infested areas. Conveniently, the insect laid its eggs in robust, portable strands that were easily inserted into prickly pear
infestations. The eggs usually incubated three to five weeks, long enough to permit transportation from the hatcheries to infested zones (Dodd 1940, 5). On hatching, the orange-and-black-striped caterpillars voraciously attacked the growing segments of the prickly pear plant, reducing it quickly to a pulpy mass and bringing about the total collapse of the whole infestation. A parliamentarian later observed that the insect seemed to have been put on earth for one purpose only—to answer the fervent prayers of Queensland farmers.

Between September 1927 and March 1929, more than 220 million eggs were made available without charge to landholders and released along roads and on other public lands (Dodd 1940, 5). The results were nothing short of stupendous. In the year ended 30 June 1930, less than three years after the first eggs were distributed, 200,000 hectares of pear had been totally destroyed, mostly at Chinchilla. Crews of governmental workers distributed egg strands along the main railway lines in southern and central Queensland and northern New South Wales (Dodd 1940, 138). The liberated insects acclimatized successfully and bred in the wild, diffusing spatially from the lines of original distribution. Their progress was marked by huge areas of dead and dying pear (Queensland Department of Public Lands c. 1930). By the end of 1931, the prickly pear menace in eastern Australia was almost ended. Tens of millions of hectares of rotting vegetation were left where impenetrable thickets of pear had stood only months before. Land that had been useless for decades was now cleared and planted with grain and pasture grasses (Fig. 7).

The collapse of the prickly pear menace was so swift that it took some time before the magnitude of the victory registered on the rural populations of Queensland and New South Wales. The jubilation was temporarily cut short, because the miracle insects themselves suffered a devastating decline in 1931–32: they literally had exhausted their food supply. New prickly pear infestations began to sprout from seeds previously scattered in the areas ravaged by the insects. By early 1933, succulent new stands of the prickly pear had reclaimed much of its former territory. Fearing that they may have wrongly placed too much faith in one insect species, governmental researchers sought new types. However, Cactoblastis cactorum proved no less resilient than Opuntia. A resurgence in numbers of insects occurred in 1933, and by 1934 the new growth of the prickly pear had been almost completely devoured (Johnston and Lloyd 1982, 221). Thereafter, a balance between pear biomass and insect numbers was established, at a level somewhat less than 1 percent of the peak prickly pear biomass. Despite occasional regional outbreaks, this balance appears to have been maintained ever since.

governments of Queensland and New South Wales were not bashful about taking credit for the spectacular success of their program against the prickly pear. Part of their elation sprang, no doubt, from the fact that the biological solution was so inexpensive. By contrast, mechanical and chemical eradication efforts by private landowners were costly and ineffectual. The governments even found that their miracle insects had the potential for generating export revenues. Biological control agents were exported from Queensland hatcheries to South Africa, Ceylon, Java, and New Caledonia during the 1930s.

Prickly pear infestation delayed by more than fifty years settlement of large areas of eastern Australia. After elimination of the pest, close agricultural settlement began energetically. By 1940, ten million hectares, formerly dense prickly pear-covered country, had been selected for settlement, and the revenue of Queensland was boosted by income from beef, cotton, grain, and dairy products. On already settled land that had suffered only moderately from prickly pear infestation, agricultural productivity also increased dramatically. At Chinchilla, production of dairy products surged sevenfold from 400,000 pounds in 1926 to 3,100,000 pounds in 1939.

Lessons learned during the contest against the prickly pear were later applied to other pests, such as the Harrisia cactus (Eriocereus martini) in northern Queensland, which yielded to biological control only in the late 1970s. Unfortunately, the general public had become accustomed to the idea that miracle bugs could be found to destroy almost any introduced pest, partly as a result of the success against the prickly pear and the Myxomatosis campaign against the European rabbit. Pressure on governments to introduce new biological agents has led to some fresh disasters, like the spread of the voracious and poisonous South American cane toad (Bufo marinus) throughout much of northern Australia. In this case, the remedy, the toad, has proven far worse than the original pest, the cane beetle.

More perhaps than any other part of the world, Australia has suffered both environmentally and economically from unwise introduction of exotic plants and animals and from inappropriate governmental policies regarding the environment, natural resources, and settlement of rural land. The object lesson of the prickly pear case study is that a quick, coordinated, properly funded and executed response by government is necessary in the face of new biological threats and that concurrent experiments should be conducted with as broad a variety of countermeasures as possible. That is far preferable to basking in the reflected and not-altogether-deserved glory of a few exotic insects that finally brought the prickly pear cactus under control.
