PERI-URBAN AGROFORESTRY IN THE BRAZILIAN AMAZON*

VANESSA A. V. SLINGER

ABSTRACT. Together, urbanization and the search for sustainable development present a dilemma in the Brazilian Amazon: how to accommodate an expanding urban population while creating and maintaining sustainable production systems that feed the people and manage the forest. A unique peri-urban agroforestry project, implemented by a municipal government in western Amazonia and concerned with a citywide influx of rural agriculturalists and former forest-dwelling extractive producers, is examined as a source of food and self-determination. Peri-urban agroforestry seems to be a viable option for other Amazonian cities that are experiencing increasing urbanization and its associated problems. Keywords: agroforestry, Amazonia, Brazil, migration, urbanization.

If agroforestry research has progressed on many a front, there remains lamentably little known about the informal agroforestry of peri-urban settings. Still less documented are planned peri-urban agroforestry projects. The small, urban backyard gardens have by and large lived outside the realm of formal urban research and planning. Instead, they exist as unsponsored and largely individual efforts, providing households with nutrition and subsistence (Alcorn 1990). To date understudied, the development of agrosilvopastoral systems that combine perennial trees and shrubs with animals and annual crops in areas surrounding cities presents an attractive, if little explored, opportunity for urban planners and policymakers working in developing countries to grapple with food provision and with urban social concerns.

The Municipal Pole for Agroforestry Production (Pólo Municipal de Produção Agroflorestal) is an original alternative being explored by the municipal government of Rio Branco, Acre, in western Amazonia (Figure 1). The project addresses the challenges of urbanization created by an influx of agriculturalists and extractivists, mainly rubber tappers, from rural areas. A 1920s decline in the price of natural rubber from Brazil was initially driven by elevated rubber production on Asian plantations and then followed by improved profits to be gleaned from the mass production of synthetic rubber. Agroforestry was adopted as an alternative strategy that could potentially produce food, generate income, and combat hunger and other social problems experienced in city slums (Prefeitura de Rio Branco 1993). URBANIZATION IN THE BRAZILIAN AMAZON

Over the past twenty-five years colonization of the Brazilian Amazon has been the subject of extensive research and writing, but studies of the frontiers of Amazonian colonization rarely consider concurrent urban developments (Volbeda 1984). Although the Amazon is journalistically thought of as an "agricultural frontier," in reality the population is highly concentrated in urban areas (Godfrey 1990; Martine 1993; Browder and Godfrey 1997).

Driving migration and concurrent urbanization in the Brazilian Amazon are the concentration of land in the hands of large property holders, the lack of formal title to properties, stagnation in areas once devoted to extraction and agriculture, and the "pull" of possibly better urban services and employment opportunities (Ramos de Castro 1989; Schmink and Wood 1992). Stagnation in agriculture and in such extractive economies as rubber tapping and brazil-nut collecting has influenced migration to the cities of Manaus, Belém, and Rio Branco (Sawyer 1990). Urbanization in the Amazon increased from 36 percent in 1970 to 59 percent in 1990, and in Acre the proportion of people living in urban areas doubled from 28 percent in 1970 to 55 percent in 1990 (Sawyer 1990).

High urbanization rates in the Brazilian Amazon are provoking many of the same problems that affect the three-quarters of the Brazilian population living in cities. Migrants have poor access to government and to state resources and services: limited piped water, sewage treatment, electricity, garbage disposal, education, and medical assistance. Spontaneous invasions of migrants to the urban periphery are a reaction to a shortage of housing. High unemployment and work in the informal sector appear in response to the lack of job opportunities.

In Acre the capital, Rio Branco, shelters at least half of the state's total population; close to 80 percent of the inhabitants are migrants from the rural zone. The declining rubber-extraction economy and the penetration of cattle ranching into the tiny western state spawned migration to Rio Branco in the 1980s. In 1970 Rio Branco had close to 35,000 inhabitants, but by the mid-1990s the city's population surpassed 190,000. Fewer than half of the arriving migrants had resources sufficient to maintain themselves for even a single month; another 25 percent could fund but one to two months (Schmink and Cordeiro 1992). The products of this were decisive pockets of misery.

Perhaps one-third of Rio Branco's working-age population has steady employment (Pinto de Oliveira 1982). Inhabitants of the poorer districts of Rio Branco, including a large proportion of former rubber tappers and agriculturalists, have low incomes and lack professional qualifications (Alves 1995). Although an estimated 38 percent of the resident workers receive a minimum monthly salary—roughly u.s.$70.00 per month in 1993—another 27 percent in the informal sector earn still less (SMAA 1993).

AGROFORESTRY IN RIO BRANCO

The first Municipal Pole for Agroforestry Production, established in November 1993, was seen as a means to provide a home, work, and income for needy families who were accumulating in city slums. The project entailed the resettlement of slum dwellers to plots of land in the municipality of Rio Branco, to produce food for their subsistence and to supply markets in Rio Branco. In this article I focus on the initial Pole, located in Floresta (Figure 2). It is an agrosilvopastoral system combining annual crops, perennial fruit trees and shrubs, and small livestock. The land designated for the project was previously a cattle ranch with degraded pasture.

Put into effect at a total cost of U.S.$410,524, the Floresta project aimed to settle between sixty and eighty families on land with a minimum of 3 and a maximum of 5 hectares per family. The projected cost of placing a family in the Pole was U.S.$5,132–$6,842—considerably less than the cost of other settlement projects implemented in Amazonia by the National Institute of Colonization and Agrarian Reform (INCRA). Estimates for government-directed colonization in the Amazon run as high as $300,000 per household (Friedmann 1996).
Other projects in the 1970s, known as “Directed Settlement Projects” (PADS), whose principal objective was to settle people in the countryside to produce food, have been assessed as unsuccessful (Schmink and Wood 1992). Over time, the negative results of PADS have become evident: concentration of land and the accelerated expansion of extensive cattle ranching, whose consequences in the region are already well known, deforestation, soil degradation, and no shortage of accompanying social conflict. The Agroforestry Production project has been promoted as a better alternative because its design calls for a rigorous selection process, high levels of technical and equipment support, focus on a relatively small area for intensive management, reliance on a production system considered environmentally and economically beneficial, and proximity to local markets.

The selection process gave priority to families with some tradition in agro-extractive activities that were living in peripheral locations susceptible to flooding and invasions. Families who owned no land were also given priority. The participants were either unemployed or held jobs in the informal sector. The people chosen had a family income of less than two minimum salaries. Families with children (available for labor) were also favored. Chosen families had to be willing to return to a rural zone close to the city and to demonstrate their interest by participating in meetings called by the municipal secretary of agriculture.

Although project participants were not given actual title to the land, they were granted use of it for five to ten years, and possibly longer (politics permitting), but they were not permitted to sell it. The main concern about giving land title to individual participants was that they would sell their lots and return posthaste to the city. INCRA is the sole government agency with authority to carry out agrarian reform in the Brazilian Amazon, so the municipal government cannot vest title with participants. The Rio Branco government hopes that, if the participants show strength through organization in a cooperative or association, eventually INCRA will give the land to the cooperative.

Growing interest in the concept of the Pole and its potential for addressing urban social concerns is spreading within Acre and across Brazil. Although power changed hands in local elections of October 1996, the Pole is surviving the vortex of political power change. The new mayor, Mauri Sergio, though part of the former opposition, intends to seek additional funding to expand the number of Poles.

Another hallmark of the Municipal Pole for Agroforestry Production is the infrastructural and technical support it has received. Seeds, seedlings, animals, tools, and a tractor were first acquired for project use. After a participating family had been on a lot for six months, living in a temporary house and beginning cultivation, the municipal government provided construction material and help to build a permanent dwelling that received electricity.

The municipal government considered proximity and easy road access factors important to success of the project. These would allow for easy marketing of any surplus produce through wholesale, retail, and street markets in Rio Branco. The project budgeted for a six-ton truck that would be the initial form of transportation for
the participants’ produce. The neighboring municipalities of Plácido de Castro, Senador Guiomard, Feijó, Xapurí, and Tarauacá were also outlets for surplus. The adjacent state of Rondônia was a potential market, vast in the size of its population and ready with year-round transportation.

Assistance in developing agroforestry systems arrived in the form of technicians from the Department of Vegetal and Animal Production in the Municipal Secretariat of Agriculture and Food Supply. Other expertise was solicited from such research institutions as the Federal University of Acre, the Technological Foundation of the State of Acre, the Brazilian Enterprise for Agricultural Research, the Brazilian Enterprise for Technical Assistance for Rural Extension, the National Institute for Amazon Research, and Research and Extension in Agroforestry Systems of Acre. For success, the municipal government depended on the organization of the beneficiaries into a cooperative.

ECONOMIC AND ENVIRONMENTAL BENEFITS

Although agroforestry systems do not maintain the original natural vegetation, researchers recognize the benefits they provide to soil, crops, and farmers (Lundgren and Raintree 1983; Young 1987; Anderson 1990; Current, Lutz, and Scherr 1995; Smith and others 1995). Agrosilvopastoral systems, multistoried and diverse in their nature, can provide food for subsistence living and sale, stable yields, varied products, plant protein energy, nutrients in the form of minerals and vitamins, wastes for animal feed, soil and water conservation and management, nitrogen fixation, pest control, and continual production throughout the year (Haryadi 1975; Nair 1990). The benefits are extraordinarily varied, as the people of Floresta have discovered.

Forty-eight families—276 people—were selected to settle on 210 hectares of land in a peri-urban area of Rio Branco. Adults above the age of fifteen constitute 45 percent of this population; 55 percent of the settlers are male. A household usually comprises a father, a mother, and children and may also include grandparents, in-laws, cousins, or other members of the extended family. During the summer of 1995 I interviewed forty-four of the families.

The participants’ lot sizes range from 2.2 to 4.3 hectares, averaging 3.6 hectares (Figure 3). Plantings comprise fruit-tree crops, palms, shrubs, vines, and other perennials, along with annual vegetables and other crops—in all, thirty-one perennials and twenty-eight annuals (Table I). Although the original plan for the Pole failed even to consider the planting of vegetable gardens, almost three-quarters of the participants have taken to cultivating vegetables, using family labor.

Most of the items produced are eaten by local families, but many participants have sold more than 20 percent of their annual harvest—the amount that the municipal government expected to be the likely ceiling. For selling this produce, the municipal government created open-air stalls in a Rio Branco neighborhood, so participants have found an enduring market not only in local restaurants but also in these street stalls. A small amount of produce is bought by a middleman, who later resells it in urban markets.
When I asked participants why they moved to the Pole, they offered two main reasons: They did not own their own house, or they did not have a job or the financial wherewithal to survive in Rio Branco. In fact, prior to moving to the Pole only 59 percent of the households had heads with any form of employment—temporary,

The nearly universal optimism of this response is especially apparent when compared with the research of Marianne Schmink and Mâncio Lima Cordeiro, who asked residents of Rio Branco about the city's ability to improve their lives (1992). Responses showed that only 35 percent of those respondents believed that their situation had improved, though 54 percent said that they felt the city would improve their lives in the future.

The rate of abandonment of the Pole has been low, and the majority of the participants do not plan to return to Rio Branco—nor do they anticipate that their children will move to the city. After all, the participants' need for employment and home ownership are being met in the Pole. Their level of commitment to the Pole is high, and so is expectation that their future will be based on life there.

Changes in the quality of life are difficult to measure, for they depend on an individual's decision about what contributes to a bettered standard of living. Basic necessities, though, such as food and health, are inescapable. A quantitative measure that is a suitable indicator of improved quality of life is data on food consumption and on health. More than three-quarters of the participants told me that their health and diet had improved since they arrived at the Pole. For some of them, dietary improvement means more meals per day, more total food per day, or more variety in the food. They might, however, have noted a decrease in the number of meals, in quantity of food, or in variety, because they are assured, by virtue of being at the Pole, that they will have something to eat even if they lack cash to buy goods and that their meals will improve even more with the passage of time and with increased production. The diversity of species in the agroforestry systems increases the variety of produce collected and also allows for a continual, year-around supply of food for subsistence and sale.

Almost three-quarters of the participants said that the money they earn every month meets their household expenses. For financially strapped families, manioc is an important survival crop: 20 percent of those whose monthly cash income fails to meet expenses responded that they eat manioc.

Furthermore, they estimated that when the integrated system was producing plentifully the farmers would be making an income of u.s.$11,000–$13,000 per year. Administrators promoted the Municipal Pole for Agroforestry Production as a so-called alternative way of dealing with lack of housing, unemployment, and hunger. They compared the Pole with cohabs (low-income housing complexes). The cohab system of providing mass housing for lower-income families was developed by the Brazilian Association of cohabs in many of the Brazilian states (Figure 4). Comparison of the Pole with Oscar Passos, a low-income housing complex in Rio Branco, reveals an almost equal cost of settling a resident: u.s.$5,132–$6,842 for each
of the sixty to eighty families at the Pole, and U.S.$5,552 for each of the 197 families in Oscar Passos.

Nonmonetary differences exist between the two projects, however. Oscar Passos, like the Pole, addresses the problems of shortage of housing and absent or deficient services, including the provision of water and electricity and the disposal of sewage. However, unlike the Pole participants, the residents of Oscar Passos are not provided with employment opportunities or with any other way to achieve a subsistence living. Pole participants have acquired a means to feed themselves and make a steady income using skills they already possess. The Pole project has decreased the population stress on Rio Branco and, at the same time, has provided food for the city’s inhabitants. Oscar Passos created urban housing but nothing in the way of opportunities for migrants with rural skills.

In 1995 residents of all group-housing projects in Acre, except for Oscar Passos, were required to earn a minimum of three to four minimum salaries, the equivalent of U.S.$210–$280 per month (Ferreira Germono de Araújo 1995); those in Oscar Passos needed one and a half minimum salaries, about U.S.$100. That Oscar Passos was the sole rock-bottom minimum-income housing in Rio Branco available to families from rural areas spells out the limited options for migrants. Migrants often have no choice but to rely on their urban relatives for a temporary or permanent place to live—or to crowd into small houses in precarious locations on the periphery of the city.

ACHIEVEMENTS AND FAILURES OF THE POLE

Many of the municipal government’s specific goals for the Pole for Agroforestry Production have been achieved. The project resettled city-bound rural migrants in an area of agricultural production. Adequate conditions have been created to induce Pole participants to remain permanently in the countryside. These conditions favored the creation of agroforestry systems that have given participants new job opportunities and an enduring—and rising—source of income. The ability to rely on produce of the land in times of hardship is hardly something to be underrated, especially in Brazil, where the traditions of landless revolt sometimes involve violence and political unrest. Furthermore, the use of diverse, multispecies agroforestry systems has provided participants with a striking degree of economic and ecological security against low product prices and pest attacks.

The total population of the Pole, 276 people, is too small for the project to have a large impact on poverty in the peripheral areas of Rio Branco. On the other hand, there is no dodging the optimistic sign that action has been taken to address the problems associated with peripheral areas. The perceived success of the Agroforestry Production project has already led to the creation of similar projects; wider adoption could have a considerable impact on the alleviation of poverty.

That participants are meeting their subsistence needs also means the Rio Branco market has been relieved of that responsibility; thus the project is a respectable net contributor to the local economy. The need to import produce from other states has been reduced, and the participants meet their subsistence needs and acquire a steady
cash flow. That said, the administrative structure of the Pole is top-down. It is also heavily subsidized by the national and state governments. The implications for sustainability of the Pole, in light of such large external investment, needs to be considered.

A SOLUTION FOR OTHER CITIES?

As the Amazon has become increasingly urbanized, efforts to sustainably manage the rain forest have strengthened. Widespread acknowledgment of the potential lack of sustainability and of the destructive consequences of current major forms of land use, such as slash-and-burn agriculture, cattle production, and logging, has resulted in a search for alternative land-use systems that combine economic, social, and ecological sustainability (Anderson 1990; Uhl and Subler 1990; Smith and others 1995). The two processes of increased urbanization and the search for sustainable development together define a major challenge in the Amazon: to accommodate the expansion of the vast Brazilian urban population while creating and maintaining sustainable production systems that can both feed the people and sustainably manage the forest.

If the Municipal Pole for Agroforestry Production is to be adopted as a strategy to address problems connected to urbanization of Amazon cities, issues remain. Agroforestry systems modify the original vegetation, so they are most appropriate for areas that are already deforested or degraded. Although agroforestry systems aim at sustainability, poor choice of species combinations, poor management practices, and lack of people's motivation and understanding may cause agroforestry to fail, just as any other form of land use may fail (Nair 1993).

Another concern is that the ability of the participants to make the income anticipated by the municipal government is based on subsidies provided by the government, on the project's proximity to the market, and on the ease and low cost of transportation. Typically, resource-poor rural inhabitants in the Amazon do not have the option to be involved in a program like the Municipal Pole for Agroforestry Production. The Pole is being used to address the specific problems created in the city by increasing urbanization. At present, it is not the goal of the municipal government to use this project to address the broader problems of poor rural inhabitants. However, it is undeniably important that the problems of the rural resource-poor inhabitant be addressed if the flow of migrants from the rural areas to the city is to abate.

Project managers need to give further consideration to the fact that Pole participants have not been given title to the land and that the amount of time they reside there may be limited to between five and ten years. Because of those limitations, participants may opt for piling up short-term gains, growing quick-maturing crops, including annuals, and in doing so may forego the benefits of the longer-term fruit trees. Lacking secure tenure, residents are certainly far less likely to make a long-term investment in conserving the land. Under continual production of annuals, the land will deteriorate instead of regenerating as planned under tree crops.

Consideration is being given to turning the land over to a community cooperative or association. It still could not be sold, but participants would at least have a le-
gal bond to it. The success of this plan relies on the creation and maintenance of an effective cooperative or association. But at best, cooperatives in Amazonia have generally worked poorly.

The Agroforestry Production project and its participants rely on the subsidies provided by the municipal government, including access to transportation, machinery, green manure, and technical assistance. As long as the municipal government is active in the Pole these subsidies are guaranteed; once the government decreases its involvement in the area there could be an associated withdrawal of these subsidies, leaving Pole participants to stand on their own.

The continued presence of the participants on the land might have depended on the maintenance of the 1996 administration in a position of power. However, the newly elected mayor of Rio Branco, Mauri Sergio, is continuing and expanding the number of Agroforestry Production projects around Rio Branco. On a larger scale, the former mayor of Rio Branco, Jorge Viana, was hired by the United Nations Development Program to spend two years working as a consultant to create Poles in other municipal districts in Acre, starting with Xapurí and Tarauacá.

The urbanization of the Brazilian Amazon is expected to continue. The number of cities in the Brazilian Amazon with more than 20,000 inhabitants increased from 7 to 20 between 1970 and 1980 (Ramos de Castro 1989) and then to 250 in 1996, when roughly 78 percent of the Brazilian population was urbanized (IBGE 2000). The decline of traditional extractive activities and conflicts over land will continue to feed the process of urbanization in many an Amazonian city. Migrants, like those in Rio Branco, come to the cities without much education and lacking the skills for anything other than rural employment. Municipal Poles for Agroforestry Production may prove significant for these Amazonian cities that are experiencing problems of increasing urbanization without a concurrent increase in infrastructure and services.
