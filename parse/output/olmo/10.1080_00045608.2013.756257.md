Influence of Latrine Proximity and Type on Tubewell Water Quality and Diarrheal Disease in Bangladesh

Veronica Escamilla,* Peter S. K. Knappett,† Mohammad Yunus,‡ P. K. Streatfield,‡ and Michael Emch§

*Carolina Population Center and School of Medicine, Division of Infectious Disease, University of North Carolina–Chapel Hill
†Lamont-Doherty Earth Observatory of Columbia University, Palisades, NY
‡International Centre for Diarrhoeal Disease Research, Dhaka, Bangladesh
§Department of Geography and Carolina Population Center, University of North Carolina–Chapel Hill

Diarrheal diseases are endemic in Bangladesh, where sanitation is poor, and untreated drinking water extracted from shallow (<43 m) tubewells could partially contribute to disease propagation. This study measures the effects of local population–environment context on tubewell water quality and diarrheal disease incidence. The study site includes six villages in Matlab, Bangladesh, with approximately 12,000 residents. Study data include monthly Escherichia coli concentrations for 100 wells, monthly diarrheal events for all children under five, and a detailed water and sanitation infrastructure database created through a submeter accuracy Global Positioning System survey. We developed sanitation metrics to measure the relationship between tubewell water fecal contamination and estimates of human fecal loadings at varying scales. The relationship between childhood diarrhea and E. coli in drinking water was measured for households that obtained drinking water from survey wells. Results show that tubewells surrounded by unsanitary latrines, latrine-polluted ponds, and higher population densities were more frequently contaminated with fecal coliforms. The analysis also showed that poor sanitation infrastructure might affect childhood diarrheal disease via tubewell contamination. Our findings shed light on the importance of integrating population and environment data to identify circumstances in which shallow well water quality is compromised and children are put at risk of contracting diarrheal diseases. Sanitation interventions should highlight the spatial separation of latrines and drinking water wells to limit contamination.

Key Words: Bangladesh, diarrheal disease, sanitation, tubewell drinking water.

Diarrheal disease remains the second leading cause of death among children under five years of age worldwide, causing 1.5 million deaths annually (UNICEF and World Health Organization 2009; UNICEF 2010). Access to a safe water supply and improved sanitation helps prevent diarrheal disease transmission but is limited in poor, densely populated places such as Bangladesh. Over the last several decades, Bangladesh experienced a widespread shift from surface to ground water consumption in response to high diarrhea-related child mortality rates. This led to the installation of an estimated 9 million tubewells throughout the country (UNICEF 2008). The majority are domestic shallow wells that were installed using a hand percussion drilling method that is affordable for individual households or baris (patrilineal household clusters; Horneman et al. 2004).

In Bangladesh, groundwater is generally considered safe for consumption, but a survey conducted in the late 1990s identified high arsenic (As) levels in tube-well water. Millions of residents are exposed to naturally occurring arsenic levels exceeding Bangladesh (50 \( \mu g/L \)) and World Health Organization (10 \( \mu g/L \)) standards (British Geological Survey [BGS] and Department of Public Health Engineering [Bangladesh; DPHE] 2001). Prolonged exposure can lead to skin lesions and chronic diseases such as lung and bladder cancer (BGS and DPHE 2001). As a result, millions of households abandoned their shallow high-As wells for neighboring, predominantly shallow low-As wells (van Geen et al. 2002).

Although well-switching effectively reduces As exposure, it can also increase exposure to microbial pathogens and increase diarrheal disease risk (van Geen et al. 2011; Wu et al. 2011). Due to the tendency for shallow high-As aquifers to be found underlying silt, shallow low-As wells placed within unconfined aquifers are more likely to be contaminated with human waste due to rapid transport and less efficient filtration of bacteria and viruses in medium-grained sand (Fontes et al. 1991). This results in a potential trade-off between As and fecal contamination (Leber et al. 2011; van Geen et al. 2011). Contamination of the shallow aquifer could have contributed to the slow decline in diarrhea morbidity over the last decade (Islam et al. 2001; Hoque et al. 2006; Luby et al. 2008; Boschi-Pinto, Lanata, and Black 2010; UNICEF 2010). It is possible that slow improvements in sanitation throughout Bangladesh compromise the shallow aquifer, exposing residents to fecal pathogens. Currently less than 60 percent of Bangladeshis have access to improved sanitation (World Health Organization and UNICEF 2012). Therefore, it is important to understand how latrine quality and placement affect shallow tubewells to prevent a potential trade-off between As and fecal contamination.

The relationship between the shallow aquifer and the sanitation infrastructure is complex. Residents often build latrines near drinking wells or ponds for convenience. Construction and maintenance of the water and sanitation infrastructure can indirectly increase exposure to microbial pathogens if the wells or the shallow aquifer are compromised by surface contamination from unsanitary latrines, spilling effluent onto the ground. Lateral migration can carry pathogens from the base of a latrine to a latrine pond (i.e., ponds collecting latrine effluent) or depression and infiltrate the aquifer. Alternatively, fecal waste might flow to a wellhead and enter the well along an unsealed annulus or through a subsurface break in the PVC pipe (Morris et al. 2003). A third possible contamination pathway is direct vertical infiltration from below latrines through the unsaturated zone into an unconfined aquifer. In this third case, subsurface contamination would presumably emanate from both unsanitary and sanitary latrines.

This study explores the relationship between surface contamination and E. coli concentrations in shallow tubewell water. Potential contamination point sources of human fecal waste include unsanitary and sanitary latrines and latrine ponds. Sanitary latrines have zero evident effluent leaking onto the surface, whereas unsanitary latrines expose individuals to human waste.
Latrine ponds are possible secondary point sources to the aquifer because they collect human waste from surrounding latrines (Knappett et al. 2011), especially during heavy rainfall in the early monsoon, when rapid overland flow washes latrine waste into depressions (Knappett et al. 2012a). During the late monsoon, latrine ponds overflow, spreading fecal matter across the surface. The primary aim of this study is to explore the relationship between shallow aquifer contamination and surface contamination at varying scales accounting for varying latrine quality.

Detailed spatial, microbial, and health data provide an opportunity to explore these relationships. Although a known relationship between sanitation and drinking water quality exists (Gerba and Bitton 1984; Bordalo and Savva-Bordalo 2007; van Geen et al. 2011), the influence of the sanitation infrastructure on tubewell microbial contamination measured at varying spatial scales, accounting for latrine quality, has not been systematically investigated. A secondary goal of this study is to explore the relationship between E. coli concentration in tubewell water and childhood diarrhea incidence. This study is guided by the following questions: (1) Does poor sanitation influence shallow tubewell water quality? (2) Does fecal contamination of tubewells influence diarrheal disease?

Research Design and Methods

The study area, Matlab, Bangladesh, is located 50 km southeast of Dhaka. It is the rural field site for the International Center for Diarrhoeal Disease Research, Bangladesh (ICDDR-B). The ICDDR-B hospital provides free treatment for severe diarrhea to all residents. Hospital and community-level records are maintained through a longitudinal Health and Demographic Surveillance System (HDSS). Community health workers visit households monthly and ask mothers whether their child under five had diarrhea, defined as a minimum of three loose or watery stools within a twenty-four-hour period.

Tubewell Water Microbial Survey

Six villages located in southwest Matlab were selected to monitor monthly variation of tubewell water microbial contamination. These villages were selected because of the high proportion of low-As shallow wells (van Geen et al. 2011). Duplicate 100-mL tubewell water samples were collected monthly between May 2008 and November 2009 (except for December 2008). Culturable E. coli was measured using the most probable number (MPN) based Colilert test kit (IDEXX Laboratories, Inc.). The MPN value reported here assumes that organisms are randomly distributed in well water so replicate samples contain the same “true” number of bacteria (Hurley and Roscoe 1983).

Geographic Database

A global positioning system (GPS) survey of the water and sanitation infrastructure was conducted in 2008 and 2009 using Trimble GeoXH receivers. Data were differentially corrected to obtain submeter accuracy. The location of all households, latrines, and ponds within 200 m of monitored wells was recorded (Figure 1). The 200-m buffer was based on the size of the survey villages to capture the sanitation infrastructure surrounding the monitored wells. The presence of an intact concrete platform was recorded and residents provided depth, installation year, and drinking water use information for all tubewells.

Residents provided pond depth and pond use information during the survey. Digital pond pictures were taken, and the long and short axes were measured using a subinch TruPulse laser rangefinder. Ponds were classified as bathing, fish-farming, latrine, or no-purpose. Ponds that did not receive latrine effluent and were not classified as bathing or fishing were designated as no-purpose ponds. No-purpose ponds directly receiving latrine effluent were classified as latrine ponds.

The majority of surveyed latrines are septic, comprised of concrete foundation rings used to prevent human effluent from leaking on the ground or into surface water. A small percentage of these latrines (7 percent) are open, usually located over a shallow pond or near a septic latrine. Latrines were classified as unsanitary or sanitary. Unsanitary latrines visibly leak effluent and are either open, septic with broken rings, or septic with a drain pipe. Sanitary latrines are intact septic latrines with no evidence of leaking effluent. Tubewell and spatial data were incorporated into a geographic information system (GIS) to explore the relationship between tubewell water quality and surface contamination.

Variable Construction

Sanitation metrics were constructed to examine the association between contamination point sources and tubewell water quality at varying scales (Table 1). Circular windows were placed around individual tubewells to measure latrine and population counts between 10 m
and 100 m in 5-m increments (Knappett et al. 2011). Population counts were measured to estimate pressure placed on surrounding latrines. Pond counts were measured between 10 m and 100 m in 10-m increments to account for the larger area covered by ponds.

In addition to surveying potential point sources of contamination, tubewell characteristics were parameterized to evaluate the impact of well construction (intact concrete platform), age, and depth on microbial drinking water quality. Further, the population drinking from each tubewell was measured. Testing these parameters helped to evaluate the possibility of rapid flow along unsealed tubewell annuli or through breaks in the casing that can develop over time (Knappett et al. 2012b).

Statistical Analysis

Analyses were restricted to shallow tubewells (<43 m) because the majority of residents drink from shallow-aquifer wells that could be compromised by poor sanitation (Carr 2001). Duplicate water samples were collected monthly from May 2008 to November 2009 (except for December 2008). A sample was considered positive if both measurements contained detectable levels of E. coli > 1 MPN/100 mL (van Geen et al. 2011). An average was taken if the well was sampled the same month in 2008 and 2009. We then calculated the frequency of E. coli detection from January through November to identify the annual frequency of E. coli detection in shallow tubewells. Statistical analyses were restricted to wells with available data for at least ten of the eleven months. Seasonal frequency of E. coli detection was also calculated to account for monsoon effects. Seasons were defined as winter (January–March), early and late monsoon (April–June and July–September, respectively), and postmonsoon (October–November; Emch et al. 2010).

Pearson correlation coefficients \( |r| \) were calculated to measure the relationship between sanitation variables (Table 1) and both annual and seasonal outcomes. Sanitation variables identified as significant (\( p < 0.05 \)) or marginally significant (\( p < 0.1 \)) predictors were then included in multiple regression models built for annual and seasonal E. coli outcomes. Diarrheal Disease Analysis

Monthly childhood diarrhea data were collected from forty-two households that obtained drinking water from survey wells. Diarrhea data were aggregated because of the small sample size. A logistic regression model was built to measure the direct relationship between the average level of \( E. coli \) contamination in drinking wells and whether a household reported diarrhea during the study period.

Results

Culturable \( E. coli \) was detected in all ninety-two survey wells during the study period. Seasonal detection varied with the lowest detection rates occurring during winter months (Figure 2). Contamination was most frequent during the postmonsoon period and it fluctuated during the early and late monsoon (Figure 2).

Annual Results

Pearson correlation coefficients show the relationships between annual contamination and sanitation metrics at multiple distances (Figure 3). The \( \bullet \) demarcates the initial distance where the correlation becomes significant (\( p < 0.05 \)). Population count and \( E. coli \) were significantly correlated between 20 m and 100 m. The correlation peaked between 20 m and 25 m. Unsanitary latrine count was significantly correlated with \( E. coli \) between 10 m and 100 m and peaked at 40 m. Sanitary latrines were correlated with \( E. coli \) between 50 m and 65 m (Figure 3). The presence of an intact concrete platform was marginally protective (\( p < 0.1; |r| = -0.19 \)). Latrine pond count, age, depth, and number of persons drinking from the well were not correlated with the annual frequency of detectable \( E. coli \).

Multiple regression models were built to measure interactions between sanitation predictors that were significantly or marginally correlated with annual \( E. coli \). The model included peak correlation values for latrine and population counts. The model with the lowest AIC is presented (Table 2). Culturable \( E. coli \) is detected more frequently as latrine and population counts increase, whereas the presence of an intact concrete platform reduces the frequency of detection.

Relationships varied by distance, and unsanitary latrines were more correlated with tubewell fecal contamination than sanitary latrines. This supports our hypothesis that surface contamination influences tubewell water quality via indirect pathways such as infiltration from latrine ponds (Knappett et al. 2012a) or the lateral transport to and leakage along unsealed annuli (Knappett et al. 2012b).

The stronger and yet further distance correlation between unsanitary latrines and E. coli contamination in tubewells in the late monsoon compared with the early monsoon suggests that transport mechanisms vary by season. During the early monsoon, transport from spatially discrete sources such as latrine ponds (Knappett et al. 2011) would be limited to subsurface transport and unlikely to reach beyond 20 m (Knappett et al. 2012a). Further, surface spreading of fecal contamination from unsanitary latrines to reach unsealed tubewell annuli would be localized at the bari scale (20–40 m). In contrast, fecal contamination in the late monsoon could result from sources aggregated across the neighborhood (70–100 m) scale via surface spreading as localized depressions become a less important source of recharge to the aquifer (Knappett et al. 2012a). A previous study in Bangladesh found that inundation of a wellhead is predictive of microbial contamination, providing a path of least resistance for contaminated surface water during the late monsoon (Luby et al. 2008). Studies have also shown inundation to be a significant predictor of well water quality in Mozambique (Godfrey, Timo, and Smith 2006). This process provides an opportunity for surface contamination from leaking unsanitary latrines and contaminated ponds to reach the water table through rapid transport down the side of the well (Assessing Risk to Groundwater from On-Site Sanitation 2001).

The presence of an intact concrete platform around wellheads was protective during the early monsoon, further suggesting that transport mechanisms vary by season. Previous studies in Bangladesh did not identify this relationship (Leber et al. 2011; van Geen et al. 2011; Knappett et al. 2012b). This difference might be the result of analyzing early and late monsoon data separately. During the early monsoon, an intact concrete platform might slow surface fecal contamination from reaching the unsealed tubewell annuli as compared to the late monsoon, when the study area is inundated and surface contamination can more easily reach the water table.

Detectable fecal contamination in tubewells indicates a need for groundwater treatment (Islam et al. 2001; Hoque et al. 2006; Luby et al. 2008; Leber et al. 2011; van Geen et al. 2011). Although fecal levels are often low, water collected from a contaminated source is more likely to become highly contaminated during storage (Hoque et al. 2006). Moe et al. (1991) suggested that a threshold of microbial contamination in drinking water exists; therefore, the quality of groundwater, unless grossly contaminated, is not as important as other mechanisms of transmission. Even if such a threshold exists, however, this poses the following question: Is it acceptable to ignore the poor sanitation infrastructure and the fact that people are ingesting water with contamination levels deemed unacceptable in other parts of the world?

A preliminary analysis measuring the relationship between childhood diarrhea and E. coli contamination was conducted. As behaviors including open defecation and use of broken septic latrines result in surface contamination, the risk of shallow aquifer contamination increases and in turn influences health. The limited sample of households drinking from survey wells was problematic for this analysis. Given the small sample size, however, the p value (0.1) suggests that a relationship might exist and warrants further investigation.

Results suggest that an improved sanitation infrastructure would enhance shallow tubewell water quality. For example, a coefficient of 0.015 (unsanitary latrines; Table 2) means that for every additional latrine within a 40-m radius of a well, the E. coli detection frequency increases 1.5 percent. A rapid universal shift to sanitary latrines is unlikely as poor sanitation is highly correlated with low socioeconomic status. Therefore, affordable modes of treatment or alternative drinking water sources are needed.

The ICDDR-B continues to introduce safe drinking water alternatives that might be applicable in similar regions with a compromised shallow aquifer. A community piped water system that provides an in-home water supply twice daily was installed in one Matlab village (near our study area). Community members are required to pay a fee to hire maintenance staff. This type of system can only be effective if residents can afford the fee. Rainwater harvesting bins provide safe drinking water but are often abandoned by residents because the water supply is not year-round, regular filter maintenance is required, and the cost can be prohibitive for individual rural households (Hoque et al. 2004; Jakariya et al. 2005; Howard et al. 2006). Another alternative recently tested in Matlab is a water purification mixture found to effectively purify up to 15 l of surface water at a time and reduce diarrhea (Islam et al. 2011).

It is unknown whether these practices will become widespread, but acceptance of safe water alternatives is more likely to be effective if communities are involved in choosing the technology and if significant education on installation, operation, and maintenance of the technology is provided (Hoque et al. 2004). In the meantime, results from this study can guide existing sanitation improvement efforts. Placement of latrines near tubewells is convenient but affects water quality and likely health. Therefore, sanitation improvement interventions should highlight the spatial separation of latrines and tubewells as well as latrine maintenance to limit contamination.
