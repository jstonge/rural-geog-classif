SPATIALIZING BLACK CULTURE THROUGH THE PLACEMAKING TRADITION OF THE RURAL LOUISIANA CREOLE BOUCHERIE

ALEXANDRA L. GIANCARLO

ABSTRACT. Before the advent of refrigeration and grocery stores, Louisiana’s rural Creoles—mixed-race French-heritage people who usually identify as black—supplemented their diets through self-provisioning activities such as the ritual of butchering of a hog and distributing its products amongst kin and neighbors. Called a boucherie, this activity continues in the state’s rural southwest and is deeply linked to present-day Creole identity. Collected through ethnographic and archival methods, this research argues that the boucherie gives spatial expression to Creole culture through reuniting family members, sharing intergenerational wisdom and memories, and, for some, meeting sustenance needs. I draw from research on black placemaking—conceptualized as the ways black Americans “create sites of endurance, belonging, and resistance through social interaction,” (Hunter and others 2016, 1)—ritualized commemorative practices, and spatializing culture. This study contributes to the burgeoning field of black geographies by demonstrating how boucheries serve culture-sustaining roles for black Louisianans.

Keywords: black geographies, black placemaking, cultural identity, Louisiana Creoles.

“When they come here and do (the boucherie), they say, ‘Well, we know we’re back home.’”—Goldman Thibodeaux, 12 October 2016

In rural southwestern Louisiana, the old and new coexist in startling juxtaposition. Centuries-old traditions are reconfigured and imbued with new resonance. Practices more fitting to an era before automobile transportation and refrigeration persist alongside decidedly modern pleasures. The Creole tradition of holding a boucherie, the practice of gathering family and neighbors together on a rotating basis to butcher a hog and prepare customary foods for winter sustenance, is a past-into-present tradition that pairs open-fire blackpot cooking with Facebook live-streaming.

Scholars of Louisiana have described the importance of the rotating ritual of butchering a hog and distributing its products amongst kin and neighbors (Smith and Post 1937; Maguire 1979; Gold 1979; Spitzer 1986). The boucherie, from the informal French for “butchery,” ensured that families had both fresh and cured meat and provided the ingredients for many traditional foods, such as cracklins/ gratons (fried pork skin), boudin (pork sausage with rice), and backbone stew

Though scholarship on boucheries is scant—as is the case for research on Creole culture and history more generally—it seems that their purpose as a livelihood-maintenance tactic decreased in the 1970s and shifted, for the most part, to a cultural “memory performance” (Roberts 2018, 8; Sletto 2014). I add “for the most part” because some families continue to rely on this self-provisioning in the present day.

Creoles are mixed-race people of European (usually French), Native American, and African ancestry who have lived for centuries alongside Cajuns, the white-identifying descendants of the Acadian expulsion. In this part of the state, people who identify as Creole usually also identify as black, and vice versa; if pressed further, they will often say that their race is black while their heritage or culture is Creole.1 Though Creoles are classified as black and Cajuns as white today, this is a relatively recent phenomenon (within the twentieth century) for a once highly blended Latin-based culture. “Creole” was once a term for both black-racialized and white-racialized residents and it has unusual semantic elasticity. It is a noun (“a Creole”) and an adjective (“the Creole way of life”). Accordingly—while bearing in mind that this approach will not capture the
nuances of every individual’s identity, which indeed may be context-dependent—I use “black” (or “African-American”) and “Creole” interchangeably in this paper. My use of “Cajun” (for white) and “Creole” (for black) is not done uncritically, but reflects the presently used terminology of both scholars and residents. For much more detail on these historically fluid categories and the negative effects of the “Cajunization” of the region on its black/Creole inhabitants since the 1960s, see Alexandra Giancarlo 2019 and Christophe Landry 2015.

Though French was the lingua franca for generations of Creoles (and Cajuns), fewer and fewer of today’s Creoles speak the language fluently.2 Creoles are likely to have cultural attributes such as Catholic religious faith and region-specific culinary traditions. Their music of choice is usually zydeco, which is an accordion-led synthesis of traditional Creole music called “la-la,” African-American musical traditions like R&B, blues (and, more recently, hip hop), with additional influences from Cajun music. A connection to agricultural life through rural land ownership, livestock raising, horsemanship (see Giancarlo 2016), and, more rarely today, cattle ranching, are also strong cultural identifiers.

Both Cajuns and Creoles initially held boucheries, though today this practice predominately endures in Creole families. Cajuns today own businesses that sell prepared boucherie favorites, especially boudin. They also spearhead and manage public boucherie demonstrations, like the annual Old Time Boucherie in Eunice—where the hog arrives already dead, the traditional on-site killing having proven too graphic for onlookers in recent years—and La Grande Boucherie des Cajuns in St. Martinville, also an annual festival.3 Regional and national media outlets—The Baton Rouge Advocate, The New York Times, Texas Monthly, Garden & Gun—have reported on public boucheries in recent years, presenting them as a uniquely Cajun tradition (O’Connor 2011; McNulty 2015; Vaughn 2015; Lotz 2018).4 Consequently, though evidence strongly suggests that Cajuns rarely hold family-or sustenance-based boucheries today, they are indeed the public face of the tradition, reflecting their broader cultural hegemony in the region (for more detail on the dominance of the Cajun identity, again, see Giancarlo 2019).

In what follows, I present research findings from a long-term project undertaken with Creole communities that argues that the boucherie serves a valuable placemaking role for residents and scattered kin. A livelihood-sustaining practice borne of survival today also serves to reemphasize ties of kinship and a shared cultural identity. It solidifies intergenerational family ties and affirms Creole “survival” identity based in resistance and cultural endurance. This work addresses calls for scholarship in the field of black geographies that attends to the plurality of black spatialities as well as to black senses of place (Hawthorne 2019), with a broader aim to capture joys and triumphs of black lives and communities to challenge works that reduce their subjectivities to a recitation of “social ills” (Wright 2017). Willie J. Wright encourages scholars of black geographies to examine how “Black communities survive the underside of civil society, all the while, dreaming of and
manifesting alternative spatialities in which Black social life may flourish” (2017, 2). Boucheries “make place” as socially constructed, symbolic sites, and the act of holding a boucherie spatializes black culture in a material way.

From 2014 to 2018, I conducted ethnographic research in Creole communities that produced dozens of semistructured interviews, which I transcribed verbatim and coded using NVivo software, and field notes garnered through dozens of hours of participant observation. Additionally, I consulted archival materials such as newspaper articles, community-based magazines, census data, and government reports. Together, these sources—when interpreted in conversation with scholarly perspectives and debates—contribute to a fulsome portrait of a segment of black America that has not had any sustained academic attention since the 1980s (see Maguire 1989 and Spitzer 1986). The present work draws on the voices of 29 participants, though not all are mentioned by name.

I begin with an overview of where this research intersects with existing scholarship in the field. Here I attempt to tease out the distinctions between the tightly related concepts I employ as my theoretical framework—black placemaking and spatializing cultural identity—while remarking on the broad contours that link them. I discuss rural Creole identity to set the context for the following section that examines the boucherie’s past and present as a survival practice. Then, I use the above concepts to frame an analysis of how the boucherie grounds Creole identity in the cultural landscape of rural southwestern Louisiana.

Black Placemaking and Spatializing Culture

If placemaking, in a more mainstream sense, has been conceptualized as people giving space meaning through processes like naming, territory claiming, and taking part in social relations (Lombard 2014), what makes black placemaking distinct? Written to provide a counterpoint to the overwhelmingly negative media representations of urban black life, Marcus Anthony Hunter, Mary Pattillo, Zandria F. Robinson, and Keecanga-Yamahtta Taylor foreground the playfulness and pleasure with which black Chicagoans reclaim space and transform it into place (2016). Their definition of black placemaking as “the ways that … black Americans create sites of endurance, belonging, and resistance through social interaction” (2016, 1) is highly resonant for the present analysis. Central to the framework of black placemaking is how African-Americans resist being cast as victims of history or of socioeconomic deprivation, but instead are “subjects (who) fashion places by inscribing them with their own interpretations, meanings, and cultural significance” (Hunter and others 2016, 4). Black placemaking occurs through social interactions that create loci of cultural durability through, for instance, community sports teams, protests against violence, and neighborhood reunions (Hunter and others 2016). For Zenzele Isoke’s 2011 study of black women’s political activism in Newark, New Jersey, black “homemaking”
is a political practice that comprises both the physical and affective aspects of place and creates “a living history of resistance” (2011, 118). These activists create “homeplaces” that nurture the community’s youth, emphasize their long-standing connections with the city’s physical spaces, and claim belonging by aligning themselves with the established tradition of black resistance in Newark to “create new spaces to affirm black life, black struggle, and black survival” (Isoke 2011, 127). For Rebecca Louise Carter, whose research focuses on an extended vigil at a memorial to Martin Luther King, Jr. in New Orleans that declared a rightful claim to the city’s public spaces and called attention to the crisis of urban violence, such black placemaking is grounded both in political action and in day-to-day survival “as individuals contest social and spatial structures and claim the place and space necessary for progress” (Carter 2014, 244; Haymes 1995).

The array of sites of meaningful experience for African Americans illustrates how placemaking activities vary across local and regional boundaries. As Bobby M. Wilson points out, “Social processes are not exportable commodities” (2002, 38); understanding geographically situated black experiences is key (Inwood and Yarbrough 2010). As LaToya Eaves further explains, geographers must shift away from static, one-dimensional treatments of black lives towards a perspective that, instead, “focuses on the way that black subjects undertake space-making practices within a specific set of circumstances and expand Black spatial possibilities, thereby enabling enquiry and resisting homogeneity” (2017, 81).

Studies that examine how placemaking is enacted through practices provide a springboard for considering the ritualized aspects of the boucherie. Sonjah Stanley Niaah’s (2008) treatment of black music types as performance geography in South Africa and across the African diaspora emphasizes how site-specific sonic and dance practices “give places identity through performance” (344). That these performances are situated in a specific place, yet also rest in the nexus of globally interlinked sounds, speaks to the simultaneous particularity and universality of black placemaking traditions. For Andrea R. Roberts (2018), descendants of the founders of east Texas freedom colonies employ ritual commemorative practices that “historicize and spatialize” (8) their heritage while also engaging in a broader black tradition of celebrating triumph over bondage. Often, this black placemaking involves the intergenerational transfer of traditions and family historical narratives through storytelling—what Roberts, drawing on the work of Bjorn Sletto (2014), describes as a “memory performance” (2018).

Through the boucherie, Creoles reaffirm kinship connections through homebound journeys, share intergenerational wisdom, and spatialize their cultural identity. Setha Low, Dana Taplin, and Suzanne Scheld understand spatializing culture as a multidimensional framework that includes, for instance, cultural place attachment: “cultural values (that) accrue to objects, buildings,
and landscapes through living in a place for a long period of time, working in a place, narrating stories and telling myths about a place, and engaging in any activity that would generate a relationship between a person or group and a particular location” (2009, 15). In addition to this cultural place attachment that reflects the social construction and production of space, spatializing culture incorporates discursive, embodied, emotive, affective, and translocal concepts of place and space (Low 2016). Diverse sociocultural users of Orchard Beach in New York City employed symbolic and performative practices (music, dance, and food) that imbued their park space with affective value and resonance. A seniors’ group installed pictures of deceased community members in an abandoned concession stand they called their “clubhouse.” Latino park users held salsa concerts and family picnics. Naturalists undertook daily walks and foraging. For these disparate park user groups, “these different strategies of meaning-making underscore the multiplicity of ways that (they) socially construct space and give it meaning” (Low 2016, 69). Similarly, for many of those interviewed, the boucherie imbues rural southwestern Louisiana with meaning and value through an embodied practice that lends materiality to their cultural identity.

BLACK RURAL SPACE, CULTURE, AND IDENTITY

“SOME OF US HAVE CITY WAYS, SOME DON’T.”—LLOYD MITCHELL, 17 SEPTEMBER 2016

The boucherie is one element of a web of cultural practices that comprises a primarily rural-based Creole identity. That “black America” is often conflated with urban America indeed confounds many Creoles who can much better relate to planting crops than to city life: “In a lot of ways I have more in common with a [white] Cajun from here than I would with a black kid from L.A.” (Dustin Cravins, 30 June 2014). The geographical diversity over time of African-American experiences illustrates how race-related practices and regimes of accumulation change across local, regional, and national boundaries and must take into account local history and place-specificity. Most of those I interviewed had a fierce affinity for their home in the rural South—sentiments that echo Charles Aiken’s conclusions that black Americans view the plantation region as a sort of nostalgia-tinged “remembered homeland” if not an actual homeland (2001). Luther Adams notes that despite the racial oppression and constrained life opportunities they faced, many rural blacks chose to migrate to cities within the region during the Great Migration, instead of relocating to the North, because the South was “home” and they wanted to improve conditions for African Americans in the region (2010, 1–2). Others—particularly those who fled the South—see the region and their memories of it in starkly negative terms: “I call it ‘sippi’ cause I don’t miss it” (Aiken 2001, 57). Margaret Marietta Ramirez’s respondents, who run a black food-justice organization in downtown Seattle, espoused a similar tension. They explained that it is difficult for their
organization, Clean Greens, to attract and retain volunteers because most are descended from sharecroppers who fled the restrictive racial regime of the South and consequently view agricultural labor as demeaning (Ramirez 2015).

The majority of the people with whom I conducted in-depth interviews were rural- or small town-dwellers and many pinpointed this non-urban heritage as a touchstone of their black or Creole identity. Working the land was a key point of pride and of making place. Multiple interviewees described their family background in small-scale farming and raising of livestock for sustenance if not as a profession:

In Promiseland in that era, you were sharecroppers. You would raise your own animal. My great-uncle had raised cattle. Raised cows and calves and raised pigs. Some of them would raise chickens. So back then in that era everybody would raise their own animals. They didn’t have to go to the market to buy chicken or beef or pork. They would raise it themselves. (Vincent Wiltz, 28 July 28 2014)

Many respondents also maintain family plots of land in the country, passed down through generations, despite having moved to small cities such as Eunice or Opelousas or to bigger cities such as Lafayette, indicating the enduring resonance of place. One elderly interviewee, an avid horseman, explained:

But you still have a great deal of country where people still live and are holding onto the land. It’s like where my father is living at over there on that plantation road. That’s what really got me started! Because if not for that I wouldn’t have (a) place to put a horse, you know? When we all moved from the country and came to the city, Daddy stayed in the country, so we were able to still have my horses. (Robert Phillips, 21 August 2014)

Matanda Hayes made an explicit connection between rural life and her Creole heritage: “Well, the Creole part is our legend, legacy. … [It] is our everyday—get up, cook, cut grass, check on the horses. … Whatever, spend time in the country, because, like I said, my momma is from Mallet [a historically Creole enclave just outside of Opelousas], so we (are) always in the country” (19 June 2014). Maggie Fontenot used her status as a rural-dweller to describe her identity, saying, “I’m just an old-fashioned country girl” (30 June 2014). Her husband, who grew up on land his father farmed and who spends his weekends trail-riding and shoeing horses, echoed these sentiments: “I’m just a country boy. I’m country” (Joseph Fontenot, 8 July 2014). Similarly, Neil Bob exuberantly described that “(My) family comes from farming and raising their own food and everything, so I am a country boy just living it up!” (11 June 2015). Raising and growing what they eat—ensuring their own and their communities’ survival—is seen as historically and quintessentially Creole; Wayne Singleton explained “When you come up in a real Creole family, you are used to working!” (28 October 2016). For many Creoles, holding a boucherie is a touchstone cultural practice that affirms their connection to their distinct identity and to rural life in southwestern Louisiana.
AN ACT OF SURVIVAL

“IT WAS AMAZING, YOU KNOW, HOW WE LIVED OFF THE LAND”—SHIRLEY BUSBY,
17 SEPTEMBER 2016

Creole communities’ cultural richness, like in so many other black American communities, has not translated into material wealth, and recent statistics indicate the entrenchment of rural poverty in Louisiana. In fact, multiple parishes (counties) of the southwestern Louisiana Creole and Cajun cultural region, known officially as Acadiana, qualify as “persistent poverty parishes” in which residents have experienced poverty rates in excess of 20 percent in every decade since the 1970s (Demographic and Economic Profile: Louisiana 2006). As Figure 3 demonstrates, poverty in Louisiana is highly racialized; the data from parishes in which most participants live (Acadia, St. Landry, Lafayette, and St. Martin) show that between 28 and 46 percent of the black population lives in poverty (U.S. Census Bureau, 2012–2016). Consequently, in addition to its cultural purposes, some Creoles continue to employ the boucherie in order to cope with limited opportunities, high unemployment, and persistent poverty.

Self-provisioning as a response to poverty and food insecurity within black communities is a well-documented practice. In the face of decreased agricultural production and increased integration into the industrial economy, rural Creoles, “like their distant kin living in plantation and neoplantation societies elsewhere, have had to develop community and individual strategies for survival” (Maguire 1989, 375). Robert Maguire conducted years-long fieldwork with Creoles in St. Martin Parish in the late 1980s. He highlights how “From that land they counted on the food raised in household gardens, pigsties and chicken coops, and fields for household use, a means to raise cash, and simply for survival, particularly over lean months. ... The land was the sole source of their bounty” (Maguire 1989, 397). At the end of the agricultural season, friends, family, and neighbors participated in coup de main (“helping hand”), harvesting sweet potatoes or other crops. In my fieldwork with older Creoles from this area at a meeting of La Table Creole, a Creole language and culture preservation group that meets monthly, older respondents recalled how common—and necessary—boucheries had been for much of their lives. Soap was even made from the hog’s head: “They say everything on the hog is good except for the eyes” (Field notes, 29 October 2016).

For black communities in Louisiana and elsewhere, this self-provisioning of food through land possession and farming have long-term associations with autonomy, dating back to plots of lands at times given to enslaved peoples (Woods 1998; Heynen 2009; Ramírez 2015). Indeed, one of civil rights activist Fannie Lou Hamer’s post-Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee (SNCC) projects was a cooperative Freedom Farm in Mississippi, founded in 1969 (Alderman and Caspersen 2018).
The prevalence of social and kin support networks is notable within African-American communities, providing both material and cultural support (Stack 1974a, 1974b; Zekeri 2006). For poor and/or racialized rural dwellers, research has shown that informal survival strategies secure the maintenance of living standards and help households to withstand short-term crises and long-term shocks (Meert 2000; Kleiner and Green 2008). Black culture, Stephen Nathan Haymes argues, indelibly shapes black survival networks through “bonds of trust, friendship, and solidarity” (1995, 12) like those undergirding boucheries. Mona Gobert-Cravins elucidated this web of culture and survival in her recollections of the boucheries growing up decades ago: “It was to help with labor, to share in the abundance, and it was an opportunity to visit. … So all three of those things” (22 October 2016).

Black scholars such as Eaves, Isoke, Katherine McKittrick, and Sylvia Wynter attend to the social and cultural practices of everyday life under conditions—historic and present—of oppression. The plantation, for instance, was “mapped onto the lands of no one,” prompting associations of blackness with dispossession and dehumanization (McKittrick 2013, 8). Yet these were the very locations where enslaved peoples “spatialize(d) acts of survival,” (McKittrick 2013, 2) creating what Wynter has referred to as “secretive histories”: cultural practices, food, and narratives that challenged systemic violence (McKittrick 2013, 10; Wynter 1971). The boucherie, which creatively used the least desirable cuts of meat to feed the most people possible, is one such example of “secretive history” enacted in place.

Historically, the boucherie functioned as a livelihood strategy in which families and neighbors depended on each other’s labor and farm feed for survival. Some interviewees asserted that its origins were in slavery, during which enslaved people had to butcher pigs and other animals for the master and his family who received the preferred cuts of meat (Jevon Hawkins, 22 October 2016). The enslaved people then repurposed the least desirable parts of the pig into some of the foods recognized today as boucherie mainstays, hence the refrain “everything on the hog is good.” Lloyd Mitchell described that “(The slaves) would just get the pig feet and the guts and the head and all of that and the pig tail. Back then, it was called trash meat. That’s what they would give the slaves—the guts, which (are called) chitlins now. … But now, at the store it’s like eight dollars a pound. It’s a delicacy” (17 September 2016). Demetric Thomas offered a similar assessment:

The slaves would actually kill the animals and harvest them for the white man, and they would take all the meat and they would give all the insides and the bones to the slaves. And little [did they know], that was the best part that they got rid of! You couldn’t make the brown gravy if you didn’t have the bones. (20 September 2016)

Both men, separately, ended their comments in a similar vein by emphasizing how their ancestors’ “acts of survival,” to draw upon McKittrick, invoked a deep,
even secret knowledge—in this case, of the desirable properties of the castoff animal products—thereby creating sites of resistance and cultural meaning. These interviewees emphasized their ancestors’ humanity under conditions of bondage, similar to the retelling of enslaved ancestors’ ingenuity at an annual Texas freedom colonies’ family reunion (Roberts 2018).

Presently, for some families the boucherie’s purpose has remained unchanged. One interviewee did not mince words when he described why he continues the tradition: “We gotta eat. That’s how we survive” (Shane Boagni, 13 June 2015), echoing Maguire’s decades-old conclusions from his work with rural Creoles (1989). Another interviewee described that continuing to “make boucherie” was indelibly linked to his cultural identity but—in a very practical way—also a matter of livelihood maintenance. He explained that they are “the Creole way of life. That’s your survival. What to eat, you know?” (Demetric Thomas, 20 September 2016). For Demetric, these practical skills are also insurance against future uncertainty: “If we go in a recession and you can’t [afford to] buy meat in a store, you’ve got to come see me!” (20 September 2016). A similar motivation drove Robert St. Andre. His backyard pigpen, dedicated trailer space, and industrial-sized freezer full of neatly packaged cuts of pork demonstrated the boucherie’s importance to him and his family. Lest it seem like a quaint pastime, Robert reminded me: “That’s to feed us! We get out there and butcher up” every other week throughout the winter (30 September 2016).

THE BOUCHERIE AND BLACK PLACEMAKING

“LET US ALL COME TOGETHER”—JEVON HAWKINS, 22 OCTOBER 2016

To boucherie is to engage in a form of placemaking that solidifies the kinship connections given spatial expression in southwestern Louisiana. One interviewee described the pleasure of the day: “It’s a day of excitement. You’re ready for that day. You’re going to have fun. You know you’re going to be with family, friends, drinking, outside … ” (Lloyd Mitchell, 17 September 2016). Another interviewee concurred, explaining that in his family

by the time it came around to me as a child [in the 1970s], boucheries were not for survival anymore, it was more for entertainment. … They would kill hogs whether or not [they needed] to stock up on meat, but just to bring the family together and have one day of being together doing something we all loved, and that’s, really, in my opinion, the only reason they did it. (“Boobie,” 8 September 2016)

It is in this way that the placemaking articulated by Hunter and others (2016), as imbuing spaces with cultural significance, is enacted or performed, per the ritualized meaning-making processes that spatialize and historicize their identities. Hunter and others provide the example of annual gatherings held by former residents of public housing complexes and explain how “the former residents pull back together and perform important acts of placemaking through reunions
and other forms of (virtual and in-person) collective gathering” (2016, 10). They meet in a meaningful and memory-rich site: a public park amid their semi-demolished former homes.

The boucherie becomes an embodied ritual that adheres to gender, age, and birth order-determined tasks. Women clean the hog intestines to use as casings for sausage and boudin, and prepare the lunch for all in attendance. The men, by and large, bear responsibility for the “heaviest work” (Goldman Thibodeaux, 12 October 2016) of shooting or stabbing the pig, scalding it with boiling water and scraping its hair off, followed by careful removal of the organs for processing. These site-specific rituals, or performances, infuse identity into place (Stanley Niaah 2008) and the intricacies inherent to learning the butchering process lend an intergenerational intimacy to this placemaking practice. The ritualized butchering procedure, interviewees remarked, was often passed down from grandfather to father, father to son, and so on. Demetric Thomas teaches his sons as his father taught him and noted, “My grandfather and them (were) raised like that, so he raised his kids like that, his kids raised us like that, and I’m going to raise my kids like that” (20 September 2016).

This placemaking practice not only reinforces bonds of kinship and establishes a sense of belonging, but also is a chance for elders to communicate important family stories within an ancestral landscape. The “repetitious bodily practices” (Roberts 2018; see also Connerton 1989) and storytelling facilitate memory transfer to the younger generations. Now in his late eighties, Goldman Thibodeaux expressed pleasure in knowing the tradition will continue: “I know it will stay. And we are passing it down to our two sons. They know what it is. They know how to butcher a hog” (12 October 2016). In Karl Arthurlee’s family, it is considered a sign of respect to defer to the older siblings’ knowledge gleaned from the generation gone before, though he expressed hope that “maybe one day I’ll get to lead. ... You never know. You never know” (28 October 2016). This storytelling is memory work that mirrors the actions of Roberts’ freedom colony descendants, for whom sharing family and community histories is a key form of placemaking that solidifies claims to their ancestral land and allows them to derive continuing strength from resistance narratives (2018).

For Creoles to continue this “performativity of ethnic affiliation” (Bell 1999, 5; Fortier 1999) is a way of honoring the sacrifices of their ancestors (“M.L,” 22 October 2016; Norman Jones, 22 October 2016), which is indeed a source of deep gratification: “It makes me proud to know that I know my dad’s smiling inside knowing that ‘Hey, I taught him that’” (Jevon Hawkins, 22 October 2016). Creole boucherie participants expect that narratives of their ancestors’ survival will be invoked either implicitly or explicitly; similarly, memory and paying homage to deceased community members are integral parts of the black reunions that Hunter and others (2016) profile. Low explains that “memory
and memory-making (are) a dominant mode of inscribing meaning at various scales,” like elderly park users’ memorialization of lost friends at the abandoned concession stand repurposed as their clubhouse (2016, 76). “Reminiscing,” Karl Arthurlee confirmed, “is a big part of it” (28 October 2016). Jevon Hawkins concurred: “I’ve heard some of the best stories about my family roots at a (boucherie). It’s a time of family and friends and fellowship, you know. It’s unexplainable” (22 October 2016). Rebecca Henry, folklorist and founder of the Creole Heritage Folklife Center in Opelousas, described in her “Creole Traditions” column in Creole Magazine that “great stories were told around the butchering table. Who grew the best crops and gardens as well as war and hoodoo (voodoo) tales” (Henry 1991, 22). The space of the boucherie creates these intergenerational black places, contra analyses that suggest African-Americans are placeless—unmoored and without claim to a legitimate homeland—or, conversely, overdetermined by place, such as being victimized by segregation (McKittrick 2011; Roberts 2018; Hawthorne 2019).

The boucherie serves as a vehicle for cultural continuity and for reuniting distant—even previously unknown—relatives. When two men realized that they were related through a second cousin, one turned to me at a boucherie in the fall of 2016 and said, “See? This is the real meaning of this” (Field notes, 22 October 2016). Respondents discussed relatives from the Creole diaspora in California; one respondent described an uncle’s annual pilgrimage to his ancestral home:

(T)he meat is not the same as buying it out of the grocery store over there [in California]. … So, he comes down here and we buy a hog and he kills it—me, him, and one of my other uncles. We kill a hog by ourselves. Make some sausage, boudin, and cracklin. Send him back with the sausage. Every year. (Demetric Thomas, 20 September 2016)

Thanksgiving, Christmas, and New Year’s Eve were identified as important dates on the Creole cultural calendar for those who hosted or participated in boucheries. Members of the sizable Creole diaspora in Texas, California, and elsewhere return to the land of their ancestors. Jevon, whose family is Creole and from the region but who spent most of his adult life in Texas, intimated that moving to Louisiana truly felt like moving home. The boucherie played no small role in this feeling of homecoming: “Let us all come together. This is a party. (The boucherie) is a celebration for me now. And it’s crazy because when I was younger, what I thought was just work that I would run from, now I run to” (Jevon Hawkins, 22 October 2016). One respondent shared that his family preferred hosting a boucherie-cum-family reunion instead of getting together for Christmas because, unlike holidays that often see family scattered visiting in-laws, at the boucherie “you can pretty much guarantee you are going to see all of your family” (“M.L.,” 22 October 2016). “Benny” (13 September 2016) further articulated how boucheries serve a reuniting
function, noting that “some of the family, when they’d get married, they’d move to the city, but they’d always come back for the boucherie.” It is through these homeward journeys that Creoles’ cultural identity is given a material dimension. Similar to the annual homecomings of former public housing residents in Chicago, boucheries are a pleasurable, even joyful, reunion in a “meaningful gathering place” (Hunter and others 2016, 10). Roberts’ interviewees engaged in storytelling performances at a similarly consequential location, a natural spring that served as the reunion point for their ancestors who fled slavery to establish freedom colonies (2018).

In many of the interviewees’ narratives, the land itself has a special, almost sacred, dimension that evokes nostalgia, resiliency, and continuity, perhaps reflecting the historical importance of black land ownership for autonomy. “Boobie” connected present-day cultural practices like the boucherie to the land where his family and preceding generations have lived, explaining that “every time I step out [my door], I step onto my DNA” (8 September 2015). That the land was severed and parcels temporarily lost only seemed to heighten its significance: “One of my dad’s sisters bought an acre and I did, [and] my dad’s second cousin bought all of the front, so we bought everything [back] eventually so no one else could move in. It will always be for family” (“Boobie,” 8 September 2015). Karl Arthurlee, who could host the boucherie at his home within the city of Opelousas as he does for other family gatherings, instead holds it with other family members on their family’s rural property. As he described, “Everybody meets out on the forty,” referring to a long-held family parcel of land outside of the city (28 October 2016).

Again, the land itself—the space made place by enacting this culturally significant ritual—takes center stage and speaks back to the ‘presumed placelessness’ of African-American lives (Roberts 2018, 4). Demetric Thomas similarly emphasized the physical location where boucheries had been held growing up, even detailing the sites of the various hog-processing activities: “Every now and then I’ll go back to the country, go back to my grandparents’ house and just reminisce on it. Or bring my kids and say, ‘Man, see that spot right there? That’s where we had a pile of wood with the fire’” (20 September 2016). This “spot,” for Thomas, is intertwined with the memories of his own past and the messages that he intends to pass on to his children, including self-provisioning for survival. Comparable to the black vigil keepers in New Orleans who emphasized the importance of holding their demonstration at the Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. memorial, for boucherie participants, holding boucheries at meaningful sites reminds them of “what we as a people are able to do” (Carter 2014, 253). In this manner, boucheries are also similar to the homeplaces black women in Newark create, which involve “making space in the community for folks to remember and re-envision earlier modes of black freedom struggle” (Carter 2014, 118).
CLOSING REMARKS ON BLACK PLACEMAKING AND IDENTITY

“I WOULDN’T TRADE (MY CULTURE) FOR A MILLION DOLLARS.”—DEMETRIC THOMAS,
20 SEPTEMBER 2016

In this paper, I have argued that through ritualized food practices community members imbue their cultural homeland, southwestern Louisiana, with continued meaning and resonance. For “Ed,” who can no longer hold boucheries due to his declining health, the boucherie was a truly cherished ritual: “Oh, I miss that. Oh yeah, I miss that. For me, it wasn’t hard. I got used to it so it wasn’t hard. I had loved it” (19 October 2016). The memory of and attachment to their cultural home serves as a “symbolic anchor” (Gupta and Ferguson 1992) for scattered contemporary Creoles, for whom the memory and symbolic importance of the boucherie often supersedes the event’s practical food-provisioning purpose. Although many Creoles no longer need to hold boucheries for purely sustenance reasons, the significance of the practice for identity, family reunification, and intergenerational cultural sharing remains clear. The ritual of the boucherie is not only constitutive of home, it makes home. By giving the rural spaces of southwestern Louisiana meaning through their actions and stories, Creoles spatialize their cultural identity (Low 2016) and make place.

I have drawn on the groundbreaking work of black geographers to examine black experiences that exist outside of mainstream or “typical” associations between African-Americans and space—to complicate, as Eaves has expressed, “Black subjects and Black space … as fulfilling normative expectations about Black communities and individuals” (2017, 84). McKittrick and Woods have encouraged movement away from the essentialization of race and space towards an appreciation of how diverse human geographies are crucial parts of the lived realities of black people (2007, 6–7) and the present paper has indicated the importance of shifting scholarly engagement from monolithic and static characterizations of black identity in order to “(promote) the humanness of place” (Eaves 2017, 86). Roberts’ work on black freedom colonies’ commemorative events provided a key framework for expansive black geographic possibilities; her interviewees’ memory performances emphasized familial bonds, legitimated black histories in place, and provided an opportunity to impart wisdom to the next generation (2018). Indeed, the promise of black geographies scholarship is in its “refusing to equate Blackness … with racism, oppression, and dehumanization, [thereby opening] up possibilities for alternative, anticolonial, and liberatory forms of geographic knowledge and world-making” (Hawthorne 2019, 9; McKittrick 2013).

One respondent who had explicitly connected conducting boucheries to his understanding of Creole survivalism described that “I fussed and complained when I was younger, but now that I’m grown and I look back, I say, boy, I’m so glad. I wouldn’t trade (my culture) for a million dollars” (Demetric Thomas,
20 September 2016). Another, in reflecting on continuing the practice in the absence of the need for meat for sustenance purposes, explained, “It’s hard work, and the reward is just to see the smile on everybody’s—the older ones’—faces ... that we did it!” (Karl Arthurlee, 28 October 2016). Among all interviewed, there was one constant: a desire to see the tradition continue. “Benny,” who no longer holds boucheries, expressed surprise and pleasure when his son—a pharmacist who lives in the city—started planning annual boucheries as part of his birthday celebrations, representing a revival of the tradition in their family. “Benny” explained:

(My son) decided five or six years ago; he said, “Daddy, guess what I am going to do for my birthday!? I am going to have a boucherie!” I said, “What brought that on?” and he said, “Remember when I was a kid,” because he is now forty, forty-one, and he said, “I remember that. That was fun back then. So now I want to do it again.” (13 September 2016)

Karl Arthurlee captures the boucherie’s importance to his family by explaining that “We might miss a year, we might miss it two years, but that year we have it, it’s right back to the same thing again. Tradition” (28 October 2016). This tradition sustains not only his family’s cultural ties and sense of identity, but those of many black extended families across southwestern Louisiana and beyond. Black placemaking is distinct because it has its roots in the “secretive histories” (Wynter 1971) of slavery that are transmuted into today’s rituals of rootedness, belonging, and collective gathering that, for Creoles, importantly take place in their cultural homeland. The boucherie calls relatives back to their roots for a generations-old placemaking practice that grounds Creole culture in the rural southwestern Louisiana landscape for the twenty-first century and beyond.
