RURALITY AS A KEY FACTOR FOR PLACE ATTACHMENT IN THE GREAT PLAINS

ANDREW HUSA and CHERYL E. MORSE

ABSTRACT. Results from an online survey of the mobility of people who grew up in Nebraska indicate that rural stayers, defined as those Nebraskans who have had some experience living in communities with fewer than 2,500 residents, express stronger place-based attachments than others. Key components of this attachment include general contentment living in Nebraska, connection to family, a desire to raise children in Nebraska, appreciation for the state’s culture and hospitality, and for some, a fondness for the state’s natural environments. The research draws on quantitative evidence and the personal stories shared by survey respondents. The findings from the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey are provocative in their suggestion that those who grow up in America’s rural areas may develop a different set of values and social and place-based attachments than their urban peers. Keywords: rurality, stayers, place attachment, Great Plains, Nebraska.

Nebraska occupies an empty space in the American geographical imagination. The entire Great Plains region may appear as “placeless” or even a “nonplace” to those who fly over or drive through it as quickly as possible to get from one coast to another (Cresswell 2015). Yet, research with people who have grown up and live in Nebraska now suggests that its rural communities are rich with social interaction and places to which residents are deeply attached. It is a place that many people have been happy to make their home. Drawing on results from an online survey of the mobility of people who grew up in Nebraska, this paper asserts that those who have chosen to remain in the state and who have experienced living in or around rural towns, defined as communities of under 2,500 people, are even more content to stay in Nebraska than those from urban communities. The findings from the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey are provocative in their suggestion that those who grow up in America’s rural areas may develop a different set of values and social and place-based attachments than urban residents.

LITERATURE REVIEW

IMMOBILITY IN MIGRATION RESEARCH

Research on residential mobility has often focused on why people leave a place, rather than why they choose to stay. This is especially true regarding migration research on the Great Plains, where rural depopulation and the region have been seemingly synonymous with each other since the early twentieth century (Loveridge, Yi, and Bokemeier 2009; Gilbreath 2012; Jacquet, Guthrie, and
Jackson 2016). Subsequently, there is little literature on “stayers,” the people who have chosen to stay in this rural landscape.

Stayers are defined as people who are born and raised in an area and have never left (Stockdale and Haartsen 2018). A stayer is someone who has made the decision to remain in a place. As many rural-migration researchers have shown, the decision to move or migrate is emotional, complex, and situational, resting on a diverse set of temporal, environmental, economic, personal, and social conditions (Ni Laoire 2000; Halfacree 2006; Corbett 2013). Until recently, less attention has been given to the conditions and decision-making processes that lead rural people to stay in their home places.

Staying has often been characterized as being “stuck,” as an inability to move. Jorgen Carling’s research (2002), for example, uncovered “involuntary immobility” among Cape Verdeans who wished to migrate but were unable to do so. Yet, new research with stayers has revealed other experiences of staying. For example, Lance Erickson and colleagues’ (2018) study of stayers in Montana found that rather than being stuck, some rural stayers make a deliberate decision to stay where they are, and in so doing, contribute to small town resilience and quality of rural life. This work is one example of the ways in which migration researchers are beginning to consider immobility from multiple vantage points. In 2018, Aileen Stockdale and Tialda Haartsen called for the adoption of an immobility perspective in migration research. This perspective considers stayers as active participants in residential decisions and staying as an active process. Similarly, Cheryl Morse and Jill Mudgett (2018) have argued for the importance of examining immobility as a choice. Their research unpacked varied reasons why people choose to stay, highlighting the different forms of mobility that stayers exercise in their immobility. This paper aims to contribute to this growing body of work that elicits the nuanced and complex factors that contribute to immobility.

PLACE ATTACHMENT IN RURAL-MIGRATION RESEARCH

Nearly a half-century ago, Yi-Fu Tuan asserted that “Place is a center of meaning constructed by experience” (1975, 15). He argued that we know places, especially places like the home and neighborhood, through our experiential and sensory engagement with them, and such engagement produces meaning in people’s daily lives. Tuan grappled with definitions of “rootedness” and “sense of place,” suggesting that rootedness describes those who have remained in place and lack an ability to reflect on their place, whereas those of a certain mobility status can develop a broader sense of place (1980). In recent years, geographers have focused on the embodied dimension of place, and even mobile experiences of place, in a variety of settings and modes (see Cresswell 2015, for a review). Given the decades-long history of investigations into the meaning of place, it is
surprising that the role of place-based experience and emotional attachment to particular geographies has not been a central concern in migration studies.

Place attachment has only recently entered into the mix of factors that migration researchers consider as they analyze factors for staying in rural places. This mix of factors includes the physical and social characteristics of place alongside the roles of family, friends, and community in developing a sense of home, belonging, and rootedness (Stockdale and Haartsen 2018; Stockdale, Theunissen, and Haartsen 2018). Family ties, especially, have been linked to place attachment to, or rootedness in, a community (Barcus and Brunn 2009; Clark, Duque-Calvache, and Palomares-Linares 2015). The broader network of social ties with communities of small population size may also enhance place attachment. In her examination of the strength of rural place attachments, Maria Lewicka (2005) found that residents of villages reported significantly higher place attachment, had stronger neighborhood ties, and expressed more interest in their roots compared to residents of bigger communities. Research on rural and urban dwellers suggests that people who live in the country are more attached to their homes and local areas than people in urban areas (Anton and Lawrence 2014; Mandal 2016). This supports Lewicka’s (2005) finding that place attachment was related to community size and that one would expect people in rural communities to be more attached to their hometowns.

Holly Barcus’ scholarship has shown that in places as culturally different as Appalachia and Mongolia, people who have grown up in rural areas and moved away retain their attachment to their home places (Barcus and Brunn 2009, 2010; Barcus and Werner 2017). Attachments to the rural community are an important source of legitimization and identity construction (Pedersen 2018). This suggests a rural childhood may construct a much different place-based identity and set of values about place than a nonrural childhood. Research with people who have out-migrated from Vermont showed that they retain a place-based rural identity even decades after leaving, and that this connection has endured for centuries (Morse and Mudgett 2017).

Similarly, research in rural communities of the Great Plains indicates that strong attachment to place informs the identities of those who have spent their lives in the region (Smith and McAlister 2015). Specifically, William Wetherholt (2016) found that rural Great Plains residents who live in small towns expressed strong community attachments, a sense of pride in the community, and a feeling of acceptance. New insights on the strong influence of place attachment on staying in Vermont prompted Cheryl Morse and Jill Mudgett (2018) to call for detailed examinations of place attachment across the urban-rural gradient. While there have been studies related to differences in values between rural and urban dwellers as middle-aged adults (Kasarda and Janowitz 1974; Erickson, Sanders, and Cope 2018) and senior citizens (Usha and Lalitha 2016; Anderson, Larkins, Beaney, and Ray 2018; Pereira, Fonseca, Lies, and Kang 2019), there has been
little research into how growing up in a rural versus an urban environment has influenced different values.

The research at the center of this paper situates itself within new studies of differences in place attachment between rural and urban residents as it attempts to address questions about place attachment in the decision to stay in the rural Great Plains, and specifically, Nebraska.

Case Background

The state of Nebraska lies in the heart of the Great Plains. Although Nebraska is the 16th largest state by land area, it was only the 38th largest in population at the 2010 census. The state’s physical landscape is divided between rolling hills and ranches in the west and flat (see Figure 1) and fertile land in the east. Several small communities dot the state’s vast rural landscape (see Figure 2). While 94 percent of Nebraska’s land is defined as rural or agricultural, only approximately 27 percent of the state’s population call those areas home. The other 73 percent can be found in the state’s urban communities, especially in its largest city, Omaha (pop. 408,958), and second largest and state capital, Lincoln (pop. 258,379) (see Figure 3). These are the only two cities in the state with populations that exceed 100,000 people. Outside of the state’s ten largest communities, the rest have populations of under 20,000 people (Archer and others 2017). METHODS

The Nebraska Roots Migration Survey builds upon an online residential decision-making survey that was first developed in 2014 (Morse 2015). The Vermont Roots Migration Survey was distributed on the social media networks of five individuals of differing ages who had attended a public high school while residing in Vermont. The aim of that survey, and the aim of the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey, was to ask people who had spent at least some portion of their adolescence in a specific state to explain in their own words why they chose to stay in, leave, or return to their home state. In addition to collecting narratives, the surveys gathered demographic data and offered a multiple-choice based list of factors for staying, leaving, or returning. For example, those people who identified as “stayers” were provided a list of reasons for staying that included choices such as “my work is in the state,” “I would like to leave but I have not had the opportunity,” “I appreciate the state’s landscape (natural environments),” and “I have stayed in the state to live near family.” A write-in box for “other” was offered. Respondents were free to select as many factors that fit their experience as they liked.
The unexpectedly high response rate to the 2014 Vermont survey—3,692 fully complete responses in a three week period (see Morse 2015; Morse and Mudgett 2018)—indicated that using social media networks of a targeted population was an effective method for efficiently generating a large data set that yielded both qualitative and quantitative data about mobility and immobility decisions. Although the Vermont survey was not intended to, and did not ultimately capture, a representative sample of the Vermont population, the high number of responses allowed for between-group comparisons by gender, educational attainment, and other areas of difference.

The Nebraska Roots Migration Survey used the original set of questions in the Vermont survey and included several additional questions, such as income level, religious affiliation, and military service. While the Vermont survey asked respondents to name the town they resided in while attending high school, the Nebraska survey went one step further by asking respondents to describe their hometowns in their own words. Hometown characteristics became a focus of the analysis of the resulting Nebraska data (Husa 2020), although they were not the focus of this paper.

The Nebraska Roots Migration Survey questions were edited from their original Vermont versions, formatted, and then uploaded onto Lime Survey (cloud-based software) to distribute the survey. The research was reviewed and received approval from the University of Nebraska–Lincoln’s institutional review board. The first two questions of the survey asked whether the respondent had attended some high school in Nebraska and if they were 19 years of age and older. It the answer to either was “no,” the respondent was exited from the survey. Four people who had grown up in Nebraska, two men and two women, ranging in age from late twenties to early forties, and who had attended high schools in different regions of the state posted the web link to the survey on their social media sites, primarily Facebook pages. The goal was to tap into the social networks of people of different age groups and geographic origins within the state. They invited others to pass on the link to others who had attended at least some high school in Nebraska. The survey was anonymous, but those who wished to be contacted for further research were invited to give their contact information at the end of the survey. The survey ran for three weeks in February and March 2019.

The Nebraska Roots Migration Survey gathered 1,827 fully completed responses. Each of the state’s 93 counties was represented in the data set. The survey responses do not constitute a representative sample of Nebraska’s population. A higher percentage of women responded to the survey (61.2 percent) than the percentage of women in Nebraska’s overall population (50.2 percent, US Census 2013–2017 American Community Survey). A higher percentage of survey respondents self-identified as “white” (93.1 percent) compared to the state estimate of persons who self-identified as “white” (87.8 percent). While
24.7 percent of the survey respondents were aged 25–34, this age group only made up 13.4 percent of the state’s population in the 2013–2017 American Community Survey. Percentages among income brackets were similar between survey respondents and the state estimate. Education percentages were much different, with the percentage of survey respondents who obtained their bachelor’s degree nearly 15 percent higher than the percentage reported by the American Community Survey (2013–2017). As the survey did not aim to be representative of the state’s population and because we conducted comparative analyses between groups, the influence of these imbalances on our findings is minimal. As we discuss in the findings, stayer survey respondents expressed favorable attitudes towards living in Nebraska. As respondents were encouraged to pass the survey link on to others, it is possible that respondents extended the survey to individuals who might give favorable responses. Or, it could be that people who were more content with their residential decisions chose to take the survey. These are some of the limitations that are inherent to open access surveys.

The quantitative data were analyzed using the SPSS (version 25) statistical analysis program (IBM Corp. SPSS 2017). Between-group comparisons of migration factors were made using a cross-tabulation analysis; chi-square test results with a p-value of less than or equal to 0.05 were considered significant. Qualitative data, which included respondents’ narratives, were coded thematically using the Dedoose software package. The text was first analyzed for themes developed from the statistical analysis. In the second round of coding, nuances related to the original themes and emergent codes were identified. The analysis therefore was driven by the findings from the quantitative analysis and the analysis of narratives was conducted to bring a “biographical approach” to understandings of how complex residential and mobility decisions are made by individuals within the context of their particular circumstances (Halfacree and Boyle 1993; Halfacree and Merriman 2015; Morse and Mudgett 2018).

DEFINITIONS

The United States Census Bureau does not specifically define “rural”; instead a rural place is defined as an absence of the urban (United States Census Bureau 2020). “Rural” encompasses all population, housing, and territory not included in an urban area that is defined as a having at least 2,500 people (United States Census Bureau 2020). Census categories do not always adequately describe the lived experience of a place. As Michael Woods (2011) has explained, rural is an elusive concept; around the world, demographers, geographers, and government leaders struggle to develop definitions of rurality that are both uniform and conform to personal experience of living in a place. Rural is relative. For the person living on a farm many miles from a town of 10,000, that town may feel urban, and to the urbanite, a trip to the suburbs may feel like a trip to the
countryside. How rurality is experienced as an identity, a place, and a representation also varies person to person and culturally (Halfacree 2006). For the purposes of clarity, we adopt the U.S. Census definition of rural in this analysis.

In our analysis, survey respondents from towns of 2,500 or more people are classified as urban while those from towns of under 2,500 are classified as rural. As of 2010, Nebraska’s population of 1,826,341 was comprised of 490,655 (26.9 percent) rural and 1,335,686 (73.1 percent) urban inhabitants. The state’s population has experienced slow growth in the last decade. The U.S. Census’ 2013–2017 American Community Survey estimated Nebraska’s population as 1,893,921, with most of that growth taking place in metropolitan counties (Olberding 2019).

For the purposes of this paper, we applied the U.S. Census’ definitions to code all communities with fewer than 2,500 residents as rural and those with 2,500 or more residents as urban to allow for between-group comparisons of those who grew up and/or presently live in (or near) rural communities with respondents who did not or do not. We applied the 2013–2017 American Community Survey’s population estimates in order to use the most current data. Those survey respondents who confirmed that they had never lived outside of Nebraska, apart from schooling or military service, for a full year or more are referred to as “stayers.” “Leavers” are survey respondents who have moved outside of the state and have not returned to reside in Nebraska. Those who have lived outside of Nebraska for at least one year and then returned to live in Nebraska are referred to as “returnees.”

This paper focuses on experiences and place attachments of the Nebraska stayers who responded to the survey. Keen to consider the ways in which rural residential experience might relate to reasons for staying in Nebraska, and to place attachments specifically, we developed three different means to track possible rural/urban difference. First, we coded the hometown of each respondent as rural or urban. This allowed us to discern any effects that growing up in a small community may have on residential choices. We refer to participants as “rural hometowners” and “urban hometowners.” We also coded the present community in which each stayer lives as urban and rural, allowing for a rural/urban comparison of the settings in which participants have chosen to reside. These groups are called “rural residents” and “urban residents.” Finally, we identified those people who both grew up in a rural hometown and live now in a rural town (not necessarily the same community). We refer to these people as “all-rural stayers.” Respondents who have never lived in a rural town, those who lived in a rural hometown but now live in an urban place, and those who grew up in an urban town or city but now live in a rural town are referred to as “not all-rural stayers.”

Findings

The Nebraska Roots Migration Survey received a total of 1,827 fully completed responses. Of these, 56.0 percent (1,024) were from stayers. About half of the stayer respondents (56 percent, n=517) grew up in a rural hometown of fewer than 2,500; a third of respondents (33.3 percent, n=340) lived in a rural town at the time of the survey. Two-hundred and sixty-five respondents (25.9 percent of all stayers) are all-rural stayers who both grew up in and now live in a community of fewer than 2,500 people. Table 1 shows the overall results of reasons for staying in Nebraska. Similar to the Vermont Roots Migration Survey results, hometown stayers showed a high level of agreement about why they stayed and expressed contentment about living in their home state (Morse and Mudgett 2018).

The large majority of Nebraskan stayers reported that they enjoy living in the state (73 percent) and that a desire to live near their family was a key reason for staying. Other reasons for staying that were true for more than half of the stayers were an appreciation for the state’s culture and community (54.2 percent) and appreciation for Nebraska’s rural hospitality (53.2 percent). Nearly half (47.9 percent) of stayers said they wanted to raise their children in Nebraska. Having work or a job in Nebraska was a reason for staying for 42.6 percent of respondents, and an appreciation for Nebraska’s landscape or natural environment was cited as true by 44.3 percent of respondents. In the analysis section of this paper, we flesh out these findings thematically, adding in the diverse, emotionally rich, nuanced, and often place-based narratives about staying that Nebraskan stayers shared with us.

Rurality appears to be an important factor in both staying and place attachment among survey respondents. Respondents who grew up in a rural hometown were more likely than urban homeowners to stay in Nebraska (61.9 percent as compared to 51.1 percent). Digging deeper into the quantitative analysis of reasons for staying, we conducted cross-tabulation comparisons of rural to urban homeowners, rural to urban residents, and all-rural to not all-rural stayers (Table 2). We found strong correlations between rurality and specific values and social and environmental perceptions. To generalize, it appears that respondents who have lived in small towns have stronger place-based attachments to Nebraska than those who have not. The desire to raise children in Nebraska as a factor for staying offers one example. Of all stayers, 47.9 percent indicated that a desire to raise their children in Nebraska was a reason for them to stay. However, those who grew up in rural hometown were much more likely to list this as a factor than urban homeowners (56.1 percent vs 39.4 percent, p=.000). A similar difference was found among those who now live in small towns, who were more likely than those who live in urban areas to select this reason (60.3 percent vs 41.6 percent, p=.000). This pattern repeats among all-rural stayers, who were the most likely to say they wished to raise their children in Nebraska, compared to the not all-rural stayers (62.3 percent vs 42.8 percent, p=.000).

To summarize Table 2, in our group of Nebraska stayers, people with some rural living experience were more likely to express contentment living in Nebraska, and appreciation for its culture and community, rural hospitality, and natural environments. They were more likely to say that they wanted to raise their children in the state. They are less likely than those without rural living experience to say they would like to leave Nebraska. In the following section, we turn to respondents’ stories to gain deeper insight into rural place-based attachments.

The results of the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey show several connections between the residents and their hometowns, and that rural Nebraska is a place that people happily choose to stay. At the same time, the survey results show the difference in values between those have lived in rural areas and those who have not, as well as the different considerations that must be made in both rural and urban settings in residential decision making. We pursued some of the findings thematically by analyzing how stayers articulated reasons for staying in their own words.

FAMILIAL ATTACHMENT

Staying to live near family was, by far, the most agreed upon reason for staying in Nebraska. Family appears to be a key factor for binding people to place, especially for those who grew up and live now in a rural town. This supports research that lists family ties among the most important measures of place attachment (Barcus and Brunn 2009; Clark, Duque-Calvache, and Palomares-
Linares 2015). A large majority of stayers, 98.4 percent, have family members who live in Nebraska. The emotional attachment to family, as well as to the connection between ancestors and Nebraska came through in the stories survey respondents shared.

When asked to describe in their own words why they chose to stay in Nebraska, respondents frequently referred to their family ties to the state. In many cases, these rural respondents stated that they were fifth- or sixth-generation Nebraskans, and that they grew up in the same town as their ancestors and still hold tight to their ancestral roots. These accounts referenced Euro-American settler history and land acquisition.'For example, a woman from Alexandria expressed this heritage-oriented familial attachment: “My ancestors came to Nebraska to live before Nebraska was even a state. We have deep roots here.” Similarly, a Sargent resident said, “I’m a 5th generation Nebraska farmer and so is my husband. Nebraska is our roots. I can’t imagine living anywhere else.” This response from a woman who lives in Julian indicates the strong tie between place and family: “My ancestors on my dad’s side homesteaded here so our roots are here and I take pride in that. I don’t plan to ever leave Nebraska because my roots are deep here.” In each of these responses, there is a sense that rootedness to land is a dimension of family heritage and identity.

For other respondents, the idea of family expresses a way of living that is not necessarily tied to land. Perhaps one man from the small town of Filley summed it up best with his written response: “Family is one of my most deep-seeded values and to leave the state would mean to leave almost all of my family, and thus, those values. I couldn’t picture not being in Nebraska, where I am close to my family.”

Stayers with rural living experience were statistically more likely to consider child raising in their decision to stay in Nebraska. Many written responses from those who said they wanted to raise their children in Nebraska made mention of their family ties in this decision. This decision is based on both heritage and community pride, with respondents writing that they want to raise their children near family and continue the heritage their parents, grandparents, and even great grandparents created in the community. Several noted that they wanted to raise their children in the same community that they were raised in, as it was important to many rural hometown stayers that their children have the same positive childhood experiences that they had. Including both familial attachment and attachment to Nebraska’s community and culture in her response, a young woman from Tobias wrote that she “Decided to stay close to my roots and family to raise my children to work hard and be a part of a community. A place to teach future generations about love, respect, and hard work.”
ATTACHMENT TO RURAL CULTURE AND HOSPITALITY

A majority of Nebraska stayer respondents expressed appreciation for the culture and social networks present in Nebraskan communities. Several respondents placed a high value on working hard, community pride, familiarity with neighbors, and of course living next to friends and family in their decisions to stay in Nebraska. This local attachment is the result of relationships that have developed over time, as family ties and general familiarity have led to many rural residents having a strong sense of belonging to the community. The results of the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey support Monica Lewicka’s (2005) research on the relation of place attachment and community size, and reaffirm her findings that people in rural communities are more attached to their hometowns.

Nebraska’s rural hospitality and culture and community were cited by several rural respondents. Several of the written responses included references to the University of Nebraska fight song: “There is no place like Nebraska. The people, the community, the lifestyle and values are wonderful” and “Not only have I chosen to live in Nebraska, but also in the same community. I have traveled to Europe twice and in most of the states, but return here as like the song says: ‘there is no place like Nebraska.’”

ATTACHMENT TO LANDSCAPE/ENVIRONMENT

Nebraska survey respondents reported fewer instances of physical place attachment when compared to research on the rural population in other states, such as Vermont (Morse and Mudgett 2017) or Montana (Erickson, Sanders, and Cope 2018). However, appreciation for the landscape was cited more frequently by those with rural residential experience (Table 2). Just over one half (51.3 percent) of the all-rural stayers listed the landscape as a reason for staying in Nebraska, compared to 41.7 percent of the not all-rural stayers.

Several respondents specifically mentioned appreciation for the Nebraska Sandhills, an area of sprawling sand dunes that cover much of north-central Nebraska. The ties to the physical landscape appear to be primarily aesthetic, with respondents discussing the positive emotions and thoughts evoked by the scenery, as in these responses: “There is so much beauty around us every day; the native grasses in the road ditches, rolling hills planted with corn and beans, the Sandhills” and “I live on the edge of the Sandhills and, to me, it is one of the most beautiful places on earth. Love the rural pastures, cattle, and amazing skies!”

The openness of the sky was a feature admired by several respondents. This is a place-based value that is geographically specific to regions with broad vistas, such as the Great Plains. One woman from Verdigre described the state’s landscape and environment, and the people who call it home, by saying, “Nebraska has things you can’t put a price tag on. Beautiful sunrises and sunsets, the landscapes, the sounds of nature, the great people of this state, the opportunities for young and old, peace.”
GENERAL CONTENTMENT

Some of the most significant differences between rural and urban residents are evident in the results of how one feels about living in Nebraska. Stayers with rural living experience were much more likely to enjoy living in the state, as well as much less likely to want to live permanently outside of Nebraska. Accordingly, many of the written responses related to feelings of general contentment from living in Nebraska were from rural stayers. For example, a respondent from Oakland wrote:

It is my home. I have visited 49 states (all except Alaska) and 9 countries, staying in some places for up to 3 months but nothing compares to Nebraska. I love the seasons, the smells, the hospitality, football Saturdays, how the farmers have coffee together, and that I can drive to see most of my family within hours. I can have an uplifting conversation with a complete stranger at a grocery store. I have neighbors who will rake my leaves or shovel my driveway while I’m at work. I have access to all the amenities that a big city has to offer but it’s mixed with a small town vibe. I love that I can look out my parent’s picture window and see miles of fields and rolling hills. And the sunsets are to die for. There’s no place like Nebraska.

DISCUSSION

Analysis of Nebraskans’ reasons for staying revealed that staying is an active decision involving a mix of complex factors including family ties of different kinds over varying lengths of time, cultural characteristics, social networks, work, and emotional attachment to place. Respondents’ narratives weave together multiple conditions and reasons to explain immobility. In contrast to Tuan’s (1980) conceptual distinction between rootedness and sense of place, the Nebraska survey respondents clearly articulated reflection on their attachments, and the reasons for their attachments to their home place. Many explained their rootedness as a way to show how they arrived at a sense of place. “Roots” was a word commonly used by stayers to describe their attachment. We are convinced that, rather than indicating an unreflexive connection to community and landscape, rootedness describes an emotional attachment born out of time spent in place and reflection on that experience.

Family and community engagement emerged as dominant factors for staying as indicated by the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey. The majority of Nebraskan stayers indicated that family ties were very important to their decision to stay. Therefore, our research aligns with other rural migration scholarship that has demonstrated family is an important factor in residential decision making (Barcus and Brunn 2009; Clark, Duque-Calvache, and Palomares-Linares 2015; Morse and Mudgett 2018). However, our results also provide evidence that ties to community and social interactions, and a particular way of behaving in the world—hospitality—are highly valued by stayers. These topics require further research, as connection to community as apart from family may reveal the ways in which both mobile and immobile populations develop attachment to place. Such work may also go a long way in challenging narratives of rural places as empty or placeless, where nothing
at all happens. The stories generated in the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey suggest that small communities are the sites for rich sets of social interactions that, aligning with Tuan’s (1975) conception of place, produce meaning. Further, the research showed that the production of attachment to place may not be evenly distributed across geographical settings. We focus on this particular finding in the remainder of our discussion.

The Nebraska Roots Migration Survey produced compelling insight into the role that rural living may have on the development of strong attachment to place. It suggests that people who lived in a rural place during their adolescence and those who choose to live in a small town as adults are more likely to enjoy living in, value raising their children in, and appreciate the culture, community, and landscape of their home state more than those who do not have rural living experience. In short, the people with rural living experience in Nebraska were more content in their residential location than others. This research indicates that a childhood spent in a rural place impacts contentment in staying, even if the individual chooses to live in an urban setting in Nebraska as an adult. It shows that people who have lived in rural communities find it very important for their children to grow up in Nebraska’s countryside places. These findings open up new areas for research and invites the generation of hypotheses into the relationships between rurality and place attachment. Following on Lewicka (2005), what is the role of scale of human settlement in attachment to place? How do rural childhoods differ from urban childhoods in terms of experience of place and mobility decisions in adulthood? How might small hometowns with different characteristics influence these findings? Is there a relationship between the time spent in a rural environment or community and the development of a strong attachment to place?

Tuan wrote, “Experience takes time. Sense of place is rarely acquired in passing. To know a place well requires long residence and deep involvement” (1975, p. 164). Our findings gesture toward a possible correlation between those who have lived the majority of their lives in one state to contentment, but is attachment to place an outcome of time spent in place, or a thoughtful reflection of engagement with place? Or, does rural living offer a qualitatively different kind of deep involvement with place, one that engages humans with environmental features and nonhuman actors, and one that builds an entirely different sense of place?

Most Americans live in urban areas; therefore, most have not had access to the kinds of experiences and conditions that the rural Nebraskan research participants so highly valued. As the Great Plains and other rural areas of the United States continue to hollow out, what is lost in terms of contented rural immobility?
CONCLUSIONS

Moving forward, we recommend further study in three areas. We call first for more research on the rural-urban dimensions of place-based attachment. Second, we encourage rural mobility and immobility researchers to continue their investigations into many facets of migration and emotion: returnees’ experience of moving home, the values and feelings of urban homeowners who become rural residents, and the emotional geographies of rural living along many axes of difference including gender, age, race, sexual orientation, class, and residential history. Finally, we see a need for coordinated research projects that allow for in-depth qualitative research with rural residents along an extended set of research sites, creating a means to compare differences among place. We suggest that the outcome of such research will be of interest not only to the scholarly community, but to those who live and work in rural communities. Rural migration research will benefit individuals and groups who are concerned about the vitality of rural communities, as mobility research sheds light on the reasons why people choose to stay in or leave small towns. Our study supports other research that demonstrates strong place attachment among residents of rural communities (Lewicka 2005; Anton and Lawrence 2014; Smith and McAlister 2015; Mandal 2016). These residential preference data, which include both the positives and negatives of living in a rural community, can be applied to efforts to not only sustain the current population of rural communities, but to attract new residents. This information is consequential for rural geographers and migration experts who study a region such as the Great Plains, where rural depopulation has endured, but deep-rooted attachment to place remains.

By adopting an immobility perspective (Stockdale and Haartsen 2018) and examining the immobility of Nebraska’s stayers as a choice (Morse and Mudgett 2018), our analysis revealed the varied reasons why people choose to stay in their home state. These people who grew up in and choose to live in a rural place were much more likely to enjoy living in Nebraska. The Nebraska Roots Migration Survey found that familial attachment and the state’s rural culture and hospitality were the key drivers in rural Nebraskans’ decisions to stay in their hometowns, or in another rural community. For these rural residents, the imagined emptiness of Nebraska’s landscape is full of rich social interactions.
