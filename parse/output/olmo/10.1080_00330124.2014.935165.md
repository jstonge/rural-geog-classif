Landscapes of Risk: The Geography of Fatal Traffic Collisions in Indiana, 2003 to 2011

Samuel Nunn
Indiana University–Purdue University

William Newby
Center for Criminal Justice Research

Potential death from traffic collisions is a common hazard faced by nearly everyone. This analysis uses geographical approaches to examine fatal collision risk in the state of Indiana from 2003 to 2011. The state averaged 757 fatal collisions annually. About 60 percent occurred in urban or suburban areas. Risk was highest in low-density exurban and rural areas and on highways, county roads, and interstates. County sheriffs and state police were more likely to face fatal collisions than municipal police. Significant black spots existed around the Illinois and Kentucky state lines. Fatal collisions were less clustered than nonfatal collisions. Fatal collisions exhibited positive spatial autocorrelation but the global Moran’s I for counties was close to random. Approximately 10 percent of ninety-two counties had significant local spatial autocorrelation in fatal collision rates. Police and emergency response resource distributions should be considered in light of fatal collision black spots in the state. Key Words: mortality risk, spatial clustering, spatial interaction, traffic fatality, traffic safety.

Risk landscapes arise from differential spatial exposure to hazards (Momomnior 1997; Jarup 2000; Cossman et al. 2007; Perrow 2011). Considering risks to which persons are widely exposed, motor vehicle travel is surely among the most common. The idea of risk landscapes is applied here as an analysis of fatal collision risk across several spatial categorizations in the state of Indiana from 2003 to 2011. How are fatal collisions patterned across space? Are patterns random? If not, what is their pattern?

These questions are important. Traffic collisions are a major responsibility for law enforcement and emergency response agencies, and the focus of a broader traffic safety infrastructure composed of state and local law enforcement, fire departments, and emergency medical services. Understanding spatial risk patterns can help inform strategies, programs, and preventative tactics to respond more effectively and quickly to crashes, prevent future collisions, and reduce mortality rates in targeted areas. Knowing spatial patterns and the types of spatial dependence among counties can help tailor better preparedness and response to situations most likely to be encountered (Kuo, Lord, and Walden 2011; Steil et al. 2011). Different enforcement strategies could be envisioned in response to dispersed or clustered patterns. If fatal collision risks are
concentrated in certain areas, knowing this could help focus emergency medical response. If fatal collision patterns are random, it might be harder to create targeted traffic mortality interventions.

Themes from geography, traffic safety, and public health are used here to analyze Indiana fatal traffic collisions from 2003 to 2011. First, are there identifiable patterns of places where fatal collisions occur within a state at various levels of spatial organization? Second, do spatial processes underlying traffic mortality operate in a random, dispersed, or clustered manner? Third, how do fatal collision rates vary within different levels of spatial organization? Finally, how much and what types of spatial dependence exist in fatal collision rates among Indiana counties?

Spatial Processes, Risk Landscapes, and Fatal Collision Patterns

Motor vehicle collisions are the leading cause of accidental deaths and always within the top ten causes of death overall in the United States (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention 2011). Nearly all segments of the U.S. population experience exposure to the risks of motor vehicle crashes. Some population segments are represented disproportionately in fatality data: Crashes are the leading cause of death among children five to fourteen years of age (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention 2011) and age-adjusted mortality rates suggest high risk for seventeen- to thirty-four-year-old men (Baker, Whitfield, and O’Neill 1987; Baker, Waller, and Langlois 1991).

From a theoretical perspective, various spatial processes likely underlie where fatal collisions occur. Haining (1993) defined a spatial process as one in which “changes of state are due to spatial properties of the attribute . . . [or] the types of processes which might generate spatial structure in an attribute” (24). He explained four types of spatial processes (diffusion, dispersion, interaction, and exchange), of which two (dispersion and interaction) seem conceptually appropriate to analyses of fatal collisions. Dispersion is a process in which “the population itself disperses and the resultant spatial structure depends on the nature of the dispersal” (25). In this context, fatal collisions could be considered the “population” that is spreading or distributed across an area and thus become dispersed throughout a region to varying degrees. If crashes are not spatially random, some areas might produce more crashes than others. As shown in the Indiana analyses, the “resultant spatial structure” can be measured statistically (e.g., nearest neighbor index) to understand whether fatal collisions are dispersed more or less equitably, organized into clusters, or random. Spatial interaction occurs when “events at one location influence and are influenced by events at other locations” (Haining 1993, 25). At the microlevel of individual traffic collisions, the forces of spatial interaction certainly operate: The interactions of drivers and vehicles within the surrounding traffic environment comprise the behavioral and situational dynamic in which fatal collisions occur. Statistically, global and local Moran’s I can be calculated to assess the nature and strength of spatial interaction. Explicit identification of all spatial processes underlying the fatal collision risk landscape goes beyond our scope, but we use the dispersion and interaction concepts to guide the analyses. In any event, a first step toward understanding the risk landscape is to identify clusters of high-mortality areas, which is our primary focus. Where is mortality risk likely to be high?

From an epidemiological viewpoint, “risk patterns have spatial and temporal components [and] it is well known that exposure and disease are unevenly distributed geographically” (Jarup 2000, 415–6). Briggs (2000) noted that “exposure does not occur at single, fixed points, but across a complex web of locations as people move through the field” (354). Geographical risk analysis often examines routes of exposure; in the context of fatal collision risk, obvious routes of exposure include roads and road networks. Whitelegg (1987) called collisions “a particularly noxious space-time event” and identified basic components comprising collision geography:

A traffic accident is the product of an unwelcome interaction between two or more moving objects, or a fixed and a moving object. The movement itself, whether of pedestrians or motorists, will be a function of the land use system, residential patterns, population densities, street geometry, location of workplace shopping precinct, health centre or other traffic generators. (162–3)

High-accident areas are considered “black spots” (Maher and Mountain 1988; Thomas 1996) or “hot spots” (Anderson 2007).

Various risk factors affect crash outcomes, and risk factors underlying black spots are not uniformly distributed (Baker, Whitfield, and O’Neill 1987; Whitelegg 1987; McMillan and Lapham 2006; Bhalla et al. 2007; Jones et al. 2008). As a result of living in a low-density rural environment, an individual is exposed to more vehicle miles traveled on higher speed county or state roads, U.S. highways, and interstates (Baker, Whitfield, and O’Neill 1987). Because high-speed crashes produce more serious injuries, a road environment conducive to higher speed has higher mortality risk (Levine, Kim, and Nitz 1995a; Ossiander and Cummings 2002; Aguero-Valverde and Jovani 2006). Living in an urbanized county creates different exposure to slower speed roads with higher traffic congestion, lower speed collisions, and milder injury outcomes (Clark 2003; Lucy 2003). Roads with high traffic volume will host larger numbers of crashes (Anderson 2007). Risk exposure differs across traffic units (pedestrian, bicyclist, motorcycle, automobile; Anutha and Chladil 1989; Arzemanian et al. 1993; Ewing, Schieber, and Zegeer 2003; Paulozzi 2006) and individual characteristics of victims (e.g., age, gender, driver history; Zlatoper 1989). This includes behavioral factors linked to those involved in collisions (e.g., use of alcohol, drugs, unlawful driving, failure to wear restraints; Ossiander and Cummings 2002; McMillan
and Lapham 2006). Thus, different groups of individuals are exposed to more (or fewer) situations that produce more (or fewer) serious injuries (Bhalla et al. 2007).

More specifically, traffic collisions have environmental, situational, geophysical, and circumstantial dimensions (Zlatoper 1989; Eisenberg and Warner 2005; Dumbaugh and Rae 2009; Dumbaugh, Rae, and Winneberger 2009). Some analyses have focused on the proximity of fatal collision activity to liquor stores and bars (LaScala, Johnson, and Gruenewald 2001; McMillan and Lapham 2006). Peak volumes of fatal collisions often occur in the summer months and around holidays. Fatality risks are also influenced by an underlying urban-to-rural continuum of traffic environments linked to population density (Baker, Whitfield, and O’Neill 1987; Ewing, Schieber, and Zegeer 2003; Clark and Cushing 2004; Dumbaugh and Rae 2009) and the changing physical settings through which people travel (Levine, Kim, and Nitz 1995a; Lucy 2003; Jones et al. 2008).

Fatal collision patterns vary across different jurisdictional settings. Aguero-Valverde and Jovannis (2006, 624) analyzed 1996 to 2000 traffic crashes among Pennsylvania counties and found “no evidence of spatial correlation in fatal crashes; however, spatial correlation was found to be significant in injury crashes.” Ewing, Schieber, and Zegeer (2003, 1541) examined 448 counties making up “the 101 most populous metropolitan statistical areas” and found that less sprawl (more compact development patterns) was linked to lower fatality rates; more sprawl had higher motor vehicle and pedestrian fatality rates. In an analysis of 747 San Antonio census blocks, Dumbaugh and Rae (2009) identified correlates of urban form that are important to different crash types (e.g., each additional mile of arterial road in a census block increases the total number of crashes 15 percent; pedestrian scale retail and commercial uses can reduce total crashes up to 2.2 percent). Their fatal crash model was significantly different from their total and injury crash models, suggesting that fatal collisions operated by a different set of spatial processes than nonfatal crashes. Levine, Kim, and Nitz (1995a) examined spatial variation of 19,598 crashes in 1990 in metropolitan Honolulu. Accidents were highly concentrated in Honolulu, but fatal and injury collisions were more dispersed than noninjury crashes. They noted that “fatalities increase with decreasing traffic density, due to an increase in speeds and deterioration in driving conditions” (Levine, Kim, and Nitz 1995a, 668). Jones et al. (2008, 531) examined traffic accidents at the local authority level in England and Wales from 1995 to 2000 and found “no evidence of dispersion or clustering for the fatal or serious” injury counts.

One implication from these analyses is that there are likely differences in the spatial patterns of nonfatal versus fatal collisions. The studies also suggest there are unique aspects of traffic mortality black spot analysis because each geographical entity (e.g., a political jurisdiction) will reflect its own internal fatal collision patterns and dynamics; to that extent, if an entity has not been measured, its pattern remains largely unknown. Identifying clusters of mortality is one way of trying to extract preventative and public safety policy issues to shape public responses to traffic deaths.

Measurement and Method

Exploring the geography of fatalities is challenging because collisions are comparatively rare and, given that a crash occurs, fatal collisions are a low-frequency event. In a broad geography, the chances of any selected space hosting a traffic collision thin substantially. To partially address the “rarity” problem, this analysis uses a nine-year aggregation of fatal collisions. The fatal collision rate is operationalized as the percentage of all collisions resulting in at least one fatality. Given a collision occurs in an area, what is the probability it resulted in at least one fatality?

Operationalizing the places of collision incidence is also challenging. There are different ways of defining spatial areas in which crashes occur. One is the point-level location of fatal collisions. The second is aggregation of collision points distributed among different areas or categories (e.g., county, road type, locality). Once we aggregate point locations, identifying the scale of the bounded geographies involved becomes an issue because it invokes the modifiable area unit problem (MAUP). Different levels of spatial aggregation produce different findings (Thomas 1996; Lassarre and Thomas 2005). MAUP’s impact is lessened by using the smallest possible areal units and different scales of spatial aggregation (Paez and Scott 2004). Therefore, three spatial units of analysis are used herein. The first is the fatal collision point. The second aggregates upward, made up of one-mile-square quadrats in which fatal collisions from 2003 to 2011 are examined for each of 36,879 square mile grids overlaid on the state of Indiana. Density analyses identify spatial patterns of traffic deaths over time, measured as fatal collision rates, for each square mile quadrat. The third unit of spatial analysis uses ninety-two Indiana counties.

Indiana traffic collision data are collected by police agencies and compiled within the Indiana State Police Automated Reporting Information Exchange System (ARIES). The Indiana Officer’s Standard Crash Report (used in all Indiana jurisdictions) feeds ARIES, which includes information on more than 150 variables organized around collisions, vehicular units, and individuals. The focus here is on fatal collisions, aggregated from 2003 through 2011 (6,814 fatal collisions within 1,802,623 total collisions). We use different approaches to analyze these data.

First, we examine the 2003 to 2011 fatal collision point patterns, followed by a comparison of fatal collision risks across road types, locality classifications, and type of law enforcement agency responding to the crash. These exhibits are driven by conceptualizing fatal collision patterns as a spatial process in which an attribute—fatal collisions—occurs within different types of broadly defined fixed populations (e.g., the

Analysis and Findings

Number and Location of Fatal Collisions

Indiana collisions declined from 2003 to 2011, with statewide fatal collisions averaging 757 annually. Figure 1 shows maps of point-level fatal collisions relative to interstate, U.S. state, and county highways in 2003, 2007, and 2011. The locations of fatal collisions vary from year to year. Visually, the interstates carve Indiana into six roughly pie-shaped wedges converging on Indianapolis in the state’s center. Inside the “wedges” are other major roads, which host the majority of fatal collisions.

The major urban areas (e.g., Indianapolis, Gary, South Bend, Terre Haute, Fort Wayne, Anderson–Muncie, Bloomington, and Evansville) exhibit fatal collision clusters due to the concentrated exposure of persons, vehicles, and roads within densely populated regions. Apart from urban concentrations, there are other patterns. There is a rectangle of regular fatal collisions in the northern tier of Indiana counties, which is effectively the I-80–I-90 corridor. In some years, there are stretches of concentrated collisions on interstates (e.g., south of Indianapolis on I-65 in 2007; northwest of Indianapolis on I-65 in 2003 and 2011). Other smaller clusters of fatal collisions in any one year can be seen. Risk of Fatal Collisions
An alternative description of the fatal collision risk landscape comes from comparing fatal collision likelihood among different locality classifications and road types. Figure 2 shows the odds (×100) of a fatal collision among four locality classifications, five road types, and three types of law enforcement jurisdictions. We switch briefly to use of odds—the ratio of fatal to nonfatal collisions, times 100—as an outcome metric because it is consistent with an interest in measuring the traffic risk landscape. Urban places consistently had the lowest odds of collisions resulting in a fatality, with little variation in the baseline risk from 2003 to 2011. The most remote localities—exurban and rural—reflected the highest odds and annual variation. When collisions occur, areas furthest from urban places had the highest risk of fatal collisions. Differences in fatal collision odds across localities reflect an implied density effect in which lower densities of people and deconcentrated building, land use, and road patterns are accompanied by increased risks of fatal outcomes when collisions occur. When a collision occurred within urban places, however, there was little change in the odds that it would be fatal. Therefore, although the proportions and counts of fatal collisions within urban areas have increased, the risk of a fatal outcome stayed largely the same.

Fatal collision odds among road types show a similar pattern, largely because road types indirectly represent development patterns embedded in the locality classifications. Local and city roads—like urban places—were least likely to produce a fatal outcome, notwithstanding the concentration of fatal collisions in urban areas. County roads, U.S. routes, and state highways reflected the highest risk of fatal outcomes. From 2003 through 2007, interstate highway fatal collision odds were similar to these roads; however, interstates showed a decline in fatal collision odds from 2005 to 2010, with odds shifting sharply upward in 2011. For most of the period, differences between fatal collision odds on county, state, and U.S. roads were largely indistinguishable but were clearly higher than city roads. Given the road types various police agencies patrol, sheriff’s departments and the state police were more likely than municipal police to respond to a fatal collision.

Clustering of Fatal Collisions
The statewide pattern of fatal collisions can be described more precisely using the NNI to examine the degree of spatial clustering. Global Spatial Autocorrelation in County Fatal Collision Rates
Aggregating the 2003 to 2011 collisions to a higher spatial organization, we examined fatal collision rates within Indiana counties by type of traffic unit involved and derived a global Moran’s I for county fatal collision rates. The global Moran’s I ranges from −1 (perfect negative spatial autocorrelation) to +1 (perfect positive spatial autocorrelation), including 0 (perfect random spatial autocorrelation). Figure 4 presents choropleth maps of low-to-high quartile county fatal collision rates and the global Moran’s I for fatal collisions involving nonmotorists, motorcycles, and all other vehicles. The global Moran’s I metrics beneath each map include the strength of the correlation and the distance within which any presumed spatial processes operate. We interpret the global Moran’s I as the average extent of spatial autocorrelation among Indiana counties—whether on average across the state a county is surrounded by counties with fatal collision rates more or less like itself.

Under a strict interpretation, the global Moran’s I results show minimal positive spatial autocorrelation, with slight differences among traffic unit types. There is very weak autocorrelation for nonmotorists (0.03), only somewhat stronger for other motor vehicles (0.09), and the strongest (but still relatively low) for motorcycles (0.19). Overall, the global Moran’s I for county fatal collision rates is closer to random than to strong positive spatial autocorrelation. In addition, the distances at which the underlying spatial processes operate differ among unit types—the forces of spatial influence operate within 25 miles for motorcycle collisions, 50 miles for other motor vehicles, and 100 miles for nonmotorists. To further examine clustering of similar fatality risk rates among Indiana counties, however, the choropleth maps are perhaps more informative.
In some ways, the fatal collision risk choropleth maps do not fully support what the global Moran’s I metrics imply. Whereas the global Moran’s I suggested only slight spatial autocorrelation, on the basis of the Indiana county quartile rankings, several county clusters are identifiable. A slight western bias of higher fatality risk counties is visible—most of the counties in the western half of the state were above median fatality rates. Further, counties along the northwestern Indiana–Illinois state boundary had high rates. There is a ten-county cluster of high motorcycle fatal collision rates (quartile 4) in the southwestern part of the state. Thus, even with low statewide spatial autocorrelation, it appears that many counties are located adjacent to counties in similar fatal collision rate quartiles. A more precise assessment of localized spatial autocorrelation is needed.

Local Spatial Autocorrelation in County Fatal Collision Rates

Using local Moran’s I, the final analysis identifies counties that have experienced statistically significant positive or negative spatial autocorrelation and the extent to which the fatal collision rates in surrounding counties tend to be higher or lower. The local Moran’s I calculations for the ninety-two counties use a spatial weights matrix based on one or more common boundaries. Figure 5 shows the location of counties with significant local spatial autocorrelation as well as the nature of the spatial relationship between the county and its contiguous neighbors (e.g., low–low, low–high, high–high, or high–low). The local Moran’s I identifies nine counties with statistically significant spatial autocorrelation relative to counties sharing common boundaries with the nine. Altogether, thirty-three different counties shared boundaries with one or more of these nine counties. In terms of their fatal collision rates, the nine significant counties are in either the lowest or highest county fatal collision rate quartiles. Four counties show negative spatial autocorrelation. Two (Tippecanoe and Vigo) have low fatal collision rates and share boundaries with higher rate counties. The other two (Tipton and Switzerland) have high fatal collision rates and share boundaries with low-fatality-rate counties. Five counties had positive spatial autocorrelation; all but one were high-rate counties surrounded by other high-fatality-rate counties. Of these, four high-rate counties (Benton, Newton, Warren, and Daviess) shared boundaries with other high-fatal-collision-rate counties, and one (Marion County) was the center of an eight-county cluster of low fatal collision rates.

Discussion and Conclusions

The geography of fatal traffic collisions was examined here in various ways. Total and fatal collisions declined from 2003 to 2011. About 60 percent of fatal collisions occurred in urban or suburban areas, although from 2003 to 2011 the forces of dispersion were somewhat centripetal: Urban places gradually increased their shares of fatal collisions. The risk of fatalities in the event of a collision was highest in low-density exurban and rural areas and accompanying state and U.S. highways, county roads, and interstates.

Based on the NNI, the spatial dispersion of all collisions is characterized as clustered, suggesting underlying spatial processes associated with higher density urban land uses are promoting clustering. Urban areas are the sites of maximum exposure (e.g., more street miles, more traffic) to the baseline risk of a collision. Fatal collisions are less clustered than nonfatal collisions, in part because fatal collisions are rarer and occur farther apart. Although the NNI classifies fatal collisions as clustered (0.71), the metric itself is closer to random (1.0) than clustered (0.0).

By identifying areas with higher fatal collision rates, density maps show different patterns of dispersion for fatal motorcycle collisions versus other fatal collisions, which is linked to both the smaller number of motorcycle collisions and likely differences in motorcycle riding patterns (e.g., in more scenic areas of southern Indiana). In addition, with Indianapolis and Marion County as the comparatively lower risk center of the state, a large number of higher risk cells are visible in the western half of the state. In particular, the Indiana–Illinois state line appears disproportionately composed of high fatal rate grid cells. There are also black spots on the Indiana–Kentucky state line, which identify a large number of the southernmost grid cells with higher fatal collision rates roughly following the Ohio River. The possible “borderline” effects on the Indiana–Illinois state line appear linked to automobiles (because many black spots disappear when
looking only at fatal motorcycle collisions), whereas motorcycles might be more responsible for the high rates at different places on the Indiana–Kentucky state line.

The overall pattern of fatal collisions in the state exhibits slight positive spatial autocorrelation but, similar to NNI metrics for fatal collision points, the global Moran’s I for counties is closer to random than to perfect positive spatial autocorrelation. Thus, there is a low level of spatial interaction occurring statewide. When examined in terms of local spatial autocorrelation, however, selected county groupings show significant positive or negative spatial correlations, and suggest the existence of discrete county clusters with fatal collision rates that vary together systematically. By examining fatal collision clustering and dispersion and spatial autocorrelation, we force the question of how to interpret any patterns. From a spatial process perspective, the broad question is whether and how a county’s rate influences or is influenced by fatal collision rates in surrounding counties. Some of the Indiana patterns can be considered in this context.

Tipton and Switzerland exhibited significant negative correlation and were both high-quartile counties surrounded by low-quartile counties. Switzerland County hosts a major riverboat gambling casino on the Ohio River, which might make it a magnet for higher amounts of traffic and possibly riskier driving behaviors (e.g., alcohol involvement at night). Tipton County has no casinos but has an almost exclusively rural driving environment (e.g., predominantly county roads and a few state highways). Two other counties with low fatal collision rates, Vigo and Tippecanoe, also exhibited negative spatial correlation and were thus surrounded by high-quartile counties. Both counties reflect predominantly urban and suburban locality classification. It is conceivably possible that the traffic safety infrastructures in Vigo and Tippecanoe counties are stronger than those in surrounding counties, perhaps through better collision response practices or targeted traffic safety initiatives.

There were four high-fatal-collision-rate counties with positive spatial interaction effects. Daviess is a high-rate county in the southwest, adjacent to higher quartile counties on the Indiana–Illinois boundary. Daviess is also part of a southwestern cluster of counties with high fatal motorcycle collision rates. Warren, Newton, and Benton largely make up the northwestern high-rate cluster and have high fatal collision rates for all traffic unit types. These counties are part of a region of high fatal collision rates. In contrast, the most populated county in the state—Marion, home to Indianapolis—exhibited an opposite form of positive spatial autocorrelation (i.e., low fatal collision rate surrounded by counties with mostly low rates). This could be partly a function of the dampening effects of highly urbanized land uses on fatal collision odds. Alternatively, the eight central Indiana counties might have possessed an effective traffic safety infrastructure over the 2003 to 2011 period. Perhaps law enforcement and emergency response agencies within the same metropolitan region participate in information-sharing and training networks that improve their overall capacity to respond to or prevent fatal crashes.

What can knowledge about spatial patterns of fatal collisions mean for strengthening the traffic safety infrastructure? Most basically, the analyses identified areas with higher and lower fatality rates, thus permitting diagnostics and possible targeted programs. For instance, in trying to explain some of the black spot counties as a result of location along a state boundary, it is possible that collisions along state boundaries might receive slower emergency response because of possible jurisdictional and technological issues. For example, 911 calls for emergency response might go to an Illinois public safety answering point (PSAP), which are subsequently routed to the proper Indiana agency, thus slowing response times. Although emergency response problems can be mitigated by mutual aid compacts, technological “failures” (e.g., routing to the wrong PSAP) might be unavoidable. More broadly, the Indiana analyses identify two broad risk landscapes: lower fatal collision risks in towns and cities versus higher levels of risk outside towns in exurban or rural areas. These latter areas are patrolled predominately by county sheriffs and state police. Hence, first response for nearly three quarters of fatal collisions in the state was assigned to county sheriffs and the state police. From a frequency standpoint, fatal collisions are rare in an urban traffic environment, but urban areas are the location of a substantial share of fatal traffic incidents (and about two thirds of injury collisions). Other agencies (county sheriffs and state police) have a smaller share of overall collisions, but within the collisions they handle, fatalities are more frequent than in more urban settings. This suggests possibly different training and equipment needs on the part of city versus other law enforcement agencies. In addition, the effects of distance on emergency responders and response times is important (Jones and Bentham 1995) and can affect the fatal collision risk landscape.

More qualitative research into chronic fatal collision black spots is needed to better understand the effects of different collision circumstances, the first responder agencies involved, and the spatial distribution of responding agencies and emergency resource providers. Such analyses might produce insights into more effective spatial allocations of responder resources. In addition, closer examination of the inherently geographical aspects of black spots could prove helpful. For example, within areas with higher fatal collision rates, more analysis is needed to determine the broad mix of risk factors present: time of day, season, weather, road surface, nature of the collision site, and so on. Many of these geographical features deserve closer research attention and might help identify other potential black spots.
