Historical Political Ecology of Water: Access to Municipal Drinking Water in Colonial Lima, Peru (1578–1700)

Martha G. Bell
Leiden Institute for Area Studies

Historical political ecology provides a powerful framework for understanding nature–society relations in the past. This approach is applied to municipal drinking water governance in early colonial Lima, Peru, with a focus on how power dynamics influenced sociospatial patterns of water access and control. Sixteenth- and seventeenth-century archival sources are analyzed for material aspects of Lima’s drinking water pipeline network and for the management strategies employed by the municipal government. Access to water is demonstrated to have shaped, reinforced, and reflected colonial social divisions and to have been linked to the spatial development of the city, including urban–rural relations. Key Words: colonialism, historical political ecology, Peru, water governance, water infrastructure.

Se aplicó este enfoque al estudio de la administración municipal del agua potable en Lima, Perú, a principios de la colonia, con interés concentrado en la manera como las dinámicas del poder influyeron sobre los patrones socioespaciales del acceso y control del agua. Las fuentes de archivos de los siglos XVI y XVII son analizadas en lo que concierne a los aspectos materiales de la red del acueducto de agua potable de Lima, lo mismo que en lo relacionado con las estrategias de manejo utilizadas por el gobierno municipal. Historical perspectives have long been considered integral to political ecology (Zimmerer and Bassett 2003; Peet and Watts 2004; Robbins 2004), although how and to what end historical content and analysis could be incorporated into this research approach has not been fully resolved and remains an ambiguous pursuit (Offen 2004). In general, the historical political ecology approach calls for the application of historical perspectives within a broadly defined political ecology point of view and has been defined as “a field-informed interpretation of society–nature relations in the past” (Offen 2004, 21). This includes using historical material to contextualize current environmental conflicts, especially the continuing influences of European expansion and colonialism, often with the explicit motivation of improving social justice or environmental conservation in the present (Brannstrom 2004; Offen 2004; D. K. Davis 2009). Historical political ecology might also include asking questions about environmental change or natural resource use in historical periods, either recent (e.g., Swyngedouw 1999; Biehler 2009; Carey 2010; Lane 2011; Carey, French, and O’Brien 2012) or more distant (e.g., Watts 1983; Sluyter 1999; Zimmerer 2000; Carney 2001; M. Davis 2004; Endfield and Fernández Tejedo 2006; D. K. Davis 2007; Endfield 2008). Although this latter purpose—using a political ecological analysis to closely examine nature–society relations in the past and not just provide context for current-day issues—is lesser used, it represents a powerful framework for historical research.

In this article, I apply such a framework to municipal drinking water access and governance in colonial Lima, the capital city of the Viceroyalty of Peru. I draw on approaches and questions inspired by political ecology to examine patterns and politics of water use in a colonial context, focusing on the influence of power dynamics on sociospatial patterns of resource access and control (following McCarthy 2002; Ribot and Peluso 2003; Turner 2009). Using material analysis of water infrastructure and technology recorded in sixteenth- and seventeenth-century archives, I explain how water access formed, reinforced, and reflected
colonial social divisions, as well as influenced the spatial development of the colonial city.

The city of Lima, originally called Ciudad de Los Reyes, was founded in 1535 as the seat of the Spanish Viceroyalty of Peru. It can be considered one of the most prominent of the Spanish colonial cities, second perhaps only to Mexico, the capital of New Spain. Lima is located on the central coast of Peru in a desert climate where true rainfall rarely occurs. All water used for drinking, irrigation, sanitation, and industry must be drawn from river watersheds and related aquifers. The urban design of Lima followed the well-known Spanish colonial grid pattern, and the city was specifically sited along the banks of the Rímac River and in relation to the numerous pre-Hispanic irrigation canals drawn from this river.1 The Spaniards, however, established their own drinking water pipeline infrastructure. This underground pipe network, first inaugurated in 1578, was controlled by the Cabildo (City Council) and was one of the earliest in the New World. The Cabildo, or municipal branch of colonial government, was officially charged with managing Lima’s water and, not surprisingly, the careful division of measured amounts of drinking and irrigation water occupied much of its energy.2 Although the Cabildo’s explicitly stated objective was to provide access to clean and copious drinking water for the entire urban population, this goal was only partially achieved. Therefore, this article seeks to examine how municipal governance strategies contributed to inequality in access to drinking water among colonial Lima’s urban residents. The methodological approach to answering this question incorporates two major components: first, asking questions inspired by political ecology and, in particular, the political ecology of water and, second, applying data collection and analysis techniques appropriate to the historical period being studied.

Three key themes drawn from the recent literature on the political ecology of water inform my interpretations of the Lima case. Most important, unequal water access (Swyngedouw 2004; Bakker 2011; Loftus 2011) and the related politics of surface water or aquifer management (Swyngedouw 1999; Norman and Bakker 2008; Brooks and Linton 2011; Jepson 2012) have proven integral themes in understanding current-day water systems and have been shown to have both important historical antecedents and long-term legacy effects. Second, analyses of urban development and urban–rural relations related to water demonstrate strong urban bias, with urban uses often privileged in cases of conflicting multiple uses of water (multifunctionality). This bias, or unequal relationship between urban and rural areas, is evidenced by the urban hydraulic reach (or water footprint; Swyngedouw 1997, 2004; Bakker 2003; Kaika 2005; Scott and Pablos 2011). Third, combining spatial, social, political, and economic aspects of the previously mentioned themes, Bakker (2003) indicated that water supply in urban areas, especially urbanizing areas of the Global South, should be understood in terms of archipelagos instead of networks, given the discontinuous and partial coverage of public water supply and the frequency of multiple and overlapping methods of public and private water delivery mechanisms. Together, these themes provide a foundational perspective from which to examine urbanization, water supply, and inequality. They suggest the primacy of urban water use and indicate unequal infrastructural coverage and access to water within urban spaces.

Although these topics, questions, and themes from political ecological research can be easily transferred to historical contexts, the research methods typically used by political ecologists might not always be applicable, especially when more distant historical periods are studied. For such older cases, it is necessary to combine multiple methods and data sources, which might include field-based ethnographic and landscape observations, archival analysis, archaeological investigation, biophysical data, mapping, and more. The multidisciplinary research area called hydraulic archaeology provides a framework for analysis of (pre) historical water systems (Kirchner and Navarro 1996; Glick and Kirchner 2000; Barceló 2004; Glick 2005). Here, practitioners seek to reconstruct the design, layout, construction, and modification of hydraulic systems, to identify the social groups participating in the use of these systems and thus to determine the relative political and demographic strengths of these groups as demonstrated by their ability to access water resources. The major unit of investigation in hydraulic archaeology is the concept of hydraulic space (Barceló 1996), which is basically equivalent to what Swyngedouw (1999) termed the “waterscape” and can be defined as a landscape of water use, at once both natural and social. It is a space designed to take advantage of a natural fluvial system, and incorporates (1) the aquifer or drainage area, (2) the local and regional topography in relation to desired water conduction, and (3) the irrigated parcels or other uses of water. It includes the biophysical context controlling the quantity of water available and the direction of its flow, as well as the human division of water among houses, fields, orchards, and other uses. The orientation and design of a hydraulic space is based on features of the fluvial system, but the conduction and distribution of water in canals and pipes follows patterns that are neither natural nor casual. This water infrastructure is closely linked to the organization of productive activities and therefore to social, political, and economic relationships.

The focus on infrastructure (e.g., canals, pipelines), achieved through consideration of a range of data sources (e.g., archaeological survey and excavation, historical documents and maps, legal traditions, toponyms, and remotely sensed imagery), makes hydraulic archaeology a useful guide for pursuing historical political ecology work on water in colonial Lima. Given the nearly 500 years of Lima’s urban development, few material or archaeological remains of colonial water infrastructure can still be found (or
reasonably accessed), so in this case archival sources contain the best information. The major source for the analysis of Lima’s hydraulic space is the Libros de Cabildos de Lima (the books of the City Council of Lima, hereafter LCL). These books, which record the minutes from the weekly or biweekly meetings of Lima’s Cabildo, span almost the entire colonial period, from the city’s foundation in 1535 to after Peru’s independence in 1821,5 and provide an incredibly comprehensive and continuous record of the Cabildo’s actions related to drinking water governance.4 In this article I examine the period 1578 to 1700, which was the interval during which most of Lima’s public and private drinking water infrastructure was installed, the vast majority of potable water connection licenses were issued, and the most significant water management and governance strategies were developed. Specifically, I analyze all references to springs, reservoirs, pipes, tanks, fountains, petitions for water connections, and other related topics in the LCL of the period in question to determine both spatial and temporal aspects of water supply in the pipeline network. I consider such basic questions as these: Where were pipelines constructed and when? Which residents and populations did these pipelines serve? How reliable was water supply in the network overall? This included mapping the pipeline routes and the locations of public and private running water connections.5 I used this analysis of written descriptions of material aspects of Lima’s hydraulic space to interpret the biophysical, social, political, and economic significance of patterns of drinking water access and control in the early colonial period and to evaluate the Cabildo’s role in contributing to or alleviating inequality in water access.

Lima’s Hydraulic Space and Its Governance

In 1671, Lima’s solicitor general called water “the element most necessary after air” (LCL 29.X.1671),6 and indeed it was the natural resource most frequently discussed by Lima’s Cabildo. The provision of drinking water was considered one of the principal responsibilities of the Cabildo: “the first thing given attention in the foundation of all of the cities of the world [is] that this element, inexcusable for human life, is provided with cleanliness and security” (LCL 17.XI.1649).
To this end, the Spanish located their capital city along the banks of the Rímac River, to provide residents with water for irrigation, drinking, sanitation, and industry. The Rímac dominates Lima’s hydraulic space (Figure 1). Like all of Peru’s coastal rivers, it flows westward, cutting down from the glacial lakes of the high Andes to the Pacific, descending from a height of over 5,500 m above sea level to its mouth near Lima’s port El Callao over a distance of about 130 km. Its flow varies seasonally, increasing during the highland rainy season (October–April), when it often threatens to overrun its banks, and all but drying up during the dry months (May–September). The Rímac was the source of the irrigation canals that supported all agriculture in the region around the city (Domínguez 1988). It was also the source of numerous springs used for drinking water during the colonial period. Seepage and filtration from the river and the Andean cordillera fills the large alluvial aquifer—of approximately 390 km² in area and 100 to 500 m in depth—located underneath the city and its surroundings, recharging this resource on a seasonal basis (Lerner et al. 1982; Quintana and Tovar 2002).

The Spanish designed and built their linked agricultural and hydraulic system (fields, canals, reservoirs, and pipelines) around the natural topography as well as the pre-Hispanic canals already in existence. This multipurpose hydraulic space linked Lima’s urban core with its rural surroundings and was officially controlled by the Cabildo, which exercised jurisdiction over the urban zone (approximately 2.6 km²), as well as the surroundings of the city (approximately a 113-km radius around the city). A diverse population resided within this larger territory, primarily made up of Spaniards, Africans, and Indians.7 Although the Cabildo claimed great authority over the city, it actually governed through a complicated relationship with other branches of colonial government, especially the Viceroy (the Spanish monarch’s representative in Peru) and the Real Audiencia (Royal Audience, the highest court in Peru). With respect to water administration, responsibilities of the Cabildo included protection of the urban riverbank and construction of levees, distribution of rights to irrigation waters, canal maintenance, concession of milling licenses, and, of primary importance here, construction and maintenance of a municipal drinking water pipeline network and allocation of permits to install private connections to this network for homes and other institutions. To manage all aspects of municipal water governance, a water judge (Juez de Aguas) was elected annually from among the city councilmen (Regidores). The water judge post was established in 1556, with power to enforce the water ordinances for the city and surrounding countryside. It was a lucrative position, with a salary of 800 pesos a year, yet election as water judge rotated among the councilmen and did not seem to require much specialized knowledge (Bell forthcoming).

To provision the city with drinking water, Lima’s Cabildo carried out three functions: (1) It secured the source of the water in springs on the outskirts of the city; (2) it installed and maintained a network of reservoirs, tanks, and pipelines; and (3) it awarded licenses for running water connections from the pipes to private houses, monasteries and convents, and other institutions. For this, beginning in 1611, each year the Cabildo named three or four pipeline commissioners (Comisarios de Cañerías).8 These pipeline commissioners were councilmen or mayors, and in fifty-eight of the seventy-nine years for which we have data in the seventeenth century, one of the commissioners was simultaneously the water judge (or

The pipeline commissioners’ work centered on evaluating petitions for private water connections through site visits and engineer’s reports, determining how much water should be awarded and from which pipeline, and setting the price and conditions of each license or concession. Commissioners also attempted to ensure that no one took more water than their title allowed and coordinated the construction and maintenance of the pipe network in general. In this case some specialized knowledge was developed; for example, Councilman Ordoño de Samudio was selected thirty times as pipeline commissioner between 1650 and 1683 and was considered an expert on the topic.

The engineer (alarife) in charge of the pipelines was another important person and one who gained influence over the course of the seventeenth century. In 1609 the Cabildo determined that the municipal pipelines and public fountains should be maintained by a specialist (LCL.14.XII.1609), and from 1625 the post was always held by one of the official city engineers (see also San Cristóbal 1993).10 In reality, only three engineers managed the pipelines for the majority of the century: Clemente de Mansilla (1625–1634), Juan de Mansilla (1635–1661), and Pedro Fernández de Valdés (1661–1706).11 The inheritance and concentration of knowledge about the urban drinking water system in these men was extraordinary, and their opinions were commonly heard (and heeded) during Cabildo meetings. Finally, it is necessary to mention that the engineers worked with African slaves, for whom little documentary evidence exists. In only one Cabildo meeting from the period in question was one of these men mentioned by name: Pedro Angola, who was Juan de Mansilla’s slave (LCL.18.IV.1654).12 Apparently this was a position requiring skill, but also entailing damp and difficult conditions.

The location of both springs, topographically above the city by approximately 100 m, provided the necessary inclination to canalize the water toward the main water tank located in the urban core, called the Caridad water tank (Caja de Agua de la Caridad) for its location next to the Caridad Hospital (Hospital de la Caridad). Water was distributed from this central storage point into the public and private pipelines. From the outset, this infrastructure contributed to inequality in water access. The pipeline network’s center of gravity was the Caridad water tank in the northeast section of the city center. Those who lived above this key point in the network, including the farmers in the rural area around the springs, had to cede their rights to the zones lower in the flow of water.13

The momentous inauguration of the first public fountain in Lima’s Plaza Mayor in 1578 was part of a larger municipal project to bring piped drinking water to city residents.14 This project, in planning since 1552, culminated in the three main municipal pipelines that were constructed by 1613 (Bromley and Barbegalata 1945; see also LCL 26.XI.1610). The pipes were ceramic, made by city potters, and buried below city streets (LCL 14.IX.1660; see also San Cristóbal 2005, 185–86). The water for all three flowed from the Caridad water tank (routes and construction dates summarized in Table 1 and illustrated in Figure 3). The Cabildo received petitions for private connections of running water from these three main municipal pipelines, to be installed in homes, monasteries and convents, and institutions (like hospitals). These connections were for specific amounts of water withdrawn from the main pipelines, called pajas or reales, which were units of measurement based on the flow of water through pipes of controlled circumference (Table 2). The majority of petitions were submitted during the twenty-year period in which the three pipelines were being constructed (summarized in Table 3).

A total of sixty-three petitions for drinking water connections were recorded in the LCL from 1578 to 1700 for Municipal Pipelines 1 to 3, and a total of eighty connections could be identified for these three pipelines using additional data from Bromley and Barbegalata (1945).15 Information about these petitions and the eventual concession of titles is listed in Tables 4 through 6, including biographical and institutional information about the petitioners and the locations of these concessions are mapped in Figures 3 and 4. Several significant patterns can be observed. Spatially, below the Caridad water tank the three original municipal pipelines were distributed fairly evenly over the central section of Lima’s original thirteen-by-nine city block grid, which would have been the main area populated when the original water system plans were initiated in the 1550s. As visible on the map of the pipeline system in 1613 (Figure 3), however, there is a clear concentration of concessions in the area just below (west and northwest of) the Caridad water tank, with distribution tapering off the farther outward one travels below this tank (especially to the southwest). The map of concessions along the municipal pipelines after 1613 demonstrates a similar geographic concentration, albeit with more connections, especially to the immediate southwest of the Caridad. The branching of Municipal Pipeline 3 speaks to the need to serve several key points, including religious institutions (e.g., the Jesuit Novitiate College and the Encarnacion Monastery, map references n and c, respectively), and residences of important people not included along prior municipal pipeline routes (e.g., Councilmen don Martín de Ampuero and don Diego de la Presa, map references i and j).

Straightforward social patterns are also apparent. Taken together, the sociospatial patterns of the water concessions paint a picture of the distribution of power in the city. The Cabildo designed and implemented the original municipal water system and, not surprisingly, it served many of the homes of the mayors and councilmen. Indeed, many of these connections were awarded for free, in recognition of service to the pipeline projects and other public works. Public fountains, ostentatiously part of the original plans, experienced delays in construction and many interruptions in service. For example, in 1596 the San Sebastián fountain was completed but no water was flowing (*LCL* 19.IV.1596), in 1612 the drainage of the San Marcelo fountain failed, causing problems, and in 1621 the public fountains were found to be in a general state of disrepair (*LCL* 5.VII.1621). When an important individual’s house was located outside of the basic layout of the three pipelines, this layout was adjusted, as demonstrated by the repeated branching of Pipeline 3 described earlier. Overall we find that powerful people were living in close proximity to one another and that the municipal pipelines passed by many of their houses. Additional water connections were easier to install near previous connections. For example, Captain Juan Fernandez de Heredia’s connection on Municipal Pipeline 3 (map reference i) was facilitated by the prior existence of a water storage tank at the corner of the Carvajal Statue (*Marmol de Carvajal*) that served both a public fountain and don Diego de la Presa’s connection (map references k and i). Religious institutions also influenced pipeline routes: The powerful Dominican, Franciscan, and Jesuit orders, among others, were all served by the original municipal pipelines. These features all contributed to the concentration of water connections in certain sectors of the city; power, wealth, status, and water connections went hand in hand.

Nearly no concessions were given to merchants or professionals (who were not royal officials) and none at all to artisans, laborers, or other lower ranking members of society. The Indian neighborhood—Santiago del Cercado—was not served at all by the three original municipal pipelines and was only provided with one public fountain.17 This neighborhood, reported to be home to 800 Indians and eighty African slaves in 1629 (Bromley and Barbagelata 1945, 59), provides a key illustration of the sociospatial patterns of access described earlier: The Cercado was actually located right along the water main leading from the springs to the Caridad water tank, very near to the main area with springs. Because it was located “above” the Caridad, however, it had only limited access to the city’s drinking water (Ramón 2011). The African population is only referenced with regard to the pipelines in terms of slave labor toward their construction and maintenance and as water sellers. These lower status populations used the public fountains, purchased water from sellers who made deliveries via mule cargo, or collected water from the river, even though from 1552 river water was considered too polluted for most domestic uses (Cogorno 2011).

This list was compiled using Bromley and Barbagelata’s (1945) reconstruction of Lima’s water system in 1613 and from the petitions recorded in the Libros de Cabildos de Lima. The connections listed as pre-1613 by Bromley and Barbagelata are not included in Tables 1 or 3. Of the twenty-seven connections, two were for public fountains, three for royal institutions, one for a municipal institution, six for religious or charitable institutions, and the remaining fifteen for private individuals. Of these individuals, nine were for mayors or city councilmen or families of Cabildo members. This includes three sitting mayors, three water judges, three pipeline commissioners, and one public fountain special project commissioner. The other private individuals were also high ranking, including families of conquistadores, a prótomédico (royally appointed doctor charged with regulating medical education and practice), two rectors of San Marcos University (the first university in Lima and in the Americas), a correo mayor (postmaster of the colonial mails), and a royal mining official. Several individuals have military titles, and about half have the honorific “don” or “dona.” The wax seller Antonio Barrera seems out of place in this list, but the location of his well-known establishment, directly on the pipeline route, might indicate why he was included. Women seem to be mentioned on water petitions only when they are high-status individuals: The two mentioned here were a widow of a conquistador and a daughter of a mayor. There were two petitions for connection on Municipal Pipeline 1 with unidentifiable locations: Francisco de Carbajal (1646) and Pedro de Castro Ysasaga (1655). Biographical information comes from the Libros de Cabildos de Lima, Bromley (2005), Bromley and Barbagelata (1945), and Lohmann Villena (1983a, 1983b, 1983c, 1993).

Private pipelines were designed to serve only their final points, and typically petitions could not be submitted to install additional connections to take water from along their courses (although there were several exceptions). The person or group who requested a private pipeline paid for its construction. These pipes generally served religious orders or neighborhoods where the municipal pipelines did not reach, which were called far neighborhoods (LCL 28. XI.1651; San Cristóbal 2005). The Cabildo supervised the construction of at least twenty-one private pipelines between 1600 and 1700. Information about these pipelines is presented in Table 7 and they are mapped in Figure 4.

Examination of the petitions for private pipelines demonstrates that from the perspective of potable water provision, the city below the Caridad water tank was divided into two zones: that of “above” and that of “below.” The zone below suffered from continuous water shortages. This zone, identified as “below the principal plaza” and “the bottom half of the city” (LCL 20.III.1629), included the far neighborhoods (LCL 28. XI.1651) like La Merced, La Encarnación, San Sebastián, San Marcelo, and Guadalupe (see also LCL 9. III.1620, 29.X.1671). These neighborhoods all had public fountains that experienced water shortages. The word below references actual topographic position but also location at the end of the pipelines; in other words, the lowest point in the water flow, to the northwest and southwest of the Plaza Mayor. These zones lacked water because those located above (along the pipeline) used the water, often taking water in excess of what their titles allowed and consequently letting little water flow to the end of the pipe (LCL 9. III.1606, 11.VIII.1621, 20.III.1629). Other “poor” zones were left totally outside of the network; their residents had to buy water from water sellers or collect it themselves from distant public fountains or the river (LCL 11.VII.1621, 20.III.1629).18

The construction of private pipelines was an imperfect solution to the problem of supplying the general population with drinking water. For example, each monastery or convent that constructed a private pipeline was also required to install a public fountain for general use. In theory, this would contribute to a more egalitarian distribution of water. In practice, though, the convents did not comply with this obligation. In 1670, for instance, the Cabildo found that none of the public fountains were running as they should, and some were not even built (LCL 3.IX.1670; see also 25. II.1671, 12.V.1671). These fountains are mapped in Figure 4, but this is an important case where installation of infrastructure did not necessarily equate to proper functioning of infrastructure. Therefore, in the second half of the century, the Cabildo decided that it was also necessary to expand the municipal pipeline network. It built two new municipal pipelines. The first was built for a new fountain in the Plaza Mayor and was finished in 1650. The second was constructed to serve the San Marcelo neighborhood. Plans for this were initiated in 1668, although the pipeline was still not completed or functioning properly in 1692, mainly due to lack of funding (LCL 20.IV.1668, 20.VII.1668, 30.VII.1668, 16.1.1669, 12.II.1669, 16.IX.1692). Details about these pipelines are also included in Table 7 and Figure 4.

In general, the Cabildo was not able to provide adequate drinking water to all of the residents of the city. Some problems were due to the slow construction of the municipal pipelines, like that of San Marcelo, which took nearly thirty years to complete. Others were due to the difficulty of controlling illegal water connections (LCL 21.I.1605, 7.I.1611, 2.I.1636), which was compounded by the fact that the Cabildo lacked a registry—either a list or a map—that detailed the legal connections. The Cabildo had sought to create such a map of the pipelines and connections at several times throughout the seventeenth century without success (Bell 2012). In 1665 it ordered engineer Clemente de Mansilla to make a pipeline map, but he died before completing this work. In 1691 Pedro Fernández de Valdés was similarly reprimanded for failing to submit his Cabildo-ordered map. As far as is known, the first map of the pipeline network was made in 1787, of which only a copy from 1862 remains (Ramón 2010). It is unclear why this project was so difficult to complete, although it might relate to the way knowledge of the pipelines was stored: as practical working knowledge held by the engineers and their slaves. This was observed in Cabildo records from 1790, which described the “depositing” of knowledge of the water system in an elderly engineer and his still living slave (Ramón 2011, 88).

From a broader perspective, it is easy to note social distinctions in the patterns of drinking water access that were both purposefully and inadvertently influenced by the Cabildo’s governance strategies. Even though the Cabildo (and some religious orders) employed a discourse supporting the necessity of providing water to all, and especially to poor neighborhoods, in reality it was the most powerful residents and the monasteries and convents that enjoyed running water. These areas were strategically located just below the Caridad water tank in the city center or, perhaps better said, the Caridad water tank had been strategically located near this neighborhood. No titles were awarded to either African or Indian individuals, and the Indian neighborhood (Santiago del Cercado) had only one public fountain, located “above” the Caridad water tank and thus in a relatively low-power location in the network. Moreover, the spatial distribution of the pipelines was one of the features that produced the differences between a rich neighborhood and a poor one. The division of water not only reflected social inequalities but also created and reinforced divisions between groups and zones. Discussion and Conclusions

Lima’s hydraulic space was oriented by the east–west flow of the Rímac River and the location of the springs to the northeast and topographically above the city. The installation of its components, though, especially the drinking water pipeline, followed the desires, objectives, and values of Lima’s human residents more than it did natural features. The LCL demonstrates that effective governance of Lima’s hydraulic space was difficult. The Cabildo repeatedly failed to achieve its primary objective of securing a clean and copious flow of drinking water in the municipal pipeline network. Problems with water supply were nearly always attributed to infrastructural and management problems, rather than “natural” issues like aquifer depletion or spring location. Although biophysical aspects of water availability and conduction were significant to drinking water supply, especially the seasonality of aquifer levels and the slope needed for gravity-based flow, drinking water access was most closely linked to the installation of infrastructure and to the spatial proximity down-flow from the Caridad water tank. The unequal distribution of drinking water infrastructure reflected colonial social divisions but also helped to create and widen gaps between groups based on access to water. Although the Cabildo made grand statements about the importance of providing water to all city residents, only the most powerful members of colonial society had running water in their homes.

This interpretation of Lima’s municipal water system was only possible through interrogation of the Cabildo archives from a political ecology perspective. Most significantly, this perspective asks direct questions about the linked biophysical, material, social, political, and economic influences on patterns of water access of individuals, groups, and regions. Political ecological analysis of contemporary urban drinking water systems shows strong inequality in access, and historical cases like that of Lima suggest that such patterns are not new (although the specific relationships between past inequality and current conditions need to be clearly demonstrated and not assumed). Bakker’s (2003) archipelago metaphor is also helpful for historical water analysis because it suggests that multiple, overlapping strategies are commonly used in urban drinking water delivery, including public and private supply systems, and that spatial coverage of water infrastructure is typically partial and uneven. This level of nuance is not always considered in historical situations, even though it was likely present. Finally, political ecological work has shown how uneven water access also leads to further social differentiation and thus shapes and influences power relations, instead of merely reflecting or responding to them (Swyngedouw 1999; Gandy 2004; Loftus 2009). This can also be observed at a broader level, in terms of urban–rural relations, and usually results in significant urban bias. In colonial Lima, the governance strategies applied in the management of potable water resources not only led to the development of the urban space and the specific sociospatial patterns of access to water within the city but also contributed to differentiation between urban and rural spaces. A significant urban bias was observed, with the city’s hydraulic reach or water footprint extending beyond the urban zone into surrounding farmland. Although these questions and approaches might seem basic to political ecology, they have not frequently been applied to the study of early colonial natural resource access and never before to water in colonial Peru.

The relationship between the history of water systems and the history of cities also warrants further consideration (Swyngedouw, Kaika, and Castro 2002; Gandy 2003, 2004; Swyngedouw 2004). As Bakker (2003, 332) wrote, “The water network is, in part, an artefact of urbanization.” Given the great investment in construction and the difficulty of changing a pipeline network already built, water systems typically reflect water needs at one point in time and generally only cover a portion of a city, usually the most affluent part. Although such observations are generally made for contemporary urban situations, they are also true for colonial Lima and likely for other colonial Spanish cities in the Americas, which were established early in the colonial period and incorporated complicated and sophisticated water systems from the start. There are interesting remnants and traces of these colonial water systems in modern ones. For example, even though the city of Lima now draws drinking water from distant Andean lakes, the location of the Atarjea spring used from 1578 is still the location of Lima’s central reservoir and water treatment plant (SEDAPAL 2013). This speaks to an enduring orientation of Lima’s hydraulic space, even amidst modernization, development, and great change. With further historical analysis, it might be possible to reconstruct additional legacies of the colonial system with greater significance to modern patterns of water access.

There are clear advantages of applying a political ecology approach to historical water systems, but moving backward in time has methodological implications for the types of questions that can be asked and the types of data that might be available. Closer attention to material traces left on the landscape, including both biophysical and sociocultural features, is necessary, and a useful guide can be found in the hydraulic archaeology approach. In this article, I used written descriptions to explain the design and construction as well as the day-to-day management of infrastructure. In other cases, archaeological analysis of infrastructure might provide a useful complement, as historical documents have significant limitations for a political
ecological analysis. With the LCL we have only the finalized or “clean” version of the Cabildo meeting agendas, and rarely hear dissent among Cabildo members, let alone direct contributions from lesser heard voices, like Indians or African slaves. The LCL and other similar sources do, however, provide key insights into “how Europeans conceptualized ‘their’ colonial landscapes and how those imaginations produced material consequences for colonized people, environments and wildlife” (Offen 2004, 29). In the case of Lima’s water, we have a clear representation of the administrative, legal, engineering, and measurement concepts developed and applied to water. Understanding these colonial practices and conceptualizations, as well as their consequences and legacies for postcolonial resource management and environmental science, is a principal goal of historical political ecology (Offen 2004; D. K. Davis 2009). Although here I have delved only briefly into one history of water access, I have endeavored to show the utility of the historical political ecology perspective for explaining sociospatial patterns of natural resource access in the past.
