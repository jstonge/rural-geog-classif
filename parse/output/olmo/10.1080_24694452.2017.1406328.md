Dig Your Own Well: A Political Ecology of Rural Institutions in Western Sub-Saharan Africa

Leif V. Brottem

Global Development Studies Program, Grinnell College

This article examines the influence of changing demographic and human mobility patterns on present-day local institutions and access to basic services in Mali, West Africa. It offers a new political ecological perspective on how local institutions function in rural West Africa and how the spatial character of these institutions shapes service access. The article brings together evidence from archival sources, census data spanning the twentieth century, field surveys, and cartographic data in a novel geospatial analysis of decentralized governance in the western Malian region of Kayes. The article's principal argument is that historically higher population densities in Kayes's semiarid Sahelian zone compared with its subhumid Sudanian zone resulted in higher numbers of officially recognized villages and smaller administrative territories in the former. As population growth took off in the subhumid zone in recent decades, this important institutional difference—codified by law under a succession of governments—has created stark disparities in access to the most fundamental public service: safe drinking water. This article measures these disparities at several geographic scales and explains their causes, which include the permanent settlement of frontier areas where disease vectors had historically kept populations sparse and mobile. These findings reveal the fundamental yet overlooked spatial form and function of rural institutions that have a critical development mandate in one of the poorest regions in the world. Key Words: administrative territory, demography, political decentralization, population mobility, West Africa.

Misunderstanding of the geographical form and function of local institutions is a significant obstacle to alleviating poverty in developing regions. Despite the substantial progress made since the spatial turn in the social sciences, uncritical and aspatial assumptions about how institutions function in rural areas tend to dominate development research and policy. This article addresses this problem by providing a deeper understanding and a way of measuring the fundamentally geographical nature of local institutions in rural West Africa. Western sub-Saharan Africa is an important case, as it is characterized by persistently high rates of rural poverty and it is representative of many developing regions in terms of its colonial history, recent political reforms, and legally plural governance structures.

This article focuses on the power-laden tension between territory and mobility and how environmental changes shape their interactions, which, in turn, drive institutional formation over decadal timescales. As a way to explicate these processes and their impacts, the article brings together theoretical work done by scholars of legal pluralism (Blomley 1994; Meinzen-Dick and Pradhan 2002; von Benda-Beckmann and von Benda-Beckmann 2016) with political ecology and its attention to local governance and access to resources (Lund 2008; Agrawal and Perrin 2009; Berry 2009; Ribot and Larson 2013). Political ecology has a long tradition of engaging with questions of power, governance, and environmental change (Blaikie and Brookfield 1987; Neumann 1998; Adger et al. 2001; Peet, Robbins, and Watts 2011), yet insufficient work has been done to critically engage with and measure the geographical constitution of local institutions, their legal frameworks, and how they reflect interactions with various ecological factors and changing human–environment relations. The close attention of this article’s analysis to the micro foundations of local institutions in developing regions provides an important complement to the recent emphasis within political ecology on neoliberal governance (McCarthy and Prudham 2004; Mansfield 2008) and the hegemonic tendencies of the central state (Harris, Goldin, and Sneddon 2015).

This article presents the Republic of Mali’s Kayes Region as a case study to argue that, in terms of basic service provision, local institutions in rural West Africa function as historical–geographic formations. This concept extends both Cleaver and De Koning’s (2015) critical institutionalism framework and Ribot and Peluso’s (2003) theory of access by demonstrating that local institutions in the region are the outcome of long-term interactions among human mobility, settlement history, environmental conditions, and political relations at specific and measurable scales (von Benda-Beckmann and von Benda-Beckmann 2016). These institutions function in ways that produce and maintain sharp geographical inequalities in access to safe drinking water and other basic services. Moreover, these disparities closely fit the institutional geography of administrative decentralization that characterizes West Africa and most other developing regions (Faguet 2014; Carlitz 2017). Decentralization increased the importance of legally plural local institutions that are still evolving in terms of legitimacy and effectiveness (Hagmann and Péclard 2010) but are nonetheless responsible for providing access to basic services such as safe drinking water (Caldeira, Foucault, and Rota-Graziosi 2012; Asthana 2013; Chauvet et al. 2015).

This article critically engages with the erroneous assumption underpinning most studies of basic service access, that local institutions function uniformly across their geographic jurisdictions. It does this by using Mali’s Kayes Region to provide the first large-scale analysis of intralocal water access coverage in the high-deprivation region of West Africa. Intralocal access refers to the pervasive situation where large percentages of rural inhabitants live in dispersed settlements that do not have access to local-level infrastructure even though official statistics report that they do. This is due to the fact that administrative villages—the fundamental legal unit of governance in Mali—are, in reality, composed of clusters of settlements that vary in number and geographic organization depending on their local histories. By explaining how access to basic services, particularly safe water, is shaped by the ways in which these institutions function as historical–geographic formations, this article accomplishes two important tasks.

First, it reveals an important yet invisible spatial barrier to basic service provision resulting from the legal distinction between administrative villages and unrecognized settlements known as hamlets. This distinction, which is inscribed in Mali’s legal-territorial administration, is the most crucial manifestation of local institutions functioning as historical–geographic formations. Second, by measuring and modeling the impacts of these formations, this article opens theoretical space and provides a new empirical approach to analyzing the political ecological underpinnings of local institutions in rural developing regions. As this
article shows, these previously overlooked underpinnings have important impacts on equitable public service access, representation, and enfranchisement in nascent democratic institutions (Ribot, Chatre, and Lankina 2008; Perreault 2014).

Decentralization and Public Service Provision

Research that focuses on public services such as safe water provision in rural areas has generally overlooked geographical obstacles by relying on aspatial and ahistorical conceptions of local institutions. In western sub-Saharan Africa, this is a problematic oversight given that local institutions in the region reflect a high degree of continuity between customary political structures and contemporary, formal ones. Whether these contemporary institutions are considered in various social science fields as syncretic (Galvan and Sil 2007) or legally plural (Benjamin 2008; Lentz 2013), decentralization in West Africa has produced local institutions that work “differently in practice due to differences in demographic and social factors” (Erk 2014, 536).

Yet, despite the general validity of this claim, its geographical implications have yet to be carefully investigated. Specifically, local institutions created through democratic decentralization embody and reflect the tensions between necessary forms of mobility and territoriality at various spatial scales (Lima 2005). Although these tensions most directly pertain to land tenure (Le Meur 2006; Idelman 2009) and resource access (Gautier, Hautdidier, and Gazull 2011), they are also manifestations of long-term and geographically complex patterns of settlement and political organization (Kopytoff 1987). Across the West African savanna belt, institutions and the practice of governance are deeply rooted in historic patterns of population movements (De Bruijn, Van Dijk, and Foeken 2001; Lentz 2013) and spatial relations (Howard and Shain 2005) that shaped the form and function of customary relations. Autochthony or “first-comer” status dictates land tenure regimes and associated rural political institutions in a system that has endured even as sparsely populated frontier areas have become more densely settled, thus obviating their original purpose.

A large body of scholarly work has documented these systems and how they have responded to political (Nijenhuis 2003; Fay, Yaouaga, and Quiminal 2006; Hagberg 2010), environmental (McMillan 1995), and demographic (Raynaut 1997) change while remaining central to the everyday politics in rural West Africa. Nevertheless, this work has had limited influence on the study of formal institutions and how the deep customary imprint influences or even constrains their basic functioning (Gonin, Kotlok, and Lima 2013). This is an important oversight because, as this article shows, democratic decentralization brings the customary and formal even closer together through legal measures and administrative practices. Through decentralization, municipalities and their constituent villages act as the mediating structures of space and power (Blomley 1994) with important and measurable consequences for the provision of basic services at the local level.

Research on local-level safe water provision and access has instead focused on nongeographical factors such as financial constraints (Gadgil 1998), economic efficiency (Asthana 2013), technology (Peter-Varbanets et al. 2009; P. Hunter, MacDonald, and Carter 2010), and health impacts (P. Hunter, MacDonald, and Carter 2010; Pickering and Davis 2012). In terms of local institutions and access to safe water, Singh (2014) suggested that political decentralization remains a contested process in India and that greater devolution in the water sector would have a democratizing effect on local institutions. The same author argued that decentralization in the Philippines could work to privatize the drinking water sector, which contrasts with the position that associates decentralization with neoliberal governance and policies of resource privatization (Perreault 2005; Harris, Goldin, and Sneddon 2015). Other studies also emphasize the fraught politics of decentralization, such as de Palencia, Fernández, and Pérez-Foguet (2011), who used a rural water supply program in Tanzania to argue that more downward accountability and policy coherence are needed for decentralization to work effectively. In another study, Brannstrom (2004) found downward accountability to be lacking in Brazil’s decentralized water management program as well.

Decentralization increases the financial burden on local governments, which inevitably face difficult choices in terms of public investment (Carlitz 2017). In West Africa, Caldeira, Foucault, and Rota-Graziosi (2012) found that decentralization improved basic service delivery among municipalities in Bénin but highlighted important differences between richer and poorer ones. Poorer municipalities, the authors found, focus more on drinking water provision to the
detriment of primary education. Richer municipalities, even in rural areas, are often characterized by social ties such as emigrant associations and nongovernmental organizations that can alleviate pressure on public finances by supporting basic service provision (Chauvet et al. 2015). This is consistent with the findings of Pullan et al. (2014), who found that across sub-Saharan Africa, inequalities of improved water access are greater among subnational units within individual African countries than between urban and rural areas compared across the continent. Based on a five-country comparison, Yu et al. (2014) similarly argued that household surveys should be designed to capture province-level variation in water and sanitation coverage.

Studies that do seek to geographically analyze local-level water access and provision face two shortcomings that this article addresses. First, local institutions are assumed to function uniformly, when in fact they vary geographically in terms of function and capacity (Benjamin 2008; Boone 2013). Second, when empirical data are used, they tend to come from sources such as the USAID Demographic and Health Survey (DHS) and UNICEF Multi Indicator Cluster Survey (MICS). Although these data are necessary to understand the problem of basic service coverage, they are insufficient for two reasons. First, they are intended to be representative at the household scale and they are based on representative clusters, so they miss any local-level geographic dimensions of service deprivation. As an example, spatial models based on DHS data, such as the one presented in Magalhães, Barnett, and Clements (2011), predict uniformly low rates of coverage across vast subnational rural areas in West Africa that, in fact, have high variability in coverage due to how local institutions function as historical–geographic formations.

Taken together, these studies highlight the need for a more geographically informed analysis of institutions and public service access. Not only do these studies overlook the imprint of customary institutions on the village and municipality—the institution legally responsible for basic services across West Africa—but local-level data are rarely if ever used in such spatial-analytic efforts.

Historical–Geographic Formations

Legal Frameworks

Mali’s administrative system reflects political and legal decisions made under French colonial rule (approximately 1880–1960), the postindependence reign of Modibo Keita (1960–1968), the military administration of Moussa Traoré (1968–1991), and several democratically elected governments since 1991. The current decentralized political-administrative system was initiated following the country’s 1991 democratic revolution, which was part of the democratization wave that transformed the political systems of many sub-Saharan countries. Since the 1991 revolution, political power has been gradually devolved to 703 newly created communes (municipalities), which replaced the arrondissement, which itself was a replacement for the colonial-era canton. Municipalities are typically composed of ten to fifteen administrative villages but can include as few as four. The hierarchy of legal-administrative units, which reflects the French administrative system, is given in Table 1.

A critical characteristic of Malian municipalities is that they were created not as territorial jurisdictions but instead as lists of villages whose residents wished to be within the same political community due to kinship or other affinities (Fay, Yaoouaga, and Quiminal 2006). This administrative designation represented an intentional avoidance of the geographical complexities of settlement history and associated land tenure concerns due to the potential for local-level conflict (Idelman 2009). Mali’s decentralization process was successfully carried out, but this particular issue was never resolved and remains embedded within the new institutional structure itself. Ironically, the avoidance of the geographical nature of local political relationships has rendered it an even more critical issue even as it remains largely invisible in the administrative realm.

Through Mali’s decentralization process, legal jurisdiction over education, health, and water provision was legally devolved from the central to municipal governments (Benjamin 2008). The administrative village as a legal and political entity in Mali is the key unit of analysis in this article. Villages have been the basic administrative unit in Mali since independence in 1960 (Law 60-3/AL/RS of 7 June 1960), although they originated during the period of French colonial rule (Mann 2014). Rural development projects, tax collection, and the national census have been organized at the village level ever since (N’Diaye Keita 1972). A crucial aspect of this administrative system is that most villages actually represent clusters of settlements, of which only one is officially recognized by the government. Inhabitants of all of the settlements, are officially counted as residents of the sole administrative village regardless of how geographically dispersed they might be. A hamlet refers to a settlement that is not legally or administratively recognized as a village by the government (Duvall 2006; Idelman 2009).

The Village–Hamlet Divide

The administrative village as a formal, legal entity was rooted in French colonial conceptions of rural social organization and customary relations from the outset (cf. Brasseur 1968). In 1912, preeminent French colonial ethnographer Maurice Delafosse conceived of the village in the indigenous Malinké terms of familial habitation (dougou or soo; Delafosse 1912) as opposed to the “farm” settlement known as the bougou or hamlet (Duvall 2006). Describing these in terms of mobility and host–stranger land tenure relations, Delafosse (1912) asserted, “It is necessary to confide administrative functions to one of the village inhabitants to facilitate public life and prevent the worsening of disputes that inevitably arise between different families” (128, author’s translation). Not coincidentally, Delafosse’s description closely fits the vivre ensemble motto that inspired the decentralization process, which sought to build on the same customary relations some eighty years later. Administrative villages thus represent a high degree of continuity between the colonial and postindependence eras.

This continuity and the persistent tensions within village–hamlet relations are clearly reflected in statements made by local officials in neighboring municipalities, but seventy-eight years apart. In a 1933 census of the former arrondissement of Sebecoro, which is located in this article’s study area, colonial officials stated:

It was necessary to oblige the isolated inhabitants to return to their villages of origin once cultivation was finished. Their bush settlements should only serve as seasonal camps. If not, one ends up with a scattering that leads to auto-proclamation of chieftainships and soon thereafter impulses of emancipation. (Delmond 1933, 4, author’s translation)

In a statement reflecting the great difficulty involved in changing the status of hamlets, a deputy mayor in the neighboring municipality of Kotouba commented in 2011 on the problem of permanent and populous hamlets by stating, “To become a [administrative] village, a hamlet needs a territory, but they do not have them” (author’s translation). The political difficulty to which the mayor spoke reflects the inseparability of political power and land tenure in rural Mali (Fay, Yaouaga, and Quiminal 2006), which lies at the heart of the village–hamlet distinction.

This long-standing village–hamlet divide is the most important embodiment of historical–geographic formation of institutions in western Mali as it pertains to basic service access and political enfranchisement. Hamlets are systematically excluded from public development plans and infrastructure investment, which is channeled through municipalities to administrative villages. The village–hamlet divide described in this article is the outcome of a series of legal-administrative decisions that have interacted with long-term patterns of population mobility and settlement, environmental conditions, customary land tenure relations, and changing rural livelihoods found throughout the West African savanna belt.

Research conducted in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Nigeria has demonstrated that precolonial settlements were frequently placed around cliffs as a defensive measure during periods of warfare, insecurity, and displacement, most recently during the nineteenth century (Jansen 2005). Settlement proximity to cliffs also reflected the imperative of water access as areas at the base of rock outcroppings provided well-digging
opportunities while providing some protection from disease vectors in low-lying areas. Regional patterns of hamlet formation and abandonment also reflect landscape topography and the need to travel distances of varying length to find arable land (Duvall 2006).

In terms of how hamlet formation has evolved, several scholars have investigated what Stone (1996, 79) called a “series of downhill movements” of settlements in West Africa over the past century. Stone cited colonial pacification as a principal cause for these movements but included other factors such as land pressure, disease, government policy, and broader economic changes. In his study of landscape ecology in western Mali’s Bafing area, Duvall (2008) described nineteenth-century defensively oriented settlements located on rock outcrops that were abandoned due to poor soil and inaccessible water. Mirroring the settlement patterns described in this article, contemporary settlements in the Bafing area are dispersed on low-lying areas where cultivation is possible and wells can be easily dug. In a corollary study, Augusseau and Pare (2004) investigated a part of southern Burkina Faso that was largely uninhabited as recently as the mid-1970s. Autochthonous groups in the area remain clustered in higher elevation settlements as migrants have established hamlets in the fertile low-lying areas.

In each case, customary political relations based on autochthony structured these settlement patterns through what is referred to as the “mother” village–hamlet (dougou–bougou) relationship. This relationship historically carried a specific meaning that referred to temporary hamlets that were justifiably seen as satellites that were subservient to “mother” settlements. Nonetheless, during the twentieth century, this system rooted in conquest, customary land tenure relations, and mobility became an integral and legal part of Malian formal institutions through the establishment of administrative villages.

This system of administrative villages, first formulated by the French, was codified at independence and left unchanged by both the military coup of 1968 and the democratic revolution of 1991. After Mali’s democratic revolution, the legal status of administrative villages was reaffirmed through several laws that were passed in 1995, particularly Article 60 of Law 95-034 (Messer 2001), which formalized the current system of subnational jurisdictions (Boko 2002).

The static nature of Mali’s administrative village structure over the past half-century belies and increasingly conflicts with the facets of rural life on which it was established—customary land tenure relations and well-established patterns of human mobility—as well as several recent decades of substantial demographic change in the country. Between 1967 and 2009, the Kayes Region’s population rose by 178 percent and democratic decentralization led to a 160 percent increase in the number of municipal jurisdictions. Yet, the number of administrative villages in the region expanded by a mere 10 percent due to the fact that the system is anchored in the customary system of host–stranger land tenure relations (Table 2).

Due to concerns about losing land tenure rights and maintaining political power, groups holding autochthonous, first-comer host status vehemently oppose the conversion of hamlets occupied by strangers, or late-coming migrants, into administrative villages even when their size and importance would justify it (Idelman 2009). As a result of this tension and the financial burden that additional administrative villages place on municipal governments, hamlets rarely gain official status.

This host–stranger system that underpins the administrative village system has long provided an organizing framework for human mobility in West Africa as individuals and communities have moved in response to conflict, changing disease environments, land scarcity, or any number of other factors (De Bruijn, Van Dijk, and Foeken 2001; Lentz 2013). These long-standing patterns of mobility and their associated tenure relations have shaped present-day institutions and the services they provide through their influence on the size and geographic configuration of administrative villages as well as the number of hamlets associated with each village.

Differing population and settlement distributions that result from this history of human mobility continue to determine where available farmland is located and internal settlement frontiers are likely to be found. In certain areas, population movements toward these frontiers have led to the proliferation of hamlets, which remain politically marginalized. The Kayes Region as a Comparative Case Study

The Malian administrative region of Kayes provides an excellent subnational case to explore the interrelated issues of human mobility, settlement geography, local institutions, and present-day water access for two reasons. First, Kayes encompasses an eco-climatological gradient from the grassland-dominated, semiarid Sahel to the subhumid Sudano-Guinean zone, where woodland is the dominant land cover (Figure 1). Importantly, Figure 1 shows how the region is bisected by the approximate historic northern limits of two diseases that kept population densities low throughout sub-Saharan Africa: trypanosomiasis (sleeping sickness) and onchocerciasis (river blindness; Courtin et al. 2008).

The disease vector boundary shown in Figure 1 divides the Kayes Region into a northern zone and a southern zone. The contrasting settlement and mobility patterns of these two zones shaped the uneven historical–geographic structures of their local institutions and form the basis of a subsequent comparative analysis, which is further supported by evidence from the region’s demographic history. Kayes has much lower population density than Mali’s other regions, except for the three located in the Sahara desert (Gao, Kidal, and Tomboctou). In 1976, its overall population density was 7.2 persons per square kilometer compared with 15.3 in Sikasso, 18.3 in Segou, and 14.2 in Mopti. By 2009, the total population in Kayes was 1,996,812 and its population density had more than doubled to 16.6 per km\(^2\). Other Malian regions had also become much more crowded, with Segou and Sikasso reaching densities of 36.8 and 39.5, respectively. As a result of these relative imbalances in population, the Kayes Region, particularly its southern zone district of Kita, is more land abundant than other parts of the country. Kita’s land abundance and the opportunity to cultivate cotton—an important source of cash for smallholder farmers (Koenig 2008)—has made it a prime destination for migrants over the past two decades, with important consequences for the geographic distribution of hamlets, land tenure relations, and access to basic services for their inhabitants.

The comparative analysis presented in this article is based on data collected from a sample of forty-four municipalities in the Kayes Region. After excluding urban areas, the sample units were selected randomly to evenly represent the northern (\( n = 21 \)) and southern (\( n = 23 \)) zones, the eco-climatological gradient that characterizes Kayes, and the significant demographic differences between the two zones. To best capture these differences, the sample focuses on the districts of Kita (southern zone), Diema (northern zone), and Nioro (northern zone). Four of Nioro’s sixteen municipalities were excluded because they were considered to be urban, lacked any hamlets to measure, or lacked sufficient data. Nine municipalities from the Diema district were added to the northern zone sample to compensate for this exclusion and more fully capture the zone’s north–south gradient. Taken together, the sample units encompass 31 percent of Kayes’s total population and 40 percent of its total area. They represent the sharpest contrast in terms of how historical–geographic formations shape present-day access to improved drinking water sources. The sources of this contrast, which involves interwoven histories of political systems, population movements, and disease exposure, are analyzed in the next section.

Spatial Analysis of Settlement Geography

To measure and analyze hamlets as a key outcome of historical–geographic institutional formation, this article necessarily draws on a mix of quantitative and qualitative methods. It uses geographic information systems (GIS) analysis, spatial statistics, and an extra sum of squares (ESS) regression model to describe and explain the causes of water access disparities at the village and municipal scales. These statistical analyses are contextualized within a qualitative narrative about rural institutions that is based on archival and census data, local-level development strategy documents, field interviews, and cartographic sources acquired from the Malian National Geographic Institute (IGM).

First, a GIS was used to quantitatively assess the relationship between area of each administrative village and the number of hamlets associated with that village. Data were acquired from the IGM, National Statistical Institute (INSTAT), and field surveys conducted in the Kayes Region during 2013 and 2014. Kita and Nioro: Contrasting Histories of Demography and Settlement

Until the late twentieth century, Kita remained sparsely populated and economically marginal relative to other parts of the country. It is situated just south of the population arc that is the legacy of precolonial kingdoms whose locations in the semiarid Sahel corresponded roughly to the northern boundary of trypanosomiasis and other disease vectors (Courtin et al. 2008). Until very recently, many parts of the Kita district still had endemic river blindness that made otherwise productive river valleys and surrounding areas largely uninhabitable (Quesnel 1999).

Kita therefore never historically achieved the same population densities as Nioro and northern zone districts such as Diëma. Nioro and parts of Diëma are situated within the historic boundaries of the Ghana Empire, one of West Africa’s most powerful precolonial states located at a strategic position along a principal trans-Saharan trade route and beyond the zone of disease risk (Fage 1962). Political continuities ran from the remnants of Ghana up to the French conquest in the nineteenth century (e.g., the Diarra kingdom) in Nioro (Mauny 1961), and this is reflected in the district’s high density of people and administrative villages, as well as accounts of the district’s social histories.

Population Loss and Recovery in Rural Kayes

Much of western Mali, including Kita and Nioro, had been depopulated in the mid- to late nineteenth century by various internecine conflicts and especially the Islamic jihad led by El Haj Oumar Tall. French pacification at the turn of the century led to a population recovery that was immediately evident in Nioro: From 1909 to 1922, its population grew by 27 percent and Kita’s actually declined by 22 percent from a much lower initial level. By 1967, Nioro’s population density quadrupled from 2.5 to 10.0 people per square kilometer. As an illustrative example, a historical account of Sandaré, one of Nioro’s municipalities, describes how the area emptied out in 1854 following Tall’s conquest and was resettled on the orders of French Colonel Archinard between 1894 and 1900 (Malian Food Security Commission 2007). Kita, by contrast, had a very low density in 1909 of 1.5 people per square kilometer, but it only increased to 3.9 over the next half-century. In terms of prewarfare population, Kita had much less to replace and the incentives to settle and exploit Kita’s open spaces were still decades away. From 1959 to 1967, Nioro’s population increase was 52 percent, whereas Kita’s was only 16 percent and its population remained substantially lower than that of its northern neighbors.

Administrative Villages, Unofficial Hamlets, and Differing Disease Environments

The population recovery in Nioro’s precolonial settlements coincided with Malian independence in 1960 and the critical moment when the blueprint for the country’s village-level administrative structure was established. This translated directly into the high density of administrative villages in Nioro and Yelimane (part of Nioro under colonial rule) relative to that of Kita that persists to the present day. The precolonial imprint in Nioro is further evident in the social histories of its municipalities, nine of sixteen of which trace their direct origins back to precolonial kingdoms, compared with only six out of thirty in Kita.

In 1960, many diseases, including river blindness, were still prevalent in Kita and settlements were still sparse. As long as sleeping sickness and river blindness were endemic in the area, settlements were compelled to either be located on high, rocky terrain or relocate periodically to uninfected areas (J. Hunter 1967), which had the effect of keeping population densities relatively low (Quesnel 1999; Kazianga, Masters, and McMillan 2014). Even well-established villages would occasionally relocate but, more important, temporary hamlets continued to play a key role in rainy season agricultural production and hunting practices (Duvall 2008). Hamlets were not granted administrative status because it was assumed that their residents could access services and be taxed through their mother villages to which they were attached through customary land tenure relations (Idelman 2009).

The policy to exclude hamlets from the legal-administrative system represented a critical decision point vis-à-vis local-level access to public services in the decades to come. The problem of the legal status of hamlet settlements was recognized as early as 1972. In his monograph on the Kayes Region, Keita (1972) stated, “In Malinké country [Kita], we observe a fragmentation of old villages into hamlets that grow quickly but have no legal recognition” (8, author’s translation).

In subsequent years, two developments would stimulate substantial population growth and a
proliferation of hamlets in the Kita district that continues to the present day. The first was the inclusion of Kita in the 1974 World Health Organization’s region-wide onchocerciasis eradication program (Figure 3). The cluster of eradication sites circled in gray corresponds to the Baoulé River valley, which demarcates Kita’s western and northern boundaries and encompasses a large portion of the district. The program came to a successful close in 2002, with river blindness vectors nearly completely eliminated from the district and many other historically infested West African river basins.

The second development occurred in the mid-1990s when land abundance in Kita and increasing land scarcity in Mali’s older cotton-producing areas led the national cotton parastatal, the Compagnie Malienne du Développement des Textiles (CMDT), to construct a new ginning facility in the district capital (Koenig 2008). The district subsequently became an agricultural frontier par excellence through the availability of land, inputs, and vastly improved transportation infrastructure. The CMDT began offering credit and extension services, which led to a boom in cotton farming and further in-migration.

Kita’s population growth, which had begun accelerating in the 1970s, continued to do so through the 2000s. Although Niioro still had nearly double Kita’s population in 1967 (206,700 to 137,630), its population growth slowed and, by the 1987 census, Kita’s had exceeded it (236,880 to 227,310). At the time of the 2009 census, the population densities of the Kita and Niioro districts were 10.9 and 21.5, respectively. Between the 1998 and 2009 censuses, ten out of twenty-six (38 percent) of the municipalities with the highest rates of population growth in the Kayes Region were located in the Kita district.

A key demographic–institutional dynamic is that Kita’s population growth has largely occurred through the establishment and growth of hamlets. Municipalities in Kita have, on average, nearly identical numbers of administrative villages as Niioro (10.9 vs. 11.7) but, because Kita covers a much more expansive geographic area than Niioro, its municipalities are on average 65 percent larger in area (1,076 km\(^2\) vs. 695 km\(^2\)). Further, due to the demographic history of the subhumid parts of the study area, administrative villages in Kita tend to be fewer in number and more clustered than those in Niioro.

These configurations have created large expanses that provide the areas for land-seeking farmers to settle and cultivate. Importantly, many of these land-seeking migrants came from more heavily populated neighboring areas, so they were automatically considered allochtonous “strangers” within customary land tenure relations and political institutions. The substantial presence of migrants in newly established farming hamlets made their transformation into administrative villages exceedingly difficult given how deeply ingrained customary tenure relations are within Mali’s legal system and everyday local politics.

An original contribution of this article is a new empirical measure, administrative reach, that builds on the village–hamlet ratio by measuring the uneven geographic coverage of municipal administration. By measuring the geographical clustering of administrative villages across the territory of an individual municipality, administrative reach plays an important role in analyzing the historical–geographic form of local institutions that is the focus of this article. Statistical Model

This article has thus far established that Malian municipalities and villages are not the homogeneous
administrative units that they are often assumed to be. Instead, they are complex historical–geographic entities that are shaped by their embedded histories of customary tenure relations and the resulting dichotomy between administrative villages and hamlets. These complex relationships shape the way they function, especially in terms of public investment and basic service provision.

An ESS test (Kuiper and Sklar 2012) was used to formally test the influence of administrative reach on the number of hamlets on the full sample of forty-four northern and southern zone municipalities. A similar test was also conducted on a subset in which the set of northern zone municipalities was limited to the Nioro district. To conduct this test, a best subsets regression analysis is first conducted on a reduced model that excludes administrative reach.

The best subsets analyses on both the full sample and the Nioro subset demonstrate that the following variables contribute to our ability predict the number of hamlets in a municipality: (1) village density, (2) distance to nearest urban area, (3) latitude of the municipality, (4) a dummy variable for protected forest area, and (5) zone (southern or northern). Tables 4 and 5 show that a linear regression model with all five variables accounts for 52.9 percent of the variance in hamlets per municipality in the full sample and 53.7 percent in the Nioro subset. Significant variables (\( p < 0.05 \)) include village density (\( \mathrm{km}^2 \)), latitude (meters in UTM zone), and presence of a protected area.

This selection of variables reflects the fact that hamlet proliferation is correlated with factors such as population density, land abundance, and disease history. More critically for the argument presented in this article, if these factors work through local institutions to drive hamlet proliferation—thus supporting the claim of historical–geographic formation—then administrative reach should make a significant contribution to the regression model. After conducting the best subsets analysis, an ESS test was used to determine whether administrative reach provides a significant improvement in our ability to predict the number of hamlets. Tables 6 and 7 show that administrative reach makes a significant contribution to each model. For the full sample, the \( R^2 \) value improves from 0.53 to 0.63 (\( p < 0.05 \)) and, for the Nioro subset, from 0.54 to 0.63 (\( p < 0.05 \)).

These results provide further evidence in the form of a statistical model that there is a clear link between the functioning of local institutions as historical–geographic formations and the demographic importance of hamlets. Among the many consequences of this is the exclusion of large segments of the rural population from officially funded infrastructure programs, including the construction of improved water sources. This exclusion is driven in part by cost. The construction of water delivery infrastructure is very expensive and, thus, more cost effective where populations are dense (World Bank 2009). It is therefore particularly challenging in places such as Kayes where the population is widely dispersed in rural settlements. For example, at a cost of $500,000, piped water adduction systems are only cost effective if a settlement has more than 1,800 inhabitants (FSP Codéveloppement Mali 2006), which surpasses the size of all but the largest hamlets. Even a relatively simple concrete well can cost more than $10,000 in rural Mali. As a result, many of the poorest rural residents are deprived of this fundamental public service.

Most administrative villages in the Kayes Region have at least one improved water source. Beyond this absolute minimum level of coverage, though, substantial inequalities in access are found in the two study area zones. Thirty-nine percent (nine of twenty-three) of southern zone municipalities and only 14 percent (three of twenty-one) of municipalities in the northern zone have two or fewer improved sources per village. Nearly half (48 percent) of those in the northern possess four or more sources per village, whereas the rate is only 17 percent in the southern zone. Figure 7 further illustrates the starkly uneven north–south distribution through a 10-kilometer kernel density (units per 10 km\(^2\)) analysis of villages with more than ten improved water sources. Figure 7 shows that only a handful of rural villages in the southern zone have ten or more sources, whereas the northern zone is characterized by dense clusters of well-served rural villages.

This uneven distribution of improved water sources is also revealed in the municipal reports produced by Food Security Initiative Mobilization Project (Malian Food Security Commission 2007). An analysis of each municipal report from the Nioro and Kita districts reveals starkly different development priorities. In Nioro, not a single municipality listed the need for additional drinking water infrastructure, whereas requests for wells and pumps are ubiquitous in the Kita municipal reports. Boudofo Kita requested six pumps and three cement wells, Gadougou II requested one pump for each of its six villages, and Djidian requested fourteen improved sources.

These two types of settlements are conflated in the census, tax rolls, and every kind of official statistic, including those that measure poverty and access to basic services.

Municipalities overwhelmingly invest in administrative villages because subsidies from the central government are based on these official statistics but also because customary authorities pressure them to do so.

Local chiefs compel municipal officials to limit their investments in hamlets not simply because they want the water pumps and other infrastructure but because investing in a hamlet can be perceived as recognition of that hamlet's independence. Certain Kita municipalities, such as Sebekoro and Kotouba, are working to designate certain well-established and populous hamlets as administrative villages, but this is a burdensome and costly process that is further complicated by the legal requirement that all administrative villages must have a territory that is recognized and approved by its mother village. Given that the demarcation of an official territory for a hamlet necessarily takes land away
from its mother village, it is a nonstarter in most cases, especially when the hamlet residents are not from the same lineage clan as those in the mother village. For each of these reasons, residents of hamlets, which are disproportionately located in the southern zone, are likely to go without official recognition or improved water access for the foreseeable future.

Conclusion: Development Consequences of Hamlet Geography

This article has presented historic and geographic factors that influence the distribution of hamlets which in turn accounts for the sizable and significant subnational inequalities of improved access to water in Mali’s Kayes Region. The concentration of deprived settlements in the southern zone district of Kita is rooted in the historical–geographic form and function of the region’s local institutions. This character is not limited to how or why administrative territorial boundaries were established. It is instead the product of a long-term interplay between specific disease vectors, their eradication, population mobility, land tenure relations, and formal laws propagated by a succession of Malian governments. Together, these findings demonstrate how the theoretical tools of political ecology can be effectively integrated with a GIS-based analysis to shed new light on how local institutions function.

Broadly speaking, it suggests that neither geography nor institutions are determinative. Instead, both are dynamic and, at the local level, influence each other in important yet overlooked ways. In this case, administrative boundaries are a legacy of older geographic factors (e.g., disease risk), even when those geographic factors are no longer an obstacle to rural livelihoods. This is all the more important given the dominance of the decentralization paradigm throughout the developing world and the likelihood that it will remain in place for the foreseeable future. Other Francophone countries in the West African savanna belt (Burkina Faso, Niger, and Bénin) share similar settlement histories and administrative systems, and there is evidence that the same village–hamlet dynamics are at work in those places, so it is likely that this article’s model and argument are generalizable across a larger area.

Practically speaking, in terms of basic service access, the uneven hamlet geography that results from this interplay is essentially invisible. The aggregation of hamlet residents within the census count of a single administrative village means that research, advocacy, and policy based on cluster sampling and, more egregious, official statistics are simply incapable of distinguishing a village with mechanical water pumps and a hamlet with hand-dug wells. This leads to overinflated estimates, falsely met development goals, and misguided policies for providing services to excluded groups. The Millennium Development Goal (MDG) drinking water target has officially been met, but nearly 800 million people still do not use an improved drinking water source (UNICEF 2017). Most of the people who still lack access to improved drinking water live in rural, often isolated areas, and reaching them will prove far more difficult than accomplishing the first MDG water target.

Finally, hamlet geography will persist. Mali will remain an agriculture-dependent economy for years to come, yet various factors such as poor feeder roads and the high cost of fertilizer hinder intensification on existing farmland. This means that most smallholder farmers still practice relatively extensive agriculture and must periodically seek out new land to cultivate. This need for land—compounded by rural population growth—will maintain the attractiveness of relatively land-abundant areas such as Kita. Smallholder farmers are likely to continue settling in dispersed hamlets regardless of their legal status or the availability of basic services.
