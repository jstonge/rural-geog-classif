Terrace Construction and Productivity on Loessal Soils in Zhongyang County, Shanxi Province, PRC

Gregory Veeck,* Li Zhou,** and Gao Ling**

*Department of Geography and Anthropology, Louisiana State University
**Ecological and Environmental Economics Division, Rural Development Institute, Chinese Academy of Social Science

Erosion on the Loess Plateau has emerged as one of China's most significant environmental problems (Beijing Review 1991; Li Rongxia 1987a:21; Sun 1988:302). Ninety percent of the sediment load of 1.6 billion metric tons/year of the Yellow River is derived from the Loess Plateau which accounts for only forty percent of the total area of the basin of the river (Luk 1990–1991:3). According to recent estimates, the surface of the Plateau loses an average of one centimeter of loess each year (Luk 1990–1991:3). For a region which receives between 400 and 600 mm of rainfall per year, this volume of transported sediment is remarkable (Agricultural Atlas of China 1989). This massive silt load disrupts river navigation and irrigation systems, intensifies seasonal flood events, and significantly reduces urban/industrial water quality throughout the middle and lower portions of the Yellow River drainage basin. Further, the farmers in the villages of the Loess Plateau are already some of China's poorest, and the region cannot afford the continued loss of precious soil upon which this impoverished portion of northern China depends.

The control of erosion within the Loess Plateau is a pressing issue, one that commands attention both within the region and beyond (Luk 1990–1991; Smil 1984). First, the cities and counties in proximity to the Yellow River within the Loess Plateau and the adjacent North China Plain are demanding improved water quality for residential and industrial uses. Secondly, the Beijing government regards reductions in erosion and subsequent siltation as vitally important for the improvement and expansion of river transportation and flood management systems on the Yellow and its tributaries. And thirdly, farmers within the region understand in the clearest of terms that continued soil loss will further reduce their already low yields—at a time when grain relief from the central government is approaching a historic low. Unlike the people in many areas of China who have enjoyed dramatic improvements in quality of life in the 1980s and 1990s, the rural populations living in many areas of the Loess Plateau find themselves poorer, increasingly isolated, and at greater risk than during the Mao era. While the reasons for such problems are complex, the decline of land quality in many areas of the Plateau is a central element of these reversals.

One solution for reducing erosion in the Loess Plateau—to take croplands out of cultivation and reforest them—is plainly unrealistic. For most of the 65 to 70 million people in the region, there are few alternatives to farming. Population continues to increase dramatically. The need for more food in many of the isolated areas of the Plateau is now critical. In Shanxi Province for example, the population density per unit of arable land increased by over 15 percent between 1982 (1,349 persons/km²) and 1990 (1,533 persons/km²) (National Population Census Office 1991). To feed this growing population, increasing areas of marginal slopeland have been brought under cultivation throughout the region (Figure 1).

Although long periods of "subsidized" fallowing or wide-spread land "retirement" programs have proven effective strategies in reducing erosion rates in similar environments in other nations, current agro-economic and social conditions found within the Loess Plateau preclude such solutions. grain production cannot be halted through wholesale environmental protection legislation in an area which accounts for about 12 percent of China's total arable land (Leeming and Powell 1990:138). The core issue, then, is not whether to permit field crop production on slopeland within the Loess Plateau, but rather how to stabilize this land to improve crop yields while simultaneously slowing the rate of erosion to a point at which water quality and transport along the Yellow and its tributaries will improve. In light of these constraints, the application of appropriate methods of land stabilization assumes paramount importance, and the ecological and economic costs and benefits of different methods of land stabilization must be clearly understood.

This research views the problem of erosion from a most specific vantage point—that of the farm families who live in this region. Our research examines the two most common stabilization techniques used in the Loess Plateau and assesses the improvements in crop production which can reasonably be anticipated from their use. The first of these techniques involves the construction of terraces built almost entirely with human labor. The second replaces most of this human labor with earth-moving equipment used to construct broader and considerably more expensive terraces. We estimate and compare differences in yields and production costs for common crops grown on these two types of terraced land as well as two types of unstabilized land. Specifically, the types of land evaluated in our study include: 1) terraces constructed by human labor alone
(rengong titian); 2) terraces constructed using earth-moving equipment (jixie titian); 3) flat unterraced land (yuandi) not yet severely affected by erosion; and 4) as a control, unimproved slopeland (podii). The crops evaluated in the research are corn, millet, soybeans, sunflower, white potatoes, and sorghum. These are the main subsistence and economic crops in the region, accounting for over 92 percent of the land sown by the 330 households in our sample. These data are used to generate production functions for each of the six crops to determine the contribution of the four types of land (two types of terraced land, unterraced flat land, and unterraced slopeland) to gross production for each plot of land. After this, multiple analysis of variance (MANOVA) is used to determine if mean yields for these six crops are significantly different on each of these four land types.

The selection of appropriate stabilization techniques presents a challenging problem. The methods promoted must reasonably satisfy the interests of a number of agents—all of whom are interested in environmental protection and restoration, albeit for different reasons. The methods which can be used to stabilize slopeland, slow erosion, and improve runoff water quality, and thus satisfy extra-regional interests, must also be cost-effective for farmers and local government officials. Moreover, any effective long-term solution must have the support of the farm households who not only secure their living from the land, but also are coerced into constructing the terraces in accordance with rural China's mandatory corvee labor laws.

At present, many of the farmers living in the Loess Plateau are restive; they are not inclined to trust any government programs. The last five years have seen real incomes in many rural areas of China decline in the face of rising production costs and controlled or stagnant prices for output (Huang and Rozelle 1994:6). Shortages of high quality fertilizers, field plastic, pesticides, and herbicides are common. Promised reservoirs and irrigation systems have not materialized. Indeed, for two of the past five years, many farmers in this region have been paid by voucher rather than cash for the share of their crops sold to the State. When new directives requiring ever-greater contributions of corvee labor are handed down, the farmers coerced to provide this labor expect, not unreasonably, to see improvements in their status, that is, to see tangible improvements in gross outputs on their own lands.

Clearly, the issues involved in erosion control in this region reach far beyond the local economy and the agricultural sector and these have significant social and political ramifications. Framing these issues as a dramatic confrontation between environmental and economic interests is naive since everyone agrees that erosion must be controlled and that the solution must permit the continued production of field crops. There is far less agreement, however, about the land stabilization techniques which are best-suited to accomplish these goals and about the means of providing labor and monies for these improvements.

The central issue generally turns on which of two methods of terracing is most appropriate given existing local economic and environmental conditions. Government planners, particularly those outside the region, tend to endorse terraces constructed solely with human labor (rengong titian), primarily because this reduces reclamation costs and the share of capital that the government must allocate for terrace construction. This solution is relatively inexpensive, since it relies almost entirely on local corvee labor inputs which the law requires of all farm households (yiwu gong). Proponents argue, moreover, that terracing with human labor significantly reduces the sediment load of runoff which often quickly improves water quality, thus serving national and regional interests. The effects of such terracing on crop yields, however, have not been well studied and the long-term structural stability of narrow terraces remains a source of debate.

In contrast, local interests represented by farmers and low-level cadre intent on serving their constituents (and so maintaining their own positions) prefer mechanically constructed terraces (jixie titian). The use of earth-moving equipment permits the displacement of greater amounts of soil, and so these terraces are generally wider, more stable, and have a greater surface area than terraces constructed by hand. They argue that these machine-made terraces also have a significantly greater impact on raising yields which in turn raises incomes and improves the local economy. Certainly, these terraces are also considered to be effective in reducing erosion, but they are much more expensive to construct
and require greater involvement and investment by regional and national agencies. Practically, the greater costs of such terraces also significantly reduces the amount of slopeland which can be stabilized each year due to limitations of capital and the availability of earthmoving equipment.

Our survey of 330 households in 33 villages was conducted during four trips to Zhongyang County in west-central Shanxi Province between October of 1991 and August of 1992. Four of the county's eleven townships were randomly selected; within each of these townships, we constructed a nested random sample of approximately 84 households from eight to eleven villages (Figure 2). The survey was designed to broadly document current conditions facing farm families in the region, and the results presented here are but a portion of the overall project.

Loess and the Loess Plateau

Although loess is distributed throughout many areas of northern China, the greatest concentrations of loess and loessal soils are in central China north of the Qinling Mountains along the middle reaches of the Yellow River. This area, the Loess Plateau, encompasses an area of about 300,000 km\(^2\) and accounts for about 65 percent of all loess deposits in China (Ren et al. 1985:190). The Plateau represents the world's largest continuous deposit of loess (Strahler 1973: 391). It is bounded by the Great Wall to the north, the Qinling Mountains to the south, the Helan Mountains to the west, and the LuLiang Mountains to the east (Zhao 1986:110, 120–121). Adding the areas of significant loess deposition north of the Great Wall and west of the Helan Mountains raises the total area to about 580,000 km\(^2\) (Li Changhua 1990–1991:82). Given the extent of this area, the introduction of cost-effective and ecologically sound land-stabilization techniques promises to improve China's capacity for food self-sufficiency as well as the living conditions for the population of the Plateau.

The problem of erosion on the Loess Plateau is hardly new. Some portions of the Loess Plateau have been cultivated for 5,000 years. An ancient work on China's geography (Yu Gong), written 2,300 years ago, indicates an appreciation of the unique characteristics of loessal soils and their tendency to erode (Wang and Zhang 1980: Preface). In early historical times (Eastern and Western Han 206 B.C.–A.D. 220), the landscape of central Shanxi was much different than at the present time. The Plateau served as a major grain and husbandry area of the expanding Chinese Empire centered on the Wei and Yellow Rivers (Li Rongxia 1987b:23). Before the Han Dynasty (221 B.C.–A.D. 206), when much of this land was opened up for agriculture and timber harvesting, the greater part of the Plateau was covered with dense forests and grasslands. But by the Sung Dynasty (A.D. 960–1279), much of the area was already deforested reflecting the great demands for lumber and grain required by dozens of political and military expansions over the course of several thousand years. The setting and resetting of massive grass fires during the Ming Dynasty (A.D. 1368–1644) caused further environmental damage albeit for the purpose of creating a broad "buffer zone" devoid of grass so that the defeated but ever-encroaching Mongols could not graze their herds and war horses within the region. The buffer zone succeeded where the Great Wall had failed, but the damage to the environment was great (Li Rongxia 1987:23).

Erosion on Loessal Soils

The origins of erosional problems on the Loess Plateau are complex (Brady 1984; Popov 1959; Eden and Furtkert 1987; Wang and Zhang 1980; Luk 1990–1991). More important for our purposes are the characteristics of loess and loessal erosion which bear upon agricultural production and problems associated with land stabilization. These characteristics include the propensity of loessal soils to erode, the high concentrations of calcium carbonate, and the frequent but localized availability of usable phosphorous in the soil.

The primary geomorphological features of the Loess Plateau are the relatively level plateaus called yuan. These high table-like plains, edged by steep slopes, were formed from overlays of loess of varying depths on the existing soil and bedrock. Initially, the Plateau’s loessal surface was extremely flat, sometimes with a gradient of only one or two degrees, with slopes confined to the edges. When cultivated, however, the fine texture of loessal soils, the high concentrations of calcium carbonate, and the unconsolidated character of the soils facilitate erosion, particularly during intense rainfall. When exposed loess is soaked with water, significant erosion can occur even on slopes with only a three to five degree gradient (Zhao 1986:120).

As erosion proceeds, the loess yuan are gradually dissected. Rills grow into gullies and, if unchecked, into valleys of varying widths and depths. As gullyling proceeds, two distinct geomorphic features emerge: elongated ridges (liang) and oblong or circular knolls (mao). As gullies work headward into the yuan, liang are formed, and when these are dissected, they form mao. Over time, and in the absence of proper management or surface cover, yuan are gradually eroded into liang and mao (Ren et al. 1985:193). Of these formations, it is the flat upper surfaces of yuan and liang which remain the most productive because these features experience less erosion and hence their surface soil horizons have higher organic proportions and more stable surfaces. Here, we will refer collectively to such flat arable land areas as yuandi. By protecting this land, by limiting erosion before it begins, local households may be making the best investment of labor and scarce resources. Still, flat land is scarce, generally accounting for less than fifteen percent of any given household’s contracted arable land.

Terraces and Soil Erosion in Zhongyang County

When erosion is reduced through mechanical or vegetative stabilization, crop production on loessal soils increases dramatically. Previous research suggests that yields for stabilized loess in areas similar to Zhongyang County could be increased between 25 to 100 percent when compared to unmanaged slopeland (Li Changhua 1990–1991:90). Certainly for county and township officials and for farmers, improvements in gross production and yields are the most important element of this complex issue at the local level. Long-term development plans for the county assume that considerable advances can be made during the next five years. The recently completed Zhongyang Year 2000, a development plan funded and designed by the government of Zhongyang County, optimistically projects a “low end” mean grain yield estimate of 158.4 kg/mu (2,376 kg/ha) for the year 2000, on the assumption that 50 percent of all arable land will
be stabilized by that date (Li Kaiming 1990:25–26). This constitutes nearly a two-and-a-half-fold increase over mean yields in Zhongyang County in 1985 (66.1 kg/mu or 991.5 kg/ha for all grains). Prospects for meeting these goals seem reasonably good given recent trends. As of 1985, 72 percent of all arable land in Zhongyang County was classified as slopeland; that proportion declined to 61 percent in 1988–1989 as a result of massive investments in land stabilization of all types during this decade (Li Kaiming 1990:26,243). But owing to the localized nature of erosion processes, the proportion of arable slopeland varies widely even at the sub-county (township) level. For the four townships incorporated in our field surveys, the proportion of arable slopeland (as classified by government surveys) ranged from 55 percent to 84 percent in 1986. Among the households that participated in our survey, that proportion was slightly lower, ranging from 48 percent to 59 percent of the cropland under cultivation in 1992 (Table 1). The “official” survey estimates must be viewed with caution since they do not distinguish between land stabilized by machines and land stabilized by human labor alone. This important distinction of terrace type is ignored in the Zhongyang 2000 Report as is the distinction of differences in productivity that are associated with different methods of stabilization.

Terrace Construction in Zhongyang County

The most extensive efforts to limit erosion in Zhongyang County involve terraces constructed either manually or by machine. These terraces are designed to improve water-retention rates, to slow the velocity of water, and thus to reduce soil displacement and erosion. The result is a more stable surface which retains more organic material and generally is more productive.

The terraces constructed almost entirely by human labor are known as rengong titian (literally human-labor terraces). Although small power tillers equipped with carts may be used to haul soil, terrace excavation relies entirely on pick-and-shovel work. In their construction, these relatively narrow terraces reflect the high energy costs of displacing soil, and redistributing and compacting it across the terrace by hand. To construct broader terraces by hand, significantly greater volumes of soil must be excavated with commensurately greater increases in labor inputs. While the rengong titian are generally considered to be an improvement over unterrated slopeland with respect to slowing erosion and improving water retention, these terraces are often unstable. The new terraces are often more susceptible to pipe erosion—the development of channels which undercut the bund (edge) of the terrace—than are untreated slopeland because the density of the redistributed soil is less than that of the undisturbed soil (Haigh 1990: 649–652). The rengong titian usually range from two to four meters in width which varies inversely with the slope of the liang or mao on which they are constructed, i.e., the greater the slope, the narrower the terrace. During construction, the terrace rise is cut at an angle of approximately 90 degrees; the run is pitched at an angle of two to three degrees towards the rise so that runoff drains toward the base of the terrace (Figure 3).

The second type of terrace, jixie titian, is constructed with mechanized equipment such as front-loaders, bulldozers, graders, and backhoes. The width of the jixie titian is always greater, though more variable, than the rengong titian (Figure 4). Depending on the slope of the surface, terrace width may range from four to ten meters or more. Backhoes and bulldozers are used for the initial work, and road graders are brought in when possible to level

Regardless of terrace type, the builders add a small bund—some 20 to 30 cm in height and a third of a meter in width—made of tamped earth on the edge of the terrace. This bund, which serves to limit runoff and deter rilling, may be planted or bare. Bunds are usually built by hand, but a particularly skilled backhoe operator is sometimes able to use the bottom of the backhoe “bucket” to tamp the soil down along the relatively narrow edge of a mechanically-built terrace. When the bunds are planted, grasses or forage legumes are used in most cases although woody shrubs are evident in a few locations. These shrubs tend to be used most frequently on steep slopes at higher elevations where only grazing is permitted.

Extension personnel prefer plantings on the edges of bunds and on unusable land upslope so as to trap sediment; some farmers disagree with this practice due to the belief that these plants compete for scarce water with the field crop. They prefer to leave these areas bare and this preference is not unfounded. Haigh (1990:648) notes a similar practice on upper slopes, terrace edges, and bunds in loessal regions of Pakistan; he also cites Yair’s (1983) work in Israel which recommends bare slopes to encourage “water harvesting” for downslope fields. The variety of opinion on bund planting underscores the conflicting interests of farm households and land reclamation personnel in China.

Costs of Land Stabilization in Zhongyang County, Shanxi

The costs associated with machine-built terraces are considerably greater than those of rengong titian. The costs of constructing machine-built terraces range from 300 to 600 yuan per mu in 1992 (1992 exchange—5.71 yuan = $1.00; 1 mu = 1/15 hectare) and vary directly with the slope. The steeper the slope, the greater the volume of soil which must be excavated and re-distributed and the greater the cost of stabilization. As for human-built terraces, the costs (200 to 350 yuan/mu) may be only slightly lower than for mechanically built terraces provided payment of market wages for labor. When corvee labor is used, as is
generally the case, costs of human-built terraces decline considerably—entailing only meals for the workers which would amount to around 30 yuan per mu of terraced land (LuLiang District Agriculture and Forestry Bureau 1992). In Zhongyang County, each working adult is required to contribute approximately 20 to 27 working days a year to the government, but reports of local cadre requiring 30 to 40 days are not uncommon. Corvee labor is used for the construction and maintenance of public works, e.g., schools, clinics, roads, railroad right-of ways, irrigation systems, and levees, as well as for terraces. It is justified on the grounds that such contributions will result in a better quality of life for local residents. While the corvee-labor laws are unpopular, the use of such labor for terrace construction requires little capital and permits internal labor transfers at the sub-county level. In this respect, land stabilization using terraces constructed by hand is more readily initiated than the construction of jixie titian which requires purchasing equipment or renting it from agencies beyond the county.

Methods: Estimating the Effect of Terrace Construction on Output

This analysis tests the hypothesis that crop yields will vary systematically by terrace type and it evaluates the differential benefits of the two land stabilization-techniques on the yields of each major crop. The statistical analysis is twofold: 1) production functions by crop are used to determine the effects of the two terrace types on gross production as well as of standard inputs such as seed and fertilizer; 2) further analysis of these data using multiple analysis of variance (MANOVA) to determine if the several mean crop yields on each type of terraced and un-terraced land are statistically different from yields for yuandi (flat land) and for unimproved slopeland (pod).

The use of these two analytical techniques thus provides a comprehensive picture of terrace effects on gross production per field and on yield per mu; the use of production functions also permits an evaluation of how inputs such as fertilizer and seed contribute to the production process.

Production Functions by Crop

The use of production functions is quite common in agricultural research (Eckert 1977; Lo and Hwang 1992; Thakur 1992). The technique requires the specification and generation of production functions for, in this case, each of six crops. With respect to specification, we define the dependent variable as gross output per field rather than as yield per unit area. This specification reflects our belief (based on the experience and expertise of local extension agents and farmers) that standardizing these data per unit area of land is not warranted given local cultivation techniques. Firstly, neither organic nor inorganic fertilizer is uniformly applied on terraced land, and so standardizing these data (yield/area) introduces error into the models. Secondly, larger terraces are assumed to be more productive because they have better moisture retention and better soils and because they receive greater overall levels of fertilization. Thirdly, labor allocations are disproportionately higher on larger terraces. For these reasons, we believe that absolute field size is a more appropriate dependent variable for estimating production functions. The independent variables used in the production functions include the standard ones such as the volume of applied fertilizers, seed, and labor along with land type. The latter is specified as a set of dummy variables. With four land types (yuandi or flat land; rengong titian; jixie titian; and podi or unimproved slopeland), three dummy variables are required for the functions. Every field is assigned to one or another of these four land types.

The linear functions for each crop were gen-
erated through OLS regression analysis using subsets of the 330 households who cultivated that crop in 1991. For example, the 302 households that produced millet in 1991 yield 302 observations. Since 1984, crop selection has been a household decision, hence not all of the households in our survey chose to produce all six crops in 1991.

The variables included in the model represent the most significant determinants of production for the farms in our survey and, we believe, within the entire study area. Initially, the production function included nine variables collected for each field: 1) the applied volume of organic manure; 2) labor per field in gong (1 gong being roughly equivalent to 8 hours of labor); 3) the volume of applied inorganic available nitrogen; 4) the volume of applied inorganic phosphorous; 5) field size (sown area in mu); 6) applied seed by volume; and 7–9) three dummy variables (4 – 1 = 3) indicating the four types of land (flat land, human-built terraces, machine-built terraces, and slopeland).

While three fertilizer variables were used in the original functions—organic manure; total available inorganic nitrogen; total inorganic applied phosphorous—only the last two remained in the final production functions. We dropped applied manure per field because of the difficulties associated with estimating the use of this input. For similar reasons, labor was also dropped from the final production functions. In no case (six crop functions) were the estimates of slope (b) associated with applied organic material or labor found to be significant—even at the .1 level. The production functions were re-calculated after removal of these two variables from the analysis.

That the estimates of slope (b) for labor and applied organic manures are statistically insignificant should not come as a surprise for students of Chinese agriculture. Micro-economic analyses of crop production in China by Wiens (1982), Veeck (1991; Veeck and Pannell 1992), and Feder and Lau (1991), among others, all report negative or insignificant slope estimates (b) for labor and manures. The small amount of arable land available to the average household in the sample encourages highly labor-intensive cultivation, despite the fact that additional units of labor may well have no effect on output.

Similar difficulties are associated with the estimation of organic manure's contributions to crop output. The range in quality, seasoning, and the base components (ingredients) of applied organic materials makes problematic any estimate of manure's contribution to yield. Our survey identified eleven common ingredients in the organic "manure" applied to fields by farmers in our sample; these include nightsoil, livestock manures (swine, cattle, donkey, chicken), kitchen wastes, crop straw, grass, soybean cake, stove ash, and residue from roadsides and irrigation systems. While we certainly recognize the contribution of such materials to the production process, the variable composition and quality of these materials makes the estimation of the coefficients associated with organic manures very difficult, if not impossible. Moreover, it is also hard for individual respondents to arrive at an accurate estimate of applied manure (or labor) per field since any given household may have up to six or seven parcels of land and because organic materials are applied throughout the year, not just during the growing season.

Omitting these two variables, we then re-estimated the models using the remaining seven variables. The ensuing estimates of b displayed t-values which were significant at the .05 level or better for at least two crops. These significant variables include: 1) the area of the field (in mu where 1 mu = 1/15 hectare); 2–4) the three dummy variables of land type; 5) the volume of applied available inorganic nitrogen fertilizer per field; 6) the volume of applied active phosphorous per field; and 7) the volume of seed used per field (in jin where 1 jin = .5 kg).

In the cases of the inorganic nitrogen and phosphorous fertilizer variables, the variables used in our function actually represent an aggregation of the several different fertilizer compounds used by farmers in the survey in 1991. For example, four types of inorganic fertilizer with different proportions of accessible active nitrogen (N) were used by the sample households (Ammonium Nitrate, 33–34 percent N; Ammonium Bicarbonate, 17 percent N; Urea, 42–46 percent N; Compound N-P-K fertilizer, 12 percent N) (Editorial Board of the Agricultural Technology Handbook 1984: 119–121). Some households, for example, apply small amounts of urea (46 percent active N) during
planting, but top-dress with less expensive ammonium bicarbonate (17 percent N) during the growing season. A single variable, active accessible nitrogen, was created by multiplying the applied volume of each type of fertilizer by the percentage of active N in that particular compound. We then aggregated the active available nitrogen within each type of commercial fertilizer to create the variable used in the model. This permits comparisons for each field regardless of the type(s) of nitrogen fertilizer applied. A similar variable transformation was used for phosphorous.

The dummy variables for land type within the production function included: land terraced by machine; land terraced by human labor; and flat land (yuandi). All of the fields used in the analysis were classified as one of these three types or as slopeland. The estimated slope (b) of each of the three dummy variables will provide an indication of the contribution of land type to production relative to the fourth category, i.e., slopeland.

MANOVA Analysis of Crop Yield

While the production functions evaluate the contributions of inputs (including land type) to total production per field, the second portion of the analysis reports the results of MANOVA (multiple analysis of variance) and related test statistics for the mean yields of six crops on four land types. The MANOVA analysis provides not only a cross-check to the results of the production function analysis, but also an additional means of determining if the actual yields per unit area per household are statistically different across land types (Hintze 1984:248–257). For each crop included in the MANOVA analysis, the classification variable was the land type on which crops are grown, i.e., flat land, machine-built terraces, human-built terraces, and unterraced slopeland. As with the production-function analysis, the actual number of observations per crop varies based on the number of farm households which produced a given crop in 1991. The sample sizes are noted in the results of the ANOVA procedures below. Estimates of statistical differences among samples were made using Duncan’s multiple range test and Fisher’s LSD test. These statistics test for the null hypothesis (at a specified level of significance, .05) that crop yields on the several land types are not independent samples; that is, yields on each type of land are not different.

Production Function Analysis

The final specifications of the six crop models and their associated test statistics are reported in Table 2. The overall fit of the six models (six crops) is quite good, with \( r^2 \) values for the production functions ranging from a low of 0.4613 for maize to a high of 0.7219 for sunflower (Table 2). The F-statistics associated with each production function (Table 2) are all significant at .001 or above. The models, then, are reliable, the signs of the coefficients are in the expected directions. All of which suggest that the models are logical and appropriate.

The several models provide a clear picture of the effects of the two types of terraces on output per field; they also offer useful information on the relationships between field size, fertilizer use, and production. The latter relationships will be addressed later in the article once we have evaluated the effects of different land types on crop production.

Perhaps most important with respect to this research are the significant effects of land type. In 14 of 18 potential instances in the models (three dummy variables by six crops), statistical significance exceeds the .001 level (15 of 18 exceed .05). More specifically, all slope estimates (b) for flat land (yuandi) or for land terraced by machine (jixie) are significant. This finding underscores what at least some of the farmers in our sample already know, namely that given a constant level of inputs, crops planted on machine-constructed terraces will consistently outperform crops planted on terraces built by hand. Several other results flow from our regression analysis. Predictably, the size of the field exerts a significant effect on production (in all cases except maize; see Table 2, sown area). Field size, of course, is an important issue in production economics in China. Our results tend to support those who see field consolidation as an important avenue for increasing production. As mentioned earlier, larger terraces do not dry out as quickly, and farmers participating in our survey report investing proportionately more labor and fertilizer in larger fields. In all probability, larger field size also facilitates more effective treatment for disease and pests. This raises an interesting question with respect to the economics of terrace construction. Given that the construction of larger and wider terraces in the steep terrain of the Loess Plateau is more expensive (owing to the excavation and transport of much greater volumes of earth), are these wider terraces more cost effective? We suspect that they are given the importance of absolute field size as reflected in the significant coefficients for this variable. Answering this question lies beyond the scope of this research, but the issue certainly is deserving of investigation.

The analysis of the two fertilizer variables in the final production functions (inorganic N and P) is also informative. The amount of applied nitrogen plays an important if variable role in the production of four of the six crops (soybeans, maize, sunflower, and potato). This result is expected. The coefficient for soybeans (.0307) is close to 0 because few farmers apply nitrogen to this crop. As for the two crops (millet and sorghum) which are statistically insignificant, inorganic nitrogen (at least at the rates applied by the farmers in our survey) does not have direct impacts on yield. This could reflect low application rates for these crops based on local understanding that increased application rates will yield little benefit. A second factor might be the low rainfall of 1991 which may have limited the returns normally expected from inorganic nitrogen. And a third factor may have to do with the fact that much of this nitrogen comes from locally produced Ammonium Bicarbonate and the possibility that the local plant (under county management) may not be producing and distributing a high-quality product or that storage after production is not adequate, e.g., exposure of the fertilizer to moisture thus reducing the actual proportion of active nitrogen in the product.

The contribution of phosphorous is a bit more erratic. Estimates of b are significant but small for four of six crops (soybeans, millet, sunflower, sorghum) reflecting negative returns to investment. The result is somewhat surprising, but this may be attributable to: 1) local variations in naturally occurring phosphorous in loessial soils; 2) the fact that more than 50 percent of households do not apply any phosphorous fertilizer; and/or 3) the low rainfall in the survey year. These counter-intuitive estimates of b for inorganic phosphorous limits systematic interpretation of these results. Efforts to encourage farmers to apply phosphorous fertilizers were only initiated by extension personnel in 1990. Generally speaking, however, the modest effects of fertilizer require further study and extension research on how and when fertilizer is applied during the crop season. Field-by-field testing for soil fertility may be required.

Discussion of Results

Although having data for multiple years and for a more typical year with respect to rainfall would certainly strengthen these findings, our results are sufficiently robust for provisional policy deliberations. The narrow terraces con-
structed using human corvee labor, while they may slow erosion and they are certainly cheaper to construct, do not have positive effects on yields nor on gross output when compared to yields and outputs on unimproved land with slopes greater than seven degrees. These results help to explain the reluctance of many farmers to devote more labor to terracing land by hand. However short-sighted their actions in the long run, their lack of enthusiasm for rengong titian is reasonable as an immediate response to the current conditions facing farm households in this poor area of China.

Our analysis of the farm household data from Zhongyang County suggests that land stabilization through the construction of mechanically built terraces exerts significant and positive impacts on the incomes of households in addition to limiting the volume of eroded material. Insofar as equipment, fuel, and skilled labor can be made available for further construction of wider machine-built terraces, farmers will be able to increase the gross value of their crops as well as their incomes.

The implications for "preemptive" conservation efforts on existing stocks of flat land (yuandi) are also clear. Protecting flat land in advance through vegetative stabilization and/or the construction of bunds will maintain yields at levels that are higher than for any other type of stabilized land. But this result simultaneously raises the controversial issue of policy priorities for land-reclamation programs. If the primary goal of land stabilization is to maintain the value of output and maximize yields, stabilization efforts should be directed first to flat lands which are as yet uncompacted by extreme erosion. Secondary stabilization efforts should then be focused on the creation of mechanically built terraces. This two-step stabilization strategy will raise household incomes as it reduces silt in surface runoff.

These local priorities for land conservation, however, may be inconsistent with regional and national goals aimed at improving conditions on the most severely eroded land and at reducing the silt load in the middle and lower reaches of the Yellow River and its tributaries as quickly as possible and at the lowest possible cost. If minimum-cost erosion control—and not localized improvements in crop production and income—is the main policy goal, then the best strategy is to expand terraces on slopeland while leaving decisions on the types of terraces constructed to local authorities who must (invariably) base their decisions on the availability of financial resources. In this case, owing to the current shortages of land-moving equipment and capital in most areas, local authorities would have little choice but to expand the emphasis on human-built terraces.

The Loess Plateau is not densely populated when compared to many areas in eastern China. But when the Plateau's population is juxtaposed with the region's limited production capacity, the levels of population and population growth are excessive. When compared with regions of similar climate and environmental conditions, the "population burden of the Loess Plateau is about ten times higher than the average figure for the world" (Li Changhua 1990–1991:83–84). While farmers on the Loess Plateau have reaped the benefits from increasing levels of modern inputs such as fertilizer, the sources of these productivity gains may well be near the margin (Carter and Zhong 1988). Under different (and better) circumstances, the ideal solution for the region's erosion problems might involve stabilizing slopes with vegetation and sharply restricting access to much of this land. Unfortunately, this solution is not a viable one in the short-to-medium time frame due to the lack of alternative sources of employment and income.

The Policy Dilemma: Local and Extra-Local Interests

Given the Loess Plateau's growing population, the diminishing returns on inputs such as fertilizer, and signs that relief support (grain) from the central government in Beijing may not be reliable, land-stabilization efforts there must be continued for economic as well as environmental reasons. The future direction of current programs will depend on the resolution of policy debates over the ultimate goals of land stabilization. In the event that immediate local needs are given priority, policymakers should adopt land-stabilization schedules that optimize yields and incomes of farming households. Existing level land, in other words, should be the primary focus of attention and investment, and terracing should be undertaken only when equipment is available.

Land-stabilization programs based on these
economic goals will select land and schedule terrace construction in ways that are quite different than programs which concentrate on environmental goals, e.g., the rapid control of erosion. The latter, of course, have legitimate claims. Note, for example, that factories in the cities along the Yellow River often cannot operate during the dry season because the high concentrations of silt (5–8 percent) fouls equipment. Similarly, navigation during the dry season on the Yellow and its tributaries remains hazardous for even medium-size shipping. These are very real economic and ecological problems that arise far beyond the villages of Zhongyang.

The policymakers’ dilemma is that focusing exclusively on erosion control for ecological reasons will doubly alienate rural farm households who must do the work of slopeland stabilization and then earn their income from the same land. A successful long-term policy will require the trust and support of local people as well as the support of government agencies at higher levels.

The controversial national and regional debates over economic and environmental goals and strategies is no less complex when translated to the local level. The key questions endure: Given that most arable land in Zhongyang has been assigned to households under relatively long-term contracts (15–25 years), which of these households should receive assistance in the form of newly stabilized land? Should the land of the poorest households receive priority? Should a particular type of land take precedence? Should priorities be determined by ecological concerns or economic concerns? In the end, we suspect, these local issues can only be resolved in the context of national policy directives on the ecological and economic goals that are most appropriate for China and its regions.

Veeck, Gregory, Li, Zhou, and Gao, Ling. 1995. Terrace Construction and Productivity on Loessal Soils in Zhongyang County, Shanxi Province, PRC. Annals of the Association of American Geographers 85(3):450-467. Abstract.

Erosion is a pressing problem throughout rural China, but the problem is most severe within the Loess Plateau of north central China. The Loess Plateau is losing an average of one centimeter of soil each year due to erosion and related environmental problems. The eroded loess clogs irrigation systems, restricts navigation on all rivers within the region, fouls urban and industrial water systems, and for agriculture represents a loss of a vital resource that this very poor area
cannot afford. While land stabilization in the region has been given a very high priority by regional and national planners for economic and environmental reasons, the selection of the most appropriate measures for land stabilization remains controversial.

This research, based on a survey of 330 households in Zhongyang County, Shanxi Province, PRC, estimates the increases in gross production and yields which can reasonably be expected from improvements to cultivated slopeland through land stabilization using two different types of terrace construction. The first of these involves the construction of terraces built almost entirely with human labor. The second replaces most of this human labor with earth-moving equipment used to construct broader and considerably more expensive terraces. We estimate and compare differences in yields and production costs for common crops grown on these two types of terraced land as well as two types of unstabilized land. Specifically, the types of land evaluated in the study include: 1) terraces constructed by human labor alone (rengong titian); 2) terraces constructed using earth-moving equipment (jixie titian); 3) flat unterraced land (yuandi) not yet severely affected by erosion; and 4) by way of a control, un-improved slopeland (podh). The crops evaluated are corn, millet, soybeans, sunflower, white potatoes, and sorghum—the main subsistence and economic crops in the region. These data are used to generate production functions for each of the six crops to determine the contribution of terrace construction to gross production. Multiple analysis of variance (MANOVA) is used to determine if mean yields for these six crops are significantly different on different land types.

Results of these analyses indicate that yields for all crops grown on mechanically constructed terraces are significantly higher than yields for crops grown on terraces constructed by hand, while yields of crops grown on both types of terraces remain lower than for crops grown on flat land which has not yet faced serious erosion. From the perspective of farm households living in this region, the proper management of existing flat land and the increased construction of mechanically constructed terraces are the best strategies for improving output and farm income while also helping to slow erosion. Government agencies beyond the region, however, continue to favor terraces constructed by hand owing to lower costs. The implications for local, regional, and national policy directives are discussed. Key Words: agriculture, China, land stabilization, Loess Plateau, production function.
