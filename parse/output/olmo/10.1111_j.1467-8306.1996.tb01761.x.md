State Hegemony in Transforming the Rural Landscapes of Eastern Germany: 1945–1994

Ingolf Vogeler

Department of Geography, University of Wisconsin-Eau Claire

Sixty years ago, Derwent Whittlesey (1935) discussed the political impact of central governments upon the landscape as expressed through various aspects of security, boundaries, land surveys, and laws. Rugg (1985) applied this approach to Eastern Europe where he identified five historical landscape types resulting from the dominance of the State's central authority: German medieval colonization, feudalism, multinationalism, nationalism, and socialism. More recently, the role of the State in transforming the economy and landscape has again been recognized, but this time from political economy and structuralist perspectives. Cloke and Goodwin emphasize that “some changes have been forced by use of state power . . . via programmes of privatization and deregulation. Other changes have been the result of persuasion” (italics in the original; Cloke and Goodwin 1992:328). Although these authors refer to capitalist economies in the West, their comments apply to the East as well. In eastern Germany, the State's activist intervention, directly and indirectly, has restructured the economy and recomposed the society three times in the last fifty years. The hegemony of two communist states—first the occupying forces of the Soviet Union and then the GDR (German Democratic Republic or, more commonly, East Germany)—and, most recently, the capitalist state of the newly reunified Germany have produced striking transformations in rural landscapes.

This paper shows how the government of the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG or West Germany) privatized the socialist agriculture of the former GDR following German reunification in 1990. Although the broad political and economic changes since the Cold War (Tutathail and Luke 1994) and for Germany in particular (Harris 1991; Corson and Minghi 1994) have been described, detailed empirical studies of this most recent transformation have not been made. To document the rural landscape and structural manifestations of reprivatization, I use three sources of data: 1) questionnaires collected by a German agricultural research institute from a representative sample of 728 farms throughout eastern Germany; 2) topographic and cadastral maps; and 3) field work at nine farm or village sites. Map analysis and field work documented field patterns (numbers, densities, and shapes of fields) before and after reunification at six of these sites: Heinersdorf, Rehmsdorf, Dedelow, and Hasenholz. Two other sites—Uelzen in western Germany and Emerald, Wisconsin (Figure 1)—were selected to compare the eastern German field patterns since 1990. The questionnaire data provide a regional context for farm and field changes in the case studies. These most recent landscape changes are also related to previous political and economic transformations in Eastern Europe in general and Germany in particular during the past fifty years.

Historically, large estates commonly held by absentee owners (Junkers) accounted for most of the land in northern Germany east of the Elbe River. Throughout Europe, State intervention through agrarian reforms had established the conditions for capitalist agriculture in various forms: tenant farming in England, modernized Junker estates in Germany, and restructured family farms in Denmark (Goodman, Jorj, and Wilkinson 1987:157–158). In Germany, even in the nineteenth century, these estate landscapes differed sharply from the landscapes of small peasant farmers west of the Elbe and in southern Germany east of the Elbe (Rugg 1985:79). At the end of World War II, several military powers occupied Germany: the Allies of the United States, United Kingdom, and France controlled the western portion of Germany; the Soviet Union, the eastern portion. During the post-war occupation, the Soviets initiated land reform. They seized large farms, distributed them to landless and small farmers, and transformed rural landscapes of eastern Germany into small farms and fields and villages to accommodate the increase in farmsteads. This phase of land reform came to an end when West Germany declared independence in September and East Germany in October of 1949.

The GDR's government gradually consolidated the many small farm units created during the Soviet occupation. Through several stages of collectivization, large field sizes were re-

stored, but this time field length was determined not by the endurance of plow horses but by the imagination of policy makers. Once personal property was eliminated and property borders and field boundaries were removed from the landscape (through collectivization), fields could be as large as political leaders thought appropriate for economies of scale and technological advance as modeled on both Soviet and U.S. agriculture (Rugg 1986:372).

Nearly fifty years later, the GDR ceased to exist and in 1990 was officially absorbed into the FRG. On the one hand, the FGR privatized farmland and encouraged family farms; on the other hand, it imposed considerable obstacles on former collective farms which were required to transform themselves or be dissolved. Despite these profound socioeconomic changes since German reunification, the GDR's legacy of large farms and fields persists and these units account for most of eastern Germany's agricultural production.

Germany's 1945 division and its 1990 reunification resulted in major socioeconomic and landscape transformations in eastern Germany. The scope of these changes is shown by the number of farms that were eliminated and created (Table 1).1 During the Soviet occupation, about 12,000 confiscated estates and other farms were divided into 617,886 new farms. By the end of the GDR's collectivization, however, these farm numbers were reduced to about 4,500 vertically and horizontally integrated agro-industrial firms. Finally, the reprivatization of farmland after 1990 again increased farm numbers to about 20,000. The latter consisted of individual, incorporated, and corporate farms. Corporate farms, though just 14 percent of the new farms, dominated the agricultural economy of eastern Germany; they accounted for about 69 percent of the rented cropland and they owned 80 percent of the region's livestock.

The principal changes in the countryside, most notably the number and size of farms and fields, were effected by State policies and actions in the last fifty years. These unfolded in three major landscape transformations: 1) the Soviet land reforms of 1945–1949; 2) the GDR's farm collectivization from 1950–1990; and 3) the German government's reprivatization of farmland since 1990.

Soviet Land Reforms, 1945–1949

The Soviet government initially instituted land reform in response to the demands of landless farm workers and small-scale farmers for sufficient amounts of land as economic security (Wädekin 1982:4–13). In the census of 1939, the last reliable one before World War II, the farm structure in eastern Germany consisted of 391,500 farm plots<sup>2</sup> of 1–20 ha (2.5–49 acres), 48,700 farms of 20–50 ha (49–124 acres), 8,100 farms of 50–100 ha (124–247 acres), and 6,300 estates of over 100 ha (247 acres) (Müller 1975:13). During their occupation of eastern Germany, the Soviets expropriated all feudal-like estates (large farms over 100 ha or 247 acres) and seized the lands of war criminals and Nazis without compensation (Volgyes 1980). Between 1945 and 1949, these lands were assigned to small farm owners, landless farm workers, and German immigrants from east of the Oder-Neiße rivers. Seventy-six percent of the confiscated land was taken from 7,160 estate owners (Junker) and other large farm owners (with an average of 350 ha or 865 acres); 4 percent from 4,537 farms with under 100 ha (247 acres) and owned by war criminals and Nazis (with an average of 30 ha or 74 acres); and the remaining 20 percent came from various kinds of state properties. Of the total of 3.30 million ha (8.15 million acres) confiscated, 1.96 million ha (4.84 million acres) was farmland; 50 percent cropland, 10 percent greenland, and 30 percent forest. Two-thirds of this confiscated land (about 2.2 million ha or 5.44 million acres) was distributed to about 560,000 farmers, of whom 210,000 were new farmers (with an average of 8 ha or 20 acres). The remaining 1.1 million ha (2.72 million acres) remained as state property (Bell 1992:8–9).

Within five years, the Soviet land reforms fundamentally transformed the regional agricultural structure of eastern Germany, particularly in the two northern GDR Länder (provinces) of Mecklenburg and Brandenburg where Junker estates had predominated. Huge estates and large-scale family farms gave way to small-sized family farms (Auerbach 1960; Gade 1957). By 1950, small farms (1–10 ha or 2.25–25 acres) in eastern Germany accounted for 73 percent of all farms and 43 percent of all farmland; middle-sized farms (10–20 ha or 25–49 acres) for another 19 and 29 percent, respectively; and large-sized farms (with a maximum of 100 ha or 247 acres) for only 8 and 28 percent, respectively (Bell 1992:19, 87).

Collective Farming in the GDR, 1950–1990

These small farms soon proved inadequate to produce sufficient quantities of food and industrial crops for an increasing urban population and for financing rapid industrialization.
The GDR, along with the centrally planned economies in Eastern Europe and China, began creating large-scale, compulsory collective farms (both cooperative and state farms) on the Soviet model of collectivization. By 1952, collectivization in the GDR had been achieved on some 24,000 farms (with an average of 29 ha or 72 acres) and on 700,000 ha (1,729,676 acres). Moreover, the State penalized "large" private farmers (with 20 to 100 ha or 49 to 247 acres), subsidized the newly emerging LPGs (Landwirtschaftliche Produktionsgenossenschaften or farm cooperatives), and increased the compulsory state food production quotas by 10 percent for the larger private farmers and reduced it by 10 percent for the LPGs (Bell 1992:20–21). From 1952 to 1954, the number of large farmers declined by 30 percent (Bell 1992:36), but the importance of the LPGs remained small—only 2.2 percent of the GDR's farm population and 3.4 percent of the farmland.

Between 1952 and 1961, when the GDR closed the border to the FRG and built the Berlin Wall, farms were being abandoned as owners fled to the FRG. These unused lands were subsequently incorporated into LPGs and VEGs (Volks eigene Güter or state farms). (On LPGs, farmers shared in the collective value of their production; on VEGs, workers were paid wages by the State.) By 1953, 1,802 farmers had left farming, 76 percent of them had been large-scale farmers with over 20 ha (49 acres), and they accounted for 91 percent of the affected farmland (Bell 1992:47). In addition, the State had established 1,906 LPGs consisting of 218,043 ha (538,777 acres) and 37,000 members. Initially, LPG members consisted mainly of farm workers (53 percent), "new" farmers created by the Soviet land reforms (37 percent), and "old" farmers who had existed before the Soviet reforms (10 percent). These groups were generally more willing to participate in these LPGs than were larger-scale and older farmers who tended to resist losing control over their property and decision making. Francisco (1979:77–80) points out that collectivization in the GDR was made easier because many of the farm families who opposed collectivization had fled to the FGR before 1961 and because the GDR made countryside life attractive by providing incentives for housing, social service, income, and working conditions.

At first, the larger-scale farmers had their production rights curtailed, but later their independence was eliminated entirely as the GDR created increasingly larger-scale and more comprehensive LPGs. The degree of collectivization resulted in three types of LPGs. Type I collectivized cropland and some of the greenland; Type II added all livestock, farm machinery, and equipment; and Type III added all remaining assets of private farmers. Type III LPGs, always the largest in size, averaged 663 ha (1,638 acres) in 1967. The distinction between Type I and II LPGs had disappeared by 1961, and by 1967 the average size of these LPGs stood at 209 ha (516 acres) (Table 2). Type III LPGs were geographically concentrated in areas of high-quality soils: the black-earth region from Magdeburg to Halle, the Thüringer Basin, and Mecklenburg and Brandenburg (Figure 1) with its large numbers of new or immigrant farmers from farther east in Poland and Russia (Heuer 1973:19). By 1959, these LPGs accounted for over 50 percent of the farmland in the northern districts of Rostock, Schwerin, and Neubrandenburg and in the southern districts of Magdeburg and Halle (Kohl et al. 1970).

Between 1952 and 1960, the move toward collectivization was complete; collective farms encompassed 80 percent of the farmland (Voges 1980:11). LPGs specialized in the production of crops [Planzen LPG(P)] or livestock [Tiere LPG(T)] were created by combining the cultivated areas of several villages. In the south, LPGs of 600–800 ha (1,483–1,997 acres) and in the north of 1,000–1,500 ha (2,471–3,706 acres) were combined into larger and centrally managed LPGs which averaged 5,000 ha (12,355 acres).

The collectivization of farmland and farmsteads also resulted in field consolidation. Rugg (1986:378) argues that the consolidated fields did not correspond in most cases to those of the old estates. He cites the village of Stresow, south of Greifswald, where historical land use and field size changes have been mapped (Benthien 1963). In 1694, the three-field rotational system was evident; by 1900, this Junker estate was divided into four large cultivated fields. By 1945, the Soviet land reform had created 67 fields which remained until 1952 when six farmers formed an LPG and worked their nine scattered fields. In 1961, with the total collectivization of the village and its land, only sixteen fields remained. Although farmland had been privatized by 1993, when I again mapped this village, only ten very large fields remained—fewer than had existed during the GDR era but more than during the Junker era! My field work indicates similar patterns in Jakobsdorf (Barth 1966) and Gröningen (Diercke Weltatlas 1991:52–53).

During the mid-1970s, the GDR created inter-farm cooperative crop production organizations (these KOGs, Kooperationsgemeinschaften, included LPGs and VEGs) and large livestock production facilities. These units relied on various kinds of consolidated agro-industrial services: AlV, Agrar-Industrie-Vereinigungen (agro-industrial combines), ACZ, Agrochemischen Zentren (agro-chemical centers), and KfL, Kreisbetriebe für Landtechnik (district centers for agricultural technology) (Rugg 1986:372). By 1989 these vertically and horizontally integrated agro-industrial complexes were quite large, averaging 5,030 ha (12,429 acres) and 4,528 ha (11,189 acres) for VEGs and LPG(P)s, respectively (Roubitschek 1993:35). For example, the Kröpelin agro-industrial crop production complex (KIM, Kombinate Industrielle Mast or combined industrial feedlot) west of Rostock included three LPG(P)s and one VEG, covering 32,000 ha (79,071 acres) (Kohl, Marcinek, and Nitz 1986:131). Animal production was also being concentrated. Many LPG(T)s milked thousands of cows, and raised tens of thousands of cattle, pigs, and chickens. Tens of thousands of pigs were fattened in Eberswalde (157,000), Neustadt (101,000), Haßleben (93,000), and Borna (37,000); and Hohen Wangelin fattened 15,000 bulls on 4,000 ha (9,884 acres) (see Figure 1). In addition, the State created four egg factories with 2.5 million laying hens and two broiler factories with 1.4 million chickens (Roubitschek 1993:41–42). As farm sizes increased dramatically through consolidation between 1962–1973, the numbers of Type III and Type I LPGs decreased by about 1,000 and almost 9,000, respectively (Müller 1975:31). And a decade later, LPGs of all types numbered just 3,969 and VEGs just 479 (Statistisches Jahrbuch 1982:169)

Large-scale LPGs and the VEGs had clear advantages for the State and for many individuals. These included:

(1) reduced risks in agricultural production from the variabilities of weather, pests, and other physical factors;
(2) greater work schedule flexibility—families could take several holidays during the year, even during the peak work cycle in the summer months;
(3) more opportunities for women to work outside of the home;
(4) protection from income loss because of illness and accidents;
(5) greater variety of work assignments;
(6) greater potential for career development in training and duties;
(7) higher utilization of buildings and machinery: fully using resources and replacing machinery with newer versions as they became available;
(8) better infrastructure in villages (schools, playgrounds, community centers, social services, and shops) (Rugg 1971:90–94); and
(9) increased economies of scale for the production of food and industrial products (Bergmann 1992b:309–310).

The LPGs and the VEGs, of course, had to work within the context of the centrally planned economy of the GDR. The State determined the kind, quantity, and price of agricultural products. Inputs of energy, machinery, fertilizers, and herbicides were regulated by state agencies. The severity of the constraints under which collectivized agriculture had to operate is illustrated by the fact that LPGs were required to buy their sugar-beet harvesting equipment from Hungary because it was the sole manufacturer of this equipment for all COMECON countries. Theoretically this high degree of specialization was efficient, but in practice it was a disaster because the State had no means for feedback from the LPGs and
VEGs. Before reunification, for example, 200 workers on the Petersberg LPG near Halle (Figure 1) harvested sugar beets; after reunification (1994), one worker with new machines did all of the harvesting. The old equipment was so poorly designed that in wet conditions often 70 percent of the weight of the sugar-beet harvest was dirt! LPG managers and workers knew what the production problems were, but they could not effect any changes. The best they could do was to make adjustments to their equipment and local operations. To make matters worse, these large-scale agro-industrial units had reached the limits of efficiency as soil erosion and manure disposal problems mounted (Stern 1990). But the GDR's formal, institutionalized, top-down, and dictatorial decision-making processes did not allow for bottom-up information feedback, except for those opinions that confirmed the views of central planners and policy makers.

Indeed the GDR system built in incentives to increase farm size and production for their own sake. Local political leaders and LPG and VEG managers mobilized local resources (to build new barns, roads, irrigate more land, etc.) to impress and gain favor from their party superiors who in turn wanted to report only success and growth in agricultural production. Clearly, the SED (Sozialistische Einheitspartei Deutschlands or Socialist Unity Party of Germany) looked favorably on such projects and so they instituted similar projects and improvements throughout the country, regardless of the value of these kinds of scales of production elsewhere—again ignoring local knowledge and decision making.

Reprivatization Since German Reunification, 1990–1994

Since the unification of the GDR and FRG in 1990, the German government has been returning private property to its previous owners without the consent of the current farm population—what Bergmann (1992a) has called forced privatization. Only owners of property (lands and buildings) that was confiscated by the GDR are eligible to have their properties returned. Ironically, these property rights are based on the Soviet land reforms of 1945–1949 and on the Soviet confiscation of the private lands owned by estate and large landowners and by war criminals, and the distribution of these lands to small-scale farmers (who in turn lost control and ownership of their land to the GDR state) (Feddersen 1991). Because the German government is returning land to earlier "Soviet" period owners, this process is called reprivatization. In doing so, the German government has delegitimated property rights acquired under the GDR and legitimated those secured during the Soviet occupation. Unofficial reports in newspapers have indicated that the Soviet Union insisted that the FGR recognize its land reforms in return for agreeing to German reunification (see, for example, Bonn, Moskau und die Suche nach der Wahrheit 1994). Although owners who had their property confiscated by the Nazis are also demanding the return of their assets, the current German government and the courts have not yet acted on these claims. Most experts think they never will (Bergmann 1994).

By December 31, 1991, the German government required that all former LPGs had to take on new legal forms or they would be liquidated. Financial incentives were given to establish reprivatized family farms. The various legal forms were of three basic types, perhaps best translated from the German as individual, incorporated, and corporate: 1) Newly created individual farms established by previous LPG farmers, pre-1945 farmers who had left eastern Germany, or farmers from western Germany; 2) Incorporated farms (on these farms, farm owners essentially do all the farm work themselves and hire only seasonal labor as needed) consisting of several individuals in partnership or individual farmers who incorporated their operations; and 3) Corporate farms (on these farms, managers and workers perform different roles and have different responsibilities) held usually by members of the former LPGs or by a small number of investor-farmers from western Germany and other parts of Western Europe.

By 1993, about 15,000 individual farms had been established. Of these, 41 percent were run by full-time farmers; another 1,844 farms were incorporated by individuals or as partnerships. Ideologically, the FRG government, particularly the ruling conservative Christian Democrats before and after reunification, is committed to a market economy, private property, and family farming, and it opposes all socialist institutions, including collective agriculture. Consequently, FRG's agricultural, financial, and legal policies have discouraged the re-emergence of the former LPGs in any form (Bergmann 1992a). Pre-reunification debts were saddled on the former LPGs. During the GDR era, this included all money borrowed for agricultural production and infrastructural investments (roads, houses, schools, water and sewage, etc.) within the areas administered by the LPGs. Most of these debts were unrelated to agriculture. Moreover, LPG members never made these investment decisions. Nor can the newly created corporate farms use LPG assets or include them with their assets ("Studie zur Altschuldenproblematik" 1992:3). Note, however, that 38 percent of the former LPGs were debt free because GDR state bureaucrats had manipulated their finances so as to demonstrate the "success" of these model agro-industrial complexes (Roubitschek 1993:37). Naturally, banks have been reluctant to make loans to these corporate farms, not least because in the event of bankruptcy, the German government has first call on the remaining assets. These farms have no land assets since they are now renting the land they cultivate; their only assets are old buildings and antiquated machinery and equipment (no longer manufactured in COMECON countries). They have, in other words, no collateral and their potential incomes are often inadequate to pay back their old debts. In sharp contrast, none of the GDR farm debts are attached to the newly formed individual, family, or partnership farms (Zimpelmann 1991).

In the new market economy, the former LPGs have had to restructure themselves legally and economically in order to survive. Instead of doing everything within their districts, they now concentrate on specific agricultural activities. Prior to 1990, they produced crop and livestock products; maintained and built all roads, houses, schools, and other social buildings; and staffed all social functions. With reunification, they focus narrowly on only those economic functions that are profitable. In 1989, the distribution of all LPGs workers was about evenly divided between direct agricultural production and related-farm and rural services (Henrichsmeyer and Schmidt 1991). By 1991, only 35 percent of them were still employed in agriculture! Of the other 550,000 rural workers, 27 percent were unemployed, 23 percent found other jobs, 19 percent were attending work-training programs, 16 percent had received pre-retirement pensions, and 15 percent were retired (BML 1992a).

Concurrently, the German government has implemented new policies of its own and of the European Union's (EU). These policies will
reduce the levels of agricultural subsidies in the five new Länder (or provinces) in eastern Germany and in the region's largest farms. Consequently, crop and livestock production have declined markedly (Dabbert 1991). From 1989–1992, potato production declined by 33 percent, cattle numbers by 42 percent, and pigs by 60 percent (BML 1992b); fallow land meanwhile increased by 8 percent. Milk production in eastern Germany also dropped by 22 percent between 1989–1991 (Scholz 1992). The noticeable reduction in livestock numbers was also reflected in the three case studies (Table 5). The number of milk cows declined from 30 to 60 percent; and none of the pig breeding and pig and cattle feeding operations in Dedelow in northern Brandenburg have been continued.

The worker-owned corporate farms that emerged from the former LPGs represent a new form of collective farms, a hybrid with characteristics of both the past socialist economy and the present market economy. Despite the capitalist market and FRG intentions at the time of German reunification, eastern German agriculturalists have used new western legal forms to carry out old social(ist) goals.3 Ironically, the corporate farms that now dominate agricultural production in eastern Germany are more democratic and accountable than were the former LPGs, albeit with substantially fewer agricultural workers. Decision making is democratic because the shareholders (former LPG workers) elect their managers and decide how their farms should be operated. Before 1990, decision making by workers was only nominally democratic. Today, these new farms are accountable to their workers who are accountable, in turn, to the market and government policies and programs. Workers who participate in decisions are concerned about achieving levels of performance that will assure their farm's financial survival. This is particularly true of former LPG workers who are shareholders in a corporate farm or who rent their land to corporate farms. LPG reorganization into corporate farms has also eliminated the old internal class conflicts between LPG managers and workers of the GDR, but it simultaneously created new tensions between managers and workers in a market economy (Szelenyi 1982).

The corporate farm workers of former LPGs differ significantly from the workers on western-style industrial farms. The former have more direct incentives to assure the financial well-being and survival of their farms:

(1) They are the decision makers because they are the shareholders;
(2) They try to employ as many former LPG workers as possible;
(3) They are familiar with one another, having worked together for many years, often more than 20;
(4) These former farmers of the LPG land are knowledgeable about local physical conditions;
(5) Many are landowners (via the Soviet land reforms) and they rent their farmland to the newly recognized farms; and
(6) They have lived in and cared about their communities as long-time residents.

The former-LPG-now-corporate farms of Dedelow (Figure 1), one of the nine cases examined for this study, illustrate how social(ist) principles have persisted within a market economy, even as the number of workers declined by 45 percent (Table 5). Before reunification one LPG(P) produced the feed for the four LPG(T)s engaged in producing milk, raising calves, breeding and fattening pigs, and fattening cattle. All five operations were separate, yet their work and sometimes their investments were coordinated. For example, all five LPGs shared in the cost of the extensive irrigation system which also distributed liquid manure to the fields. Following reunification, eight separate corporations were formed from the five LPGs, yet all continued working cooperatively on a range of projects:

(1) Fifty workers cultivated 4,000 ha (9,884 acres) producing the feed for the other corporate farms in the group;
(2) Forty-five workers milked 2,700 cows;
(3) Thirty workers raised calves for the milk production farm and worked 2,000 ha (4,942 acres) in hay and other fodder crops;
(4) Twenty-five workers distributed all the manure from the livestock farms onto the cultivated areas and maintained the drainage ditches and hedges on all 6,000 ha (14,826 acres);
(5) Twenty workers repaired the farm machinery for the other member farms and sold farm machinery to whomever wanted to buy equipment;
(6) Ten workers operated a retail store, once associated with the LPGs but today completely independent;
(7) Five workers ran a hotel and restaurant in the building once used to cook and serve the 340 LPG workers; and
(8) Three workers operated a recreational airport formerly used for the airplanes that sprayed the large fields of the LPGs.

In the past, the five LPG managers met each week to discuss farm and village matters; in 1994, the eight managers met once a month to talk about and, wherever possible, coordinate work for the benefit of all eight corporate entities in a market economy.

Of Dedelow's former work force, 45 percent still work on the former LPGs; 20 percent have found new jobs, often away from Dedelow; 15 percent are unemployed; and 20 percent receive early retirement payments. In the newly formed corporations, ex-LPG workers received shares proportional to their previous assets. Major shareholders select the new managers and can remove them by vote of the members. All of the farmland is now rented from both private owners who have settled their land claims with the State and the BVVG (Bodenverwertungs- und -verwaltungsgesellschaft, a branch of the Treuhandanstalt—the federal German trust company responsible for selling all GDR dispossessed and collectivized properties) in cases where property claims have not been settled.

Dedelow's LPG farm workers now perform their old jobs as well as new ones. Consider the tractor drivers who once performed only one function, even if they waited for hours before they had any work to do. Today, these workers perform multiple tasks resulting in a greater variety of work, more responsibility, and the full utilization of their abilities and of the work day. These changes in their work routine have improved the welfare of employees, shareholders, and farmland renters, but it also means that they work harder and longer hours with less job security. The new conditions, alas, neither restructure the capitalist market nor eliminate labor exploitation.

Corporate farms and livestock often go together in eastern Germany despite the overall decline of livestock production. The mix permits farmers to utilize existing barns and animals and to use labor more intensively than in crop production alone.5 The role of livestock is apparent in the case of the Agricola corporate farm in Rehmsdorf (Figure 1), another of the nine cases examined for this study. Even with substantial reductions in the work force, farmland cultivated, and cows milked, the farm has continued milk production and it tries to employ as many former LPG members as possible. The farm's dairy barns are underutilized, however, because EU quotas limit the milk herd to 600 (Table 5). Current workers pooled their financial resources to buy the dairy cows and barns from the three liquidated LPG(T)s, thereby avoiding the old debts from these farms. Unfortunately, they could not do the same for their own LPG(P) debts because they had to continue this farm in order to retain their EU milk quota. On this newly organized farm, assets of the LPG(P) were divided among the former workers, 80 of whom still work.

Corporate farms also have adjusted work schedules in response to the smaller number of workers on each farm. Instead of taking vacations during the summer, the Agricola workers decided not to take their vacations outside of the three peak summer months. Everyone now performs multiple jobs and they work in the evenings and weekends when necessary. Although activities are substantially reduced now, Agricola continues to use the office, farm, machine buildings, tractors, trucks, and equipment from the former LPG, and it leases 90 percent of the 4,000 ha (9,884 acres) that it cultivates. The 700 leases average just eight ha (20 acres) in size.

The BVVG rents almost 1.1 million cultivated ha (2.7 million acres); 69 percent to farm corporations, 19 percent to former LPG farmers, 6 percent to former (1945–1949) large farm owners, 5 percent to newly arrived farmers from the West, and 1 percent to large farmers who had their land confiscated after 1949 (Belognen und Betrogen 1995:133). On average, three to four leases exist for every hectare of crop and pasture land in eastern Germany ("Im Zweifel" 1995:85–86). With so much land under lease, hundreds of land parcels have been traded between individual owners so that parcels could be consolidated spatially to facilitate more effective farm operations. Most lease agreements allow one party to pay the rent while another party actually farms the land. Because the number of land parcels is so large and the location of the parcels so dispersed,
farmers in an area exchange land to consolidate their operations without necessarily re-writing their leases. One farmer consequently cultivates land that another farmer pays rent on and vice versa.

Quite different from these reorganized LPG farms are investor-owned corporate farms. They conduct operations on the basis of a different set of principles. These include:

(1) Maximization of profits for shareholders (who are not the farm workers);
(2) Top-down decision making with employee inputs only when managers deem them valuable;
(3) Ownership of farm assets by absentee investors;
(4) Operation of farms by managers; and
(5) Field work done by workers who sometimes lack attachment to the local villages and know little of the local physical conditions.

In eastern Germany, the usual investor-owned industrial farms differ from those found in the United States. Although the former ex-LPG workers have farmed and lived in the local area for many years, are familiar with local physical conditions, and are attached to local communities, they have less of a stake in the farms than do worker-shareholders. My field work on the ABG farm in Bageritz east of Halle (Figure 1) illustrates the kind of western-style, investor-owned corporate farms that have been established in eastern Germany since reunification. In this case, a large landowner in eastern Germany before World War II has reestablished a large-scale farm with two new large machine sheds and an ultra-modern office building. The 4,600 ha (11,366 acres)—the western half of the former LPG in this area—are being worked much more extensively than during the GDR, but considerably less intensively than farmland in western Germany. By specializing in only a few crops (sugar beets, wheat, and oil plants), labor use is minimized and the use of newly acquired western machinery is maximized. The owner has hired a former LPG manager to operate the new farm and he has a staff of 10 full-time workers: two office workers, two machine repair persons, four tractor operators, two field foremen, and five part-time seasonal employees. With so few workers cultivating so much acreage throughout eastern Germany, the average ratio of labor to land is now less than half of that found in western Germany. Consequently, these large farms have significantly lower costs of production and higher profitability (Fuchs 1994).

Rural Landscape Manifestations of Reprivatization

In this section, I examine the responses to the latest reorganization of the rural landscape in eastern Germany brought about by the social power of the State (in this case expressed through the reprivatization of farmland). Landscape analysis assumes that space is the container of social power held by individuals and by private and public institutions. Consequently “the reorganization of space is always a reorganization of the framework through which social power is expressed” (Harvey 1989:225). In eastern Germany, the policies of the FDR government restructured and reorganized agrarian space. These changes are reflected in the numbers, types, and sizes of farms and the numbers and sizes of fields. Although FDR policies sought to return land to the persons who owned it between 1945 and 1949, plans for the restoration of a landscape of small-scale farms and small fields have been thwarted by fifty years of agricultural changes, by field consolidation, and by removal of field boundaries. The persistence of large-scale corporate farms with large fields continues to reflect GDR collectivization. To document the landscape manifestations of reprivatization, I have drawn on questionnaire data collected from a sample of farmers throughout eastern Germany; analyzed topographic and cadastral maps; and conducted field work on nine farms or villages. The questionnaires provide information on the number of new farms created, their field changes (smaller or larger), the reasons for these changes, and plans for future field changes. Field work examined field sizes and densities on specific farms and villages.

Farm and Field Changes

Although the German government has collected detailed information on each of the newly formed farms in eastern Germany
through its agricultural subsidy and adjustment programs, these data are available neither to researchers nor to German agricultural institutes. In 1991, the staff at the federal research institute for agriculture (Bundesforschungsanstalt für Landwirtschaft, Institut für Betriebswirtschaft, hereafter called FAL) in Braunschweig (König 1992) surveyed 250 LPGs (of a total of 3,969 or 6 percent) within a representative sample of 70 administrative centers for consolidated cooperatives (Kooperation). (The sample was selected in conjunction with agriculturalists at universities in Berlin, Halle, Leipzig, and Rostock.) In 1989, the average Kooperation consisted of one LPG(P) and three LPG(T)s and it cultivated 4,500 ha (11,119 acres). The sampled LPGs were found throughout the five new eastern Länder and in all ten physical regions (Figure 2). Privatization of this sample of 250 LPGs resulted in 728 farm operations; all were interviewed in early 1991 and again a year later. In 1993, the FAL staff developed another questionnaire (Isermeyer 1994) that was administered to the same farm sample in early 1994. The FAL staff subsequently entered the questionnaire responses into a computer database that provides the most detailed and comprehensive accounting of post-unification farm and field changes in eastern Germany.

The FAL data have several shortcomings, however. First, the FAL was interested in post-1990 microeconomic changes in the structure of agriculture as eastern Germany adjusted from a centrally planned economy to a market economy. The institute's agricultural economists were not especially interested in the spatial or landscape consequences of the adjustment process. The questions of most interest to geographers were incidental to them. My geographical analysis, then, was necessarily limited in scope. Second, although these data covered 728 farm operations, many questions were not answered by all respondents. Third, the questionnaire failed to specify the meaning of "field," nor were respondents asked about acreage changes in field sizes. The data are inadequate therefore for documenting the actual field changes in the rural landscape.

Although the questionnaire did not ask about the different kinds of fields, the landscape reveals three of these, each resulting in different patterns (Figure 3): 1) The most obvious field form is delineated by landscape features such as roads or dirt trails, rows of trees or bushes, fences, and drainage ditches. These are called landscape fields. 2) Within these easily identified fields are often found smaller areas of different crops, cultivated fields. 3) Fields in which different crops are rotated from one year to another in the same cultivated field are called crop rotation fields; these only exist over time (Stern 1990). Despite these shortcomings of the survey, the data provided useful information on the number of new farms that emerged from the former LPGs; on the presence or absence of field changes; on whether fields became smaller or larger; and on past and future reasons for changing field sizes. Division of LPGs into New Farms. Given the biases of German government policy toward and the financial incentives for establishing individual and family farms on reprivatized farm-

land, one would expect large numbers of new farms to have been carved out of the former LPGs. The FAL data show that the reverse is the case, however. Almost 32 percent of all farms continued as single operational units; another 20 percent were divided into two farms; 13 percent were divided into three farms; and 10 percent into four farms. In total, 75 percent of all new farms surveyed in eastern Germany resulted from a subdivision into four farms or less (Figure 4). Farm division also affected field changes. Generally, the fewer the subdivisions into new farms, the less the number of field changes, and conversely (Figure 4).

The number of new farms created from the former LPGs varied with a province's past farm structures and environmental conditions. Generally, LPGs were subdivided into many farms (eight or more) in the three southern Länder of Thüringen, Sachsen-Anhalt, and Sachsen (Figure 4). In these mountainous areas and in former small-farm areas, farm divisions often reached ten or more. Fewer farm divisions occurred in the northern glaciated areas, which once were the sites of large Junker estates and more recently of very large LPGs. In these areas, Brandenburg had noticeably more farm divisions than its northern neighbor, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern, but most of these were concentrated in the southern portion of the province where land and farming resembled Sachsen's.

In other words, most farmers in the sample (and by extension most in all of eastern Germany) did not change the sizes of their fields. Nor were changes in field sizes more likely under any of the three legal forms of farms. After four years of adjusting to the market economy imposed by the German government, most farmers continued to use the fields established by the LPGs. The legal and economic restructuring by the German state since reunification notwithstanding, the field legacy of the GDR persists.

This reluctance to change field sizes arises for two reasons. First almost 70 percent of the cultivated area in eastern Germany was rented. Indeed at the time of the FAL survey, the Truehandanstalt was issuing mostly one-year leases because of unresolved landownership disputes. Uncertainty thus has fostered stability. Second, GDR-era farm equipment is expensive, large, and most efficient for cultivating large areas and large fields. Given uncertainty in land ownership and the need for extensive cultivation, most large-scale farmers continued to use the large fields created by collectivization.

Of those farmers who responded to the question on field changes, field changes were most frequent in areas where both a large and small number of new farms emerged. Farmers on either end of the size continuum were most likely to change fields, but in opposite directions. Generally, smaller-scale farmers created larger fields and the large-scale farmers created smaller fields. Statistically, smaller-scale farmers also changed their field sizes more often as they adjusted their new operations to smaller parcels of land; medium- and large-scale farmers were significantly less likely to change field sizes (Table 6).

These changes in field size varied regionally along the lines of the farm divisions noted earlier. Statistically significant changes were disproportionately concentrated in the southern, mountainous regions of Sachsen Hills and Erzgebirge and in the northern glacial-lake district of Northeastern Mecklenburg (Figure 2 and Table 7). Before World War II, these two regions were characterized by small farms and large estates, respectively.

Field Change Direction. Farmers who changed their fields were also asked if these increased or decreased in size. Of the 30 percent of farmers (188 respondents) who changed field sizes, 175 also noted the direction of field changes. Forty-three percent of these respondents created larger fields, and 57 percent, smaller fields. Unfortunately, the survey did not ask for precise figures (in ha) on the changes in field sizes. Generally speaking, the more the number of new farms (over five) that were formed from an LPG, the more their farmers made larger fields; note, however, that a substantial number of these farmers did make fields smaller (Figure 4). Regionally, the trend was toward smaller fields in Northeastern Mecklenburg and in the Sachsen Hills, and toward larger fields in the Middle Brandenburg Lowlands and the Thüringer Basin—the former having sandy soils, the latter loess soils (Figure 2 and Table 8). Moving from north to south, the largest farms accounted for relatively fewer and fewer of the changes to smaller fields, and conversely. In Northeastern Mecklenburg, for example, the farms of 41 percent of interviewees who created smaller fields ranked among the largest in size; another 41 percent were of middle size. In Sachsen Hills, by contrast, 60 percent of the farmers who made smaller fields had the smallest-sized farms. The creation of larger fields exhibited statistically significant differences in regional pattern. Regardless of region, farmers with smaller-sized operations increased their field sizes in greater proportions than did the largest-sized farms; small-sized and medium-sized farms increased and decreased their field sizes in about the same number; and almost all of the large-sized farms decreased field sizes (Table 9).

Farmers failed to cite several reasons for field changes that might have been expected. They made no mention of changes due to new hedges, ditches, and roads, or trails. These once-common features of landscape fields were largely eliminated by field consolidation during collectivization. After four years of reunification, few efforts have been made to recreate them. This is consistent with the fact that only 30 percent of the surveyed farm operators changed field sizes, and of these only 42 percent reduced their field sizes. In this context, it is unlikely that many of the pre-1950 landscape features will reappear. Very large fields, in other words, seem to be a permanent feature of the eastern German countryside.

Farmers were also asked about their plans for field sizes over the next ten years (to the year 2004). Farmers who had increased their field sizes already planned to increase them more in the future, while farmers who reduced field sizes planned to continue doing so (Table 11). The plans for future changes thus were similar to their reasons for past changes. Farmers who had already increased their field sizes planned disproportionally to increase farm acreage, construct new buildings for livestock, and start ecological farming, while farmers who had already decreased field sizes planned disproportionally to decrease farm acreage, decrease or eliminate livestock production, and develop cooperative relations with other farmers.

The changing pattern and sizes of fields are evident on maps and field work. Topographic and cadastral maps at scales of 1:25,000 and 1:10,000 are sufficiently detailed to show all roads, paths, trails, rows of trees or hedges, and ditches that defined the visual borders of landscape fields (what Stern [1990:61] calls Geländeschläge) as well as all farmsteads and major buildings. The annual (1990 through 1994) field patterns and crop uses on the corporate farm in Klein Ziethen, Brandenburg serve to illustrate the pattern of permanent (landscape fields) and annual (crop rotation) field divisions. The farm of 1,696 ha (4,191 acres) consisted of 30 landscape fields, apportioned into 41 cultivated fields (Table 12). Twenty-two of these cultivated fields were bounded by permanent landscape features, while 19 of the cultivated fields were subdivisions of the eight other landscape fields. The landscape fields averaged 57 ha (141 acres) and the smaller cultivated fields averaged 30 ha (74 acres). The more numerous annual cultivated (rotated) fields were even smaller, averaging 27 ha (67 acres) in 1990–1991 and 23 ha (57 acres) in 1993–1994. Over the four-year period, the number of cultivated fields varied with annual crop production and rotation. A third to a half (49 to 35 percent) of the 30 landscape fields were divided into two or more cultivated areas in each year save for 1991–1992 when the proportion fell to 19 percent. Note, however, that individual farmers who began farming in 1990 (either because they had re-acquired pre-1949 land or because private owners had rented the land to them) did not divide existing fields, but merely took them over as a whole. Their privatized fields tended to be small, under 15 ha (37 acres).

Changes in field sizes were modest in the case of large-scale farms and more frequent in the case of small-scale farms. Table 13 reports the results of this comparative-historical map and field analysis. In the eastern German examples, both the landscape fields and cultivated fields were counted before and after major transformational events, that is, Soviet land reforms and reunification. The lowest densities of fields—about two fields per section (640 acres or 259 ha)—and of landscape fields were found in the former VEG Heinersdorf and LPGs Rehmsdorf and Dedelow. As of 1991, privatization had not changed the number of Dedelow’s landscape fields, but had slightly increased the number of cultivated fields (Figure 5). In southwestern Dedelow, where several individual farmers had established themselves, the number of cultivated fields increased as fields became smaller (Figure 6).

The Soviet land reforms in the village of Hasenholz established a large number of landscape fields—about as many as in western Germany and more than in Emerald, Wisconsin. Collectivization and privatization of farmland had little effect on the density of landscape fields because field sizes were sufficiently large for economical cultivation and because the wide, thick field hedges planted by individual farmers in the late 1940s are cumbersome and expensive to remove. However, once hedges had been planted and cultivated fields were consolidated, their density decreased by almost a fifth. This pattern has endured thereafter during the GDR era and since 1990 (Table 13). Collectivization in Hasenholz did not obliterate the hedged landscape as it did elsewhere in eastern Germany where fences, hedges, and even drainage ditches were mostly eliminated.

The sample areas were also examined for changes in field sizes since reunification (Table 14). The VEG Heinersdorf and the LPGs Rehmsdorf and Dedelow contained the largest proportion of large cultivated fields (over 65 ha or 160 acres); indeed, 64 percent of all of Dedelow’s fields were in this category. The continuity of large fields in this northern estate landscape is remarkable. Fields that were large initially were made even larger by the socialization of GDR agriculture. These have persisted under privatization with all its invisible (legal) small ownership parcels. Similarly, the small fields in Hasenholz (unusual for eastern Germany) have persisted after 1990. When compared to fields elsewhere, eastern Germany today has a larger proportion of large fields than Wisconsin or Niedersachsen.

Concluding Comments: Collective and Capitalist Agriculture

The analysis of collectivized agriculture in eastern Germany and the subsequent privatization to capitalist agriculture raises a number of perplexing issues with regard to behaviors that are voluntary or coerced; influences that are direct or indirect; consequences that are immediate or long term; and changes that are fast or slow.

The problem with the centrally planned economies had less to do with the public ownership of property than with their undemocratic institutions for decision making. These impeded self-management, decreased efficiency, and created social inequality. Planners, state bureaucrats, local managers, and technocrats in the GDR monopolized decisions and workers carried out their orders, often (not surprisingly) with little enthusiasm or effectiveness.

To assure compliance with state policies, central planners granted LPG and VEG managers authority over workers. Central planners and local managers then used their power to assure higher salaries, better housing and health care, and superior schooling for their own children. As did the political elites in the Soviet Union, Eastern Europe, and China, they substituted their personal preferences for those of the larger society (Konrad and Szelenyi 1980). Certainly the State collectivization of agriculture in centrally planned economies was direct, abrupt, and coerced, while the less-visible forces of the market, government farm policies and programs, and tax codes in the West were indirect, gradual, and voluntary. But for individuals and their families, these distinctions as to how they were forced out of agriculture are largely insignificant. Yet most farm families, policy makers, and scholars perceive these two rural transformations as fundamentally different—the former as undesirable, the latter as inevitable. In the end, the words and concepts that we use to describe each of these agricultural restructuring processes may say more about our political ideologies than about their realities. It might be wise to remember, therefore, that the collectivization of agriculture, whatever its deficiencies, resulted in substantial economic and social gains for most of the farm population and for the State. To be sure, we are in need of critical analyses of socialist collective farming. But analyses that condemn all aspects of the socialist past, while uncritically accepting the capitalist present, preclude our taking the best of both models and abandoning the worst.

The fundamental transformation of the eastern German rural economy and landscape since German reunification in 1990 is only the latest in a series of far-reaching changes brought on by the central authority of the State. In the past fifty years, no less than three distinctive State regimes have imposed profound changes on these rural areas and their patterns of farmland ownership, field size, and land use. The farm economy of eastern Germany currently consists of three complex geographies: one of land ownership parcels; a second of farmland rental agreements; and a third of cultivated fields. Although the boundaries of these geographies occasionally coincide, they usually do not. Corporate farms in particular have tended to preserve the field sizes and shapes established during collectivization, albeit with less land under cultivation than during the GDR. Ironically, the prevailing pattern of small farmland ownership represents a legacy created during the (communist) Soviet era and subsequently legalized by the (capitalist) German government. But the resulting cadastral map is largely invisible on the landscape; correspondingly small field sizes simply did not re-emerge in large numbers. Equally ironic is that private ownership of land has reinforced the landscape consequences of collectivization. These will persist for the simple reason that large-scale farm operations and large field sizes are well suited to industrial capitalist agriculture. Indeed, the extensive but profitable system of land use in eastern Germany increasingly poses a threat to the economic viability of the intensive small family farms in western Germany. In the end, the vanquished might yet triumph!

Vogeler, Ingolf. 1996. State Hegemony in Transforming the Rural Landscapes of Eastern Germany: 1945–1994. Annals of the Association of American Geographers 86(3):432–458. Abstract.

The West German state has transformed the collectivized agriculture of the former German Democratic Republic (GDR) since reunification in 1990. The government's reprivatization of land, based on Soviet land reforms during their World-War-II occupation of eastern Germany, has required GDR cooperative farms (LPGs) to reorganize themselves or be dissolved and encouraged the establishment of individual private farms. These hegemonic changes have
transformed the rural landscapes in eastern Germany, adding new farms, re-establishing previous farms, abandoning cooperative farm buildings, and resulting in smaller fields. Yet, the landscape impress of earlier State hegemonies persists. The centrally planned economy of the GDR gradually consolidated individual farms and fields (which had been largely established by the Soviets in the northern half of the GDR) and added barns, apartments, and social services to the villages. Reprivatization has produced profound changes in land use, farm management, and farmland ownership changes, yet the large-scale corporate farms that now dominate agricultural production continue to work the large formerly collectivized fields. Despite the privatization of farmland and farming, the GDR's imprint on the landscape in eastern Germany is likely to continue. The efficiency of these new corporate farms threatens the small-scale farms in western Germany.
