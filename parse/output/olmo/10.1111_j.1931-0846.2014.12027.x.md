SEARCHING FOR A BETTER LIFE: PERI-URBAN MIGRATION IN WESTERN PARA STATE, BRAZIL*

TRILBY MACDONALD and ANTOINETTE M. G. A. WINKLERPRINS

ABSTRACT. The world today is an urban world. While rural to urban migration has contributed significantly to the increased urbanization of the globe, that process has not always been as clear and permanent as it appears. In this paper we report on the migration of smallholders to peri-urban interface (PUI) zones in western Pará state, Brazil. We found that rural-urban migrants were constrained by their lack of marketable skills and as a consequence had few job prospects and experienced a low quality of life in the cities to which they had migrated. This led them to resettle in places at the PUI where they have the ability to supplement limited wages with more familiar subsistence activities, which reflected cultural identity and rural antecedents while enjoying a higher quality of life. The recent implementation in Brazil of a variety of conditional cash-transfer programs has added to the attractiveness of peri-urban places and contributes to a regional shift to livelihoods based on a combination of agriculture and extractivism supplemented by support from federal assistance programs. Keywords: Amazon, caboclos, colonos, migration, peri-urban interface.

While rural to urban migration has contributed significantly to the increased urbanization of the world, that process has not always been as clear and permanent as it first appears. In many low- and middle-income countries, including Brazil, employment in cities for people with low skills is often unstable, and government services and living conditions are frequently inadequate. Many rural to urban migrants therefore find that the cities they have been attracted to do not offer the resources and opportunities they seek. Consequently, many migrants circulate between the rural and urban zones, following economic opportunities (Waters 1997; WinklerPrins 2002a; Padoch and others 2008; Zhu and Wenzhe 2010), or resettle in the peri-urban interface (PUI) zone as we describe below.

According to Brazil’s latest census, 84 percent of its population lives in urban centers, compared with 55 percent in 1970 (Brazilian Institute for Geography and Economics [IBGE] 2008). At 73 percent, the Brazilian Amazon (most of IBGE’s “Região Norte”) today is less urbanized than the national average, but has increased substantially since 1991 when it was only 55 percent (IBGE
2000; IBGE 2008). This percentage continues to grow as more small farmers abandon agriculture and seek wage labor in urban areas (Walker and others 2009). In Amazonia, growing numbers of former smallholder farmers are choosing the PUI zone over cities as ultimate migration destinations (Browder and Godfrey 2006; MacDonald 2010). This paper considers why migrants are choosing to resettle in this zone by examining the trend in western Pará state, in northern Brazil, near the city of Santarém (Figure 1).

Researchers and analysts have long regarded rural and urban as distinct zones, with the urban fringe serving as a temporary transitional zone without lasting significance (Simon 2008). This distinction was always more rigid than reality, but recent rapid urban growth as well as advancements in transportation and communications technology, as well as externally driven macroeconomic adjustment policies have made this dichotomous distinction increasingly problematic (Simon, McGregor, and Thompson 2006). The PUI refers to the urban fringe that is characterized by change but is an ongoing part of an urban area. It is defined as the “zone of (dynamic) transition or interaction between urban and rural areas; usually used in the context of rapidly urbanizing poor

The specific structure and function of PUIs differ from city to city depending on city size, population demographics and dynamics, transportation challenges, and conflict over jurisdiction between urban and rural authorities (Allen 2003; Simon, McGregor, and Thompson 2006). These factors complicate the collection of comparative population datasets and other variables, however, and scientists continue to rely on dichotomized rural and urban growth datasets (Simon 2008).

PUI zones receive mainly two types of migrants: wealthier urban migrants looking for a more rural lifestyle and cheaper accommodation, and poorer rural people searching for work and education for their children (Douglas 2008; Simon 2008). While vulnerable to being engulfed by the city’s expanding reach, many PUI zones retain rural qualities such as low crime rates and access to abundant natural resources, including enough space for home gardens, all of which allow residents to engage, at least partly, in resource-dependent livelihood practices, which are important for cultural identity as well as subsistence (Brook and Dávila 2000; Slinger 2000; WinklerPrins and de Souza 2005; MacDonald 2010). Using findings from a qualitative case-study, this article focuses on the second type of PUI zone migrants in western Pará: rural to urban migrants who found urban life to be less satisfactory than anticipated, and therefore chose to resettle in a PUI zone rather than return to their rural origins.

People and Migration in the Brazilian Amazon

The Amazon region, though never densely settled, has a long history of human occupation (Roosevelt and others 1996). During the prehistorical era, cultural groups migrated within as well as into and out of the basin, some settling and practicing sedentary agriculture (Heckenberger and others 2008), others remaining more mobile and engaging in warfare (Mann 2005). European colonists and missionaries began to settle the Amazon region in the 1500s, bringing conflict and disease that caused the indigenous population to collapse from an estimated peak of 5–6 million at contact (Roosevelt 1999; Denevan 2003). During the last few hundred years, but especially since the mid-20th century, there has been significant population growth in the region as a consequence of resource booms as well as deliberate governmental colonization efforts (Hecht and Cockburn 1990). The current population of the Brazilian Amazon is about 22.4 million (IBGE 2011).

Caboclos, a mestizo people of mixed Amerindian, European, and African descent identified by anthropologists as Amazonia’s historical peasantry, have long practiced varied, adaptable, and opportunistic livelihood strategies, and are the culturally dominant population group living in the Brazilian Amazon today (Parker 1989; Nugent 1993; Harris 1998; Adams and others 2009). They originate from the more accessible areas of the Amazon Basin such as the floodplains and many of the towns and villages located along the rivers, and
most of the peri-urban migrants in our case are part of this ethnic group. Social scientists who have studied caboclos agree that it is difficult to generalize about them as they,

tend to be quite mobile, with migration facilitated by kinship ties which give residential rights to affines; they hunt, fish, and practise extractivism as well as agriculture; they spend periods in towns and cities; they engage in wage labour as well as in aviamento debt-credit relationships, and move seamlessly between monetised and non- or partly monetised spheres of the regional economy (Cleary 1993, 335).

Mark Harris characterizes caboclo lifeways as a “constant renovation of the past” and an ability to live in the present (Harris 2009, 69). This adaptability is currently exemplified by a regional shift away from livelihoods based on farming and extractivism, supplemented periodically by wage labor, to livelihoods based on wage labor and conditional cash transfer programs,\(^{1}\) supplemented by agriculture and extractivism (Steward 2007, 2013; van Vliet and others 2013). Caboclos embrace the intersection of the rural and urban zones, often maintaining households in multiple sites (floodplain and uplands; urban and rural) and move fluidly between these zones (WinklerPrins 2002a, 2002b; WinklerPrins and de Souza 2005; Padoch and others 2008; Brondízio 2009).

Historical migrations into the Amazon region and subsequent cultural assimilations contributed to the caboclo population. The rubber boom (1850–1920) was one of the first major internal migration flows from elsewhere in Brazil to the Amazon region, when landless peasants from Brazil’s Northeast region came to seek their fortunes producing and selling rubber (Weinstein 1983). When more efficient production in Asia pushed Amazonia out of the market, these rubber tappers either returned to the Northeast, integrated into existing rural caboclo society or migrated to urban centers, starting the slow but steady urbanization of the Amazon region (Parker 1989; Hecht and Cockburn 1990; Browder and Godfrey 2006).

The next significant wave of migration into the region occurred under the military dictatorship (1964–1985), when the Brazilian government attempted to use the Amazon to solve three national problems at once (Hecht and Cockburn 1990). Brazil’s northern border was thinly populated and poorly monitored, and needed to be brought into the national fold; the Northeast suffered from land conflict and frequent drought; and the economic potential of the Amazon region was thought to be underutilized. In 1966, the government initiated a series of tax exemptions, credit lines, and colonization programs to develop the region, and in the famous words of President Emílio Medici, “Bring people without land to land without people” (Schmink and Wood 1984; Hecht and Cockburn 1990). Tens of thousands of eager colonos, or settlers, mostly from the Northeast and South of Brazil, moved into hastily constructed settlements along new dirt roads, but with few resources and little knowledge of how to farm in the difficult and unfamiliar rainforest environment. These colonos dif-
fer from caboclos in cultural heritage and experience and therefore have a different knowledge system and skill set from caboclos (Brondizio 2009). However, long-term colono settlement in the Amazon is integrating this population into caboclo culture.

Ironically, in retrospect, the government colonization programs of the 1960s and 1970s were ultimately more successful in populating Amazonian cities than the frontier. Without adequate government support, the colonization plan was a failure for smallholder settlers as incentives for large-scale agricultural development helped to establish a successful agro-industrial sector for largeholders (Hecht and Cockburn 1990; Browder and Godfrey 2006; Walker and others 2009). The dream of the Amazon frontier as the land of opportunity faded for many disenfranchised farmers and today competition for land on the frontier is intense and violent as smallholders are forced off the land by large cattle operations and soy producers expanding northwards (Alston and others 2000; Simmons and others 2007; Walker and others 2009; Baletti 2012). Largeholders and corporate agribusiness, benefiting from economies of scale, have driven down the cost of production and forced small farmers unable to compete off the land, thereby leaving land open for appropriation (Schmink and Wood 1992; Simmons and others 2007; Grau and Aide 2008; Parry and others 2010). In response, many smallholders move to urban areas where there is hope for employment, education, better infrastructure, and government services (Browder and Godfrey 2006; Mondardo 2006; Romero 2012). In 1970, 36 percent of the region’s 7.5 million inhabitants lived in urban centers. By 2010, 73 percent of the region’s 22.4 million inhabitants lived in urban centers (Celentano and Veríssimo 2007).

Dietmar Stoian (2005) has noted that benefits of rural-urban migration are usually not accrued until the second generation, which is able to obtain an education and pursue a nonagricultural career. This means that the first generation of rural-urban migrants often suffer from unrealized dreams of full employment in the city, finding themselves ill-prepared for the urban workforce. This is the case in eastern Pará. Although Brazil’s economy has grown steadily in the last decade (The Economist 2013) the growth of Pará state’s gross domestic product has far outpaced the rest of the country, growing at 8.1 percent in 2010 (IBGE 2010). There are a number of development projects taking place in the Amazon, however new migrants to Amazonian cities have not necessarily benefited from this growth. The majority of new jobs are for people with higher-level skills and often go to workers from outside of the region, resulting in fewer opportunities for low-skilled former farmers from rural areas.

Despite this, former agriculturalists and their descendants continue to move in search of a better life, and this has propelled some to the PUI zone. Government programs have helped, especially the combination of the expansion of government-sponsored infrastructure development in rural areas, for electricity,
roads, and schools, and the continued availability of pensions for retired farmers. More importantly, peri-urban communities have become more attractive migration destinations relative to cities with the arrival of conditional cash-transfer programs (Steward 2013; van Vliet and others 2013). Brazil’s former president, Luis Inácio ‘Lula’ da Silva, initiated a successful suite of these programs aimed to pull people out of poverty and expand opportunities, which have been continued by his successor, Dilma Rousseff (The Economist 2006; 2010). Although many programs were designed to help the urban poor, the result in the Amazon has been that many smallholders who once farmed full-time now rely on these programs and maintain subsistence plots to supplement their income (Steward 2007; 2013).

Official government statistics on internal migration are not nuanced enough to demonstrate cyclical migration or migration to PUI zones. Several studies have demonstrated the importance of cyclical migration between rural and urban zones for caboclo livelihood strategies (Nugent 1993; WinklerPrins 2002a, 2002b; Padoch and others 2008), and research demonstrates that for some colonos migrants the move from rural to urban locations is also not permanent (Walker 2004; Browder and Godfrey 2006). The PUI in many ways represents the best of both the urban and rural worlds for many former smallholders. Furthermore, these areas may have the advantages necessary to stabilize caboclos, who are traditionally an itinerant population. Our case study examines three peri-urban communities near the city of Santarém, Pará, whose populations have grown significantly in recent years.

THE STUDY AND ITS METHODS

This case study was conducted in the western part of the State of Pará, Brazil. We used a suite of qualitative methods, such as ethnographically oriented household surveys, semistructured interviews, and participant observation (DeLyser and others 2010). Fifty surveys were conducted by the first author, as were over sixty hours of semistructured interviews and four weeks of participant observation. All interviews were conducted in Portuguese, the official language of Brazil and the language spoken by the study population. Our sample included both caboclos and colonos, migrants and nonmigrants, smallholder subsistence farmers, and midsized commercial farmers. Participants were almost evenly split between men and women and interviews were conducted mostly with one or the other, although in some cases interviews took place with a couple together. After initial contacts were made in the community, “snowball” (purposive) sampling was used to locate research participants (Heckathorn 2002; Secor 2010).

Research was conducted in three communities within the municipalities of Santarém and Belterra (Figure 1). Municipalities in Brazil are the equivalent of counties, containing cities and other-sized communities as well as rural areas within them. Our main study site was the community of Alter do Chão,
located thirty kilometers from the city of Santarém, within the Municipality of Santarém. The other two communities, São Jorge and Piquiatuba, are located within the Floresta Nacional do Tapajós (FLONA), the Tapajós National Forest a federal conservation unit, about forty and fifty kilometers respectively from the city of Santarém, in the Municipality of Belterra. The FLONA permits the managed use of natural resources by local registered users, who are subject to environmental regulations and oversight by the Brazilian Institute for the Environment (IBAMA). All three communities were experiencing population growth and an expansion of infrastructure and services. Until recently, the majority of their populations earned a living from agriculture and extractivism, supplemented by wage labor, but livelihoods were shifting towards a focus on wage labor and government assistance, supplemented by agriculture and extractivism, not unlike shifts in livelihood occurring elsewhere in Amazonia (Steward 2013; van Vliet and others 2013).

Alter do Chão is a town of 8,078 people situated along an inlet of the Tapajós River in the Municipality of Santarém (IBGE 2010). Often considered “the Caribbean of the Amazon” due to its white sandy beaches and clear water,2 Alter do Chão is a popular destination for weekenders from Santarém and tourists from elsewhere, who come to enjoy its beaches and ecotourism expeditions during the low-water months from June through October. This peri-urban community draws migrants from rural as well as urban areas due to its relatively high amenities; its population has risen 20 percent since 2000, when it was 6,726. Alter do Chão offers a combination of rural and urban qualities that is particularly attractive to migrants who left rural areas in search of a better life but were not willing or able to live in a big city. It has basic infrastructure including intermittent electricity, running water, access to K–12 schooling, a health post, and abundant, though seasonal, wage labor in the tourist sector. Alter do Chão also has the benefit of being well connected—via a major road with continuous bus service—to the city of Santarém. Our research took place in the adjoining neighborhoods of União and Nova União, which formed in 2001 and 2006, respectively, out of squatter settlements on the edge of central Alter do Chão. Ninety percent of residents interviewed in União and Nova União had been smallholder farmers, and identified mostly as caboclo.

Research was also conducted in two smaller, emerging peri-urban communities: São Jorge and Piquiatuba. São Jorge is a small agricultural town located inside the FLONA, between the BR 163 highway and the Tapajós River. It began as a tiny settlement in the 1930s in the middle of dense forest (Figure 2). Primary forest is several kilometers away today, and the community stretches along a grid of tree-lined dirt roads. São Jorge is populated mainly by colono farmers, with a small but growing number of formerly urban residents not engaged in agriculture. Infrastructure is relatively good for a small town: K–12 schools, a health post, electricity, and with the main highway only three kilometers away, regular bus service. PERI-URBAN MIGRATION TODAY

As rural smallholder agriculture continues to decline as a viable lifeway in the Amazon region, smallholders, after an unsuccessful urban migration experience, often chose not to return to rural areas but moved instead to a PUI such as those we studied, which offer a range of opportunities and access to needed infrastructure and amenities (MacDonald 2010). We found that peri-urban migrants chose to leave urban areas since, despite the relatively higher income they earned in the city, this did not provide them the quality of life they were seeking. Population statistics reflect this trend. Forty-five percent of rural villages inside FLONA decreased in size from 1997 to 2008 (Bauch and others
2008). Meanwhile the population of peri-urban communities with amenities in the same region, such as São Jorge, grew 55 percent between 1996 and 2006, and the population of Alter do Chão grew 20 percent between 2000 and 2010.

About three-quarters of our respondents had lived in a city such as Manaus or Santarém at some point in their lives. Several push and pull factors contributed to decisions not to return to the rural interior after a trial in a large urban center, but instead to go to peri-urban locations. Pull factors included access to infrastructure such as education and health care, and the availability of wage labor opportunities while still having access to a natural resource base to continue subsistence activities. Push factors from urban areas included a perception of less tranquilidade (calmness, peacefulness) and a lack of safety. The majority of interviewees in Alter do Chão cited a lack of schools, especially secondary schools, in their rural villages as their primary, initial reason for migration. These residents also felt strongly that they wanted their children to complete high school and work as office professionals. In the FLONA communities this motivation was less strong, but was still held by the majority of the population. Interestingly, the premium placed on education differentiates migrants to Alter do Chão from those in the FLONA communities. Residents of Alter do Chão are more educated than those in São Jorge and Piquatuba, with 85 percent of those in Alter do Chão having completed elementary and 20 percent high school, whereas only 20 percent of respondents in São Jorge and Piquatuba having completed elementary school and 15 percent high school. Nelson and Laura’s situation is typical of this trend.4 They are a couple who initially moved from the remote rural interior of Pará to Santarém, and then decided to resettle in São Jorge where they farm, work for hire, and own a small shop. Nelson compares their experience with that of many people from the region:

The people sold everything they had and went to the city. They didn’t find work and came back, but didn’t have the money to buy back what they had. So instead of doing better they did worse. It even happened to us in a way. We had to leave our place in the country because we didn’t own land, and we moved to the city for a short time but it didn’t work out. Work was scarce for someone like me who doesn’t have a profession. So we came back to the countryside. The city was horrible, I prefer the countryside... If you don’t have a profession in the city you only earn minimum wage and this is not enough to survive. Here we don’t earn minimum wage but we survive, we don’t starve, we have enough. Here we can pick fruit, eat some manioc, and get by. There on one salary you can’t live.5

Nelson and Laura have no formal education, but their daughter is attending school. Part of their reason for moving to São Jorge rather than returning to the remote interior is so that she can be educated. They want her to finish high school and have a career outside of agriculture, and they wish her to stay in São Jorge near them.

Migrants to PUI areas do not necessarily want to move from rural areas. We found that 66 percent of young people interviewed in Piquiatuba and São
Jorge said they would happily remain in their rural villages if agriculture were lucrative enough or they could find alternative economic opportunities. Of the people we interviewed, 55 percent had migrated to Manaus or Santarém, where opportunities were perceived to exist for a viable livelihood. However, these urban opportunities were not all they sought. Seventy percent of these migrants said that despite higher incomes, their economic condition was similar in the city as compared with the rural areas due to the higher cost of living, and 65 percent believed their quality of life to be better in Piquiatuba and São Jorge than it had been in the city. This indicates that for many migrants, the benefits of living in the city did not outweigh the disadvantages when compared to life in a rural setting and they had opted for a PUI-zone location instead.

The larger urban centers such as Santarém have better schools and a much larger economy than the peri-urban communities; however, the only place most migrants could afford to live were poor neighborhoods on the outskirts of town. Typically, these areas are underserved by public transportation, lack government services, and are characterized by violence, unsanitary conditions, and general lawlessness. Many of the people interviewed said that they felt uncomfortable living in such neighborhoods, but a lack of stable employment prevented them from moving to better neighborhoods. A move to a PUI community was seen as a better alternative.

For many, even in the PUI zones, the dream of a good job was still out of reach. Of those interviewed in Alter do Chão, 45 percent cited the lack of stable employment as their primary complaint. In the dry season, when the river beaches are exposed and tourists abound, there is a work in construction, tourism, and selling goods in the informal sector. In the rainy season, when the beaches and tourists dwindle, all work slows to a trickle. During the off-season, most residents got by doing a series of odd jobs, sometimes in nearby Santarém, while maintaining home gardens, fishing, and collecting wild fruit and game. All qualified for conditional cash-transfer programs. The people we interviewed who worked outside the home were engaged in an average of 3.8 different kinds of work over the course of a year, switching between these jobs numerous times or performing them simultaneously. Several had full-time jobs in addition to occasional part-time work. In many families every member worked at least part time, including children and adolescents.

Maria José moved from the rural interior to Santarém, and then to Alter do Chão where she has a house in Nova União with her husband and three children. Her experience is similar to many regarding employment opportunities. Her husband works odd jobs, and she is one of the few with steady employment, working as a cook in the local elementary school. She said,

Santarém is more evolved. There is more going on, you can learn a lot. But if I had to choose I would live here. The quality of life is better here. The work here is ok, but not every month. There are festivals, parties, these things that bring in some money. We earn well, depending upon what we do. Here we
don’t see drugs, which Santarém has. I haven’t heard anything like that. We don’t have assaults. It is very tranquil, for us and our children. Here is 100 percent better than in Santarém.

Migrants in all three research sites reported income to be virtually equal in the rural and urban zones after factoring in higher living expenses in urban centers, which indicates that quality of life is viewed as separate from income and is a key factor in migrants’ decision to either return to rural origins or to a peri-urban community. Migrants mentioned education, infrastructure, and job opportunities as the major factors pulling them to both cities and the PUI. The desire for safety and peacefulness were pull factors particularly to PUI areas. Push factors causing migrants to leave cities include violence and the lack of skills for the available jobs. One return migrant in Piquiatuba explained it this way:

Here it is easy to save money, it is tranquil, and we have energy, water, security, education. This is attracting people. Many people from here went to Alter do Chão but are returning because of the improvements.

Quality of life is highly subjective, but we attempted to identify what factors contributed to positive quality of life for these migrants by asking our subjects about their use of natural resources, about the availability of public services, and about feeling peaceful and at home in their communities. Many of the interviewees openly preferred the rural to the urban lifestyle. Manuel, who migrated from Santarém to Alter do Chão with his four children, plans to stay despite some challenges.

It’s good here in Alter do Chão. Many people are coming here wanting to buy land, people like it, because it isn’t violent yet, it’s peaceful. It’s good to live here, even though it’s expensive. I can go hunt, even though I have to hide a bit. It’s just as if I were living in the rural area, the only difference is that I can’t farm. But there are people who have gardens here. I plan to plant a little on my land, fruit trees, manioc; I have already planted a few. Everyone is going to plant.

While it is illegal to hunt in conservation areas and few admit to it, a third of our respondents fished regularly, which is legal. Reliance on the gathering of wild fruit is high (65 percent of our respondents did so), while a majority (75 percent) cultivated fruit and medicinal plants, and 45 percent maintained a home garden. All these activities are common rural practices and can also be practiced in PUI zones. They represent an economic necessity as well as a cultural link to rural heritage that is comforting to migrants, and are an important source of food to sustain families during the difficult rainy season.

Our research found that migrants were drawn to PUI zones because they offered a relatively higher quality of life than they experienced in a city. Seventy-five percent of our subjects had moved from rural areas to cities, most
often Santarém, in search of jobs and education. The scarcity of good jobs and the high cost of living in cities motivated them to move again and resettle in PUI zones which have relatively good infrastructure, K-12 schools, abundant natural resources, formal and informal employment opportunities, as well as easy access to the city of Santarém with its larger range of goods, services, and jobs. Our informants cited violence, drugs, lack of sanitation, pollution, noise, and a lack of economic opportunities as factors that pushed them out of cities. By practicing hybrid-livelihoods strategies including home gardening, extractivism, wage labor, and participating in conditional cash transfer programs, migrants found it easier to make a living in the PUI zone. Nearly all of our informants hoped that their children would find stable nonagricultural professional career jobs they themselves did not qualify for. Conditional cash-transfer programs have in recent years become a vital component of the livelihood strategies of both the urban and rural poor, and in our case, have helped make the PUI zones an attractive place for low-skilled, rural-urban migrants to resettle.

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION

In the mid-20th century, Brazilian government planners envisioned transforming the Amazon into a breadbasket for the world through an ambitious colonization plan that granted farmland to hundreds of thousands of formerly landless peasants from the south and northeast of the country. They succeeded in part as indeed Brazil has become a major global producer of agricultural commodities such as soybeans. This production is happening not with smallholders, however, but through large-scale industrial agriculture using little labor. While current improvements to infrastructure, economic diversification, and perceived quality of life are all important factors in stabilizing rural smallholder agricultural communities, it is doubtful that these will be enough to maintain rural populations in the long term. Smallholder agriculture is no longer economically viable in the region and the few career jobs that are available in rural areas often go to more skilled outsiders. Conditional cash-transfer programs may become essential to the stability of the rural poor.

The smallholder farmers who remain in Amazonia’s rural areas are struggling to survive as they are being squeezed out due to a variety of structural programs and processes not in their favor. As a consequence, they have been steadily migrating to urban centers since the 1960s. More recently, a poorly documented trend of urban to peri-urban interface zone migration among former rural smallholders has been identified, and on which we have reported here. These smallholders, Amazonia’s traditional peasantry, caboclos, as well as colonos who have settled permanently in the region and have adopted similarly complex-livelihood strategies, are well suited to succeed in PUI zones. They have always been influenced by global economic forces, and have created their own lifeway that reflects their cultural heritage and lived experience. Being culturally and in large part economically oriented to rural areas, many identify
more with and prefer a rural lifestyle. In the PUI zone, they can easily practice complex-livelihood strategies and move fluidly between the rural, peri-urban, and urban zones.

Some researchers believe that caboclo culture will not survive the modern era and that globalization, deforestation, land concentration, and vertically integrated agricultural markets with associated economies of scale will all converge to displace caboclos from their traditional homes and make their complex-livelihood strategies obsolete. We take the opposite view. We see evidence that hybrid-livelihood strategies, and the cultures that practice them, are increasingly prevalent in both urban and peri-urban settings. The emergence of home gardens in urban areas, improvements to rural infrastructure, transportation, economic diversification, and the growth and spread of the PUI zones reflect the increasingly blurred distinction between urban and rural in the Amazon region. Caboclos’ and increasingly settled caboclized colonos’ adaptive, composite-livelihood strategies are highly successful in these newly hybridized areas. Growing pride in and identification with caboclo culture may in fact lead to a resurgence and choice for caboclo lifeways over the next decades. Rather than being at risk of extinction, caboclos may be uniquely suited to thrive in the changing economy where a variety of skills are needed and the PUI zones are on the rise, and colonos who adopt composite-livelihood strategies may better their chances of success.

In time, the popularity of the PUI zones may cause them to lose their rural characteristics. Recent improvements in services, infrastructure, and economic conditions in São Jorge and Piquiatuba will continue to attract growing numbers of urban and rural migrants. The combination of services, infrastructure, and wage-earning opportunities available in Alter do Chão has attracted large numbers of former smallholder farmers. Despite the challenges of living in União and Nova União, relatively new neighborhoods in Alter do Chão where infrastructure exists but remains precarious, residents feel that through hard work and solidarity they can improve their quality of life. Although the majority of migrants in Alter do Chão said they might move again, it is likely that these migrants will stay and more like them will arrive.

Migration to the PUI is a trend that has received little attention, and together with urban to rural, urban to urban, cyclical, and seasonal migration, is in need of more research in order to understand how conditional cash-transfer as well as government-led development programs are impacting population dynamics in a the Brazilian Amazon. It would be valuable for peri-urban migration to be considered from a longitudinal perspective to observe if caboclos, who have traditionally migrated fluidly between the rural and urban zones, settle permanently in the PUI, and how they transform this new emerging space. Caboclos are continuously adjusting, and find a hybrid-livelihood strategy to be perfectly suited to living in these urban fringes. Rather than watch their stories fade into the history books, the Amazon may witness a
caboclo renaissance as colonos and others learn to optimize what urban as well as rural areas have to offer.
