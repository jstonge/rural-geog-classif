PARTICIPATORY RISK MAPPING OF GARBAGE-RELATED ISSUES IN A RURAL MEXICAN MUNICIPALITY*

ANDREW M. HILBURN

ABSTRACT. Garbage generation and garbage management are two critical human-environmental issues in Mexico and across the globe. Yet, an understanding of garbage as a broader set of human environmental issues beyond generation and management is lacking, amid a significant urban bias on garbage research. Using a modified form of participatory risk mapping, a method that rank-orders risks or issues by incidence and relative importance, this study highlights the diversity and heterogeneity of garbage-related issues as reported by 433 respondents in the rural Mexican municipio of Coxcatlán, Puebla. Results show that burning, dumping, service provision, public cleanliness, and other issues affect rural households. When the data are stratified by place and demographics, and applied to inferential statistics, the results show a more nuanced, more place-contingent picture of garbage-related issues than simply the institutional narratives on generation and composition. garbage, participatory risk mapping, Mexico.

Today, garbage¹ generation and the disposing of it remain two critical environmental issues across the globe. Increasing rates of garbage generation make generation and formal disposal particular preoccupations for development agencies such as the United Nations, the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, as well as governments across the globe (UN 2009; OECD 2012). This is especially true in Mexico, where garbage generation has increased 36 percent since 2000 with 47.8 million metric tons produced in 2011 (SEMARNAT 2012). As a result, the largely technical issues of garbage generation and formal disposal take precedence around most environmental debates and policy directives about garbage in Mexico and abroad. Furthermore, much of the focus on the management aspects of garbage there is on urban areas (Bernache and others 2001; Buenrostro, Bocco, and Bernache 2001; Bernache 2003; Ojeda-Benítez, Armijo de Vega, and Ramirez 2003; Buenrostro, Marquez, and Pinette 2008; Gómez and others 2008).

However, garbage-related issues that rural households in Mexico experience daily, such as inadequate access to garbage service, improper dumping, keeping a garbage-free home, street littering, and burning, to name a few, go largely unmentioned. Additionally, the narrowing of the garbage issue to generation and formal disposal conflates a plurality of different meanings of garbage (Moore 2012). This study assumes that garbage and its management are issues that are not homogeneously and unilaterally understood by all, but are
conditional upon perception, opinion, and environmental discourses (Barr 2002; Moore 2012). These perceptions are contingent upon place, uneven access to infrastructure, and global environmental discourses, making garbage not a straightforward management concern of generation, disposal, and environmental impact, but a political issue of environmental governance about control over the end point of consumption and the practices to deal with it (Bulkeley, Watson, and Hudson 2007).

More specifically, this study addresses how residents across the rural Mexican municipio of Coxcatlán, Puebla (territorial division below the state) construct garbage as a set of place-specific and demographically contingent, human-environmental issues. Using participatory risk mapping, a method used in development research to identify sources of risk, this study answers the following two research questions: What are the important garbage-related issues to rural Mexican households? and How are particular garbage-related issues contingent on place and demographic attributes?

Answering the first question forms the core of this study. The results address the first question by highlighting difference in what residents identify as garbage-related issues according to place and a number of relevant demographic variables. Additionally, chi-square and logistic regression analyses test whether place and sex have any effect on garbage issue perception. Statistical testing with participatory risk mapping can identify any within group variation on the perception of garbage-related issues (Baird, Leslie, and McCabe 2009). Tackling both research questions provides a much more nuanced, place-specific, and diverse description of garbage’s effects on rural places beyond the dominant narratives of generation and disposal. In addition, this study demonstrates the successful employment of the participatory risk mapping methodology for human geographers and allied researchers wishing to understand a human-environmental issue as stakeholders perceive and construct it. In this case, the method can illustrate the effects of governance, infrastructure, accessibility, and everyday practice on the public perception of an issue such as garbage.

Participatory Risk Mapping and Garbage

Participatory risk mapping² is used to identify and rank perceived risks in a population. The approach was developed by Kevin Smith, Christopher Barrett, and Paul Box (2000, 2001) to study perceived risks in Ethiopian and Kenyan pastoralist communities. Since then it has been used in other studies to gauge perceived risks of rural stakeholders in Tanzania (Quinn and others 2003; Doss and others 2008; Baird, Leslie, and McCabe 2009), but as of yet has not been employed beyond identifying risk.

Participatory risk mapping elicits the heterogeneity of perceived risk among seemingly homogenous populations. It is “a two-stage system of ordinal rankings wherein respondents first identify risks and then rank them” (Baird, Leslie, and McCabe 2009, 467) in order to examine differences in risk perception
within and among study populations. Participatory risk mapping not only notes the incidence of a particular risk being mentioned across a study population, it also provides an index of severity or importance of that particular risk compared to other risks. Risk, in the context of this method, is defined as “exposure to uncertain and potentially unfavourable consequences” and is used synonymously with “hazard” (Smith, Barrett, and Box 2001, 3). The concept of risk has application to a study of garbage because of the hazards, perceived or real, to public health and environmental sustainability that poorly managed garbage poses. However, risk implies some negative or “unfavorable” connotation that may be unwarranted regarding garbage or waste. One also cannot assume that all perceptions of garbage are risk related. A lack of service or desire for recycling does not intimate notions of direct risk. Therefore, this study approaches this two-dimensional method—incidence and severity—of gauging perception in a more openly defined way by not focusing entirely on a narrow definition of risk, but instead expanding the method’s scope to all human-environmental issues regarding garbage and its management. This inclusive approach is more applicable to the previously stated research objectives and it does not impose \(a\) priori assessments of garbage or garbage-related issues as risks that may be notable but less important to a study respondent.

The two-step procedure for collecting participatory risk mapping data is simple and noted to be easy to administer (Smith, Barrett, and Box 2000, 2001; Quinn 2003). The interview from which the participatory risk mapping data is obtained is open-ended, allowing the respondent to describe the most relevant issues or risks. First, the researcher asks the respondent to list what they consider the most important risks—or in this study, issues related to garbage. A respondent may list as many or as few risks as they choose, but a typical respondent provided two or three risks in this case. This study applied the more open approach to gauging not only risk, but any issue related to garbage. Instead of “risk” I used words and phrases such as asunto or “issue,” or lo que nos afecta or “that which affects us,” in the questions and instructions during the interviews, all of which were conducted in Spanish. If respondents inferred the words problema (“problem,” “trouble”) or riesgo (“risk”), it is intended that these understandings came from the respondents themselves. The broadening of participatory risk mapping beyond the concept of risk utilized in this study is only conceptual and does not change any analytical considerations as devised by Smith, Barrett, and Box 2001. Thus, the rank-ordering of garbage-related issues by their importance instead of risks and their severity does not change the way the data are calculated or are graphed. In this case, the respondent discussed garbage-related issues as the researcher recorded their responses on the questionnaire while allowing the respondent to observe their recording. Lastly, the respondent is asked to rank-order by number the issues they mentioned from most important (1) to less important (subsequent numbers). Ties were permitted and numbers that follow account for these ties (1, 2, 2, 4...). These
data3 allowed the calculation of incidence and “importance” values for particular issues which can then be subsequently “mapped” or plotted on a two-dimensional coordinate grid.

The incidence score, representing the proportion of respondents that listed that issue, illustrates the expansiveness of a particular issue across the sample or subsample of the study population. The index is measured from 0 (no mention) to 1 (always mentioned). A particular issue, such as dumping, may have an incidence value of 0.35, indicating that 35 percent of the sample population listed it. This incidence index can also be used as a variable in correlation-regression and chi-square hypothesis testing.

Calculating the “importance” or “severity” index, as it is listed in other uses of participatory risk mapping, is less straightforward than the incidence index. The reason behind a more complex calculation is that the number of issues listed varies by respondent, presuming the need to derive uniform intervals. One respondent may list four issues and another may list two issues and both may list the same variable with different rankings. Setting uniform intervals for each respondent mitigates the effect of differing numbers of issues between respondents. Uniform intervals are calculated by 1/n with n representing the number of issues mentioned by that respondent. Utilizing these intervals, the “importance” factor for a given issue by a respondent is termed \( R_{ij} = 1 - (r_{ij}-1/n_i) \), meaning the importance factor, R, is the rank or r of the issue j in the context of the group of n issues noted by i respondent (Smith, Barrett, and Box 2001; Baird, Leslie, and McCabe 2009). The mean of an identified issue’s “importance” index values allow it to be mapped with incidence values on a two-dimensional grid. The scale goes from 0 (least important) to 1 (most important).

STUDY AREA POPULATION

The study area is the municipio of Coxcatlán (N 18°14'46", W 97°7'5"), located in the southeastern corner of the Mexican State of Puebla (Figure 1). The municipio’s approximately 250 square kilometers are bifurcated east to west with the semiarid Tehuacán Valley forming the western portion and the pine-oak clad slopes of the Sierra Negra mountain range comprising the eastern half. As of July 2010, 19,639 people lived in the municipio. The majority of the population (73 percent) resides in small towns in the western, valley half, with 6,300 people in the cabecera municipal or municipal seat, also called Coxcatlán (INEGI 2010). The cultivation of irrigated sugarcane on social property lands (ejido) that is processed in a local sugar refinery makes up the most obvious economic activity here. Small-town goods and services provision and labor at nearby blue jean maquiladoras, and chicken and hog processing plants are also important sources of economic activity in the valley. In the eastern sierra half, 27 percent of the municipio’s population live in smaller, subsistence agriculture, day labor, and migrant labor–oriented communities. Here, in the sierra, Nahuatl is primarily spoken, but nearly all are fluent in Spanish.

In terms of official governance and service provision, garbage management across Mexico officially falls under the responsibility of the municipio with financial and technical assistance provided by the state government. In Coxcatlán, current municipal garbage and recyclables collection service extends only to the communities along the paved state highway. This includes the cabecera and the settlements in the more accessible valley half of the municipio (Figure 2).

Collection service is provided at least once weekly on planned days on a prescribed schedule along a normal route through each valley town. There are about 3,500 households within the collection service provision area in Coxcatlán municipio (INEGI (Instituto Nacional de Estadística, Geografía, e Informática), 2010). All collected garbage is deposited in an open-air dump approximately 2.5 kilometers south of the municipal seat on land donated by the local ejido. The fieldwork for this research was conducted from August to November 2011 by the author and a field assistant during a year-long stay in Coxcatlán. While the primary intent of this study is to simply but effectively describe the heterogeneity of perception regarding garbage as a set of environmental issues, statistical tests can quantitatively infer difference within the total sample population based on demographic and place-contingent variables. The “incidence” factor allows for hypothesis testing with binary logistic regression (Baird, Leslie, and McCabe 2009). Pearson’s Chi-Square statistic can also highlight relationships between spatial and demographic variables and the incidence of particular issues being mentioned. Using logistic regression, I focus on the effects of age and educational attainment on the odds of mentioning the nine most frequently listed issues. The binary spatial and demographic variables were cross-tabulated in SPSS to test for statistically significant relationships among them and the ten garbage-related issues with the highest frequency. Relating the incidence of issues with spatial and demographic groups can identify group or place-specific issues apart from the entire sample.

Interpreting the Participatory Risk Maps

Reading and interpreting the “issue maps” or incidence-importance graphs is straightforward. The x-axis represents the incidence, which is the proportion of respondents that listed a particular issue. The y-axis displays the “importance” factor that represents a normalized rank of the importance of that issue across respondents that mentioned it. When the map is divided into quadrants, the top-right quadrant encapsulates those issues that are both noted across a sizable portion of the respondent sample as well as maintain a notable rank in terms of importance as a garbage-related issue. Conversely, the bottom-left quadrant represents issues that are not widespread nor are of high relative importance. The relative location of these plotted issues effectively visualizes the differences or similarities of garbage-related issues by the total sample or between a sub-sample of respondents. This provides a demographically and spatially informed “map” or graph of how garbage is a much broader human-environmental issue beyond its constructions as generation and formal disposal issues.

The three most frequently cited issues in order from greatest to least were the problem of burning garbage, followed by general dumping, and the maintenance of public cleanliness. PROBLEMATIC BURNING

Problematic burning was the most frequently cited issue related to garbage in the entire study population, with 27 percent mentioning it in the participatory risk mapping study. With the presence of municipal garbage collection, burning was perceived by those who mentioned it as culturally backwards and environmentally damaging. This judgment was moderated to a degree by those who still must contend with their garbage outside of the collection service area, but a noteworthy number of sierra respondents elaborated that burning garbage was a necessary if less-than-ideal practice. The importance index value was also moderately high. Men more frequently listed burning as an issue and also attributed a moderately high importance value to it. Yet, female respondents listed problematic burning second in frequency with a similar importance value than that of male subsample moderating any real gender effect on this issue.

From survey data collected simultaneously as the risk mapping data, burning remains a common practice where at least weekly collection is available, with 28 percent of all valley households burning some of their garbage. Of the respondents that burned some of their garbage, almost one-third of them still listed problem garbage burning as an issue during the participatory risk mapping exercise, indicating its necessity or practicality in spite of regulations and social consensus against the practice where municipal service exists. Valley respondents listed burning as the most frequently noted issue, but not a comparably important concern. To many valley residents, most of whom have regular weekly garbage pickup, burning was viewed as an unnecessary and dirty method of garbage disposal that also posed a fire risk that can damage private
property and public space. Both the cabecera and the rest of the valley settlements mentioned burning frequently. The cabecera had a higher incidence, but lower importance index than the rest of the valley settlements. The stricter governance through customary ordinances (usos y costumbres), as well as a sense of greater neighbor surveillance in the smaller valley settlements, can be attributed to the higher importance value there. Yet the high frequency of problem burning as an issue here and across the valley was likely an indicator of the normative reliance on the garbage collection service. In other words, the arrival of garbage-collection service turns a previously ubiquitous practice such as burning into a maligned, but still extant, method of garbage disposal.

For households who lack municipal collection, controlled burning of garbage was considered a positive management practice, indicating a well-run household with notable incidence and importance values. This issue was exclusive to the sierra subsample of respondents. In contrast to the negative perspective on burning garbage, this issue situated garbage burning as a practical and inexpensive solution to ridding one’s household of garbage. It was considered a responsible alternative to garbage storage or dumping it outside the home. This positive view of burning was likely the result of efforts by public health workers in village clinics to promote controlled, low-intensity burning of garbage in lined pits as an alternative to keeping garbage around the house. Before municipal collection service was present in the valley settlements, the preferred method of removing non-decomposing garbage from the household was burning it in small cubic meter pits lined with stone or brick. Supporting the importance of a pro-burning discourse is the notable incidence and importance of the issue of keeping a garbage-free home. Relatedly, the practice of collecting and organizing garbage for disposal had similar incidence and importance values.

Respondents who burned garbage noted the often noxious smells and acknowledge the potentially harmful chemical by-products of burned plastics, but given the almost non-existence of plastic recycling service in the sierra and no municipal collection, most sierra respondents viewed a garbage-filled home as a bigger risk. Through interviews and participant observation, I noticed that most sierra respondents typically only burn toilet paper and plastic bottles that cannot be repurposed. In spite of this, burning as a problem featured an almost similar incidence value, but a lower importance value than that of positive burning. The high incidence and remarkable importance of negatively perceived burning highlights the presence of a consensus understanding of soil and air contamination by garbage burning.

Lastly, there is the notion of people saying that burning was an important garbage-related issue, regardless of their true beliefs on the subject. While the data accurately reflect the perceptions of the respondents, there can be honest incongruity with believing something is important but ultimately ignoring it. Thus, someone in the study area who burns their garbage knowing its ill effects can still contend that it as an important environmental issue.
DUMPING

Just behind the negative perception of burning in the incidence index was dumping, which when considering that other forms of dumping (dumping in streams, dumping in the woods) were counted as distinct issues, generally made it the most frequently noted issue. Dumping also carries a higher importance index value than that of burning, illustrating it collectively is held as a more pressing issue. Dumping was a major concern of the valley subsample where the not-too-uncommon practice to collect and store garbage until a sufficient quantity merits a trip to a tiradero clandestino (clandestine dump) or other hidden, neglected space like a roadside or barranca\(^4\) (intermittent, highly incised stream) to dump household garbage exists. The valley settlements outside of the cabecera had a higher incidence and higher importance values than that of the cabecera. Respondents in valley settlements outside of the cabecera that are more dependent on land-based resources listed dumping as a greater threat to their livelihoods than residents from the more service economy-oriented and urbanized cabecera. They also lived in greater proximity to the large clandestine dumps in the valley. Additionally, dumping defies the stricter environmental governance associated with customary ordinances that govern small town civic life.

In the cabecera, dumping was mentioned with almost the same incidence, but higher importance, in the centro (downtown) than the outlying colonias. This was likely due to the greater threat to orderliness in the more urban, built-up centro than the arguably more dumping-prone and informal semiurban landscapes of the peripheral colonias. Like burning, dumping was perhaps a more common practice prior to municipal collection service (Hayden and Cannon 1983; Deal 1985; Sutro 1991), but today is an illegal practice. Also, burning destroys, or rather converts to ash, the garbage itself, thereby removing it from future management, whereas dumping places the burden of final disposal on someone else or just persists, visually and physically contaminating the surrounding area. Improper dumping of garbage was listed as the most frequent concern for respondents from the eight sierra communities sampled. It also registered high in terms of its importance value. Land-based economic activity like smallholder farming, small-scale forestry, and ranching is the primary economic activity in the sierra communities. Dumped garbage threatens the productivity of the finite landholdings of sierra communities where livelihoods are more dependent on the intensive use of land for food-staple and cash-crop or livestock production. Dumping was the most frequently listed issue among women respondents, with a substantially high importance value among them. It was substantially less frequently mentioned, but, ranked slightly more important by male respondents.

Related to dumping is the issue of street littering. Street littering’s potential to visually contaminate and degrade the perceived order of a place (Pandey 1990) was usually given as the reason for its importance. Respondents made the
distinction between the two explicit. Across the study area, street littering had the fourth highest incidence with notable importance. This issue was reported similar between the valley and sierra, with the valley having marginally greater relative incidence in the valley than the sierra, but slightly lower importance.

More important spatial differences related to the street-littering issue were apparent. The more built-up urban landscape of the cabecera had higher incidence and importance than those of the rest of the valley’s smaller settlements. While there was no standard empirical evidence of greater litter presence in the cabecera, the more dynamic town landscape of Coxcatlán appears to have greater litter compared to smaller valley settlements. Furthermore, the centro of the Coxcatlán had greater incidence and importance than that of the aggregated peripheral colonias, suggesting a similar relationship with greater public activity and litter abundance. The second-largest town, Calipan (pop. 4,100), even had higher relative incidence than other, smaller valley settlements like Tilapa, San Rafael, and Pueblo Nuevo. One of the most obvious differences in the reporting of street littering is the higher reporting among women than men. Some studies have shown a link between women and reported responsible environmental behavior (McStay and Dunlap 1983; Ojedokun 2011) and a few have correlated gender with antilittering perspectives (Arafat and others 2007; Al-Khatib and others 2009).

Public Cleanliness

Public cleanliness, or aseo público, was an often-noted issue, with 21 percent of total respondents mentioning it. There was little difference regarding its frequency based on the previously noted spatial distinctions. This issue, as described by respondents, encapsulates general notions of public order regarding dirt, unclean things, and garbage. Aseo público is seen as a responsibility that both citizens and the town or municipal government must uphold. Individual responsibilities involve keeping areas around private property clean, enforcing littering and property-maintenance ordinances, and public refuse receptacle provision. Public cleanliness had the highest importance value and the second highest incidence of the issues noted by the sierra subsample. This issue had a similar incidence value in the valley but a much lower importance value. While numerous factors contributed to public cleanliness’ high incidence and importance as an issue in the sierra, local forms of governance help to contextualize it. The municipal government is the formal legal authority in the municipio, yet communities that are small and socially well organized enforce local customary ordinances. These ordinances, while not formally recognized, govern practical matters such as sanitation, infrastructure maintenance, and informal policing of civic order (Eisenstadt 2007). Under these rules, towns levy small fines on those caught dumping garbage, littering, or failing to maintain standards of order and cleanliness on their property. Given the small size and close-knit nature of many of these communities, self-policing is a strong factor
in maintaining what is observably a much less-littered and ordered sierra townscape.

In select small valley settlements, customary public ordinances regarding public cleanliness are strictly enforced. In one such town, mandatory weekly sweeping is enforced by small fines and garbage cannot be stored. Appointed town sanitation committee members inspect houses and also patrol for litter. Besides cleanliness in public spaces, respondents from sierra communities noted the importance of home cleanliness. The more independent-by-default sierra households understand the need to take a more active, willed approach to garbage management to keep their private spaces clean. The campaigns of public health officials also bear their mark regarding the importance of a clean home in the sierra.

Other Issues

Issues such as collection-service accessibility, recycling, public-trash cans, and environmental contamination also received relatively high incidence frequencies and notable importance index values. Considering that there is no collection service in the sierra communities of Coxcatlán municipio, lack of service only had a small incidence value there, but had a high importance value given by those who noted it. In the valley, where nearly universal collection service exists, lack of service was a prominent issue and respondents mentioned that the schedule and routes were inconsistent or that their street or block did not receive service with any predictable frequency. Interestingly, there were no apparent differences in incidence and importance of this issue between the cabecera and the rest of the valley settlements. However, within the cabecera, the less-served colonias noted the lack of service far more frequently than the centro.

Recycling was also notable for its incidence, but not its importance. Its remarkable incidence suggests the recognition of recycling as a pro-environmental practice, but one that is less important or trivial in terms of overall goals of minimizing waste. Some respondents noted that recycling was not worth the effort or were unsure where the generated revenue went. There is some spatial or demographic variation for the incidence and importance values on recycling across the study population. The centro of the cabecera, where the recycling truck passes by frequently, had remarkably high incidence but only moderate-to-low importance. The act of separating garbage was of similar incidence to recycling, but had greater assumed importance. Surprisingly, in the sierra, where separation is not rewarded by the state, separation has slightly higher incidence, but less importance than those of the valley settlements. The need to separate the compostable and reusable from the truly disposable is the responsibility of each sierra household as garbage collection is not available there. Campaigns encouraging participation in the relatively new (2009) recycling program add to separation’s high importance in the valley.
Across the study area, the desire for more public garbage receptacles was a commonly listed issue due to the perception of their presence being the only real behavioral solution to litter. Making nonlittering behavior more convenient was the intended effect of the barrels, which have an empirically derived positive effect of litter reduction (Finnie 1973; De Kort and others 2008). The perception of greater governmental rather than communal or individual responsibility for litter and garbage management also likely influences its higher frequency and importance among valley residents than sierra residents who contend with quasi-governmental community statutes like usos y costumbres.

Water is a precious natural resource in the semiarid Tehuacán Valley as well as its adjacent sierra. Water contamination by garbage in streams, wells, irrigation canals, and other small bodies of water was a more or less frequent concern of relatively high importance. In Calipan, a sugarcane-dependent and water-abundant community, the issue of garbage blocking irrigation canals and contaminating irrigated lands had a notable incidence and importance. Outside of the survey, a resident of a small valley settlement noted how the extensive irrigation canal system of the entire valley can act as a conduit for waterborne garbage, with more populated and perceivably “dirtier” upstream communities essentially dumping on smaller, “more ordered” downstream communities.

Lastly, the association of vermin, garbage, and the public health risk they imply were also notable, with mosquito incubation in garbage and stray dog subsistence having comparably low but remarkable incidences and importance values.

The gender distinction showed significant relationships, with street littering (p < 0.05) and dumping (p < 0.05) having greater incidence of these issues being among women. Research has shown that women are less likely to litter or at least admit that they litter (Cialdini, Reno, and Kallgren 1990; Meeker 1997). Also in the study area and across Mexico, antilitter campaigns generally involve women, both in leadership and practice. Additionally, garbage management and the maintenance of sanitation are locally relegated to female domestic labor. Thus, a practice that challenges the control of garbage and general order is expectedly more often mentioned by women.

There were only two statistically significant logistic regression models for educational attainment with regard to the ten issues with the highest incidence. Only the incidence of the issues of public barrels and service provision with educational attainment showed a statistically significant relationship representing a positive prediction with increasing educational attainment. Research has shown the effect of environmental education campaigns with increasing effectiveness of public-waste receptacles (De Kort et al., 2008; Schultz and Others 2013). Also, increasing formal education as a predictor of the incidence of the
service provision issue may indicate that formal education has an effect where state-controlled, nontraditional, “modern” garbage management such as collection is preferable. Conversely, this relationship may indicate a perception of “traditional” methods as unsanitary or environmentally unsustainable. In Mexico, and especially in Puebla, environmental education at a number of levels has been effective in promoting proenvironmental behavior (Guevara-Martínez and Fernández-Crispín 2009).

CONCEPTUALIZING GARBAGE

Besides indicating the heterogeneity of garbage-related issues across the study area, the participatory risk mapping results point to how waste is conceptualized in the study area. Dumping, burning, public cleanliness, and littering are important issues across the municipio, as well as others. Many of the issues, like those above, reference garbage-management practice. Using participatory risk mapping data, one can see that Coxcatlán residents frame garbage issues around what Sarah Moore calls “waste as manageable object” (2012). This garbage conceptualization centers it as subject to varying systems of governance, both formal and informal. Garbage in this sense is everyday out-of-place matter (Douglas 1966) and it is something apart from society and needs to be managed by it to maintain order (Moore 2012). Furthermore, the data suggest a desire for a growing role for more formal waste service in line with larger debates about garbage management.

The management regime evident by this management-centric conceptualization of garbage appears more formal than informal. On one end, the management options available in the home, such as burning or even dumping, are seen as negative practices by the study population. While the practices persist, both of necessity and choice, their assessment is mostly negative. The relatively high incidence and importance given to the provision of waste receptacles also suggests a larger role given to the state in managing garbage. The notable incidence of recycling also indicate a shift to formal, outside-the-home garbage management.

The denigration of dumping and burning stems largely from their conceptualization as a health and environmental risks, which is yet another way garbage is perceived. This view of burning and dumping can be framed by what Ulrich Beck (1992) and Anthony Giddens (1990) call the “risk society” theory or the perceptual replacement of agency toward human and technological causes of risk as opposed to natural causes in modern societies. Thus, garbage, by its perceived increasingly inorganic constitution, further embodies this technocentric risk (Wakefield and Elliott 2000; Moore 2012). Moore claims that the waste-as-risk conceptualization challenges existing notions of formal governance (2012). These practices indicate a lack of capacity of formal government to protect their constituents from environmental degradation and health risks. Thus, the presence of burning and dumping as issues indicate dissatisfaction with the lack of universal service.
CONCLUSION

The production and disposal of waste is one of the most basic and intrinsic human ecologies (Peet and Others 2011). From its broadest contours, this study contributes to a dynamic body of human-environmental geographical research on garbage and garbage management in less-developed areas of the world. Paul Robbins has listed “the political ecology of trash, waste management, and garbage subsistence” under a list of “substantive research mandates,” pointing to productive spaces for its investigation within political ecology (2004, 215). Since then, garbage has seen a growing presence in human-environmental geography (Nchito and Myers 2004; Myers 2005; Davies 2008; Moore 2008, 2009, 2011; Hartmann 2012), yet the existing literature remains small in small-town and rural landscapes.

In Mexico, this study adds to a growing body of geographical research on garbage management in areas outside of major metropolitan areas (Buenrostro, Bocco, and Bernache 2001; Buenrostro and Israde 2003; Hanson 2013; Hilburn 2014). Its attempt to center the household in garbage management joins it with other important geographical research on household waste management (Barr 2002; Barr, Gilg, and Ford 2001; Barr and Gilg 2006; Bulkeley and Gregson 2009).

This study highlights the utility of the participatory risk mapping methodology in identifying how garbage or any other human-environmental theme affects a target population as they report it (Baird, Leslie, and McCabe 2009). For geographers and allied researchers, this method allows one to see the heterogeneity and breadth of issues according to place and demographics. Lastly, since the study respondents themselves give the issues, participatory risk mapping points to how residents perceive of garbage themselves, as managed everyday matter, as a hazard, as risk, and as a challenge to control.
