BLACK DISTRICTS IN SOUTHEASTERN FLORIDA

DAVID LEE

ABSTRACT. Early in the twentieth century, rural blacks in southeastern Florida clustered on farm- or scrubland. In urban areas, black zones arose on vacant fringes and later expanded into white sections. By the 1920s, the black urban areas grew as contiguous corridors along transportation lines. Small black communities also remained separated from each other by commercial or white residential districts. During the 1950s urban expansion was extensive, but discrimination forced blacks into small, disconnected communities and to travel longer distances for goods and services than did whites. This pattern suggests that separate sets of central-place hexagons developed in areas of racial bias.

THIS study examines the location of the black population in southeastern Florida, specifically the eastern parts of Palm Beach, Broward, and Dade counties. The settlement patterns of blacks and their relationship to those of the white population are traced historically, and generalizations are offered to explain past and present racial locations. Furthermore, this analysis challenges certain generalizations that seek to account for the location of blacks, especially in Palm Beach and Broward counties. The classical central-place model explains the location of population in southeastern Florida, but the specific configuration differed by race before the civil-rights era. The central-place network of blacks was rapidly reconfigured during the 1960s. This shift suggests that separate sets of central-place hexagons may develop in areas where bias restricts the mobility of a disenfranchised group.

According to the 1990 census (U.S. Bureau of the Census 1990), Palm Beach, Broward, and Dade counties contained slightly more than four million people (Table I). Racially, the population was 78.2 percent white, 17.2 percent black, and 4.6 percent other races. Though not a racial category, Hispanics constituted 27.8 percent. Of the 642 census tracts in the three-county region, twelve, most of them in Palm Beach County on the shores of Lake Okeechobee, were omitted from the analysis because they were far west of the regional ecumene and functionally were discrete from the coastal communities.

GROWTH OF BLACK SETTLEMENTS

In the antebellum southern city, four patterns of black housing existed: small black districts on out-of-the-way streets, houses of free blacks who were homeowners; shantytowns near a city edge where slaves not needed in agricultural or domestic activities were quartered; and back-alley sites. The last, the most universal of the patterns, interspersed blacks in white residential districts. Those blacks were usually domestics who were housed near the residences of their masters. After the Civil War, freed slaves migrated not only to the North but also into southern cities. The new immigrants located in marginal areas on the outskirts of town that were often poorly drained or otherwise unesteemed sites. Owners of such land made handsome profits by dividing and selling plots to the poor blacks. This became a classic southern pattern (Rose 1969, 327; Kellogg 1977, 312; Ford and Griffin 1979, 156). The districts contained substandard housing and were often bounded by obvious barriers such as railroad lines and natural features. The barriers eliminated any zone of transition: black districts directly abutted white ones, separated by the sharp line of the barrier. This pattern was typical of Florida in the post-Reconstruction era. As a black district was surrounded by white housing, a new black area, often with substandard housing, arose elsewhere (Kharif 1985, 170).

Southeastern Florida was settled late in the nineteenth century after the Florida East Coast Railway was built from West Palm Beach to Miami. Miami and West Palm Beach were the two largest urban centers. Between them, as well as in Dade County to the south of Miami, numerous small agricultural communities arose along the railroad corridor. Recruited from outside the area, much of the labor to work the farms consisted of blacks from the Deep South. There were few blacks in the rural areas, and they generally lived close to whites. Some lived in shanties for agricultural laborers on white-owned farms. Some occupied isolated squatter shacks of subsistence farmers or farm laborers. Separated from white urban centers, both types of black settlement arose under the always watchful eye of white authority.

As the urban centers of southeastern Florida grew and incorporated, they attracted more black residents. Towns adopted segregation laws of the state and passed Jim Crow ordinances of their own. As a result, blacks were restricted to areas with inadequate services. Tiny communities of blacks dotted the landscapes. Often the areas initially set aside for black housing
were adjacent to railroad tracks, removed from white zones by unoccupied scrubland. With the arrival of increased numbers of workers, blacks began to settle the vacant zones in agricultural communities (George 1978).

The population of both races increased greatly during the 1920s, 1930s, and 1940s, and agriculture declined in relative importance. White settlement expanded beyond the confines of the railroad-focused urban centers. Beach and Intracoastal Waterway sites were highly desired by wealthy whites. As population grew, the areas of both white and black housing expanded, and a continuous band of settlement extended north and south along the railroad and highway corridors. The former agricultural centers agglomerated into an urban conurbation from the northern border of Palm Beach County to south of Miami.

Urban Development

One result of that urban expansion lay in efforts by white-dominated municipalities to restrict blacks to specific areas. Palm Beach, for example, had its Styx neighborhood, a black community of fairly substantial housing. However, this community was razed with the development of Palm Beach as a winter haven for wealthy whites (Curl 1986, 38). A new black district was formed in neighboring West Palm Beach. At the beginning of the twentieth century, white and black residential zones in West Palm Beach were separated by a hill between two lakes (Utz 1972, 54).

Most of the black districts in southeastern Florida followed the classic southern pattern, in which new residential areas for blacks began on vacant fringe lands. As the urban areas of the region expanded, especially in the boom of the early 1920s, black districts became surrounded by industrial, commercial, and white residential areas. When a district could no longer expand spatially, various patterns ensued. In some cases, the density of the existent districts increased, as was the case in Overtown in Miami during the 1920s. By contrast, the blacks of the Styx community in Palm Beach moved to a new location in West Palm Beach. In other cases, the black population, originally concentrated in one location, later shifted to establish new districts some distance away.

The last pattern marked Miami during the 1950s (Rose 1964). At the beginning of the decade, blacks were housed in three zones: a core, an inner ring, and an outer ring. The core had two sections, Overtown and discontiguous but larger Liberty City and Brownsville. As a result of in-migration and high birthrate the core had a growth rate of 318 percent during the 1950s. By the end of the decade Liberty City and Brownsville had fused into one settlement. Expansion advanced into a zone of transition, from which whites departed as blacks entered. The inner ring had five separate communities that housed workers who commuted to the central city for employment as well as persons who were employed locally. The outer ring was
a semirural setting more than twenty miles from the central city. The residents had little direct dependency on the city as a source of income. Fragmentation of blacks into many residential areas characterized the Miami metropolitan area during the 1950s.

Subsequent expansion of the core and inner-ring communities into white areas by the 1980s created a continuous zone of black settlement from Overtown on the south northward to the Broward County line. The process was accelerated by urban-renewal projects that eliminated thousands of substandard, high-density dwellings in older black neighborhoods (Fernald 1981, 95; Winsberg 1983, 310). The outer-ring communities remained spatially separated, and new ones arose. By 1980 fifteen communities accounted for four-fifths of the blacks in Dade County (Rose 1989, 475-476). The black communities initially displayed the southern pattern of residential development on vacant land, but in recent years black expansion into white areas, a process associated with northern urban centers, has been operative.

MODELS OF BLACK RESIDENTIAL LOCATION

Urbanization in eastern parts of Dade, Broward, and Palm Beach counties is currently contiguous, restricted on the east by the Atlantic Ocean and on the west by the Everglades. The most populous black tracts, 97 tracts in all, contain 450,730 blacks, or approximately two-thirds of all blacks in the study area. The most populous white tracts number 234 and contain 2,104,297 whites, or approximately two-thirds of all whites in the study area (Fig. 1). Tract information was generalized to reflect the actual location of these groups, not arbitrary tract boundaries that, especially on the west, poorly fit the actual location of population. The fact that two-thirds of the white population is spread over 234 tracts, whereas two-thirds of the black population is confined to 97 tracts, shows that the blacks of south Florida are much more consolidated and concentrated than are the whites.

In Miami and West Palm Beach, north-to-south-trending residential zones developed. On the east, fronting the ocean or the Intracoastal Waterway, are condominiums and expensive estates. The central area contains older, decaying, and largely black housing adjacent to the north-south railroads and highways. Here the terms belt and corridor would be appropriate descriptions of the spatial concentration of blacks. The western area, which is the faster growing, contains moderate to elite housing. This area is primarily occupied by whites who arrived since the 1950s and by better-off blacks who migrated there during the 1970s and 1980s (Winsberg 1983, 311).

The pattern is less defined between Miami and West Palm Beach. The area occupied by each race initially was central, that is, along the coastal railroad and paralleling the north-south highways. These districts were separated by vacant land. As new white residents arrived in southeastern Florida, they located in the remaining areas of vacant land near the railroad, in expensive beach or intracoastal areas, or in new developments to the west. The terms belt and corridor have been invoked to describe the location of black residential areas between Miami and West Palm Beach. These terms suggest narrow zones of contiguous, uninterrupted housing. To determine the accuracy of that description, I analyzed the population of Broward and Palm Beach counties by zone and by transect. The 364 eastern census tracts in the two counties were aggregated into five north-south zones and twenty transects (Fig. 2; Table II).

Zone 1 extends from Florida’s Turnpike westward to the Everglades. It contains the remaining agricultural land in the eastern portion of the two counties and new housing developments. Accounting for more than 35 percent of the 1990 population, it is the most populous of the five zones. However, it contains only 15 percent of the two-county black population, most of which are agricultural workers living in labor camps in or near the fields. These tracts are only 5.8 percent black.

Zone 2 extends from the turnpike and the Seaboard Coast Line, which is generally midway between the turnpike and the Florida East Coast Railway. In many locales the Seaboard Coast Line corresponds with Interstate 95, so that an effective barrier is created. This zone has a mixture of old housing, especially in Broward County, and new developments. Overall, approximately one-third of the two-county population lives in this zone, as does one-third of the black count.
Zone 3, from the Seaboard Coast Line to the Florida East Coast Railway, has much of the oldest housing in the region. This zone contains almost 42 percent of all the blacks in the two-county area and has the highest black percentage, at 33.8 percent. Zone 4, a band of census tracts immediately east of the coastal railway, contains more than 10 percent of the total population and 6.6 percent of the black count. Housing is a mixture of single-family dwellings and multiple-unit structures. Zone 5 comprises expensive estates and condominiums. It is the least populous of the five zones, with only 6 percent of the overall population, of which the 474 blacks account for only 0.4 percent.

From these aggregate figures, it is clear that the central parts, especially zones 3 and 4, contain more than three-quarters of the blacks in the two-county region. At this level of aggregation, it would seem that blacks may inhabit a corridor between the two railroads. However, a more detailed examination shows areas of concentration as well as areas virtually devoid of blacks. Twenty transects were isolated, each approximately three miles north to south, extending from the ocean to the Everglades. Each transect was spread over the five zones, for a total of one hundred cells for analysis.

The central zones 2 and 3 show that there are definite maxima and minima in the location of blacks. Riviera Beach and northern West Palm Beach are the first maximum, after which come lesser ones at Boynton Beach, Delray Beach, Deerfield Beach, and Pompano Beach. Southern Fort Lauderdale, transect 17, is a giant in terms of black population, mainly in zone 2, where 60,870 blacks reside. Between these maxima are areas where few blacks live. For example, in transect 13 (Boca Raton) the total population of zones 2 and 3 is 50,188, but blacks number only 820.

The central zones are much more complex than can be suggested by belt or corridor labels, because the areas of black housing are not contiguous. Instead, there is a series of black communities of varied size, separated from each other by areas of light industry, commercial developments, and white housing. This pattern evolved from the 1920s through the civil-rights era of the 1960s. The black districts had been consciously and deliberately located where they would not interfere with white housing. As both populations increased, the problem of future areal expansion arose. After being established, the black areas expanded into vacant land until they reached the defining barriers or until they abutted white districts. Barriers to black expansion could be natural features such as lakes and swamps or cultural features such as canals, railroads, and highways. Rarely did black districts directly abut white ones, and when it did occur, radical measures were sometimes taken to ensure segregation. For example, in Boca Raton, the black community was contained on three sides by a railroad, a highway, and a commercial development, but on the fourth side it abutted an area of white condominiums. Their owners erected a high concrete fence that totally enclosed the area and isolated pedestrian traffic (Evans and Lee 1990, 136).
When growth was restricted in certain directions, it flowed in another, usually westward. Thus many large black districts in Palm Beach and Broward counties have older housing in the eastern portion and progressively newer structures toward the west. This pattern is especially true for Fort Lauderdale. The original black district, near the Florida East Coast Railway, was unable to expand northward or southward. Further expansion occurred to the west. Currently the black community in Fort Lauderdale occupies a sizable district that is spatially separated from other black communities to the north or south.

Some small black communities were surrounded before they could expand westward. Some lost their identity as commercial or other landuses expanded at the expense of black housing. A few tiny communities remain, but they are likely to disappear in the near future through sellouts. Pearl City, the small black district in Boca Raton, is located on land that is highly valued for commercial purposes, and black homeowners may not resist selling their property to developers (Evans and Lee 1990, 147).

Yet another recent pattern is identifiable. In the post–civil-rights era, many formerly all-black districts have been integrated. The inexpensive houses, well located relative to highways, beaches, and employment, appeal to young white families of modest means. Gentrification is under way in these districts. Some neighborhoods that are now racially mixed are likely to become progressively more white.

CENTRAL-PLACE MODEL

The central-place model is an appropriate analytical device to explain the location of black districts between Miami and West Palm Beach. After World War I, persons of all races were located on farms or in towns separated by fields or scrubland. The isolated towns provided employment and supplied low-order goods and services. Higher-order goods and services were available in larger centers such as Miami or West Palm Beach. Settlement during that period would correspond more or less to a classical central-place model, at least a narrow sector of one (Fig. 3). In 1920, Miami, with a population of almost 30,000, was the dominant, or fifth-order, city in the model. West Palm Beach was a fourth-order center, with a population of almost 9,000, and Fort Lauderdale, with slightly more than 2,000 people, was a third-order center. Lower-order places were distributed between these larger places. In central-place theory, each community has its own service area, and service areas of separate-order centers are nested: small ones within large ones. Communities are separated by woods or farmland, and the rural population travels to nearby communities to obtain urban services.

That generalized sequence of growth applied only to white residents. The blacks, until the civil-rights movement of the 1960s, lived in communities
that were isolated from each other. As late as the 1960s, blacks in small centers had access only to low-order goods and services in their own neighborhoods. Because of small numbers and limited buying power, blacks could support only a basic level of functions, not the higher-order ones. Black-owned businesses here, as elsewhere, were small (Wilson and Martin 1982). In small communities higher-order services such as doctors, dentists, and morticians were not accessible to blacks, but they were available in Fort Lauderdale or West Palm Beach. Blacks had to traverse the intervening distance, often bypassing white suppliers close by, to places where services were available. The black communities during the 1950s and 1960s were functionally similar to white ones four decades earlier—isolated neighborhoods surrounded not by scrubland and farms but by urban developments that were off limits. This pattern has also been identified in Miami, though without specific reference to the central-place model (Rose 1964, 1989).

The civil-rights era brought blatant racism to an end. Blacks, if they can afford them, now live in houses scattered throughout the districts of all three counties and participate in the suburbanization process. One of the geographical effects of the civil-rights movement was to redraw the configuration of the central-place hexagons (Fig. 4). Before the reforms, blacks and whites coexisted in differing sets of service areas—more broadly, in different behavioral worlds. Because of civil-rights reforms, blacks and whites alike could freely use local shops and services. Both races could take the shorter journey to obtain low-order functions. Almost overnight, blacks found themselves with access to the range of goods and services that had been nonexistent to them but had been available to whites.

Central-place networks generally develop slowly, as towns and supporting rural populations grow generation after generation and the hexagonal geometries ensue. However, here is a case of the central-place network altering rapidly. The civil-rights movement caused a radical redefining of the networks of the black population. Central-place theory assumes that all customers in a region have the same opportunities to obtain urban functions. This proposition may be valid for the majority, but perhaps not for a minority. Notions such as the range of goods, the geometry of service areas, and size and spacing of communities may not be applicable to all segments of society. A case in point is the blacks and whites in southeastern Florida in the era of segregation. The two groups occupied the same general area, but behaviorally lived in separate sections or, in central-place terms, separate hexagons.
