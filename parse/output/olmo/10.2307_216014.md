BEEKEEPING AND LOCAL SELF–RELIANCE IN RURAL SOUTHERN AFRICA*

PETE M. ILLGNER, ETIENNE L. NEL, and MARK P. ROBERTSON

ABSTRACT. Beekeeping has the potential to supplement incomes in rural southern Africa. In light of regional economic constraints, self-reliance strategies that draw on local knowledge and skills take on a renewed importance. We consider the advantages and disadvantages of beekeeping and examine appropriate forms of development support. A short case study from Zimbabwe illustrates these issues. Keywords: honeybees, rural southern Africa, self-reliance, Zimbabwe.

Beekeeping may be seen as a perfect model of responsible, sustainable agriculture. It can greatly benefit rural communities by providing additional food, medicine and income.

—Elize Lundall-Magnuson, “Developmental Beekeeping,” 1997

Economic marginalization, the frequent failure of past development interventions, and the need of local communities in rural Africa to secure economic survival have encouraged a focus on indigenous knowledge and self-reliance strategies (Burkey 1993; Binns 1995). The academic and policy attention increasingly being paid to these considerations represents a fundamental shift in development foci and a concerted response to the perceived negative effects of structural adjustment programs in rural Africa (Taylor and Mackenzie 1992). Where local self-reliance strategies exist and, in certain instances, where they are supported by external agencies, they often “constitute the only means of survival for local communities, providing an invaluable basis for reviving local self-reliance and restructuring regional development on more indigenous and self-reliant lines” (Gooneratne 1992, 3). These issues have particular relevance to indigenous beekeeping in the rural areas of the countries that make up the Southern African Development Community (Louw 1992) (Figure 1). Beekeeping, it can be argued, is an ecologically and technically appropriate form of income generation for communities in some of the most economically and environmentally poor areas of Africa. Its role in promoting economic self-reliance and the need to enhance this role were identified in the important Banjul Bee Declaration of 1991 (Bees for Development 1991).

Although beekeeping can only rarely become the sole source of income and livelihood for people in the Third World, its role as a source of supplementary earnings, food, and employment should not be underestimated. Key points in the argument that beekeeping is a key element in promoting rural self-reliance are that:

• Beekeeping promotes rural diversification and hence is an alternate source of income and employment, particularly in areas where arable land is restricted and demographic growth is resulting in insufficiently profitable landholdings.
• Beekeeping is an activity that has successfully been adopted by women in many parts of the continent (Clauss 1992).

• Beekeeping clearly is a low-cost, sustainable undertaking with a low environmental impact. The spin-off of enhanced plant pollination is an invaluable one.
• Although honey is not a primary source of food, it can be used as a dietary supplement. In addition, its cultural significance should not be ignored.

Recorded European evidence of traditional beekeeping in southern Africa dates back to 1594 in Angola. David Livingstone noted the presence of log and bark hives in the upper Zambezi area in 1854 (Clauss 1992). The relevance today of this form of economic activity is evidenced by statistics from a single district, Babati, in Tanzania, where some 6,000 bee colonies produce a total of 60,000–90,000 kilograms of honey a year, with a value equivalent to u.s.$11,000–$17,000 (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991). A growing importance of honey-based self-reliance strategies is suggested by a doubling in the number of rural beekeeping clubs in Malawi, from forty-two to ninety-two, over the course of only two years (Mensing 1993a). In this article we first explore the relevance of locally appropriate self-reliance strategies in southern Africa, then discuss southern African bees and indigenous beekeeping and the potential of this activity to supplement rural income. Our focus then shifts to an examination of the limited support given by certain aid agencies to increase the effectiveness of traditional beekeeping. The Bondolfi Beekeeper’s Association in Zimbabwe is presented as a case study.

THE MOVE TO SELF–RELIANCE

Desperate economic realities in Africa both oblige and compel many communities to seek solutions to the circumstances in which they find themselves. According to the United Nations, “for almost 900 million people, approximately one-sixth of mankind, the march of human progress has become a retreat. In many nations, development is being thrown into reverse. And after decades of steady economic advance, large areas of the world are being thrown back into poverty” (Gooneratne and Mbilinyi 1992, 22). This statement is particularly pertinent in Africa, where the effective collapse of government services, infrastructure, and social provision in many areas is coupled with hyperinflation, vast debt burdens, and the disastrous effects of global trade and structural adjustment packages (Taylor and Mackenzie 1992). This situation militates against any significant socioeconomic intervention on the part of national governments. Aside from often tokenistic interventions by aid agencies and nongovernmental organizations, many communities are forced to look inward at their own resources and potential in order to carve some form of future for themselves and their children. Frequently, this leads to a reliance on, or a rediscovery of, traditional forms of livelihood and income generation.

The switch in applied development strategies to a bottom-up approach at both the community and policy levels accords with W. B. Stöhr’s argument that the failure
of modernization approaches to tackle poverty requires new forms of intervention, in which control is vested in the host community (1981). Communities need to draw on local skills, resources, and indigenous knowledge. These principles are endorsed by recent writings that discuss the potential of indigenous technical knowledge (Binns 1995). Research undertaken in various parts of Africa reveals that economic marginalization is encouraging a new or enhanced focus by communities and aid agencies on the development of local potential through indigenous strategies (Taylor and Mackenzie 1992). An argument for self-reliance is inherent within this switch in focus, a concept that finds accord in the writings of the South Commission, which has come out in support of a “revival of economic growth which is inward-orientated and self-sustaining, providing full employment and the development of both agriculture and industry” (Gooneratne and Mbilinyi 1992, 3). According to J. Galtung, self-reliance is “development on the basis of a country’s own resources, involving its populations, and based on the potentials of its cultural values and traditions” (quoted in Gooneratne and Mbilinyi 1992, 3). Traditional beekeeping clearly lies within this conceptualization.

Numerous locally driven African development endeavors are emerging that speak to local needs and encourage greater participation by the indigenous population (Gooneratne 1992). Local initiatives that use indigenous technology not only help to ensure human survival but also provide a basis for participation in the mainstream economy. High capital returns can be generated through local improvements in indigenous techniques and in the marketing of produce.

Agricultural activities clearly have a key role to play in any rural development strategy: “Agricultural development is indispensable to guarantee local food security, to generate widespread employment and income sources and conserve environmental resources” (Gooneratne 1992, 5). Overpopulation, environmental degradation, drought, marginal farming conditions, and supplementary income needs require production that is more than just subsistence. Transportation and storage difficulties, however, work against the attempts of small-scale farmers to compete as major suppliers of food to external markets. Honey and beeswax, though, are a high-value commodity with which to complement existing agriculture, are easy to transport and store, and provide subsistence farmers with a valuable source of income.

Beekeeping has considerable merit as a self-reliance strategy that can augment the income of small-scale farmers with only a minimal financial outlay. Traditional beekeeping is being enhanced as a form of rural activity and income through the introduction of small, technologically appropriate improvements. Often this is due to limited, nondictatorial support offered by aid agencies that acknowledge the considerable benefits which beekeeping offers. Intervention has been designed to remove obstacles and increase productivity and income, thereby improving rural community self-reliance. Evidence from countries such as Malawi illustrates how suitable interventions can address existing constraints (Mensing 1993a).
TRADITIONAL HONEY–HUNTING AND BEEKEEPING IN SOUTHERN AFRICA

Honeybees and their products have been an important part of the rural economy in parts of Africa for hundreds of years. Honey is a food source, an ingredient in traditional medicine, and an additive in the brewing of honey beer, and beeswax has been important to local craftsmen (Svensson 1991). In areas where beekeeping is practiced, honey also plays a key role in traditional rituals such as initiation ceremonies (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991). Evidence from Zambia suggests that more than 50 percent of honey produced by traditional methods is sold or bartered locally within tribal groups (Clauss 1992).

Honey has been collected in southern Africa since ancient times. Harald Pager has identified a large number of Stone Age rock paintings depicting honeybees, honeycombs, and honey hunters (1973). One painting from an overhang near Toghvana Dam, in the Matopo Hills of Zimbabwe, clearly shows an early honey hunter using a burning stick to protect himself from stings. More than one hundred scenes of bees and associated features have been identified in the Drakensberg and Midlands areas of KwaZulu-Natal in South Africa (Mazel 1982). Farther south, the name of the Outeniqua area was reported by François le Vaillant to mean “man loaded with honey” (Tribe 1982). It is apparent that “traditional beekeeping has always been part of the life-style of most families” in many districts throughout southern Africa (Jones 1995, 74), although there is currently very little traditional beekeeping south of the Limpopo River, except in the Brachystegia-Julbernardia Woodland of southern Mozambique (Guy 1971).

Techniques of beekeeping and honey collection are handed down through the generations (Clauss 1992; Jones 1995). Given the range of traditional uses of honey, beekeeping often exists as a way of life rather than as a form of commercial enterprise, although commerce is a consideration that development agencies are seeking to enhance (Clauss 1992).

If honey hunting from naturally occurring honeybee nests has been widely practiced in southern Africa, traditional beekeeping has been particularly prominent in the northern, savanna portions of the region under consideration (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991). Traditional beekeeping differs from honey hunting in that the colony of honeybees is not destroyed but manipulated: The honey is extracted without eliminating the honeybees. The resource is protected, and repeated harvests can be made from the same colony. Traditional beekeeping in southern Africa is most widespread in Zimbabwe, Zambia, Tanzania, and Mozambique, where the honeybees have been kept in bark hives. The bark hives are made by ring barking the desired length from a large tree. Ring barking entails removing a section of bark of even width from the circumference of a tree. The two ends of the hive are then plugged with coils of woven grass, leaving a small hole as an entrance. The hives are hung in trees, out of the reach of children, pests, and predators. Traditional beekeeping, though frequently carried out on a small scale or as a supplement to other activity, can range from an individual with fewer than ten hives to a beekeeper who possesses up to one thousand hives (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991; Clauss 1992).
The important role that beekeeping plays as a source of employment is clear: Of the approximately 400,000 people in the North-Western Province of Zambia, nearly 15,000 are beekeepers (Clauss 1992). The significant and potential earning capacity of beekeepers is vividly illustrated by evidence from Tanzania, where the more successful beekeepers can earn incomes of up to approximately u.s.$370 per year, compared with a state official, whose annual earnings average about u.s.$56. It is evident why “large-scale beekeepers in the villages have solid social and economic position[s]” (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991, 3).

SOUTHERN AFRICAN BEES

The honeybees of Africa belong to the species Apis mellifera, which is subdivided into ten subspecies or races (Ruttner 1988). Four races of honeybee occur in southern Africa. The two major races are the Cape Bee, Apis mellifera capensis, and the Highland Bee, Apis mellifera scutellata. The other two minor races are a little-known bee from the coastal regions of Mozambique and northern KwaZulu-Natal, Apis mellifera litorea, and Apis mellifera adansonii, which occurs in Angola and northwestern Zambia (Ruttner 1988; Hepburn and Crewe 1991; Radloff, Hepburn, and Villet 1997). The Cape Bee is largely restricted to the fynbos biome along the southwestern and southern coast of South Africa, which stretches inland to the mountains of the Little Karoo. The fynbos biome is a heathlike plant community with high biodiversity and a large number of endemic plant species. Farther north and east the Cape Bee hybridizes with the Highland Bee. The vast majority of the subcontinent is populated by Apis mellifera scutellata. The main difference between these two races is that the Cape Bee has a unique trait known as thelytoky, whereby the workers lay diploid eggs in the absence of a queen. Traditionally the Cape Bee has been regarded as smaller, darker, and less aggressive than the Highland Bee (Hepburn and Crewe 1991). All of the honeybees we discuss in this article belong to the race Apis mellifera scutellata.

ADVANTAGES AND DISADVANTAGES OF BEEKEEPING

Honeybees provide direct benefits to beekeepers both as a source of food that has a high nutritional value and as an additional source of income through the sale of surplus honey and wax. Wax is an important by-product of beekeeping that can be melted down and stored almost indefinitely. Tanzania produced 1,050 metric tons of wax in 1984, of which better than one-third was exported (Crane 1990). Honey beer is a valued commercial product, particularly in areas such as northwestern Zambia (Ngolofwana 1994).

A wide range of other advantages exist. When practiced on a small scale, beekeeping is not particularly labor intensive, and tending can be restricted to periods outside peak work times (Mensing 1993a). As a result, the beekeeper can carry on with usual agricultural activities, such as growing maize and raising cattle. Beekeeping therefore usefully complements crop production and may lead to increased yields and cash income if enough honeybees are present for pollination (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991; Murless 1995).
At its most basic level, beekeeping commands a relatively minimal financial investment, especially compared with the many cash crops that require purchase of plants or seedlings, pesticides, and fertilizers (Mensing 1993a). A subsistence-level farmer who keeps honeybees needs not purchase land and can construct hives from locally available material. Bees can be obtained by placing empty, baited hives in trees, which are then occupied by wild swarms. A particular advantage in southern Africa is that beekeeping can even be carried out in areas with a low agricultural potential, where little or no arable land exists or where rainfall is unreliable.

Beekeeping encourages the conservation of local habitats (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991). It is in the beekeepers' interest to conserve local nectar- and pollen-producing vegetation. Beekeeping is a passive form of agriculture, for it does not require that indigenous vegetation be cleared to make way for crops (Murless 1995). Beekeepers also discourage the use of pesticides on crops, because they could kill the honeybees. In some areas, as in northern Malawi, beekeepers may actively protect conservation areas (Mensing 1993b).

The practice is a culturally accepted one in most of the region. Almost all population groups in southern Africa have some knowledge of honeybees and honey collection (Guy 1971). In addition, it can lead to the empowerment of women within a community. For example, both the chairperson and the treasurer of the successful Bondolfi Beekeeper's Association in Zimbabwe are women. In the Kasempa District of Zambia honey hunting is traditionally a male-dominated activity, but in recent years female heads of household have begun to keep bees because of the other economic constraints they face. Beekeeping can therefore encourage gender empowerment, allowing women an improved status and community role through their capacity to generate an income. Bernhard Clauss (1992) quotes examples from Zambia, where women producers are harvesting up to 500 kilograms of honey each and are now able to pay for their children's education.

Beekeeping is a mechanism that can help address the employment challenges created by a growing population and increasing landlessness (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991). Employment opportunities are also formed for craftsmen who manufacture hives, smokers, gloves, and other beekeeping equipment (Sosu 1991). For example, participants in a beekeeping project in the Mpumalanga Province of northeastern South Africa earn additional income by manufacturing hives for newcomers to beekeeping (Lundall-Magnuson 1997).

Beekeeping, particularly when it is undertaken as a part-time activity, lends itself to cooperative action in collecting honey and purchasing or manufacturing equipment. The need for such action is enhanced if beekeeping is undertaken on a semi-commercial basis because of what can become a high cost of equipment. The practice and its labor requirements also easily graft onto traditional, communal ways of life that are prevalent in many parts of the region. This is evidenced in the recent upsurge in the number of beekeeping clubs in Malawi (Mensing 1993a). G. M. Ntenga and B. T. Mugongo assert that "beekeeping offers great opportunities for both co-operation and self-reliance, as it is normally more successful when prac-
ticed collectively, and is an avenue for generating income without government support" (1991, 3).

Rural beekeeping, and steps to enhance it, do, however, face certain constraints. Some of the most apparent ones are: If honey is not marketed or consumed it may eventually start to ferment; inadequate hive management may lead to the spread of bee diseases and pests; and vandalism, theft, animals, and bush fires can be a problem. Another key problem is that the construction of bark hives can result in the loss of a tree and, sometimes, in the demise of valuable bee-forage plants (Towry-Coker 1995). Overexploitation of trees has been noted in the Babati District of Tanzania, where a shortage of traditional materials may oblige some beekeepers to abandon traditional beekeeping altogether (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991). The members of the rural Bondolfi Beekeeper's Association in Zimbabwe found that the construction of bark hives led to the depletion of certain trees. As a result, if bark hives are constructed they now tend to be made from fallen branches (Mutude 1997). An additional problem is environmental degradation caused by frequent bush fires that inadvertently start when hives are smoked to collect honey (Guy 1971). Tropical honeybees are known to be very aggressive, which ideally requires beekeepers to take precautions, such as using protective equipment and placing colonies away from dwellings or animals.

Inadequate research into African bees is decidedly a problem. Although a vast amount of research has been done on the races of honeybees in temperate regions of the world, the same cannot be said for some of the races in tropical Africa. The temperate bee races do not always behave the way tropical races do. Therefore, beekeeping techniques developed in temperate areas may be inappropriate in tropical ones. For example, whereas temperate colonies are able to survive cold periods by clustering and building up large food stores during times of plenty, tropical colonies have developed nomadic tendencies (Bradbear 1991). Apis mellifera scutellata readily absconds, and beekeepers must constantly ensure that hives are in good condition to prevent the bees from moving on (Ngolofwana 1994).

A final, potential problem is that of the illiteracy of many beekeepers, given the importance of keeping records on each colony of honeybees. Careful record keeping enables beekeepers to determine the amount of honey and the state of the colony at particular times of the year and allows them to choose the best time to harvest honey. Illiteracy also makes the dissemination of information to the beekeepers more difficult (Makalo 1991).

Enhancing the Potential of Traditional Beekeeping

Traditional beekeeping is constrained by these problems. If its potential is to be enhanced, positive interventions need to be made. It is, however, critical that any program use appropriate technology and not simply be imposed on the recipients. Improvements should be worked out in careful consultation with local beekeepers in order to enhance their capacities for self-reliance.
Ntenga and Mugongo’s research in Tanzania (1991) and our field observations in Zimbabwe clearly indicate that the absence of scientific input in traditional beekeeping constrains yields, poses risks to the environment, and limits the economic return. Traditional bark hives and honeybee colonies are frequently destroyed when honey is extracted. The need for reusable hives is clear, as is the need to adopt methods that do not threaten the colonies or deplete timber resources. In northwestern Zambia, traditional beekeepers have recognized that increased beekeeping, as near Kabulamema, has resulted in the absence of bark resources within an 8-kilometer radius of the settlement (Clauss 1992). Low yields per hive, the poor siting of hives, and the poorly developed nature of marketing systems all constrain the development potential of traditional beekeeping at present—but all of these can be remedied (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991).

Such problems, as well as language barriers between the beekeepers and outside advisers, further restrict improvements and adaptation to modern beekeeping techniques. According to Robin Guy, traditional people are “bound by their cultures, often to their material and nutritional disadvantage” (1971, 18). Modern Western techniques, hives, and safety equipment are simply too expensive for most rural communities, and they present problems of transportation, adaptability, and complexity. In order to address these difficulties and to increase honey yields in a sustainable fashion, new, technologically appropriate hives have been developed and promoted by nongovernmental organizations. The top-bar hive was developed in Kenya using locally available timber, rather than tree trunks. Its design permits the easy removal and repeated extraction of honey from the hives with minimal disturbance of the colony. It works on the principle that combs hang down from removable slats that cover the top of the hive. They can easily be lifted off and the combs removed (Figure 2). Only one slat needs to be removed at a time, limiting disruption and reducing aggression. The frame for the hive can be constructed out of small branches and twigs, covered with a mixture of clay and cow dung. Local variants include the Kupfuma Ishunga, Kenyan, and Tanzanian top-bar hives (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991; Towry-Coker 1995). There is, as a result, very little necessity for dependence on foreign technology. If beekeeping becomes popular, some individuals may even construct additional hives for sale at low cost. These new hives are reported to be in use in Mozambique (Jones 1995) and Tanzania (Ntenga and Mugongo 1991); and we observed them in Zimbabwe and Botswana in 1997. The principles applied have built on the reality first identified in Kenya, namely that “with beekeeping, as with other agricultural inputs, the question . . . is not one of seeking new knowledge only, but rather one of improving and implementing what exists already” (Kigatiira 1976, 144). These interventions serve to enhance the economic independence and hence self-reliance of beekeeping communities.

Programs designed to offer guidance and improvements to beekeepers and not to impose inappropriate changes have been pioneered by the Deutsche Gesellschaft für Technische Zusammenarbeit (gtz; German Agency for Technical Cooperation) in Malawi and Mozambique (Mensing 1993a; Jones 1995). THE BONDOFLI BEEKEEPER’S ASSOCIATION, ZIMBABWE

A prime example of the potential of rural beekeeping for improving socioeconomic conditions in a rural community can be found at the Bondolfi Mission, near Masvingo in central Zimbabwe. The area is semiarid, so opportunities for agricul-

ture are restricted. Low incomes are exacerbated by structural adjustment and poor growth rates in the regional economy. Beekeeping has always been pursued as a traditional activity but seldom as a primary form of income (Mutude 1997).

Residents of the community recently realized that production from traditional hives was no longer viable because of the negative impact of bark stripping. To their credit, they concluded that outside support was needed to maximize local potential and earnings from beekeeping. This coincided with an interest shown by an external aid agency, the United Nations volunteer program in the area. Material assistance was also provided by the European Union (Chale 1997). Based on this measured advice, the residents acquired better beekeeping skills and marketing strategies (Mutude 1997). One of their first actions was to establish the Bondolfi Beekeeper's Association in 1995 to coordinate activities and undertake the bulk collection and processing of honey (Figure 3).

The association now has seventy active members, forty of whom are women. The endeavor assists women, who are traditionally disempowered in Zimbabwean society, to acquire an independent livelihood. Fewer than ten of the members still work with bark hives; the balance use the improved top-bar hives (Figure 2). On average each member manages two hives, although it is the intention of the United Nations volunteer to increase the number of hives to at least ten per member. The production of new hives has, in itself, generated local employment opportunities for
carpenters, who manufacture hives at the association's small shop in the village. Other direct spin-offs include the establishment a beehive-smoker enterprise in Bondolfi. A small leather-processing industry has been established that manufactures gloves for beekeepers, and a sewing group produces protective veils for the collectors (Chale 1997).

The association now successfully coordinates the collection, processing, marketing, and sale of honey and wax, eliminating the extreme difficulty that individuals previously experienced in trying to sell their produce. The association has also overcome the constraints that individuals encountered in trying to transport their goods to Masvingo, the closest major urban center. Honey is now collected and bottled centrally and then sold in bulk, in jars bearing the association's logo. In addition to obvious savings in time and effort, the sale of a product that has become recognized for its quality commands higher prices and turnover than individual producers could have hoped for. Bulk sales to a trader who markets the honey on their behalf assists with penetration of the urban retail market, ensuring a greater competitive edge and higher prices (Mutude 1997). Increased sales have resulted; these, in turn, have had a cumulative effect in Bondolfi, where income and employment have risen and additional hives are being constructed. Although it is too early to see noticeable material improvements in Bondolfi, the attainment of a more reliable form of income promises considerable long-term benefits for the community.

CONCLUSION

Traditional or enhanced beekeeping practices can serve as a reliable means of increasing rural self-reliance. Although beekeeping per se will never become a panacea for rural development problems in Africa, its role as an adjunct to other development activities is not to be underestimated. Low-technology interventions have considerable merit in enhancing productivity and efficiency. They can help to ensure that rural communities are better able to generate meaningful income and eventually become even more self-reliant. The support offered by aid agencies has the capacity to facilitate beekeeping, as evidenced in Malawi and at Bondolfi. Any intervention undertaken should not prescribe to a local community but suggest alternatives that can improve traditional techniques. These alternatives should not introduce costly, inappropriate, and imported First World technologies. This conceptualization is endorsed by Guy, who writes that "better beekeeping will help them [traditional beekeepers] to live in harmony with both their traditions and their increasing pressure for productivity" (1971,18). This scenario was identified in the 1991 Banjul Bee Declaration, which advocated the use of transitional and appropriate technology and the development of suitable support systems and structures (Bees for Development 1991).

Local knowledge of honeybees through honey hunting and traditional beekeeping exists in most areas of southern Africa. This knowledge needs to be tapped and aided through the introduction of low-technology, high-return beekeeping projects. Rural beekeeping is a sustainable activity with great potential for boosting the
incomes of poor, small-scale farmers through minimal financial outlay. Beekeeping is an environmentally friendly, economically viable, nonintrusive activity that can complement existing agricultural practices in rural communities. “Increasing interest in tropical beekeeping is fueling research into the biology and behavior of tropical honeybees. . . . Armed with understanding, we can derive ways for beekeepers to benefit most efficiently from these bees” (Bradbear 1991, 23). These principles have clear developmental ramifications that governments and aid agencies should recognize and pursue.
