WHICH TRUTH? CULTURAL POLITICS AND VODKA IN RURAL RUSSIA*

MELINDA HERROLD

After what level of alcohol consumption does an informant become unreliable? How do I distinguish between an emphatic opinion, delivered under the candor-producing influence of alcohol, and unrestrained hyperbole, wrought by intoxication? When should I stop taking notes? (And I’m sure some of you are wondering, “At what point is the alcohol-consuming interviewer no longer reliable?” On that, more later. . . .)

Why should I have voiced questions about alcohol and fieldwork? I wasn’t plying my interviewees with bottles of vodka—they were alarmingly capable of doing that on their own. My problem was that I was doing fieldwork on conflicts over natural resources in rural Russia. Vodka is so much a part of daily life there that few—almost no—gathering takes place without shared libation (Figure 1). Additionally, I was asking people to discuss with me controversial matters, such as poaching or resistance to the establishment of a park, that could, on occasion, elicit immoderate responses.

We hunt all of the time. They call me the “Killer”! When I go out hunting with my friends I always come home with pheasants and fowl. I shoot everything, even deer. We are all poachers here!

No, my friends and I don’t hunt anymore. There isn’t that much left to hunt around here, some geese, ducks but not many deer. And it’s the simple guys like me who are the ones who get fined if we’re caught, so we just don’t hunt.

The quotations are from the same young man, and the seemingly irreconcilable statements were produced under very different levels of alcohol consumption. The first expression, full of a hunter’s braggadocio, came as he was drinking. Its successor was issued in the cold light of day, and when he was stone sober.

So, does he hunt all the time, or does he no longer hunt? (Maybe the answer is that he hunts some of the time.) Between an intoxicated boast and an un intoxicated wariness about admitting to illegal acts, how does a researcher know which statement more accurately reflects the actual behavior of an informant?

Most researchers who look at hunting around a protected area will probably have had similar experiences. It’s only natural that one doesn’t admit to poaching. Of course, hunting isn’t the only topic that elicits such contradictory statements.

BACKGROUND

Muraviyovka Nature Park became, in 1992, the first private nature reserve in Russia since before the Soviet period. This 5,200-hectare wetland reserve, created through a cooperative effort of Russian, American, and Japanese organizations, protects nesting habitats for several endangered species of birds, including the red-crowned crane, the white-naped crane, and the oriental white stork. Muraviyovka Nature Park was carved out of a larger, state-run wildlife refuge that lacked the resources to adequately protect breeding grounds for these birds. The land for the park was leased from the local, district-level government by a Russian nongovernmental organization (NGO) with moneys put up by a Japanese corporation. Additional funds from U.S. NGOs supported building construction, vehicle acquisition, and educational programs.

The district government generally welcomed this moneymaking opportunity, but many officials in the provincial and village-level governments were strongly opposed to the establishment of an independent and private park. Some were against it for ideological reasons; others, for economic ones. Although the park was created within a refuge where hunting was already restricted, neighbors were afraid of losing all hunting rights. The hunting administration that managed the refuge was vehemently opposed to ceding control of any part of its territory to another organization. Some say that communist ideologues in the hunting administration were opposed to nonstate control over the land. Others suggest that the hunting admin-
istration was operating its own highly profitable, but quite illegal, hunting tours for local bigwigs in areas closed to hunting.

The park is in Amur Province, a highly militarized border region some 8,000 kilometers by train from Moscow—and even farther away in mind. The People’s Republic of China borders Amur to the south. During the Soviet period the province was closed to most foreigners because of its special frontier status—even non-resident Russians needed a special permit to visit the area. To protect a strategic frontier, decades of Soviet propaganda induced no small degree of xenophobia in the citizens of Amur. Many local people told me that, prior to the establishment of the park, they never saw foreigners. All they knew about them was that they were dangerous and would inevitably try to undermine the socialist peace reigning in the Soviet Union. My interviewees’ comments are echoed in John J. Stephan’s 1994 history of the Russian Far East. He notes that “from the 1930s on, Soviet leaders cultivated a siege mentality in the Far East against external enemies of changing identity but of common purpose: to infiltrate the region ‘to harm [Soviet] life in every way—to blow up bridges and factories, to poison wells, to kill people’” (p. 227).

During the summers of 1992 and 1993, when Japanese, Korean, and American biologists began to establish tent camps for the viewing of nesting birds, local people were highly suspicious. Foreigners, they asked, were doing what in a tick- and mosquito-infested marsh? Watching birds?? To veterans of ideological repartee, this explanation seemed neither candid nor likely. Some thought that the Americans were imperialist spies and that Muraviyovka Park was part of a larger reconnaissance operation. Some believed that Americans had located oil in the marsh and were using the park as cover for a profiteering exploration venture. Others thought that it was the vanguard of an imminent colonial occupation. Those who were perhaps less ideologically inclined, but well-armored with an infinitely broader cynicism, assumed that they were facing a well-tuned money-laundering scam. Virtually everyone believed that the Americans had an unvoiced ulterior motive for their visits; only a figurative handful believed that they were there just to observe birds. Those individuals and organizations who supported the creation of this private park were accused by their colleagues and neighbors of having “sold out” or “surrendered the motherland” to the Americans.

It is interesting that the park is overwhelmingly perceived as an American venture, when it was a Russian professor of ornithology from Moscow State University who spearheaded the campaign to establish it. A Japanese corporation financed the land lease, and a Russian conservation organization is the lessee. The only U.S. presence there is for some three weeks during the summer, when a group of about twelve American teachers help run a conservation-education summer camp for local children. The rest of the time only Russians reside and work in the park. In the minds of local people, though, the park is inextricably linked to the presence of Americans.

My research focuses on NGO-sponsored programs designed to reduce conflicts around the park. Such activities consist in the main of environmental education programs, including, significantly, the conservation-education summer camp for
local children. This is jointly run by local biology and geography teachers and the visiting American teachers. Curiously, one of the causes of conflict with local people, the suspicious presence of Americans, is an integral part of attempts to mitigate conflicts. I am evaluating the extent to which this program has changed people’s attitudes to the park and whether the program has led people to modify their behaviors—poaching, fire setting—deemed detrimental to park habitat.

People have mixed feelings about the visiting Americans. For most, their presence reinforces perceptions that the park is an “American park.” However, among the parents of the children who attend the summer camp, attitudes toward Americans and the park have dramatically changed for the better. In interviews, many mentioned the novelty of their children being in the company of living, breathing Americans. They even bragged about the participation of their child in an “American camp.” Those who were more thoroughly steeped in ideology were won over when their children and grandchildren returned unscathed from their encounters with the putative enemy. People who have come into contact with Americans practically gush about their interactions with these curious foreigners. Cooks and drivers, teachers and nurses, parents and grandparents always smile broadly when they recall the friendliness, openness, and enthusiasm of the visiting teachers. Many said that they had expected the Americans to be aloof, conceited, and condescending toward Russians. They had been pleasantly surprised by the unpretentious manners and unconventional teaching methods of the extroverted teachers from Wisconsin and New Jersey. One informant, whose response was echoed by many, explained that she was more positive toward the park because “We have seen concrete results in the park. Work has continued there for several years. Our children study there. The birds are protected. Research is being conducted. Now we know the park is not just another scam. Most people are very supportive of the park now.” However, among those whose children had not been invited to the camp, skeptical attitudes, unsurprisingly, remain.

On a more practical note, economic aspects of the park immediately and significantly benefit the local community. The park employs five farmhands for a small organic demonstration farm and several drivers and temporary summer personnel for the summer camp. Unemployment is high in the villages, because the local collective farms have either collapsed or significantly scaled back operations. Therefore this employment at the park, though small in scale, is meaningful to the local economy.

Informants also told me that the summer camp provides a much-appreciated service for local children. Rest and recreation, particularly in the form of a summer camp, are considered fundamental components of a child’s educational and physical development. People told me that during the Soviet period summer camps, particularly pioneer camps, were provided practically free of charge to all. These camps were regarded as a necessity, not a luxury. Most such camps disappeared with the Soviet Union, and those that remain charge fees most parents cannot afford. Therefore, parents, teachers, and some local government administrators appreciate the
service the camps at Muraviovka provide for the community. Parents also add that the park provides tastier and more nutritious meals for children than their cash-strapped parents can prepare for them at home.

I lived in the villages of Muraviovka and Kuropatino from June 1999 to June 2000 (Figure 2). Both villages, each with about 800 inhabitants, are agricultural communities that border Muraviovka Nature Park. I interviewed most of my respondents at least twice. The interviews, all conducted in Russian, were semistructured. When I first arrived in the villages, the responses I received were lackluster:
What people told me about relations with the park was fairly neutral, with lots of “I don’t know,” “I never really paid much attention,” “It didn’t matter to me,” and other noncommittal statements. This may have been true, but I thought it unlikely because I had heard a great deal about local opposition to the park from park officials.

As might be expected, local people hardly felt comfortable discussing controversial topics and disclosing personal information to an outsider. Although I had spent three summers working with local children and teachers during the park’s environmental camps and knew local officials and many villagers, I remained something of an outsider because I was associated with the park and the American presence there. After three months of less-than-stellar interviews, I decided to interview people more than once, framing my questions differently and allowing time for respondents to get to know me. This afforded me an opportunity to see how the opinions of individuals varied with their blood-alcohol levels.

THE ALCOHOL QUESTION

Returning to the question of the influence of alcohol on interview responses, I use the following quotations to demonstrate the divergent responses a single individual gave under different levels of alcohol consumption. The statements come from one of the most educated residents in her village. This upright official, in a private conversation with me at an official function, after two shots of vodka—an amount no local would consider intoxicating—told me that the establishment of the park signified an imminent American invasion.

Many people believe that the park is the first step in an American colonization project of Russian lands. The Americans will take our natural resources because they are running out of their own. I think these people are right. Why else would Americans invest so much money to establish a “nature park” in some remote, backward part of the world? Why else would Americans spend weeks during the hot summer months in a tick-infested marsh where they don’t have toilets or electricity or running water? We know from TV how Americans live. It is hard for me to believe that Americans would live in such poor conditions just to see birds or to teach our children. They must have other reasons.

Several months later, during an interview in her home, where we drank nothing but tea, she responded:

Only the most uneducated people in our village believe that the Americans who come here are spies. There are a few who believe that the Americans want to take over Russian soil and that the park is a front for this operation. But these people are a small minority. Most people like me understand that the Americans who come here are devoted to environmental protection. The American teachers who teach our Russian children are interested in promoting cultural exchange and improving mutual understanding between our countries. This has been a very positive thing for our community.
So, when does the ice-breaking effect of shared vodka shots lead less to candor and more to exaggeration? There is no easy breath test to apply if you’re conducting interviews in Russia, where people have been imbibing vodka and homemade spirits for the greater part of their life. They have a high tolerance for alcohol. Rare is the gathering of two or more people at which no alcohol is consumed and no toasts are given. And when I, as the first American ever to come to their house, visited them, many considered it an occasion for toasts.

The vast majority of my interviews took place in the interviewee’s home, a significant proportion of them without any alcohol. Usually, one-on-one interviews with women occurred in their kitchen while we made stuffed pies, dumplings with cabbage or potatoes, Siberian meat dumplings, or Russian crepes. Here the main mood-altering substance was good food, occasionally spiced up with a shot of vodka but more generally accompanied by a cup of tea. Also, interviews while working in vegetable gardens, feeding pigs, or milking cows tended to be very sober, and sometimes tedious, affairs.

Inebriated interviews were more likely to occur in the evening, over dinner with a married couple or a small group of friends. For most people who had jobs outside the home, evenings were the most convenient time for an interview. An evening interview, however, usually meant a lively exchange that revolved around dinner, when vodka, homemade spirits, beer, and/or wine were consumed. Amid periodic toasts, shots were downed in a single gulp. As you can imagine, details about relations with the park and about attitudes and behavior with regard to it would acquire greater color as the evening proceeded. After the fourth shot, comments sometimes became more dismal and negative; at other times they became positively rosy. Of course, not all meals went to a fourth shot; some only had one or two shots. Somewhat arbitrarily, I decided to stop taking interview notes after the fourth round of toasts.

A major caveat is in order here. I’m not suggesting that all of the inhabitants of the two villages in which I worked are alcoholics. They aren’t. It’s just that various quantities of vodka or homemade spirits are often consumed with dinner, especially during the long winters when temperatures drop as low as –40°C. Although villagers would admit that alcoholism is a major problem, most of those whom I interviewed are not considered heavy drinkers by other villagers.

I conducted some interviews in workplaces in order to avoid the vodka. At the local clinics and schools, for example, I had some very alcohol-free interviews with staff. But a workplace interview wasn’t an option for everyone. I couldn’t interview tractor drivers when they were driving their tractors, because Russian tractors are only one-seaters. Mechanics didn’t like to be distracted for extended periods of time, even by a visiting American, when they were rebuilding an engine.

Additionally, being in the workplace didn’t always guarantee an alcohol-free environment. I had a rather frightening mobile interview with a drinking ambulance driver . . . but I won’t go into detail about that one here. And in several office
interviews vodka had been flowing prior to my arrival. In one such case I showed up after an inspection tour given by higher authorities. The tour had been capped by a lavish—by local standards—buffet, which, of course, included copious ablutions. My interviewees were definitely in high spirits by the time I arrived. During the interview nothing exceptionally unusual was expressed that contradicted statements they had made in prior interviews, so I had no reason to consider my interviewees unreliable. However, on another occasion the workplace interview, this time accompanied by homemade spirits, gave me some surprises. It took place at a school after the students had gone home. The staff was celebrating some special occasion with pickles, sausage, and berry-flavored spirits. During the interview my interlocutors voiced some very strong criticisms of the park and the visiting Americans, which they’d never expressed to me before. In this case the alcohol seemed to have loosened tongues, and the informants may have felt more comfortable expressing criticisms as a group than as individuals. Here, I don’t know how to separate the effects of alcohol from those of the group interview; they combined into an animated, even entertaining, interview.

Then there’s the question of my own alcohol consumption during interviews. Declining a drink altogether is more than just awkward; it’s a refusal of hospitality. The offerer is offended. I, unfortunately, have a very low tolerance for alcohol (I probably didn’t go to enough frat parties in college). I can’t drink more than one or two shots of vodka without inducing a monster migraine. Additionally, alcohol is not sipped over the course of the meal, the way a glass of wine would be, but downed in large, discrete gulps. Initially no one believed me when I said I couldn’t drink. However, after local people witnessed the post-vodka, pale death mask of this lightweight drinker, word got around the villages that the “American’s organism cannot process alcohol.” I finally got people to understand that I had a weakened liver from a relict case of hepatitis. This meant that, after proving to people that I really couldn’t drink, during social gatherings—which my interviews inevitably became—I was permitted to nurse my one shot of vodka, or medicinal, plant-laden moonshine, throughout the evening without offending anyone.

In general, interviews in which some alcohol was being consumed produced statements that were more critical of the park and its programs. With a shot or two—or more—my respondents were more likely to make controversial statements, veer away from the official line, make negative comments about the park, and express suspicions about the Americans who visit the park. Should I assume that these were “truth-serum” effects of alcohol? When a sober informant told me something that contradicted what she or he had previously said after a glass, should I just disregard the sober statement? Should I assume that the person was too afraid to be honest when he or she was sober? That doesn’t quite seem fair.

Alcohol also produced some outrageous statements that may not reflect actual behavior. The young fellow known as the “Killer” is a possible example. Had he been sober he might not have exclaimed so defiantly, “We are all poachers here.” A certain amount of alcohol probably did encourage him to discuss illegal activities
he would otherwise have been reluctant to disclose, especially to a foreigner. But how much of his drunken statement is a boast? Does he hunt as much as he said he does? Does he bring home a game bag stuffed with as much booty as he asserted? Don’t lots of hunters exaggerate the proportions of their kill? As for the official who once complained about incipient American colonialism, then later welcomed the cooperative Russian-American conservation effort, I wonder whether she really is paranoid that Americans will take over her land. Although she admitted to not being particularly enamored with the United States, when she was sober she said she doesn’t really believe that the Americans are coming to take over.

Alcohol consumption seems to explain the imparting of such contradictory responses. A difference in venue for the interview (the office versus the home) may account for some of the divergence in responses (for example, replying in an official capacity at the workplace but responding more personally in the home). However, many of those who gave contradictory responses had no office and were interviewed in the home on both occasions. The type of interview—individual or small group—may also play a role in explaining different responses by the same individual. I need to look into that. Also, whether the interview was the first or second with an individual didn’t seem to play much of a role in explaining contradictory responses. Sometimes I heard positive reactions to the park in the first interview but negative comments in the second or third one; and sometimes the opposite was true.

So, how do I reconcile all of these contradictory responses? Should I have just not interviewed people when they were or had been drinking? Had I done this in Muraviiovka and Kuropatino, I’m not sure that I would have gotten very far with my research—and my year in those two villages would have been very long and very lonely.

The field, for a researcher, includes many a hazard. It used to be said, in a kind of field-worker cliché, that “informants always mislead you.” No one believes that anymore, at least not as a matter of course. But informants’ consumption of refined distillates or the Russian equivalent of bathtub gin can assuredly make field research, and sorting out the viable and the feasible, all the more difficult.
