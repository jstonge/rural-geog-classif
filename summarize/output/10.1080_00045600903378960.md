# 10.1080_00045600903378960

We examine how access regimes—defined as a set of interrelated institutions and organizations—facilitated the flow of production resources and benefits to drive patterns of land change in the Brazilian Cerrado. Adopting a mesoscale analytical framework grounded in new institutional economics (NIE) and the theory of access, we move beyond a narrow focus on property rights to include land-leasing and production contracts, as well as the role of cooperatives and firms as distinct organizational actors. We analyzed remotely sensed data (Landsat MSS, TM, and ETM) for eastern Mato Grosso state across three time periods: 1972/1973–1986, 1986–1992, and 1992–2002. These geospatial data were integrated with qualitative and archival data to evaluate how land managers accessed natural and productive resources, such as credit, technology, and inputs. By comparing lands under private colonization schemes with those under no such schemes, we identified spatial and temporal variability in Cerrado conversion and land-use intensification. This approach allows us to connect macroeconomic drivers to place-based socioeconomic factors, demonstrating how specific institutional and organizational synergies influence land-use decisions and regional land-cover outcomes.
