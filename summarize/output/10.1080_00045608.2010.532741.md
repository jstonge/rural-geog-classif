# 10.1080_00045608.2010.532741

We investigate the social and economic transformations altering the function of the household in rural Sri Lanka by examining domestic and international migration as constitutive elements of place. Our research is situated in two contrasting rural sites: Kumburegama South in the Galle District of the south, and Alut Henegama in the Kurunegala District on the margins of the Dry Zone. To capture the complexities of household-migration interactions, we employed a mixed-methods approach consisting of a survey questionnaire administered to one hundred households, complemented by semi-structured interviews with a subsample of fifty migrants or their family members. Data collection took place in 2007, focusing on key variables such as household size, land access, mean income, and remittance dependency. We specifically contrast the drivers and outcomes of domestic migration—primarily young, unmarried individuals moving to garment factories in Colombo and Galle—with international migration, largely comprising married women working as housekeepers in the Middle East. By looking inside the household, we apply a lens of cooperation and conflict to analyze how these different migration streams reshape bargaining power, status, and livelihood strategies within the family unit.
