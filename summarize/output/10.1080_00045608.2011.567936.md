# 10.1080_00045608.2011.567936

We develop an integrated research approach to model the spatially detailed carbon balance between consumption-based emissions and vegetation carbon sequestration. Using Florida as a case study for the year 2001, we estimate household carbon emissions at the U.S. Census tract scale, focusing on the energy and transportation sectors. Residential energy emissions are calculated by combining Census 2000 household size data with average consumption levels from the U.S. Energy Information Administration, while transportation emissions are derived from National Household Travel Survey transferability data and U.S. EPA emission coefficients. To quantify carbon sinks, we employ biophysical remote sensing techniques to estimate gross primary production (GPP) at a 1-km resolution, utilizing the 2001 National Land Cover Database, AVHRR normalized difference vegetation index (NDVI), and solar radiation data. These GPP estimates are adjusted for plant respiration and aggregated to the Census tract scale. Finally, we utilize univariate analysis of variance (ANOVA) to examine the relationship between carbon balance—the difference between household emissions and vegetation assimilation—and four settlement density categories: urban, suburban, exurban, and rural.
