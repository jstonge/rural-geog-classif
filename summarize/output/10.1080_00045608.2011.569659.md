# 10.1080_00045608.2011.569659

We examine energy poverty in the rural United States by conceptualizing it as a geographical assemblage of networked materialities and socioeconomic relations. To illustrate this approach, we focus on the rural landscapes of Greene, Lenoir, and Wayne counties in eastern North Carolina, a region characterized by high poverty rates and a declining economic base. Our methodology integrates quantitative socioeconomic data from the American Community Survey and the recipient database of the Wayne Action Group for Economic Solvency (WAGES, Inc.) with qualitative ethnographic insights. Specifically, we employed a typical case sampling technique to select participants from the WAGES weatherization database, conducting in-depth interviews with seventeen households receiving weatherization assistance during the summer of 2009. By analyzing these interviews alongside data on energy provision infrastructures and the material conditions of the home, we trace the intersections of individual biographies—including health crises and economic precariousness—with the techno-social networks of energy distribution. This multi-scalar approach allows us to highlight the lived realities of energy poverty and the specific networked configurations that produce and sustain it in a rural context.
