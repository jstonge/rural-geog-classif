# 10.1080_00045608.2011.584289

Using a case study of the Perote Valley in central Veracruz, Mexico, we employ an integrated social, environmental, and geospatial approach to analyze how urban-driven demand for construction aggregates affects rural agricultural lands and livelihoods. To examine land-cover changes associated with aggregate surface mining between 1995 and 2006, we compared aerial photographs, satellite imagery, and Global Positioning System (GPS) field maps of the valley. This geospatial analysis allowed us to quantify changes in mine surface area and identify spatial patterns of extraction relative to accessibility. To complement these data, we conducted sixty-six formal household interviews across three agrarian communities to investigate the socioeconomic drivers of land-use conversion. Key variables analyzed include the price of aggregates, agricultural returns, and the rate of land recultivation following extraction. By synthesizing these data, we evaluate the transition from agricultural to aggregate-based land use and the resulting impact on the long-term agricultural potential of the region.
