# 10.1080_00045608.2011.596392

Drawing on a decade of qualitative fieldwork, I employ an actor-oriented livelihoods approach to examine how Hmong ethnic minority households in the Sa Pa district of L`ao Cai province, Vietnam, negotiate the tensions between economic liberalization and authoritarian socialist political structures. To understand the nuanced decision-making processes underlying rural livelihoods, I utilize the notion of the "social interface" as a heuristic device to analyze the points of linkage and confrontation where discrepancies in power, knowledge, and cultural interpretation are mediated. This framework is further extended by integrating concepts of everyday politics and covert resistance—specifically the use of "hidden transcripts"—to reveal how actors may shirk or sidestep market integration. The study focuses on three primary diversification strategies—cardamom cultivation, textile trade, and tourism trekking—that supplement otherwise subsistence-based livelihoods. By weaving together ethnographic data with an analysis of state policy, the actions of local officials, and ethnically embedded social relations, I investigate how Hmong individuals navigate new economic opportunities while resisting unwanted market dependence, thereby highlighting the micro-foundations of macro-processes in the northern Vietnam uplands.
