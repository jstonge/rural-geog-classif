# 10.1080_00045608.2011.620501

We employ a mixed-methods approach that fuses the conceptual frameworks of political ecology with the methodological rigor of land change science to examine "contentious land change" (C-LC) in the Amazon's arc of deforestation. Our study focuses on southeastern Pará, Brazil, a region characterized by a long history of antagonism between wealthy largeholders and the rural poor. To describe the social processes expediting forest loss, we combine a detailed case study of land conflict over a specific largeholding with a regional-scale statistical model. By integrating the theory of contentious politics with structural political ecology, we analyze how the interaction of diverse and adversarial agents—including ranchers, landless farmers, and social movement organizations—drives deforestation. This methodology allows us to test whether land cover change is the result of independent microeconomic optimization by isolated land managers or is instead enhanced by social inequalities and competing land claims. Through the use of unique geographic and historical data and regression analyses, we evaluate the impact of Direct Action Land Reform (DALR) and agrarian conflict on environmental degradation across the study area.
