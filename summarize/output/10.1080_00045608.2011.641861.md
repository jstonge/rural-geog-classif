# 10.1080_00045608.2011.641861

We employ a multimethod, semi-reflexive approach to examine the technical and political dimensions of a six-year United Nations (UN) development initiative in the Brazilian Amazon. Our study focuses on the intersection of environmental governance and peasant livelihoods, specifically targeting artisanal and small-scale gold mining (*garimpeiros*) communities. To bridge the gap between legal policy abstractions and material geography, we utilized GIS and Shuttle Radar imagery to map contested areas and natural preserves as defined by the 2002 and 2006 amendments to the Brazilian Forest Code. This technical mapping was integrated with multistakeholder consultation processes involving development planners, government ministries, NGO activists, and rural miners to explore the political implications of "crime mapping" and surveillance. By combining high-resolution geospatial analysis with a political ecology framework, we analyze the risks and limitations of UN technical interventions. We specifically investigate how the translation of GIS knowledge can either empower rural communities through "grassroots GIS" or undermine local trust when deployed primarily as an enforcement tool. Through this interdisciplinary lens, we evaluate the tensions between the UN's mandate for environmental protection and the socio-economic realities of informal mining in ecologically sensitive zones.
