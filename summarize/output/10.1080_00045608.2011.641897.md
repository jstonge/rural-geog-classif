# 10.1080_00045608.2011.641897

Drawing from the framework of critical legal geography, I examine the contested political geography of potable water access in the *colonias* of South Texas. I argue that the trajectory of inadequate water access in these predominantly Mexican-American rural and periurban communities was determined by water politics and territorial exclusion rather than solely by poverty or public policy failure. To investigate this, I analyze two companion legal cases from 1971 (*Jimenez v. Hidalgo WCID 2 et al.* and *Fonseca v. Hidalgo WCID 2 et al.*) that challenged the ability of farmer-controlled water control and improvement districts (WCIDs) to unilaterally exclude *colonias* from their territories. My methodology involves a detailed analysis of legislative archives, court records, and the Mexican American Legal Defense and Education Fund (MALDEF) archives. Rather than focusing exclusively on final rulings, I trace the evolution of legal reasoning and shifting discursive tactics throughout the litigation process. By interrogating these legal texts, I explore how the debate shifted from political voting rights to narratives of market forces and service provision, effectively transforming *colonias* residents from political actors with governance standing into passive consumers of dysfunctional public services.
