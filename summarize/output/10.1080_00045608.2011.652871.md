# 10.1080_00045608.2011.652871

We employ a regional ethnography to examine the intersection of neoliberal socioeconomic change and transnational migration in the Totonacapan region of Veracruz, Mexico. Our approach focuses on how macro-level processes of neoliberal restructuring are filtered through local historical trajectories and cultural understandings to produce varied landscapes of economic subjectivity. We conducted three months of ethnographic fieldwork in 2007 across four municipios: Papantla and Tecolutla in the predominantly Mestizo coastal plain, and Cuahuitlán and Filomeno Mata in the more indigenous highlands of the Sierra Madre Oriental. 

Our data were derived from fifty-three semistructured interviews with state and regional officials, migrants, their families, farmers, and local leaders across sixteen small communities. These were supplemented by seven half-day focus group sessions with men, women, youth, and community leaders to trace agrarian histories and migration patterns, as well as collaborative projects with local youth. We synthesized these observations and experiences through a qualitative analysis of interview and focus group transcripts, utilizing content analysis to identify recurring themes and compiling community-specific histories of government programs and major crops. Through this immersive method, we analyze how differing conceptions of social responsibility and ethnic identity influence whether communities experience migration as a source of severe dislocation or as a managed process for the collective benefit.
