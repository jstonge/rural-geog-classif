# 10.1080_00045608.2011.653732

We employ a critical theoretical approach to disclose the elements of Nazi spatial theory, specifically examining how the "reactionary modernism" of the Third Reich manifested through the interrelated processes of deterritorialization and reterritorialization. To delineate this "dark geography," we focus our analysis on the contributions of two key figures: the legal and political theorist Carl Schmitt and the geographer Walter Christaller. We analyze Schmitt’s conceptualization of *Großraum* (greater space) as the politico-judicial justification for the geographical expansion of the Reich and the creation of a new international spatial order. Simultaneously, we examine Christaller’s application of central place theory to the planning of "empty space" in conquered Eastern territories, particularly Poland, following the forced removal of non-Germanized populations. By synthesizing these theoretical contributions within the broader context of Nazi biopolitics and the pursuit of *Lebensraum*, we argue that the regime utilized a combination of instrumental rationality and antimodernist romanticism to impose a violent new relationship between people and space. Our analysis situates these figures alongside the established geopolitical work of Karl Haushofer to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the spatial strategies used to construct the Nazi empire.
