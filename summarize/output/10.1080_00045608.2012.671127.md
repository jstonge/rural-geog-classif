# 10.1080_00045608.2012.671127

Drawing on a nature-society geography approach to health and well-being, I examine the public health crisis of chronic arsenic poisoning in rural Bangladesh. To expound upon the multifaceted nature of health within the context of development, I analyze the socioecological parameters, economic factors, and political drivers that have contributed to the mass poisoning of millions of people through the consumption of arsenic-laced groundwater. My approach integrates insights from political ecology and feminist geographies of well-being to investigate the complex intersections of environmental change, development interventions—specifically the large-scale promotion of tubewells—and embodied health. I pay particular attention to the production of "contaminated citizens," focusing on the interlinked ways that water contamination influences not only physical illness and morbidity but also social stigma, ostracization, and emotional distress. By analyzing the lived experiences of those affected, I highlight how the trajectories of health and well-being are shaped by the contradictions of development endeavors and the uneven social access to safe water alternatives.
