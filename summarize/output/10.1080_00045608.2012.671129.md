# 10.1080_00045608.2012.671129

We present a case study demonstrating the simultaneous application of social network and spatial analytical methods to model the transmission of cholera and shigellosis in rural Bangladesh. Utilizing a spatially referenced longitudinal demographic database of approximately 200,000 people in Matlab from 1983 to 2003, we analyzed disease incidence patterns across both geographic space and kinship-based social networks. To operationalize these relationships, we constructed distance matrices to model spatial proximity and kinship-based matrices using a complete network design, where nodes represent *baris* (household clusters) and edges are defined by individual-level migrations. We first calculated Moran's I statistics to measure clustering within the social and spatial matrices independently. To further isolate the influence of social ties from the local neighborhood context, we developed combined spatial effects and spatial disturbance models. These models allowed us to analyze simultaneous spatial and social effects while controlling for known covariates and unknown underlying spatial autocorrelation. Through this integrated approach, we sought to determine whether person-to-person transmission via kinship networks or environmental transmission via geographic proximity served as the primary driver for each bacterial diarrheal disease.
