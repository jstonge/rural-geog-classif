# 10.1080_00045608.2012.671133

Drawing on long-term research initiated in 2000, I employ a mixed-methods approach to analyze health discourses and the use of traditional medicine in rural northeast South Africa. The study focuses on the Mzinti community in Mpumalanga Province, a region characterized by significant political and spatial transitions following the 1994 democratic elections. To generate a contextually rich understanding of health decision-making, I integrate quantitative data from a structured survey of 478 randomly selected households—which captured demographic characteristics, livelihood patterns, and the collection of medicinal plants—with qualitative data derived from fifty semistructured interviews with male and female household heads. These interviews specifically explored perceptions of medical options and the use of *sangomas*, while participant observation and additional interviews with health care providers and government agency representatives provided further depth. By comparing the discrepancies between survey reportage and interview narratives, I work to uncover "subaltern health narratives" and examine how sociocultural systems, historical geographies, and conflicting regulatory frameworks mediate the intersection of traditional and biomedical health practices.
