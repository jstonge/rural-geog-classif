# 10.1080_00045608.2012.707591

We present a multilevel modeling framework, the FUTure Urban-Regional Environment Simulation (FUTURES), designed to simulate the emergence of landscape spatial structure in urbanizing regions by integrating field-based and object-based representations of land change. Our approach couples three interacting submodels to integrate nonstationary drivers: POTENTIAL, which utilizes multilevel logistic regression to quantify development suitability based on socioeconomic, infrastructural, and environmental factors; DEMAND, which estimates subregional per capita land consumption rates; and a stochastic Patch-Growing Algorithm (PGA). The PGA bridges cell- and object-based representations by using a region-growing mechanism to aggregate cell-level transitions into discrete spatial objects, with parameters empirically estimated to control patch size, shape, and dispersion. We applied this framework to the metropolitan region of Charlotte, North Carolina, simulating land development dynamics from 1996 to 2030. To evaluate the model, we assessed spatial variation in outcomes along an urban-rural continuum using cell- and patch-based metrics of correctness and error. Through simulation experiments, we examined how variations in per capita land consumption and development distribution parameters influence the emergent spatial structure of forests and farmlands.
