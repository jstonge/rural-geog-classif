# 10.1080_00045608.2012.720228

Drawing upon an anti-essentialist reading of Foucault’s theories on power and governmentality, I employ a theoretical framework merged with concepts from grounded theory, infrastructure studies, and boundary objects to examine the power dynamics inherent in cadastral mapping. Rather than focusing on the map as a static artifact, I invert the analysis to focus on the *use* of maps, decomposing cadastral mapping activities into interactions, boundary objects, and contextual factors to establish how empowerment and limitation occur. This approach is applied to fieldwork conducted in Poland between 2006 and 2008, involving 120 to 138 interviews per year across three distinct sites. The study area is characterized by significant discrepancies—up to 40 percent in rural areas—between officially mapped ownership and actual property usage following the 1989 collapse of socialist governance. By analyzing these discrepancies across multiple scales, I explore the interrelated economic, strategic, legal, and technical dimensions of property rights and the state's construction of calculable territory. Furthermore, the methodology accounts for the contemporary influence of geographic information systems (GIS) and the regulatory controls of Common Agricultural Policy disbursements to analyze competing meanings of accuracy and the limits of the cadastre's calculative power.
