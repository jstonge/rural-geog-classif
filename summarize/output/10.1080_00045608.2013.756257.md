# 10.1080_00045608.2013.756257

We conducted this study in six villages in Matlab, Bangladesh, focusing on a population of approximately 12,000 residents to examine the effects of the local population-environment context on tubewell water quality and childhood diarrheal disease. Between May 2008 and November 2009, we collected monthly duplicate 100-mL water samples from 100 shallow tubewells (< 43 m) to measure culturable *Escherichia coli* concentrations using the most probable number (MPN) Colilert test. Simultaneously, we utilized a longitudinal Health and Demographic Surveillance System to track monthly diarrheal events in children under five. To analyze the spatial relationship between infrastructure and contamination, we developed a detailed geographic database using submeter accuracy GPS surveys to map all households, ponds, and latrines within 200 m of the monitored wells. Latrines were categorized as sanitary or unsanitary based on effluent leakage. We constructed sanitation metrics using circular windows (10–100 m) to measure latrine and population counts, alongside tubewell characteristics such as age, depth, and the presence of a concrete platform. Statistical analyses included Pearson correlation coefficients and multiple regression models, with model selection guided by Akaike's information criterion (AIC), while a logistic regression model was employed to evaluate the relationship between *E. coli* concentrations and childhood diarrhea incidence.
