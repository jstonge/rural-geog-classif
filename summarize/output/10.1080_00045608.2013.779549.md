# 10.1080_00045608.2013.779549

Drawing from geographic engagements with theories of racialization and NIMBYism, I investigate the connections between economic development and the relational construction of racial identities in Madras, Oregon. My analysis focuses on the discourses of local white leadership surrounding two interconnected economic agendas designed to remake the town into an upscale, white community: the entrepreneurial recruitment of a state correctional facility and an urban renewal project emphasizing high-income residential construction and the removal of "blighted" housing. By examining these developments within the specific racial geometry of Jefferson County—a region characterized by entrenched poverty and a diverse population of white, Latino, and Native American residents—I interrogate how "yes in my backyard" (YIMBY) prison politics are galvanized to maintain geographies of racialized privilege. Through this empirical focus, I employ a racial economy perspective to demonstrate how white officials utilized a normative racial framework to reaffirm their own privileged status while stigmatizing marginalized groups. Ultimately, I argue that these redevelopment strategies simultaneously institutionalize carceral expansion and reproduce unequal spaces, illustrating the centrality of race-class formations in shaping local geographies of opportunity and disadvantage.
