# 10.1080_00045608.2014.944453

Drawing on a synthesis of Marxist theories of primitive accumulation and Foucault’s conceptualization of governmentality, I explore the relationship between the techniques of colonial administration and the stability of rule in colonial Zambia (Northern Rhodesia). I specifically examine the transition in the 1920s from the coercive charter rule of the British South Africa Company to the administration of the Colonial Office, a period marked by the cessation of widespread rural unrest and the expansion of an extractive economy. Utilizing archival and secondary sources, I analyze two primary spatial and political interventions: the implementation of indirect rule through Native authorities and the constitution of Native reserves. I argue that these mechanisms functioned as a new mode of governing that sought to align the political landscape with the requirements of extractive capitalism by producing new economic subjectivities. By weaving together political economy and poststructural notions of power, I examine how these techniques worked to stabilize the proletarianization and dispossession of African populations, ultimately resolving the central contradictions of colonial rule—the tension between promoting radical social transformation and maintaining political order—across the sub-Saharan African colonial context.
