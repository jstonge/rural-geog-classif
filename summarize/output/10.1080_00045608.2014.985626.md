# 10.1080_00045608.2014.985626

We employ a case study approach to examine mobilizations for agro-ecological alternatives and food sovereignty in the Biobío region of southern Chile, focusing specifically on a network of peasant women known as *curadoras de semillas* (seed curators). Our analysis is grounded in a combination of ethnographic and collaborative methods conducted between 2010 and 2013. The primary data consists of twenty-three in-depth interviews—eight with movement leaders and fifteen with *curadoras*—obtained through a snowball sampling approach. These interviews, which averaged two hours in length and were mostly conducted in the participants' homes, were recorded, transcribed, and coded using NVivo 10 software. To supplement these interviews, we drew upon participant observation at seed exchange events and a long-standing collaboration with Chile's National Association of Rural and Indigenous Women (ANAMURI). We frame our analysis by integrating three theoretical perspectives: food sovereignty as an analytical and activist principle, postdevelopment and alter-globalization approaches to capitalist alternatives, and literature on the spatial politics of local mobilizations. Through this methodology, we explore how these women utilize horizontal networks and traditional knowledge to challenge the hegemonic agro-export regime and its universalizing spatiality.
