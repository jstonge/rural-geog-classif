# 10.1080_00045608.2015.1060921

We employed a mixed-methods approach, combining quantitative analysis of household surveys with qualitative interviews and participant observations, to investigate the cross-scale processes shaping rural land-use decisions in the Poyang Lake Region (PLR) of China. To account for locational and environmental variability, we stratified villages based on adjusted elevation (high vs. low risk of flooding) and proximity to county capitals (within vs. beyond 5 km), randomly selecting eight villages across these four categories. Household surveys were conducted in January 2007, targeting approximately 25% of households per village. 

To analyze the data, we developed a three-level regression model for the region's two dominant crops—one-season rice and cotton—to capture the nested effects of plot-level biophysical properties (Level 1), household characteristics (Level 2), and village location (Level 3). This quantitative framework allowed us to evaluate the influence of urban job markets, represented by off-farm income, on individual plot crop choices. We supplemented these findings with qualitative data from interviews and field observations to account for macrolevel forces—such as national policy reforms affecting agricultural markets and regional government interventions—that were not fully captured by the surveys. Together, these methods enabled us to examine how macrolevel forces interact with microlevel biophysical, demographic, and locational factors to drive household land-use decisions.
