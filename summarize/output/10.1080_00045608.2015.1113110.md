# 10.1080_00045608.2015.1113110

We investigated the space-time paths of twenty-seven widowed women in the village of Nakagongo, south central Uganda, to examine the relationship between mobility and livelihood opportunities. Adopting a methodological pluralism that integrates quantitative and qualitative data, we employed GPS tracking to accurately map the subjects' activity spaces and daily routines over a three-month period, avoiding the distortions common in self-reported time diaries. These data were triangulated with participant observation conducted during three two-week intervals over one year, as well as two-hour semistructured interviews with each participant. Our analysis utilized ArcGIS to construct space-time paths and time prisms, allowing us to evaluate the spatial budget and time use of women with varying levels of livelihood diversification. We specifically examined variables such as age, education, family capital, and health, alongside the frequency of use of various transportation modes—including walking, bicycle taxis, motorcycle taxis, buses, and cars. Finally, we applied statistical regressions to determine the effect of these transport modes on livelihood, treating time as a critical resource for pursuing income-generating activities.
