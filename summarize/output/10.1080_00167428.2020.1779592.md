# 10.1080_00167428.2020.1779592

Drawing from the burgeoning field of black geographies, I conducted a long-term ethnographic study between 2014 and 2018 to examine how the *boucherie*—the ritualized butchering of a hog—serves as a mechanism for black placemaking among rural Creole communities in southwestern Louisiana. My methodological approach integrated dozens of semistructured interviews with 29 participants, which were transcribed verbatim and coded using NVivo software, alongside dozens of hours of participant observation. To provide a fulsome portrait of this community and the socio-economic context of the study area—specifically within Acadia, St. Landry, Lafayette, and St. Martin parishes—I supplemented my primary fieldwork with archival research, including the analysis of census data, government reports, community-based magazines, and newspaper articles. By weaving together these qualitative and archival sources, I analyze the *boucherie* not merely as a livelihood-maintenance tactic, but as a "memory performance" and an embodied practice. This approach allows me to demonstrate how the ritual spatializes Creole cultural identity, transforming rural landscapes into symbolic sites of endurance, kinship, and resistance.
