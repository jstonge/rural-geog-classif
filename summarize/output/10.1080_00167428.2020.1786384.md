# 10.1080_00167428.2020.1786384

We utilized the Nebraska Roots Migration Survey, an online instrument adapted from a previous study in Vermont, to examine the residential decision-making and place attachments of individuals who spent at least part of their adolescence in Nebraska. Distributed via social media networks in February and March 2019, the survey gathered 1,827 fully completed responses representing all 93 counties in the state. We collected demographic data, multiple-choice factors for staying, leaving, or returning, and open-ended narratives. To facilitate between-group comparisons, we adopted the U.S. Census definition of rurality, classifying communities with fewer than 2,500 residents as rural and those with 2,500 or more as urban. We specifically categorized "stayers" as those who had never lived outside Nebraska for a full year or more (excluding schooling or military service), further distinguishing between "rural hometowners," "rural residents," and "all-rural stayers." Quantitative data were analyzed using SPSS via cross-tabulation and chi-square tests ($p \le 0.05$). Qualitative narratives were then coded thematically using Dedoose, employing a biographical approach to provide nuanced context to the statistical findings and explore the complex emotional and social drivers of immobility.
