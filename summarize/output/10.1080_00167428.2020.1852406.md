# 10.1080_00167428.2020.1852406

We use the Mongolian Kazakh population as a case study to examine how strong place attachments evolve into place-based narratives of home and identity for diasporic communities. Drawing upon a conceptual framework that integrates the literatures of nostalgia, place attachment, and the rural idyll, we specifically employ Scannell and Gifford’s Person-Process-Place (PPP) model to analyze the multidimensional nature of these bonds. Our approach focuses on recent urban in-migrants in Ulaanbaatar whose ethnic and cultural histories are tied to the remote, rural region of western Mongolia. We explore the permutations of place identity as expressed through daily life, investigating how these attachments are communicated to younger generations—who may have limited personal experience with the rural homeland—via stories, material artifacts, and community celebrations. By analyzing the interplay between memory, imagination, and mobility, we seek to demonstrate how former residents of western Mongolia (re)create a "rural idyll" that serves as a social symbol of Kazakh culture and history, thereby maintaining collective place valuing despite residence in a globalized urban environment.
