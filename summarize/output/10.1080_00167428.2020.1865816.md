# 10.1080_00167428.2020.1865816

To examine the emergence of distinct forms of citizenship in rural commodity frontiers, I employ a qualitative, multi-disciplinary approach centered on the salmon industry in the Los Lagos region of Chile. Drawing on six years of fieldwork (2012–2018) conducted by a team of geographers, anthropologists, and sociologists, the study utilizes data collected across at least eight districts. The methodological framework integrates ethnographic research—including six-month immersions in the localities of Calbuco and Cochamó—nearly 70 semi-structured interviews, and four participatory mapping experiences in Cochamó, Quellón, Quemchi, and Curaco de Velez, with workshops ranging from four to ten sessions each. 

I analyzed the resulting data using Atlas.ti software, coding transcripts through analytical categories derived from resource frontier literature and the concept of rural citizenship. This approach allows for the exploration of the trialectic relationship between place identity, commodity production, and democratic institutions. By focusing on the spatial patterns, monetary flows, and state-subject interactions within this specific regional context, I identify how the neoliberalization of nature and the transformation of the landscape into a salmon-producing territory shape the political participation and identity performance of rural inhabitants.
