# 10.1080_00167428.2021.1897812

To examine the emergence of a contemporary mountain imaginary in the Galician Highlands, we employed a qualitative research design centered on the Trevinca Massif. Drawing upon Debarbieux’s conceptualization of social imaginaries of space and existing theorizations of mountains as socially framed spatial categories and national discourses, we analyzed recent local narratives to determine whether they reflect the construction of a national landscape or are driven by global processes. Our empirical approach consisted of conducting semistructured interviews with local actors, the resulting data from which were processed through analytical coding. We then utilized semiotic clustering to derive three distinct narratives. By contrasting these results with the broader evolution of Western mountain imaginaries and the historical genealogy of Galician national landscape imaginaries—which have traditionally privileged coastal and agricultural motifs—we sought to explain the socio-spatial emergence of the "Galician Highlands" as a category. This methodology allowed us to evaluate the influence of global forces, specifically tourism and promotion, and the role of disputed "natural borders" in shaping the current perception of the region.
