# 10.1080_00167428.2021.1925897

We employ a multi-case study approach to explore young people's visions for life in the countryside across four independent research sites in Latin America: north Argentina, east Bolivia, south Ecuador, and south Mexico. Our methodology is grounded in the principles of social constructivism and reflexivity, treating research as a joint process of knowledge production. Given the diversity of local contexts—ranging from tropical rainforests and neotropical forests to semideciduous dry forests and transitional Puna-Yungas zones—we avoided a fixed category of "youth" and instead applied flexible, participative methods adapted to each site. 

Between 2015 and 2019, we collected data using a variety of qualitative and quantitative tools, including participatory workshops involving drawing and acting, semi-structured and ethnographic "go-along" interviews, non-participant observation, and structured surveys. Participants included secondary and university students, as well as young adults and indigenous peasants. Data analysis involved triangulation of visual and textual outputs, utilizing Grounded Theory, framework analysis, and descriptive statistics via software such as atlas.ti and IBM SPSS. By analyzing similarities and complementarities across these case studies, we identify crosscutting themes regarding (im)mobilities, place-based belonging, and the motivations that drive young people to either stay in or leave their rural communities.
