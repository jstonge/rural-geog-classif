# 10.1080_00330120903543756

To assess the potential risk to humans from wildfires within the U.S.-Mexico border zone of San Diego County, California, we developed an integrated analytical framework combining three spatially explicit GIS-based models. First, we employed a GIS overlay model—utilizing data on fire history, fuel flammability rankings, and proximity to roads—to identify high-danger zones. Within these zones, we tested two hypothetical scenarios: an undocumented immigrant traveling north toward a road and a border agent with communication devices capable of moving in any direction. To estimate evacuation timing, we developed a Pedestrian Mobility Model (PMM) using the Path Distance function in ArcGIS. The PMM integrated 30-m resolution digital elevation model (DEM) data, vegetation, and digitized road/trail networks, applying travel rate percentages based on slope and aspect to calculate least-accumulative cost travel times to safety zones. These travel times served as critical thresholds for the Wildland-Urban Interface Evacuation (WUIVAC) model, which utilized FlamMap fire spread parameters (including wind speed and direction) to generate evacuation trigger buffers (ETBs). By integrating the outputs of the overlay model, PMM, and WUIVAC, we generated wildfire risk maps to determine whether pedestrians in worst-case locations could reach safety before the arrival of a flaming front.
