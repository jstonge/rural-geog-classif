# 10.1080_00330120903546312

To examine the structural changes in Polish agriculture following the breakdown of socialism, we employed a multi-method approach combining quantitative statistical analysis, remote sensing, and qualitative interviews. Our study focused on Pyrzyce County in northwest Poland, a region characterized by a history of both state-owned and private family farming. We analyzed agricultural micro-census data from 1996 and 2002 to track changes in the total number of farms, the distribution of farm sizes (categorized as small, medium, and large), the prevalence of professional full-time farming, and total private farmland area. To assess the spatial manifestation of these changes, we utilized Landsat TM/ETM+ satellite images from June 1991 and June 2000, applying image segmentation to map individual fields and calculate changes in field size distributions and aggregation patterns. Finally, we conducted a survey consisting of 35 interviews with local farmers and 20 interviews with stakeholders to identify the socio-economic drivers and internal strategies associated with these shifts. By integrating fine-scale census data with spatial analysis and qualitative insights, we sought to explain the polarization of farm sizes and land use patterns within the context of emerging agricultural value chains.
