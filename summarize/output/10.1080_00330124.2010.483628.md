# 10.1080_00330124.2010.483628

Drawing upon ethnographic and ethnobotanical literature from across the Amazon basin, with a particular emphasis on northwestern Amazonia, we examine the role of local seed systems in traditional indigenous and folk agricultural societies. We argue that the geographic, seasonal, and social scarcity of planting material—specifically seeds, cuttings, and pseudostems—shapes the socioeconomic relations and agricultural practices of rural Amazonian peoples. Our approach focuses on the material significance of planting stock access over symbolic or cultural dimensions, analyzing how the high bulk, perishability, and low stock-yield ratios of vegetatively propagated staples like manioc and plantain create distinct constraints compared to cereal crops. We analyze the mechanisms of acquisition, including the use of home gardens and swiddens as primary sources, as well as the operation of intra- and intercommunity exchange networks involving gifting, loans, and market transactions. By synthesizing data on crop-varietal diversity and the frequency of planting stock exchange, we demonstrate how these informal supply systems are critical to the maintenance of agrobiodiversity, the assurance of subsistence security for lowland farmers, and the facilitation of market product specialization among traditional farmers.
