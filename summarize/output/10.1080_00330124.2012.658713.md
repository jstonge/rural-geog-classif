# 10.1080_00330124.2012.658713

To assess whether the emergence and growth of ethnic minority groups in the Great Plains between 1970 and 2008 represents a localized phenomenon or a region-wide shift in population composition, we employ a multi-methodological approach utilizing quantitative spatial analysis. Our data are drawn from the 1970, 1980, 1990, and 2000 U.S. Decennial Censuses, the 2008 Population Estimates Program, the 2002 Census of Agriculture, and the Regional Economic Information System. We focus on five demographic groups—Latino, black, Asian, Native American, and white populations—across a study area encompassing the major regional delimitations of the Great Plains. 

To evaluate ethnic restructuring, we utilize choropleth maps alongside the Hoover and Simpson indexes; the former measures the concentration and dispersion of populations across the region, while the latter assesses the probability of diversity within individual counties. Additionally, we conduct a nonhierarchical cluster analysis to identify the characteristics of places experiencing demographic change. This analysis utilizes six categories of variables, including economic structure (employment percentages in manufacturing, agriculture, construction, wholesale-retail trade, and services), percentages of Asian and Latino populations, proximity to interstates, and the presence of universities or reservations. Statistical analysis was performed using SPSS (version 17.0) and mapping was conducted via ArcGIS (version 9.3.1).
