# 10.1080_00330124.2012.681517

We utilized a combination of freely available Light Detection and Ranging (LiDAR) data and ground survey data to examine stream channel cross-section changes across an urban-to-rural land-use boundary in the North Carolina Piedmont. Our study area focused on two headwater streams, North and South Buffalo Creeks, within the Buffalo Creek watershed. Between 2005 and 2008, we conducted ground surveys of twenty-six channel cross sections across ten reaches on North Buffalo Creek to determine bankfull capacity, width, and depth. To extend these observations, we developed a digital topographic model using 3-m bare-earth LiDAR point sets and a 6.1-m hydrologically correct raster. We enhanced the model by manually digitizing top-of-bank breaklines from orthophotography and merging the 3-m data with raw, unclassified LiDAR point clouds to increase point density within the channels. We then calibrated the LiDAR-derived model by regressing modeled dimensions against field-surveyed data. This calibrated model allowed us to extract high-density cross-sectional data downstream, which we analyzed alongside independent hydraulic geometry curves and land-use mapping to evaluate the relationship between channel enlargement and the proportion of urbanized watershed area.
