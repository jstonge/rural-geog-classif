# 10.1080_00330124.2012.681584

We employ a case study approach to measure the temporal and spatial distribution of ranchette development in La Plata County, Colorado, an amenity-rich region characterized by high rates of recreation- and retirement-induced migration. To quantify land fragmentation, we utilize archival and contemporary land ownership data across three time intervals spanning a twenty-year period from 1988 to 2008. Following Colorado state statutes, we define ranchettes as rural parcels ranging from a minimum of 35 acres—the threshold for exemption from county subdivision regulations—to a maximum of 69.9 acres. This specific range ensures that the parcels are too small for viable commercial ranching yet large enough to avoid standard development codes. We supplement our county-level analysis with broader longitudinal data from the U.S. Department of Agriculture’s Census of Agriculture to contextualize La Plata County's trends within the wider patterns of "mountainous" Colorado counties. By analyzing changes in parcel size and ownership patterns, we aim to delineate the degree to which these low-density residential developments transform the rural landscape and identify the spatial mechanisms, such as contagious growth around initial development nodes, that drive exurban expansion.
