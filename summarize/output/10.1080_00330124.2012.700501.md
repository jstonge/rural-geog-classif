# 10.1080_00330124.2012.700501

We evaluate the effect of moving window (MW) size on observed spatial fragmentation patterns and propose a method to identify an effective MW size using Simpson's diversity index. To test the robustness of this approach, we demonstrate its application across six cities in the Phoenix metropolitan area characterized by substantial variation in land composition and configuration. Our primary data sources are the 30-m resolution U.S. Geological Survey National Land Cover Data sets (NLCD) for 1992 and 2001, which we reclassify into three categories: developed, undeveloped, and cultivated. We test a series of MWs with side lengths ranging from 90 m to 2,370 m to determine the size that captures the maximum diversity of land classes. Additionally, we explore the effects of observation scale on urban-to-rural fragmentation gradients, comparing two popular approaches: concentric rings (at 1-km and 5-km widths) and transects (using gliding boxes of 15 km × 15 km and 15 km × 1 km). Through this methodology, we examine how the selection of window size and observation scale filters noise and influences the representation of land fragmentation patterns in a rapidly urbanizing region.
