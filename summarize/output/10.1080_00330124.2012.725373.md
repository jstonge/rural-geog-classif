# 10.1080_00330124.2012.725373

To understand the factors influencing decisions to return to rural U.S. communities and the barriers preventing such moves, we employed a multisited, interview-based qualitative methodology. Our research focused on demographically struggling and geographically isolated rural communities with limited natural amenities, specifically targeting individuals in their late twenties to late forties—a critical "settling down" life course segment where career and family obligations overlap and return migration typically peaks. 

To capture a comprehensive perspective, we conducted 309 interviews during 2008 and 2009 at twenty-seven high school reunions across twenty-one communities in seventeen states. High school reunions served as unique venues that allowed us to simultaneously engage with common cohorts of stayers, return migrants, and nonreturn migrants. Our analytical strategy integrated life course perspectives with social network theory, conceptualizing migration as a family-related and community-embedded process. By recording the personal views of these cohorts, we evaluated the complex interplay between economic circumstances, family incentives, and social and physical place settings to determine how ties to people and place operate in combination to drive or inhibit rural return migration.
