# 10.1080_00330124.2014.886922

In this study, we analyzed the diurnal spatial patterns of ozone ($\text{O}_3$) and respirable suspended particulate (RSP) matter in Hong Kong over an eleven-year period from 2001 to 2011. We utilized hourly air pollution data obtained from the Hong Kong Environmental Protection Department across fourteen monitoring stations, which were categorized into three groups: urban (including roadside), new town residential, and rural background stations. To reveal the diurnal cycles within seasonal-level spatial patterns, we employed harmonic analysis, aggregating data into four seasons: spring (March–May), summer (June–August), autumn (September–November), and winter (December–February). 

Using a harmonic equation, we estimated the air pollutant concentration frequency to determine the phase angle—interpreted as the time of maximum concentration—and the average hourly frequency. We further calculated the standardized amplitude to assess the relative importance of the diurnal cycle at each station and computed the portion of variance explained relative to the standard deviation of the twenty-four-hour concentration values. This methodological approach allowed for a comprehensive spatial analysis of pollutant behavior across the territory, accounting for the influence of vehicular traffic, titration effects, and the Asian monsoon system.
