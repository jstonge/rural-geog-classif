# 10.1080_00330124.2014.905158

Drawing on the theoretical frameworks of megapolitan geographies, urban political ecology, and urban metabolism, we theoretically and empirically explore the concept of "megapolitan political ecology." We first establish a conceptual framework tailored to southern Appalachia—specifically the Piedmont megapolitan region—positing that the megapolitan scale is uniquely suited to capture the urban metabolic connections and socio-spatial flows of people, resources, and capital that drive rapid urbanization in historically rural hinterlands. By integrating perspectives from exurban political ecology and the study of "global cities," we argue that these metabolic processes are central to the region's pressing socioecological crises. We then empirically ground this approach using data from the Coweeta Long-Term Ecological Research (LTER) program. Through the analysis of LTER data, we illustrate the human and nonhuman metabolisms across the Piedmont region, specifically highlighting the emergence of a "ring of asphalt" as a spatial manifestation of changing metabolic patterns. This methodology allows us to trace the circulation of matter and value across the urban-rural matrix, demonstrating how regional infrastructural networks and economic linkages transform the ecological and social vitality of southern Appalachia.
