# 10.1080_00330124.2014.935165

We examine the geography of fatal traffic collisions in Indiana from 2003 to 2011 using a multi-scalar spatial analysis to identify risk landscapes and patterns of mortality. To address the rarity of fatal events, we utilize a nine-year aggregation of data sourced from the Indiana State Police Automated Reporting Information Exchange System (ARIES), operationalizing the fatal collision rate as the percentage of all collisions resulting in at least one fatality. To mitigate the modifiable areal unit problem (MAUP), we employ three distinct spatial units of analysis: individual collision points, one-mile-square quadrats (36,879 grids) for density analysis, and ninety-two Indiana counties. 

Our methodological approach integrates several spatial statistical techniques. We first analyze point patterns and compare risks across road types, locality classifications, and responding law enforcement agencies. We then apply the nearest neighbor index (NNI) to determine if statewide fatal collision points are dispersed, clustered, or random. To assess spatial interaction and dependence among counties, we calculate the global Moran's I for statewide spatial autocorrelation and the local Moran's I to identify specific clusters of positive (high-high, low-low) or negative (low-high, high-low) autocorrelation. These analyses were conducted using ArcGIS v.10.0 and Stata v.12.1.
