# 10.1080_00330124.2015.1028324

We examine household-level characteristics that predict water insecurity in low-income rural and periurban communities, specifically *colonias*, located in Hidalgo and El Paso Counties along the Texas-Mexico border. Our approach integrates qualitative and quantitative methods to operationalize water security as a multidimensional construct comprising water access, quality, and water affect. We first conducted semistructured interviews and focus groups in Hidalgo County to inform the development of a household water security survey. This survey was administered to households across various *colonia* classifications (red, yellow, green, and unclassified) to capture a representative range of infrastructural conditions.

To analyze the data, we employed an experiential scalogram technique to draw inferences about the latent variable of water insecurity across the three identified dimensions. Using the resulting scale scores, we clustered households into four categories—water secure, marginally water secure, marginally water insecure, and water insecure—via the centroid method and squared Euclidean distance. Finally, we employed two logistic regression models (binary and ordered) to identify which household characteristics, including socioeconomic factors and immigration status, significantly predict the likelihood of household water insecurity.
