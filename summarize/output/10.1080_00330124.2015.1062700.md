# 10.1080_00330124.2015.1062700

I apply a historical political ecology framework to examine municipal drinking water governance in colonial Lima, Peru, between 1578 and 1700. My objective is to analyze how power dynamics influenced the sociospatial patterns of water access and control, and how these patterns reinforced colonial social divisions and shaped urban-rural relations. To achieve this, I combine theoretical themes from the political ecology of water—specifically unequal access, urban bias, and the concept of "archipelagos" of supply—with the multidisciplinary methods of hydraulic archaeology. 

Because the city's extensive urban development has obscured most physical remains of colonial infrastructure, I rely primarily on archival analysis. My central data source is the *Libros de Cabildos de Lima* (LCL), the minutes of the City Council, which provide a continuous record of municipal actions. I analyze all references within these records to springs, reservoirs, pipelines, and petitions for water connections to reconstruct the material aspects of Lima's hydraulic space. By mapping pipeline routes and the locations of public and private connections, I evaluate the spatial and temporal distribution of water and the role of the *Cabildo* in managing resource inequality during the period when the city's primary potable water infrastructure was established.
