# 10.1080_00330124.2015.1065545

We employ a sociospatial framework to investigate the interaction between social network position and natural resource access among households in four rural communities in South Africa (Tokwe and Muncu in Willowvale; Sewefontein and Zaaimanshoek in Baviaanskloof). Our methodology integrates social network analysis (SNA) with geographic position data obtained via Global Positioning System (GPS), satellite imagery, and participatory mapping. To quantify social embeddedness, we calculate four measures of network centrality—degree, betweenness, closeness, and eigenvector centrality—based on household exchanges of food, money, firewood, water, manual labor, and disease information. These social variables are analyzed alongside geographic variables, specifically the mean and summed area of polygons encompassing natural resources (wood, water, bush medicine, and livestock grazing), as well as the mean and sum distance from households to these resource polygons. By combining these datasets at the household level, we test for statistical associations between an actor's topological position within the community network and their spatial proximity to essential natural resources, seeking to determine if social relations bear upon spatiality or vice versa within these marginalized rural contexts.
