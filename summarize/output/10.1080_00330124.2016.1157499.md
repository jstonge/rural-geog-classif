# 10.1080_00330124.2016.1157499

To investigate the geographical and social dimensions of food access in rural Cuba, we employed a mixed-methods approach centered on the use of food diaries. Our study was conducted in March 2015 within a rural community in the province of Sancti Spiritus, specifically targeting forty households across two adjacent Committees for the Defense of the Revolution (CDRs) to capture varying levels of connection to local agricultural cooperatives (CPAs and CCSs). Following a pilot study with five core families to identify primary food sources, we distributed detailed worksheets to all forty households, requiring them to record everything they ate and the specific origin of each ingredient for one full week. To ensure data reliability and accuracy, we conducted daily visits to the participating households. This quantitative tracking of food items and sources—ranging from state-run bodegas and markets to individual gardens and informal gifts—was integrated with qualitative data gathered through interviews and participant observation. By combining these methods, we aimed to move beyond the "spatial-only" limitations of GIS-based food desert research to uncover the complex, relational networks and informal social relations that facilitate food access in a non-market-based rural context.
