# 10.1080_00330124.2016.1198264

I develop and deepen the concept of the rural-urban interface through a mixed-method, comparative case study conducted in Calaveras County, California. Situated in the Sierra Nevada foothills, this study area serves as a representative site of rural restructuring, characterized by exurban residential development, amenity migration, and the pressures of the urbanizing Central Valley. Drawing from a larger study of divergent land management outcomes, I utilize a specific case of contested ecologies to (re)conceptualize the rural-urban interface as a multidimensional construct of meaning, model, and metaphor (MMM). My empirical approach, conducted between January and June 2010, integrated participant observation, a short political-ideological survey, and semi-structured, in-depth interviews with fifty-one key stakeholders, including ranchers, farmers, business owners, community activists, public servants, and planners. By synthesizing these qualitative and quantitative data with extant scholarship on exurbia, I examine how the interface functions not merely as a spatial boundary, but as a conduit for sociocultural, ecological, financial, and material flows. This methodology allows me to situate contrasting people and places within an ongoing negotiation of environmental meaning, arguing that perceived boundaries are instrumental for understanding physical-material change and mitigating social conflicts along the urbanizing edge.
