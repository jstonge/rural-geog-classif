# 10.1080_00330124.2017.1365308

I introduce an inverted two-step floating catchment area (i2SFCA) method, derived from an extension of the classic Huff model, to measure the "crowdedness"—defined as resource scarcity or competition intensity—of service facilities. While the regular 2SFCA is resident-oriented and assesses the ease of obtaining a service, the i2SFCA switches the supply and demand terms to examine the supply side. I illustrate and validate this approach through a case study of hospital inpatient services in Florida, utilizing data from the 2011 State Inpatient Databases (SID). The dataset includes 268 hospitals (supply capacity defined by staffed bed size), 983 ZIP code areas (demand defined by the number of patients generated), and road network travel times. To determine the distance decay function, I analyzed patient flow patterns, ultimately employing an exponential function with a distance decay parameter of $b = 0.14$. I further examine the connection between accessibility and crowdedness by using a spatially adaptive filter to compute average accessibility within a window of the five nearest ZIP code areas around each hospital, allowing for a scale-consistent comparison between resident-based and facility-oriented metrics.
