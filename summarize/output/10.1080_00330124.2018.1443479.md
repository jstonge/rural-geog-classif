# 10.1080_00330124.2018.1443479

This case study examines collective strategies for sustaining organizational resilience among nonprofit organizations in the wake of the 2010 and 2011 earthquake sequence in Christchurch, New Zealand. Undertaken three to four years after the most severe events, the study utilizes a retrospective approach to model nonprofit collaboration during midterm recovery. Data were collected in late 2014 from thirty-six social service-oriented nonprofits—comprising thirty-four organizations and two agency connection groups—recruited via the Community Information Network Christchurch website and snowball sampling. Inclusion criteria required formal organization status, location within the central business district or suburbs, receipt of philanthropic funds, and a social service mission.

The methodological framework employed semistructured interviews with managers from each entity, focus groups with staff from five selected organizations to capture size-based variances, and semistructured reviews of staff responses. Participants were asked to reflect longitudinally on changes in operations and partnerships dating back to two years prior to the disaster. A cross-case analysis was then conducted, with transcripts themed according to nonprofit management, emergency management, and national governance literature. By analyzing longitudinal and categorization patterns—such as field of work, date of emergence (preexisting vs. emergent), and affiliation—the study quantitatively and qualitatively evaluated shifts in partnership participation and service demands to propose a scalable model for sustaining network resilience.
