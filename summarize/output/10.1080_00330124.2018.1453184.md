# 10.1080_00330124.2018.1453184

In this article, I employ a situated and positioned personal account to illustrate the diversity of community-engaged and activist scholarship within geography. Eschewing a formal debate on the boundaries of activist research, I instead share two contrasting narratives of engagement to demonstrate how such opportunities often emerge organically at the intersection of personal ethics, commitments, and research agendas. The first case involves the establishment of Los Puentes, a Spanish-English dual-language immersion program in a low-income rural public school system in eastern North Carolina during the early 2000s, where I served as a program founder, scholar, and parent. The second case describes my collaboration on the "Building Austin, Building Injustice" project in Austin, Texas, a participatory action research (PAR) initiative conducted with the Proyecto de Defensa Laboral (Workers Defense Project). In the latter, a community-based workers center maintained ownership and control over the research design and data collection to document labor conditions and lobby for construction worker protections. By reflecting on these experiences, I seek to highlight the potential for activist scholarship to emerge outside conventional arenas and to discuss the professional challenges and opportunities for geographers committed to grounded, social-justice-oriented praxis.
