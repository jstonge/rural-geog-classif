# 10.1080_00330124.2018.1455524

We engage with findings from an ongoing research project evaluating the impacts of flooding variability on rural livelihoods and environmental perceptions in the Okavango Delta of Botswana. To analyze the region as a hydrosocial waterscape, we employ an integrated mixed-methods approach combining qualitative and quantitative techniques, including semistructured interviewing, remote sensing analysis, vegetation transects, and livelihood mapping. Between 2010 and 2016, we conducted 131 household interviews in the villages of Seronga, Mababe, and Etsha 1, 6, and 13. We utilized a spatially stratified random sampling procedure to account for the role of spatial location in determining household exposure to flooding variability. Specifically, the Etsha villages were selected to provide a social transect for examining microvariations in flooding extent. Data were processed using ATLAS.ti to code and categorize themes related to environmental dynamics and dominant livelihood systems. Our sampling strategy targeted three sites with distinct flooding regimes—ranging from regular seasonal fluctuations in Seronga to subdecadal changes in the Etsha villages and decadal change in Mababe—allowing us to examine how different temporal frequencies of environmental change shape resource access and governance.
