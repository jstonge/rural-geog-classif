# 10.1080_00330124.2018.1501707

I argue that traditional cultural or economic regionalizations of the American West are limited by a reliance on state or county boundaries and a failure to account for spatial patterns of land management and ownership. To address this, I propose a new conceptual framework that divides the region into three distinct "Wests" based on opportunities for economic activity and population growth: the Booming West, the Protected West, and the Bypassed West. 

To map these regions, I define the Booming West using 2010 Census data, aggregating urbanized clusters of at least 1,500 people. The Protected West is delineated using the Protected Areas Database of the U.S. (PADUS), encompassing national park units, national forests, military bases, Department of Energy lands, wildlife refuges, Indian reservations, and specific BLM designations such as national monuments and Areas of Critical Environmental Concern. The Bypassed West is defined residually as all remaining land not captured by the first two categories, including private land outside booming areas and general BLM holdings. By synthesizing these data sources, I move beyond administrative boundaries to highlight the sharp transitions and interrelationships between areas of rapid growth, legal preservation, and economic stagnation.
