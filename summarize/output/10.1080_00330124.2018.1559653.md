# 10.1080_00330124.2018.1559653

We investigate the relationship between regional innovation and income inequality using a panel dataset of Chinese city regions from 2004 to 2012. To capture the heterogeneity of the Chinese landscape, we utilize the city region scale rather than provincial data, categorizing the study area into eastern, northeastern, central, and western regions. We employ a regression model where income inequality—measured both as the ratio of urban per capita disposable income to rural per capita net income and as the ratio of the urban-rural income difference to city region per capita income—is estimated as a function of innovation and several control variables, including industrial structure upgrades, the proportion of primary sector activities, market openness, human capital, GDP per capita, and city size. Innovation is proxied by the number of patent applications per million residents. To test for a nonlinear relationship, we incorporate the square term of innovation into our model. Furthermore, we apply endogenous threshold effect models to determine if GDP per capita exerts a triple-threshold effect on the influence of innovation on inequality. The robustness of our findings is further validated through subsample analysis across the four major geographic areas and a disaggregate analysis based on individual incomes.
