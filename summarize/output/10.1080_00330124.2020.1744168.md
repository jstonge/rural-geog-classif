# 10.1080_00330124.2020.1744168

Using General Social Survey (GSS) data spanning from 1972 to 2018, we explore the urban-rural schism in party preference by gender within the United States. Our dataset comprises 64,814 individual observations (28,614 men and 36,200 women), with the dependent variable constructed as a seven-point ordinal scale of political affiliation, ranging from strong Democrat to strong Republican. To analyze this relationship, we employ a series of ordinary least squares (OLS) models and specifications. 

Our modeling strategy proceeds in stages: first, we regress party preference on locational variables—categorizing residence as rural (base case), suburban, or urban (divided by city size)—to verify the existence of the urban-rural gradient. Second, we introduce control variables for individual traits, including income quintiles, education, marital status, race, national origin, and health condition, to ensure the gender gap persists regardless of demographic influences. Finally, we test two competing hypotheses by incorporating variables for subjective happiness and compassion (measured via GSS responses regarding governmental action to solve social problems and reduce income inequality). Through this approach, we assess whether the spatial variation in the gender gap is driven by an urban-rural happiness gradient or a preference for collective, governmental solutions to social problems.
