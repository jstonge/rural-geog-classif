# 10.1080_00330124.2021.1880943

This study employs a theoretical and conceptual approach to extend the definition of rurality within rural geography, specifically introducing the concept of "acoustic rurality" within the context of the Chinese countryside. Drawing upon a multidisciplinary theoretical review, we synthesize perspectives from humanistic geography, soundscape ecology, and the "sonic turn" in geographic research to argue that the multisensory characteristics of the rural—particularly its auditory properties—are essential to authentic rural experience and place attachment. We analyze the evolution of rural soundscapes in China by contrasting historical evidence from pastoral poetry and geomantic traditions (Fengshui) with contemporary observations of "rural syndromes," such as noise pollution and ecological degradation resulting from rapid urbanization. By integrating the "hi-fi" and "lo-fi" frameworks of Schafer and the biophony/anthrophony gradients of Farina, we develop a scientific conceptual framework for rural soundscape planning. Finally, using China’s Beautiful Rural Construction and Rural Vitalization Strategy as practical references, we propose four systematic guidelines for the conservation and rehabilitation of acoustic rurality: noise control to protect tranquility, the conservation of wildlife habitats for wild soundscapes, the safeguarding of cultural soundscape heritages, and the development of recreational soundscapes for rural tourism.
