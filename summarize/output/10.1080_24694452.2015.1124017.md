# 10.1080_24694452.2015.1124017

We employed a two-phase, mixed-methods approach to examine the mobility practices of teenagers (ages 14–18) within the Burlington, Vermont, metropolitan area. Our study focused on two high schools representing contrasting land-use contexts: School A, a suburban town with mixed-use corridors and public transit, and School B, a more rural setting characterized by low density and high automobile dependence. In the first phase (October 2011), we administered linked online surveys to the entire student populations and their parents/guardians to collect quantitative and qualitative data on sociodemographics, vehicle access, family rules, and the use of information and communication technologies (ICTs). In the second phase (June 2012), we conducted an in-depth session with a small subset of students involving individual web-mapping of routes and "denied places," collaborative orthographic mapping of community destinations, and a "text review" methodology—adapted from oral history techniques—where participants reviewed their own text messages to identify travel and activity coordination. Finally, we facilitated a focus group on mobility constraints. To analyze these diverse data sources, we synthesized the information into detailed narrative vignettes for the participating students, allowing us to identify themes regarding the intersections of the built, digital, and social landscapes.
