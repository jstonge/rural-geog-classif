# 10.1080_24694452.2016.1159502

We conducted a case study in two postearthquake rural communities in the Wolong National Nature Reserve of Sichuan Province, China, to examine the relationships between catastrophe experience, risk perception, social interaction, and household resilience. Our methodological approach employed a mixed-methods design, primarily based on a quantitative household survey of 371 local residents. To analyze the complex, nonlinear relationships between these variables, we utilized regression analysis based on structural equation modeling. This quantitative inquiry was further supported by qualitative data derived from additional in-depth interviews and a comprehensive review of key spatial and social changes within the neighborhoods under study. By integrating these data sources, we sought to evaluate whether social relationships and norms mediate the link between catastrophe experience and the intention to prepare for future disasters. Specifically, we examined how the spatial features of postearthquake resettlements—such as increased proximity between residents—function as geographical contextual factors that facilitate neighborly interaction and risk communication, thereby transforming social space and influencing household behavioral responses.
