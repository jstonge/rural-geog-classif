To evaluate the sources of error in regional migration modeling, we employ a three-step methodological approach applied to interprovincial migration in China for the period between 2005 and 2010. First, we estimate a network spatially filtered Poisson migration model to generate a set of estimated migration flows. This model incorporates socioeconomic and demographic variables and utilizes an eigenvector spatial filtering approach to account for spatial autocorrelation caused by missing spatially correlated variables. Second, following the framework of Rogers et al. (2002), we decompose both the observed census migration matrices and the estimated matrices into four constituent factors: the overall effect (constant $K$), relative emissiveness ($P_i$), relative attractiveness ($Q_j$), and the level of spatial interaction between pairs of regions ($G_{ij}$). Finally, we compare these factors to isolate and calculate the specific contributions of each to the total modeling error. By assuming the constancy of three factors while varying the fourth, we derive the weighted absolute mean errors associated with the constant, emissiveness, attractiveness, and spatial interaction. This decomposition allows us to move beyond a "black box" evaluation of model performance and identify which specific components of the migration process are most difficult to model accurately.
