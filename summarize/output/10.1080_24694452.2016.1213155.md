# 10.1080_24694452.2016.1213155

Informed by geographical scholarship on hybrid sovereignties and vital materialism, we employ a qualitative and empirical approach to examine state-nature relations in the Hasbani Basin of southern Lebanon. Our methodology centers on the concept of "fluid sovereignty" to analyze how the materiality of hydrological flows and hydroclimatic contingencies unsettle Lebanese territorial claims and volumetric control over water resources. To capture the ontopolitical dynamics and the lived experience of this fractured sovereignty, we conducted regional field research in 2013 and 2014 focusing on rural agricultural communities, who are economically dependent on water access and sensitive to both hydropolitical instability and climate change. Our data collection included a purposive survey of 294 farming households, a focus group in Hasbaya, and a series of follow-up interviews. Through these methods, we examine the affective conditions of fluid sovereignty, specifically how farming households—predominantly from the Druze population—pragmatically navigate uncertain water access and the dangers of conflict-affected borderlands. By integrating this field data with theoretical frameworks of "wet ontology" and "divisible sovereignty," we illustrate the co-production of state authority by the fluid materiality of the basin's waters.
