# 10.1080_24694452.2016.1232616

Employing a materialist-territorial analysis, I examine how new road development projects affect social and sovereign relationships within a trans-Himalayan borderland between Chinese Tibet and Mustang, Nepal. I utilize a dialectic framework of mobility and containment to reveal how transborder infrastructure and the resulting consumption practices condition state-making processes at both social and geopolitical levels. By following the cross-scalar trajectory of a rural road project—tracing its evolution from a local grassroots initiative to a national development program and eventually an international transportation network—I analyze the expanding presence of the Nepali state apparatus in a historically peripheral space.

Data for this study were generated through multisite, participatory fieldwork conducted in twenty villages in Upper Mustang during the spring and summer of 2015. To gain grounded experiences of the material and social impacts of new vehicle road systems, I adopted actively mobile methods, traveling throughout Upper and Lower Mustang via motorcycle, Jeep, truck, horse, and on foot. These mobile insights were supplemented by qualitative and ethnographic data derived from 100 semistructured interviews and 60 survey questionnaires, with research informants identified through a combination of random and snowball sampling.
