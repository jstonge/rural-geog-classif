# 10.1080_24694452.2016.1249726

I critically evaluate the discursive and material practices of rural masculinity in Southeast Kansas (ancestral Osage Territory) by foregrounding ability and place as essential constituents of gender. Drawing on empirical data gathered through autoethnographic participant-observer research, I analyze qualitative data from sixty interviews and focus groups conducted with working-class, primarily white men over a nine-month period. My methodological approach employs crip theory as a critical lens to "crip" hegemonic notions of manhood, examining how men utilize their bodies as conduits to express, affirm, and reproduce cultural norms of masculinity. By integrating a synopsis of critical masculinity studies and rural geography with an in-depth overview of crip theory, I investigate the intersection of heteronormative able-bodiedness, (hetero)sexuality, and sociospatial context. This approach allows me to demonstrate how compulsory able-bodiedness and able-mindedness impose strictly policed social boundaries regarding belonging and inclusion within the specific rurality of the region.
