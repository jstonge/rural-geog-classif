# 10.1080_24694452.2016.1261684

To explore how nongovernmental organizations (NGOs) renegotiate their legitimacy and moral capital amidst the competing pressures of environmental governance and extractivism, I employed a qualitative research design centered on the frontier island of Palawan in the Philippines. My methodological approach integrated thirty-two in-depth key informant interviews, participant observation, and document analysis conducted throughout 2014 and 2015. Utilizing a purposeful sampling strategy based on referrals, I interviewed a diverse array of actors, including NGO representatives, Barangay officials, tribal leaders, leaders of palm oil cooperatives, palm oil laborers, and activists engaged in anti-extractive campaigns. Most interviews, which lasted between two and three hours, were conducted in Tagalog within the case area of Biluan, specifically focusing on the town proper and lowland coastal *sitios*. To maintain ethical standards and protect participant anonymity, I assigned pseudonyms to all respondents and locations, with the exception of major public figures. Through these methods, I examined the sociopolitical tensions arising as the NGO consortium CODE REDD attempted to implement the REDD+ carbon governance mechanism in a rural economy characterized by shifting expectations and the tangible draw of oil palm expansion on ancestral lands.
