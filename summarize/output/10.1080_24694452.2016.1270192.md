# 10.1080_24694452.2016.1270192

We focus our investigation on the experiences of ethnic and religious minority young people in Scotland who are mistaken for being Muslim, exploring their understandings of and responses to these encounters of misrecognition. Our study draws on a large qualitative dataset comprising 382 young people from diverse ethnic and religious backgrounds—including Sikh, Hindu, Black, and Caribbean youth—growing up across urban, suburban, and rural Scottish contexts. To capture these experiences, we conducted forty-five focus groups and 224 interviews. We analyze these encounters across a range of mediatized, geopoliticized, and educational spaces, specifically examining sites such as schools, taxis, airports, and other public spaces. Our methodological approach seeks to uncover how the homogenization of the "Asian" community, geopolitical events, and media representations construct participants as "Muslims," while simultaneously documenting the agency and creativity young people employ to navigate these situations through strategies such as humor, clarification, social withdrawal, or avoidance.
