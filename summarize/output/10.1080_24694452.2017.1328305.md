# 10.1080_24694452.2017.1328305

This study examines the shifting property access regimes in northeastern Maine, specifically focusing on how the restructuring of the timber industry—from vertically integrated forest products companies to institutional investor-owners—has threatened the state's "open lands" tradition. Using a case study of the Farm Cove Community Forest project in the town of Grand Lake Stream, I argue that financial actors generate profit by enclosing long-standing common access regimes to command monopoly rents. 

My methodological approach draws on two months of fieldwork, incorporating nineteen semistructured interviews with a diverse range of stakeholders, including land trust staff, sporting camp owners, timber economists, foresters, longtime residents, and representatives from the Passamaquoddy Tribe. To supplement these qualitative data, I analyzed parcel ownership data from the James W. Sewall Company, local press articles from the *Bangor Daily News* and *Portland Press Herald*, and archival resources and historical maps sourced from the Maine State Archives, Princeton Public Library, and the Woodland Pulp Mill. By synthesizing these sources, I examine two primary mechanisms of rent extraction: lease lots and working forest conservation easements, contextualizing these processes within broader geographical literatures on the commodification of nature, rent, and enclosure.
