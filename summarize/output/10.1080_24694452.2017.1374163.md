# 10.1080_24694452.2017.1374163

To explore innovation within the sparsely populated spatial divides of regional Australia, we employ a methodological approach that combines social network analysis (SNA) and econometric modeling. Our study focuses on the role of long-distance labor commuting as a potential interregional conduit for tacit knowledge and social capital. We utilize commuting data to map labor networks across local government areas (LGAs) and integrate this with patent data from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD) spanning the period from 2000 to 2013. To determine the drivers of innovative activity, we perform a regression analysis of these patent data against a selection of socioeconomic variables and SNA-derived commuting indexes. By analyzing these variables, we examine how population size, professional employment, and labor mobility influence innovation, specifically testing whether the movement of labor allows smaller peripheral communities to overcome the proximity barriers typically associated with low industry density and geographic isolation.
