# 10.1080_24694452.2017.1403879

This study employs an ethnographic research design conducted in Bemni, a rural village in the Chamoli district of Uttarakhand, north India. Drawing on seven months of fieldwork between March 2012 and March 2015, and building upon prior doctoral research from 2003–2004, I examine the economic, social, and political practices of marginalized youth. The study focuses specifically on educated but unemployed or underemployed individuals between the ages of sixteen and thirty who act as "mediators" or "advisors" in disputes regarding premarital elopements and extramarital affairs. 

Methodologically, I integrated a household census with participant observation and semistructured interviews. I interviewed forty-five of the fifty young people in the village who had completed at least a Tenth Class education and identified as unemployed or underemployed. These interviews explored key variables including educational and employment experiences, marriage, intimacy, and views on politics. By analyzing the narratives and practices of these youth within the specific socio-spatial context of rural Uttarakhand—a region characterized by agro-pastoralism and rigid caste and gender hierarchies—I investigate how these individuals deploy concepts of romantic love to resolve conflict and navigate social change.
