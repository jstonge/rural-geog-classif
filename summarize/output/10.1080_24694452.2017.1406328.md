# 10.1080_24694452.2017.1406328

We employ a political ecological perspective to examine how the spatial character of local institutions shapes access to basic services in the Kayes region of western Mali. To analyze the influence of changing demographic and human mobility patterns on decentralized governance, we integrate a diverse array of data sources, including archival records, census data spanning the twentieth century, field surveys, and cartographic data. Through a novel geospatial analysis, we measure disparities in access to safe drinking water across several geographic scales, specifically focusing on the legal and administrative distinction between officially recognized "administrative villages" and unrecognized "hamlets." We argue that these institutions function as historical-geographic formations, where historically higher population densities in the semiarid Sahelian zone—compared to the subhumid Sudanian zone—resulted in a higher concentration of recognized villages and smaller administrative territories. By modeling the impacts of these formations, we demonstrate how the permanent settlement of former frontier areas, where disease vectors historically maintained sparse and mobile populations, has created stark contemporary inequalities in public service provision.
