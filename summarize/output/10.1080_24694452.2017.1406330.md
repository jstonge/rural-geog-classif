# 10.1080_24694452.2017.1406330

Drawing on a political ecology framework and the tools of agrarian studies, I examine the emergence of a "climate change adaptation regime" in Bangladesh, specifically focusing on the coastal district of Khulna. My analysis is based on two years of fieldwork conducted between 2014 and 2015, during which I carried out over 150 interviews with representatives from NGOs, donors, government and UN agencies, and research organizations, as well as approximately 130 interviews with farmers and rural laborers in Khulna and twenty migrants who relocated to Kolkata, India. To supplement these primary data, I engaged in participatory research at international climate change conferences in Rotterdam, Paris, Kolkata, and Kuala Lumpur, and conducted a comprehensive review of government, NGO, and donor planning documents, newspaper reports, and other gray literature. By synthesizing these diverse sources, I analyze how the adaptation regime operates through the interrelated processes of imagination, experimentation, and dispossession. I argue that this regime reframes the ecological crisis as an opportunity for structural economic transformation, effectively governing the landscape and its people by presenting urbanization and export-led growth as inevitable futures for the rural poor.
