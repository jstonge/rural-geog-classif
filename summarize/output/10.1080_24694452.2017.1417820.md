# 10.1080_24694452.2017.1417820

To explore and explain rural-urban differences in gender ideologies, I employ a comparative ethnographic approach centered on Zambia, complemented by a geographically diverse review of international literature. By traversing the rural-urban spectrum, I seek to identify the distinctive attributes of the urban environment—specifically interconnectedness, heterogeneity, and population density—that may disrupt gender inequalities. My methodology distinguishes between internalized stereotypes and norm perceptions to better understand the causal relationship between social practices and individual behavior. I analyze how exposure to women performing socially valued, masculine roles in densely populated areas incrementally erodes traditional gender ideologies and increases flexibility in the division of labor. Additionally, I examine the role of spatial access to essential services, such as health clinics and police, in enhancing women's autonomy regarding fertility and protection against gender-based violence. This comparative framework allows me to contrast the experiences of residents in remote, agrarian localities with those in urban centers, thereby nuancing the drivers of social change and continuity in gender norms within the Zambian context.
