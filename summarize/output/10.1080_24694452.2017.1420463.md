# 10.1080_24694452.2017.1420463

We examine the role of settler irrigation systems and water governance in establishing and reinforcing imperial geographies of whiteness in Australia and the Western United States. Drawing on a hydrosocial perspective, we investigate the racialized sociospatial processes that bound whiteness to water during the foundational period of modernist water governance from 1880 to 1920. Our methodological approach centers on a comparative analysis of the lives and work of two influential figures—Alfred Deakin (1856–1919) in Australia and Elwood Mead (1858–1936) in the United States—who functioned as agents of science, technology, and politics. By analyzing their own writings and research, alongside biographies and secondary documentary sources, we trace how these men shaped and circulated transnational flows of knowledge regarding racial hierarchies and irrigation. We specifically look for the cultivation of a "white water citizenry" by applying a framework that identifies the marking of white as a location of social privilege, the cultivation of a white imaginary, and the naturalization of whiteness through dominant institutional values. Through this analysis, we illustrate how their hydro-imaginaries, endorsed by their respective governments, facilitated the dispossession of Indigenous peoples and the marginalization of non-white water users, particularly Asian migrants.
