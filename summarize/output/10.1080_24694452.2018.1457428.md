# 10.1080_24694452.2018.1457428

This study employs a qualitative approach to examine the social and ecological impacts of HIV/AIDS and the resulting relationships between citizens and the state in northeast South Africa. Drawing from an ongoing research project, we conducted fieldwork between May 2013 and June 2014, and again from January through June 2016. Our methodology integrates qualitative interviews with residents across three rural communities with ethnographic observations conducted at a rural primary care clinic. By combining these data sources, we document the ways in which residents and healthcare institutions manage HIV in the era of expanded access to antiretroviral therapy (ART). We specifically analyze the intersections of "therapeutic citizenship" with existing cultural norms, spatial economies, and historical systems of inequality. Key variables of interest include the role of nutrition and food access, the use of traditional medicine, and the distinct burdens placed on female-headed households. Through this political ecology of health framework, we evaluate how public health interventions and ART messaging map onto the uneven landscapes of rural South Africa, highlighting the mediation of health management by cultural, ecological, and political systems.
