# 10.1080_24694452.2018.1484685

Drawing on a historical political-ecological approach, I examine the long-standing conflict over resource control between a rural village, a national park, and an agro-industrial investment project in the Bagamoyo District of Tanzania's Coast Region. My analysis is grounded in ethnographic fieldwork conducted over 18 months between 2013 and 2016. To address the contradictions and irregularities encountered in the field, I triangulated data from multiple sources, including oral histories with local elders, semi-structured and unstructured interviews with villagers, state officials, and corporate actors, and direct participant observations within farms and village meetings. I further employed participatory community mapping and participatory photography—specifically photovoice and photoelicitation—alongside the analysis of archival materials and secondary documents. By integrating these diverse methods, I seek to privilege the lived histories and collective memories of local resource users. This methodological framework allows me to analyze "land-water" as a coupled resource and to explore the dialectic of postcolonial nation-building, specifically the tension between the state's drive for global capital accumulation through resource enclosure and its need for political legitimation among the rural majority.
