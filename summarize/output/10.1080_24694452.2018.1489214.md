# 10.1080_24694452.2018.1489214

This article interrogates the concept of technical memory within smart city systems, challenging retentional accounts that view memory as a static archive or trace. Drawing on the work of Garcia, I theorize technical memory as a form of "artificial comprehension," wherein smart systems are designed to disclose specific qualities of objects for particular purposes, thereby shaping how users perceive and respond to their environments. To demonstrate this approach, I analyze the UK’s Automatic Urban and Rural Network (AURN) air pollution monitoring system. I examine the technical processes by which AURN stations artificially comprehend pollutants—such as the use of chemiluminescence to measure nitrogen dioxide—and further explore how this data is subsequently re-comprehended and presented through two smartphone applications, Plume and London Air. By analyzing the differing logics these apps employ to make air pollution intelligible, I argue that artificial comprehension provides a productive framework for geographers to understand the digital geographies of smart and non-smart technical systems beyond the dominant narratives of corporate surveillance and dataveillance.
