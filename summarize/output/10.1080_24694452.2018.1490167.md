# 10.1080_24694452.2018.1490167

I examine the limitations of payment for ecosystem services (PES) carbon forestry programs in alleviating rural poverty and mitigating climate change by focusing on the Scolel'te program in Chiapas, Mexico. Utilizing a commodity chains framework, I analyze how "market risk"—defined as the shifting supply and demand dynamics within the global carbon credit economy—influences the governance of the program and the experiences of its participants. My data were collected between August 2011 and August 2012 through a combination of participant observation and semistructured interviews. I conducted participant observation with program managers from the managing NGO, AMBIO, attending training meetings and accompanying managers on community visits to understand their decision-making processes. These observations were supplemented by a small-group discussion with program directors and managers, as well as four separate interviews with leadership. To capture the on-the-ground experience of carbon credit producers, I engaged in participant observation and conducted over twenty-five semistructured interviews with enrolled farmers in two communities within the western Sierra Madre region. By linking these local experiences to broader trends in the voluntary carbon market, I illustrate how the relative power positions of program managers and their responses to diminished international demand produced unintended social consequences and led to farmer attrition.
