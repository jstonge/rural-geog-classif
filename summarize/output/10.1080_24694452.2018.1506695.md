# 10.1080_24694452.2018.1506695

Through a political ecology framework attentive to culture, discourse, and history, I examine how resource extraction is mobilized within "extractive populism" to reinforce cultural, geographic, and class divides. I focus on the emblematic case of the rural Iron Range mining region in northern Minnesota, analyzing the recent rightward political shift of this historically Democratic stronghold in relation to the micropolitics of proposed copper mines. My methodological approach combines ethnographic fieldwork conducted between 2015 and 2017—including immersive residency in Ely, Minnesota, and observations of daily life, public events, and pro-mining rallies—with twenty-nine in-depth interviews with local community and political leaders, workers, and residents. To supplement these qualitative data, I performed a discourse analysis of social media, mining advocacy websites, and local and state newspaper coverage spanning from 2004 to 2017. By synthesizing interviews, textual data, and field notes, I identify emergent themes—such as the dichotomy of insiders versus outsiders, resource nationalism, and the renewal of a way of life—to explore how place-based identities and racialized, gendered emotions motivate support for authoritarian populism and the reproduction of extractive capitalism.
