# 10.1080_24694452.2018.1525272

This article employs a visual discourse analysis of a set of peopleless photographs taken in 2006 depicting a falling home in the village of Shishmaref, Alaska. These images, which have become "place-images" widely circulated in global media and at international climate summits like COP22, serve as the primary data source for investigating the hidden narratives and performative politics of Arctic climate change reporting. The author situates the study within the village of Shishmaref, an Inupiat Native Alaskan community on Sarichef Island facing severe shoreline erosion and relocation. 

To analyze the work done by rural ruination, the author adopts a theoretical framework that bridges contemporary scholarship on postmodern urban decay in U.S. Rust Belt cities—specifically the "ruin porn" aesthetic of Detroit—with theories of pastoral landscape representation and postcolonial photography. Using a Barthesian semiotic approach, the author deconstructs the photographs through three specific readings: the tension between the romantic aestheticization of decay and active socioenvironmental engagement; the "return to nature" as a commentary on human-nature relationships in the Anthropocene; and the disordering of normative conceptions of time and materiality. Through this methodology, the paper probes whether the visual contract between the spectator and the absent victim transcends empathy to call for restorative action.
