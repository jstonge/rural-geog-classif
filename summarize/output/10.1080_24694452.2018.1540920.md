# 10.1080_24694452.2018.1540920

To explore the impacts of future extreme events on vulnerable rural populations in the Northeastern United States, we employed a methodology that integrates climate projections with socioeconomic data. We defined the study area as 14 Northeastern states and the District of Columbia, focusing specifically on rural counties (populations under 250,000) and those in the bottom income quartile. To quantify climate hazards, we utilized gridded temperature and precipitation observations (LI2013) for a historical baseline (1976–2005) and modified these using four global climate models (GCMs) from the CMIP5 ensemble to project conditions for the future period (2046–2075). We analyzed two greenhouse gas emissions scenarios—RCP 4.5 (moderate) and RCP 8.5 (high)—focusing on four key variables: annual maximum daily maximum temperature, annual minimum daily minimum temperature, annual maximum daily precipitation, and annual minimum two-week summer precipitation. These projections were then coupled with county-level population and relative income estimates for 2060, derived from the Integrated Climate and Land Use Scenarios (ICLUS) program and the U.S. Census Bureau. By combining these datasets, we calculated changes in "extreme person-events" to identify where substantial increases in climate hazards overlap with high socioeconomic vulnerability.
