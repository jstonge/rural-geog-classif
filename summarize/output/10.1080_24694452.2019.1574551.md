# 10.1080_24694452.2019.1574551

We examine perceptions of living and working with toxic pollution in two periurban petrochemical areas in Nanjing, China, a heavily polluted megacity on the Yangtze River. Our research focuses on Nanjing as an illustrative and exemplary case study due to its status as a leading national petrochemical base and the notable absence of widespread environmental protests despite high levels of industrial concentration. We employ a nested case study design, focusing on the Nanjing Chemical Industry Park in the northeast and a second industrial area south of the Yangtze River. 

Our methodological approach draws on twenty-five semistructured interviews and participant observation conducted during two fieldwork trips in 2016. We engaged a diverse range of participants, including current and retired petrochemical workers, local residents, migrant workers, shop owners, taxi drivers, village leaders, and government officials, as well as NGO representatives, lawyers, and researchers. By extending the conceptual framework of the "hidden injuries of class," we analyze the cumulative effects of social and environmental inequalities. We specifically investigate how these "accumulated injuries of environmental injustice" manifest through intersecting effects on health and well-being, epistemic injustices regarding toxic exposures, inadequate compensation for environmental harm, and collective political powerlessness.
