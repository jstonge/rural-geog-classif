# 10.1080_24694452.2019.1587285

We report on a research project examining emergent urban formations in India, specifically focusing on the rural-urban transition where geographically dispersed urban growth often escapes official measurement. Our methodological approach combines observations "from above" and "from below" to address the conceptual and empirical deficiencies in current urban data. We utilize a critical reading of detailed Indian census and government data, complemented by two extended periods of fieldwork conducted in northeastern India (West Bengal and Bihar) between February and May of 2017 and 2018. To operationalize this analysis, we developed a geographic information system (GIS) for West Bengal and Bihar to track the emergence of "census towns" (CTs)—small settlements defined by population size, density, and non-agrarian employment structures. By linking 80,000 administrative census units with primary data on population and employment, we analyze the lower echelons of the urban hierarchy. This dual-pronged methodology allows us to test hypotheses regarding the nature of in situ urban transformation and to develop a preliminary typology of emergent urban formations that challenges traditional, agglomeration-based urban theories.
