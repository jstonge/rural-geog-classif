# 10.1080_24694452.2019.1638750

Drawing on ethnographic research conducted in Bayanhongor province between 2012 and 2014, with follow-up visits in 2017 and 2018, I examine contemporary state-making practices at the subdistrict level in rural Mongolia. My approach shifts the analytical focus away from the "retreat of the state" discourse toward an "analytics of government," focusing on how the state is invoked and made visible through everyday interactions between local government administrators and pastoralist households. I specifically analyze the implementation of two government initiatives: the distribution of a State Herder diploma, medal, and stamp, and the use of a government-herder communication notebook. Utilizing a conceptual framework grounded in governmentality studies, performativity theory, and the lens of improvisation, I treat the state not as a stable, reified object but as a performative effect produced through relational and material practices. By employing ethnographic vignettes, I illustrate how administrators and herders creatively improvise within existing constraints, leveraging historical socialist legacies and cultural norms of obligation to negotiate political membership and responsibility in the 2010s.
