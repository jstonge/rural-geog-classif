# 10.1080_24694452.2019.1638753

We provide a framework for systematically identifying unusually devastating tornadoes (UDTs) in the United States, defined as events where the observed number of casualties substantially exceeds the predicted rate. We fit a log-linear regression model to the casualty counts of all casualty-producing tornadoes occurring between 1995 and 2016. Our model expands upon previous research by utilizing energy dissipation (tornado power) and population density as primary predictors, while incorporating the number of mobile homes and the year of occurrence as fixed effects, and the month and hour of occurrence as random effects. Data were sourced from the Storm Prediction Center’s historical tornado database, with population and mobile home estimates derived from U.S. Census Bureau and American Community Survey data using a dasymetric approach at the tract level. By examining the residuals of this model, we identify UDTs as those falling within the 90th percentile of underprediction. We then analyze the geographic distribution of these events across the United States and synthesize the socioeconomic and demographic profiles of six specific communities affected by seven example UDTs to highlight place-based vulnerabilities.
