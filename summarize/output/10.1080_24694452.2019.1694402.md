# 10.1080_24694452.2019.1694402

To characterize the "smart divide" as an emerging form of social inequality, we conduct a multidisciplinary literature review drawing from geography, electrical engineering, and rural studies. We redefine the smart divide—distinguishing it from the traditional digital divide—as a qualitative disparity rooted in the imbalanced penetration and maturity of smart infrastructure and smart applications and services (SASs), alongside intellectual and skill gaps among communities. Our methodological approach focuses on the technological dimensions of this divide within the context of smart cities and communities (SCCs), specifically identifying gaps between urban and rural America. We analyze the evolution of ICT infrastructure across three layers—computing, networking, and software—and evaluate how the requirements of the "SCC age," such as big data capabilities and 5G deployment, create systemic barriers for decentralized rural regions. By synthesizing these technological origins and the specific vulnerabilities of rural areas, we identify potential gaps in infrastructure and services to culminate in a synthesis of strategies aimed at bridging the smart divide in rural America.
