# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1723396

Drawing upon a feminist political ecology (FPE) framework, I conducted this qualitative study to expose gender-based violence as a hidden cost of the socioenvironmental network surrounding the Sariska Tiger Reserve in Rajasthan, India. My research focused on a 10-km buffer around the reserve, utilizing a spatially stratified approach across four quadrants to capture varied histories of tiger removal, reintroduction, and dispersal. Between 2014 and 2017, I facilitated fifty-two focus groups involving 416 participants; while initial mixed-gender groups were dominated by male voices, I subsequently implemented twenty women-only focus groups to create a safe space for women to disclose experiences regarding household dynamics and risk. To supplement these data, I conducted forty interviews, twelve walking tours with women during resource collection, and over 300 hours of participant observation while volunteering with wildlife rescuers. By employing criterion and opportunistic sampling and stratifying participants by age to capture generational shifts in perception, I examined the intersections of patriarchal power relations, gendered divisions of labor, and human-wildlife conflict. This methodology allowed me to analyze how the reintroduction of apex predators interacts with restrictive social hierarchies to produce embodied consequences and psychological costs for women.
