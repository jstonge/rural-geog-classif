# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1723397

We examine how migrant-sending families in Southeast Asian "source" countries—specifically Indonesia and the Philippines—sustain family life and negotiate the well-being of left-behind children during the long-term absence of one or both parents. Our analysis is grounded in a conceptual framework of care temporalities, utilizing the temporal concepts of rhythm, rupture, and reversal to explore how modalities of care intersect with gendered power geometries. We first analyze how routinized care rhythms centered on mothers naturalize traditional understandings of family well-being, then explore the ruptures to these rhythms triggered when mothers migrate as breadwinners, and finally examine the conditions under which gender role reversals become enduring or are undone upon return migration. This study is based primarily on research conducted in 2017 focusing on rural households in Indonesia and the Philippines. Our methodological approach employed paired life story interviews, conducted with children and their corresponding parental or nonparental adult caregivers, to capture the intersecting temporal experiences of those navigating the "transnational stretch" of the family.
