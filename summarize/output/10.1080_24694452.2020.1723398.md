# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1723398

Adopting an explicitly political-ecological perspective, we examine the consequences of the rise of nonstate conservancies in the Maasai Mara region of southern Kenya. Our analysis is based on fieldwork conducted in 2017 and 2018, including a joint expedition in November 2018 and earlier research by the second author. We purposively sampled five conservancies—Olarro, Naboisho, Siana, Mara North, and Pardamat—to capture a spectrum of performance, ranging from documented "worst case" community conflicts to award-winning "best case" outcomes. Data collection involved in-depth interviews with fifty-three local residents and landowners, twenty key informant interviews with ecotourism investors, camp managers, government officials, and civil society personnel, and participant observation at three lease negotiation meetings. These primary data were supplemented by an extensive review of institutional frameworks, business annual reports, management plans, and conservancy lease agreements. Through this approach, we analyze the interplay between donor- and investor-driven environmental crisis narratives and the material reconfiguration of land use, focusing on variables such as lease payment values, grazing rights, and the capture of differential ground rents to theorize the process of rural gentrification within the conservancy frontier.
