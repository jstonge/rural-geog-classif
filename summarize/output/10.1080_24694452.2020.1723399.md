# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1723399

I reveal the connections between forests, neoliberalism, authoritarianism, and cronyism through an in-depth ethnographic study of the Romanian Carpathian forests following the fall of socialism in 1989. Drawing on the analytical tools of political ecology and a conceptual framework that integrates studies of resource frontiers and political forests with the anthropology of postsocialism, I examine the entanglements between forest extraction, party politics, and informal territorial governance. My research focuses on the village of Tulnici in the Vrancea Mountains, utilizing a narrative ethnographic writing approach to analyze the role of the "timber baron" in maintaining provincial authoritarian control. I draw on extensive field research conducted between 2004 and 2016, during which I collected data through systematic fieldwork, including over 300 recorded interviews and a database of 342 surveys. For the specific case of Tulnici, I utilize forty interviews and thirty-seven household surveys conducted in 2004, 2007, and 2016. These primary data are complemented by the analysis of official reports, video recordings, and media coverage from regional and national presses published between 2011 and 2019.
