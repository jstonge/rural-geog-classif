# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1727307

We apply a novel mixed-methods approach to examine whether the benefits of wildfire risk reduction created through hazardous fuels reduction (HFR) on federal forest lands were equitably distributed among nearby populations. Our study focuses on twelve national forests across four western U.S. states, specifically targeting dry mixed-conifer forests where the U.S. Forest Service (USFS) conducts vegetation treatments to mitigate megafire risks. To address the challenges of analyzing dispersed rural populations, we employ a quantitative pattern analysis—building on a method we previously developed—to identify correlations between the spatial distribution of HFR activity and the concentration of EJ-protected populations (low-income and minority residents). Beyond general associations, we analyze local areas to determine if EJ-protected populations are disproportionately excluded from these risk-reduction benefits. We complement this spatial analysis with qualitative interviews with forest managers to investigate how environmental justice is factored into decision-making processes regarding the implementation of HFR and the extent to which EJ populations engage in collaborative project planning. Through this integration of quantitative geographical analysis and practitioner interviews, we evaluate agency compliance with the Executive Order on Environmental Justice.
