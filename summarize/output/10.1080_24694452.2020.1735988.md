# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1735988

Drawing on an empirical case study of the *Fondo para la Protección del Agua* (FONAG), a water fund payments for ecosystem services (PES) arrangement in the Ecuadorian highlands, I examine how territorial claims are constructed and maintained through the labor of local intermediaries. To illuminate the processes enabling water funds to operate and the dynamics of power central to territorialization, I employ a qualitative methodological approach utilizing data collected between 2012 and 2014. My data sources consist of archival documents and materials from FONAG and its collaborating organizations, key informant interviews conducted with FONAG administrators and *páramo* guards (PGs), and participant observation of a two-day PG training workshop held in October 2013. I specifically focus on the labor of the PGs—local residents employed as brokers between the urban-based fund and rural Andean communities—analyzing their dual role in patrolling high-elevation *páramo* ecosystems "above" and recruiting collective conservation labor from neighbors "below." By analyzing these practices through the lens of governmentality, I investigate how the guards' position as both agents of neoliberal governance and environmental subjects contributes to the production and circulation of value necessary for the commodification of ecosystem services.
