# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1747971

Drawing on fieldwork conducted over fifteen months between January 2016 and August 2017, I examine the social and spatial contexts of social reproduction among women in rural food-producing businesses and organizations in Jordan. Focusing on the traditional agricultural regions of Irbid, Ajloun, Jerash, and Salt, I employed a qualitative approach combining participant observation and semi-structured interviews. I conducted several months of participant observation within one women's charitable organization—engaging in activities such as packaging herbs, writing grants, and selling products—and attended various trainings and markets. These experiences facilitated one-on-one and group interviews with at least forty women from diverse educational and class backgrounds, as well as a small number of development agency employees and spouses. Interviews were conducted primarily in Arabic, with a native-speaking research assistant assisting in the process to ensure cultural nuance and efficiency. I utilized MAXQDA to code Arabic and English transcripts, identifying recurring themes related to gender, rural life, and organizational structures. By framing food production as social reproduction rather than through the lens of Western-centric empowerment, I analyze how these women navigate development networks and marketize domestic labor to provide for their families.
