# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1747972

We employ a case study approach focusing on two pioneering rural areas in France to examine how the fight against light pollution reconfigures regional development and planning. Our methodology is grounded in a transdisciplinary framework that pairs the research traditions of social geography with the ecosystemic approaches of experimental sciences, combining constructivist and realist epistemologies. We analyze the process of "resource specification"—the intentional transformation of darkness into a place-based resource—by identifying three distinct rationales: an anthropocentric utilitarian approach (economicization of the environment), an ecocentric conservationist approach (ecologization of the economy), and an integrated socio-ecosystemic approach. Drawing on the sociology of controversies and actor-network theory, we investigate the "specification controversies" that emerge between these rationales, treating these rural areas as incubation zones for sustainable innovation. We specifically examine how the interplay between the energy transition and biodiversity preservation creates a "place-based transition operator"—manifested as the *trame noire* (dark infrastructure) or the nocturnal landscape—which enables local authorities to redefine their development trajectories and mediate global environmental changes within a local context.
