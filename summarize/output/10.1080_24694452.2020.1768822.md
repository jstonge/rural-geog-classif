# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1768822

We propose "centaur VGI," a hybrid human-machine approach designed to mitigate global mapping inequalities, particularly in remote and rural regions of the Global South that are often overlooked by traditional volunteered geographic information (VGI) efforts. Our methodology combines the spatial cognitive abilities and object recognition skills of human volunteers with the speed and efficiency of machine learning (ML) to automate feature extraction from remotely sensed imagery. By integrating these two agents, we aim to create a synergy that overcomes common VGI limitations, such as volunteer motivation gaps and the "map seeding" effect, where a lack of initial data discourages further contributions. We describe a conceptual model for this hybrid system and situate our argument within a case study of the Acholi subregion of northern Uganda. This area serves as a critical test site due to its status as an inadequately mapped region where we have provided outreach health care services since 2017. Through this approach, we seek to address the political and technological factors that produce disparities in map coverage, quality, and currency.
