# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1774351

We empirically investigated the socioenvironmental transformations of eastern Montana's Bakken oil and gas region to advance critical urban studies literature on "planetary urbanization." Specifically, we sought to chronicle the diverging political subjectivities of the region's inhabitants as they navigate the shift from a productive agricultural urban rurality to an extractive resource urban rurality. To achieve this, we employed a mixed-methods, multiyear research project focusing on the six-county Bakken region in Montana. Our methodological approach integrated qualitative and quantitative analyses, utilizing semistructured interviews with local residents alongside a regional survey to deepen our understanding of the social ontologies pervading life in the Bakken. By weaving together these data sources, we interrogated Lefebvre’s notion of the "blind field" to determine how personal hardships and firsthand experiences of industrial production influence whether local exploitation is accepted as a necessity or rejected. This approach allowed us to examine the "everyday struggles" of those living within an industrial hinterland, treating the region as a site of extended urbanization where the urban-rural binary is rendered a relic category.
