# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1806027

This article employs a comparative and theoretical approach to examine the motivations and methodologies for research replication within geography and its cognate disciplines, specifically medicine, psychology, economics, and criminology. The author begins with a historical case study of Hudson’s rural settlement theory, utilizing a personal replication attempt in Saskatchewan (1882–1971) to contrast with Hudson's original Iowa study (1870s–1960) via point pattern analysis. To establish a framework for replication and reproducibility (R&R), the author reviews disciplinary strategies, highlighting the adoption of systematic review protocols such as PRISMA and STROBE in medical research and the use of formal meta-analyses across the MPECG disciplines. 

The methodological discussion focuses on two primary tools for geographical replication: meta-analysis and geographically weighted models. The author details a case study of a meta-analysis on Rift Valley fever virus risk factors, which utilized six databases, odds ratios (OR) as a measure of association, and StatsDirect software to calculate pooled effects estimates and $I^2$ statistics for heterogeneity. Additionally, the author presents geographically weighted regression (GWR) as a "replication machine" for testing theories across space, illustrated by a project on media influence in Peruvian elections (2004–2007) using adaptive spatial filters to analyze the relationship between election results and socio-economic variables.
