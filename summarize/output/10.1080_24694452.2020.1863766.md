# 10.1080_24694452.2020.1863766

To investigate how ecology in China has been repurposed to articulate green modernization and state power, I employ a historical and epistemological approach that situates scientific knowledge production within specific political and global contexts. Moving beyond diffusionist accounts that view science as a singular body of knowledge emanating from the West, I historicize the emergence of "eco-developmental logics" by analyzing the writings, global engagements, and scientific contributions of key Chinese intellectuals, earth systems scientists, and ecological economists from the Republican period (1912–1949) through the early twenty-first century. 

My analysis traces the conceptual evolution of ecology through a series of intellectual ruptures: beginning with the Japanese roots of the term *shengtai*, moving to Li Dazhao’s early Marxist theorizations of the peasantry as a revolutionary vanguard, and concluding with the reform-era shift toward viewing the rural population as a backward force impeding modernization. I specifically examine the "social-economi-ecological complex systems theory" of Ma Shijun and Wang Rusong, as well as the industrial ecology models of Ye Qianji and Pan Jiahua. By weaving together these diverse intellectual threads, I demonstrate how the production of ecological science provided the epistemological foundation for the state-led technocratic practices of socioenvironmental engineering and the legitimization of "ecological civilization building."
