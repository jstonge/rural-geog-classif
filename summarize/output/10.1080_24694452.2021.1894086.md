# 10.1080_24694452.2021.1894086

To examine whether overseas military service predicted the postwar occupational mobility of rural Americans, I employ a quantitative approach using a novel linked dataset of big historical microdata. Focusing on North Dakota, I constructed a longitudinal dataset by linking the state's World War I military roster—containing records for over 30,000 servicemembers—with the 1930 U.S. Census and county-level aggregate data from the 1910 agricultural census. By geocoding registration and entry locations to county FIPS codes, I associated individuals with specific prewar contextual characteristics, including average farm value, the percentage of tenant-operated farms, and the urban proportion of the county population. 

I operationalized occupational mobility by condensing roster and census occupations into categories (farming, white collar, blue collar, and none) using a dictionary derived from IPUMS OCC1950 codes. My analytical strategy progressed from logistic models predicting the likelihood of overseas service to a series of regression models analyzing postwar occupational change. I first utilized a univariate model to test the contemporary perception that overseas service encouraged farm leaving, subsequently developing increasingly complex models to determine how civilian individual characteristics and contextual variables, and their interactions, significantly predicted the likelihood of individuals exiting agriculture.
