# 10.1080_24694452.2021.1907172

We investigate the nature of information sharing regarding missing persons in rural Sweden by adopting a platial perspective to geographical information, moving beyond traditional geometric $x,y$ coordinates to focus on relational and semantic connections. Our methodology employs a combination of qualitative and quantitative techniques, underpinned by Geographical Information Systems (GIS) and visualization tools. We focus on three specific cases of missing persons, analyzing a dataset of 1,104 scraped tweets alongside conventional media archives to compare the frequency and nature of coverage across different platforms. 

Our approach first maps the temporal and spatial distribution of social media engagement to uncover broad patterns of information spread. We then apply a platial lens to evaluate the relational nature of the data, examining the specific content of posts to distinguish between informational and emotional support over time. Finally, we employ a relational approach to reconceptualize policing as a networked activity, analyzing how civil society and voluntary organizations enroll as actors within emergency service networks. By focusing on low-population-density contexts, we critically evaluate the added value of social media as a problem-solving resource in rural emergency services compared to traditional media and large-scale disaster responses.
