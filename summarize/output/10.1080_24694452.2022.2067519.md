# 10.1080_24694452.2022.2067519

We explore the social impacts of agricultural skill development programs in the state of Himachal Pradesh, India, specifically examining how the acquisition of new skills may challenge discriminatory gender and caste norms. Drawing on critical capabilities theory and a geographically sensitive approach to power relations, we investigate the conditions under which "transgressive skills" can expand positive freedoms and drive progressive social change. Our data were collected between late 2018 and early 2020 through two rounds of interviews and participant observation. The first round involved 53 trainees at training sites—focusing on organic farming, mushroom cultivation, dairy, and poultry—to explore their backgrounds and aspirations. A second round of 31 follow-up interviews and ethnographic visits to farms and villages were conducted approximately one year later to assess the long-term impact of these skills on the trainees' lives and communities. While most participants deployed skills in normative ways, we focused our analysis on eight individuals who experienced social backlash due to their transgressions. From this group, we selected four detailed vignettes—representing a high-caste man, a low-caste man, a high-caste woman, and an intermediate-caste woman—to comparatively analyze how intersecting positions of privilege and marginality shape the nature of transgression and its subsequent social consequences.
