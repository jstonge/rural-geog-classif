Drawing upon the convergence of cultural and political ecology, we employ a multi-methodological approach to examine the disjuncture between global environmental discourses and local biophysical realities in the northern savanna of Côte d'Ivoire. Our research focuses on two contrasting rural communities in the Korhogo region—Katiali, a low-density settlement, and Tagbanga, a densely settled zone—to analyze patterns of environmental and social change. To confront the data gaps inherent in the World Bank’s National Environmental Action Plan (NEAP), we integrate sociocultural and biophysical data. We conducted household surveys with Senufo and Jula farmers and focus-group discussions with Fulbe pastoralists to record local perceptions of environmental change. These qualitative insights were triangulated with quantitative land-cover analysis using aerial photographs, GIS mapping, and vegetation transects utilizing the contact-point method. Finally, we conducted interviews with government officials, NGO representatives, and World Bank personnel to illuminate the institutional interests and received ideas shaping the NEAP process. By contrasting these diverse data sources, we aim to evaluate the accuracy of the "desertification" narrative and address the policy implications of ignoring actual versus imagined environmental problems at different spatial scales.
