# 10.1111_0004-5608.00218

To explore the relationship between metaphysical frameworks and rural agricultural systems, I employed a hybrid methodology combining qualitative texts and quantitative data. Moving beyond the individual as the unit of analysis, I focused on the community to better capture shared meanings and social boundaries. The study area comprised five farm communities in Iowa—Hull, Wellsburg, Wayland, Paullina, and Lamoni—selected for their strong commitments to specific theological positions (Dutch Reformed, German Reformed, German Mennonite, Quaker, and RLDS, respectively). All selected communities had populations under three thousand and economies dominated by farming.

Data collection involved three primary methods. First, I organized two gender-segregated discussion groups of farmers and spouses within each community, using narratives of farming dilemmas to elicit responses. These transcripts were analyzed via content analysis, validated by two independent raters and a test group. Second, participants completed questionnaires providing demographic and farm data, alongside a ranking of thirty value statements designed to discriminate between key variables. Finally, I utilized these data to examine the conceptualization of society along a scale from individualistic to communitarian. This approach allowed me to test the hypothesis that distinct worldviews, grounded in metaphysical commitments, are associated with specific social practices and discernible differences in land use, social capital, and agricultural agency.
