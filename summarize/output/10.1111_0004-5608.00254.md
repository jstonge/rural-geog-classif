# 10.1111_0004-5608.00254

I present an in-depth case study of elementary school accessibility within three predominantly rural counties in West Virginia. Utilizing an extensive dataset of distances between student resident locations (at the block level) and eighty-four elementary school locations, I examine whether the distribution of travel costs is equitable. The methodological approach focuses on revealing the spatial patterning of school accessibility to determine if it conforms to a nonrandom, logical spatial pattern or if substantial spatial inequities exist. I further investigate social equity by analyzing the relationship between accessibility and the socioeconomic status (SES) of resident populations, testing for patterned versus unpatterned inequality. Finally, I extend the analysis to evaluate the effect of spatial access on student achievement, specifically utilizing third-grade test scores as a key variable to determine if distance to school has a significant and inversely related effect on academic performance. This empirical investigation involves the digitization of attendance zone boundaries to shed light on the role of spatial equity in the context of rural school consolidation.
