# 10.1111_0033-0124.00131

Using a vulnerability conceptual framework, we examine the changing geography of HIV/AIDS infection in Ghana from 1986 through 1995 to explain the country's distinct epidemiological patterns. We utilize national AIDS data reported by the Ghana AIDS Control Program (GACP) and the World Health Organization (WHO) to calculate cumulative incidence rates for each of Ghana's ten administrative regions. To supplement these figures and address potential underreporting in rural areas, we incorporate HIV/AIDS surveillance and seroprevalence data from the Health Studies Branch of the United States Census Bureau, specifically focusing on infection rates among pregnant women as surrogates for the general population. Sociodemographic variables, including urbanization and polygamy rates, are derived from the 1993 Ghana Demographic and Health Survey (GDHS) and the Ghana Living Standards Survey (GLSS 3). We employ Spearman rank correlation coefficients to analyze the relationship between formal polygamy and infection rates. By synthesizing these data sources, we analyze the spatial distribution of the virus to determine whether diffusion follows a hierarchical or contagious pattern and evaluate the role of commercial sex work and cultural practices in shaping the female-male sex ratio and regional variations in prevalence.
