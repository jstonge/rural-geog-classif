# 10.1111_0033-0124.00208

We examine how the concepts of rural and urban are interpreted and applied within the western United States by analyzing both the perceptions of county commissioners and county-level census data. Our study area encompasses eleven western states, utilizing a modified stratified random design to sample 139 counties to ensure a representative spectrum of county types. We employed a mixed-methods approach, beginning with a mail survey of 512 commissioners using Dillman's total design method. Respondents rated their home counties on an urban-rural continuum (1 = most rural, 10 = most urban) and provided qualitative justifications via open-ended questions. We further supplemented this with personal and phone interviews with 51 respondents to assess the relative importance of five key attributes: total population, population concentration, growth and development, the presence of industry, and changes in the agricultural land base.

Our analysis proceeded in three phases: first, we examined the qualitative and quantitative attributes commissioners used to characterize their counties; second, we used multiple regression analysis to correlate these perceptions with census variables; and third, we applied cluster analysis techniques to census variables to identify patterns of rurality and urbanness. This methodology allowed us to identify statistically significant variables—specifically population concentration, total population, and the agricultural land base—and categorize counties into five distinct types.
