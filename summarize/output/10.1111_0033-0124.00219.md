# 10.1111_0033-0124.00219

I use a gender framework to examine the health-seeking behavior of women infected with sexually transmitted diseases (STDs) and reproductive tract infections (RTIs) in India. To map the social networks women utilize to discover and resolve these health problems, I implement a mixed-methods approach combining ethnographic information with Q-analysis. Data were collected over an eight-month period (August 1994–March 1995) through group discussions, interviews, and questionnaires administered to a purposive sample of 172 rural and 37 urban females. The study area encompasses four districts: Faridabad (rural), Haora (rural), North Arcot-Ambedkar (rural and urban), and Pune (urban). 

Due to the stigma surrounding STDs, I identified participants through three primary subgroups: medical records from government hospitals and STD/AIDS clinics, referrals from social workers and block nurses, and local practitioners, including both registered doctors and indigenous healers (such as *vaids* and *hakims*). Using STARPACK software, I applied Q-analysis to map the relationships between these women and four key systems of their social networks: kinship, belief, traditional medical, and Western medical services. This methodology allows for the exploration of how gender, location, and socio-economic status influence the geographic dimensions of these networks and the subsequent choices women make in seeking treatment.
