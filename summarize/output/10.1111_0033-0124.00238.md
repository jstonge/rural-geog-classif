# 10.1111_0033-0124.00238

To analyze the spatial ramifications of economic reforms in China, we conduct a disaggregated empirical study of Jiangsu province, a coastal region undergoing dramatic economic and spatial restructuring. We employ a conceptual framework of "developments from above, below and outside" to identify and evaluate the roles of three primary agents of regional growth: the state, local agents, and foreign investors. Our methodology integrates the analysis of comprehensive county-level statistics provided by the Jiangsu Statistics Bureau with qualitative data synthesized from interviews with local officials and researchers. By focusing on subprovincial data, we aim to reveal intraprovincial inequalities—specifically among cities and counties—that are often concealed in studies using larger spatial aggregates. We examine the interplay between state-led macropolicies (development from above), the growth of rural industrial enterprises and township-village enterprises (development from below), and the impact of foreign investment in state-designated open zones (development from outside). Through this approach, we assess how these agents jointly shape the economic landscape and influence the historical gap between northern (Subei) and southern (Sunan) Jiangsu.
