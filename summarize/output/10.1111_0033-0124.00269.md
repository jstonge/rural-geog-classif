# 10.1111_0033-0124.00269

This study employs a case study approach to analyze the spatial transformation of urban-rural interaction in China's Pearl River Delta (PRD), utilizing the region as a natural laboratory to test the Ginsburg-McGee model. We integrated documentary research and field investigations conducted between 1980 and 1997, supplemented by national and provincial statistical yearbooks. For the quantitative analysis, we focused on the period from 1980 to 1990 to utilize a comprehensive, county-level dataset measured at constant 1980 prices, thereby avoiding inflationary distortion. 

To identify and delineate the zone of urban-rural interaction across thirty-one cities (*shiqu*) and counties, we implemented a two-step statistical procedure. First, we conducted a principal components analysis (PCA) to extract condensed components from a suite of eight economic and demographic variables, including population density, percent urban and temporary population, per capita income, export output, employment rate, and per capita cultivated land. Second, we applied cluster analysis to classify these spatial units based on their loadings on the extracted components. This methodology allowed us to test hypotheses regarding the existence of a distinct peri-urban zone, its intermediate functional role between central cities and the periphery, and its impact on regional economic inequality.
