# 10.1111_0033-0124.00273

This study critically assesses the prospects of "productive conservation" in the Brazilian Amazon by evaluating its capacity to improve rural livelihoods while conserving tropical forests. I employ a case study approach focusing on the promotion of beekeeping among colonist farmers in the municipality of Campo Alto, Rondonia. The analysis centers on the implementation of beekeeping within the World Bank-funded Rondonia Natural Resources Management Project (PLANAFLORO), specifically examining the Community Initiative Support Projects (PAICs) which provided grants to local cooperatives. 

To evaluate the human and environmental dynamics of this initiative, I examine the socioeconomic and ecological characteristics of honey production in the region. The methodological framework involves situating the promotion of beekeeping within the broader historical context of Rondonian development—from the infrastructure projects of the 1960s and the POLONOROESTE program of the 1980s to the subsequent "greener" reforms of the 1990s. Key variables analyzed include the impact of donor-funded grants on the financial sustainability of beekeeping cooperatives, the resulting changes in household income, and the ecological relationship between the introduced Africanized honeybee (*Apis mellifera scutellata*) and the deforested landscapes of Rondonia. Through this case study, I evaluate whether the practice of beekeeping aligns with the stated goals of productive conservation or if it creates dependencies on external funding and relies on temporary environmental degradation.
