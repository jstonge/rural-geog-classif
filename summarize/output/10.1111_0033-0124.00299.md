# 10.1111_0033-0124.00299

To examine the spatial patterns of population growth and income migration in the rural Mountain West, we employed a data-clustering approach to differentiate between "Old West" extractive economies and "New West" amenity-based economies. We defined the study area as 246 rural (nonmetropolitan) counties within the U.S. Mountain Census Division, utilizing rural/urban codes from Butler and Beale (1994). To identify the New West, we performed a k-means cluster analysis using SPSS, utilizing continuous variables from the Economic Research Service (ERS) county typology—including farming, mining, manufacturing, government, and services—alongside the ERS natural amenity index and recreation-based economy measures from Beale and Johnson (1998). 

After establishing five distinct county clusters based on these economic and geographic characteristics, we analyzed demographic and locational data from the 1990s, drawing on U.S. Census Bureau population estimates. We specifically evaluated key variables such as population size, net domestic and international migration, natural increase, and median income (1995). To further test the geographical concentration of this growth, we calculated a Hoover index to measure the spatial focus of population change across the identified clusters, determining the extent to which migration is concentrated within high-amenity, service-based counties.
