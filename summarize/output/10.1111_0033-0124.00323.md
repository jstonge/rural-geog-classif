# 10.1111_0033-0124.00323

To assess the degree of financial "dependence" of forest peoples on forest resources, I analyzed market activity among the Tawahka Sumu of eastern Honduras within the Tawahka Asangni Biosphere Reserve. Drawing on a multiyear dataset, I utilized three household-level surveys: one conducted by the Harvard Institute for International Development in 1995 and two conducted by myself in 1996 and 1998. I organized these data into two distinct sets: a cross-sectional analysis of market production (1997–1998) for approximately 100 households across five villages, and a longitudinal analysis covering three years (1994, 1995, 1997) for a subset of 49 households from a single village. 

I categorized market income into seven sectors, combining geographical and functional criteria to distinguish own-account production—such as the sale of wild-harvested forest products—from wage labor and other business activities. Key variables included the gross value of goods and services sold or exchanged, with specific attention paid to the disaggregation of "farm" and "forest" sectors to isolate direct investments in resource marketing. By analyzing this data across nested spatial scales (group, village, and household) and temporal scales, I sought to demonstrate how the aggregation of economic data conditions the characterization of forest-dependent livelihoods.
