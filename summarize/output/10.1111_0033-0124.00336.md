# 10.1111_0033-0124.00336

To investigate the growth of business services and their relationship with local economic conditions in Michigan, I developed three models analyzing the interplay between business-service industries—defined specifically as Standard Industrial Classification (SIC) 73—and three key local economic indicators: overall average wages, manufacturing wages, and manufacturing productivity. The study employs a comparative approach to determine if the economic trajectories of metropolitan and nonmetropolitan counties diverge, testing the hypothesis that an expanded division of labor has resulted in two distinct economic geographies. Using county-level data from 1977 to 1997, the analysis focuses on the spatial distribution of SIC73 employment across Michigan's industrial corridors and peripheral regions. By modeling these variables, I examine whether the localized benefits associated with the business-services sector, such as increased productivity and wage growth, are more strongly associated with metropolitan economies than with nonmetropolitan ones, while also tracking how the interdependencies between these services and the manufacturing sector have evolved over the twenty-year period.
