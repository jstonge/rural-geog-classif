# 10.1111_0033-0124.00349

I adopt an explorative approach to analyze the sources of urban and rural population growth in Estonia during the Soviet period (1945–1991), seeking empirical evidence to evaluate universalist versus specific theories of urbanization under central planning. Utilizing data from the Estonian Statistical Office (ESO), I examine population totals, vital statistics, age composition, and migration records. To account for improvements in data quality and shifts in growth drivers, I divide the analysis into two distinct timeframes: 1946–1959 and 1959–1991.

The methodology involves decomposing population change into three primary constituent variables: administrative changes (specifically the reclassification of settlements), net migration (further disaggregated into internal rural-to-urban flows and external immigration), and natural increase. By quantifying these components, I assess how the interplay of industrialization, Soviet ideological mandates, and the systemic inefficiencies of a shortage economy influenced the spatial distribution of the population. This longitudinal analysis allows me to compare Estonia's urbanization curve against broader cross-country patterns while identifying specific Soviet legacies that shaped the country's demographic landscape leading up to the postsocialist transition.
