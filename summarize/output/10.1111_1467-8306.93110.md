# 10.1111_1467-8306.93110

We initiate a critical analysis of the discursive geographies underpinning U.S. agricultural legislation, employing a poststructuralist framework to examine how farming and farmers are socially constructed. Our methodological approach is bifurcated along two dimensions. First, we analyze the "geography of discourse"—the production and dissemination of ideas—by tracing a partial genealogy of American agrarianism from the nineteenth through the twentieth centuries. We identify key discursive sites, the role of expert groups, and the "upscaling" of regional socioeconomic contexts into national federal policy. Second, we examine the "semantic geography" of discourse, focusing on the use of binaries (e.g., family/corporate, welfare/investment) to valorize specific norms. To operationalize this, we focus on the U.S. Senate as a primary discursive site, specifically analyzing the debates surrounding the 1996 Freedom to Farm Bill. Our data sources include verbatim transcripts and news summaries from the *Congressional Digest* and the *Congressional Quarterly Weekly* covering the period between June 1995 and April 1996. Through this analysis, we explore how farming is spatially situated within the constructs of the marketplace, the rural, and the United States to justify legislative shifts toward neoliberal market orientation.
