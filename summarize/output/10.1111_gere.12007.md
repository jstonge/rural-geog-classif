# 10.1111_gere.12007

In this long-form essay, I employ a method I term "geographical portraiture," utilizing a single, scenic landmark—the scarecrow—as a cipher to explore the broader narratives of agricultural society, rural values, and landscape politics. My approach begins with what might be described as an "unsystematic transect," conducting opportunistic field observations from the vantage point of cross-country rail travel. By utilizing the picture windows of railway carriages as frames for a rapid scan of the rural scene, I sought to identify the presence or absence of traditional bird-scaring contraptions across the British countryside. This observational data is then woven together with a cultural history of the scarecrow, drawing upon literary references, folk art archives, and regional dialect maps to situate the object within the traditions of vernacular art and "native survivalism." To deepen the search, I transitioned from passive rail observation to active, haphazard fieldwork in the arable shires, utilizing Ordnance Survey maps and binoculars to conduct a "missing-person hunt" for the scarecrow. Through this blend of subjective, affective writing and opportunistic sampling, I examine the scarecrow not merely as a functional deterrent, but as a threshold figure reflecting the tensions between human and nonhuman lives and the shifting nature of the pastoral myth.
