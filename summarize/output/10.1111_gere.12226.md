# 10.1111_gere.12226

We analyze the perspectives of economic-development stakeholders on boomtown dynamics within the Eagle Ford Shale of south Texas, focusing specifically on the impacts regarding roads, wages, and housing. To capture these local, place-based development characteristics, we employed a semistructured interview framework, purposively sampling respondents from four key policymaking organizations: chambers of commerce, local development corporations, county governments, and municipal policymakers. We conducted eleven face-to-face interview sessions between August 2014 and March 2015, a period encompassing the peak and subsequent decline of global crude oil prices. To account for the volatility of this period, we conducted follow-up and new interviews with stakeholders in June 2016. 

Our methodological approach utilizes a grounded theory framework to transcribe and code interviews via Atlas.ti, allowing emergent themes to structure our findings. We use these data to describe and analyze boomtown processes through the lens of Gilmore’s 1976 "problem triangle" model. By integrating these stakeholder views, we extend and amplify Gilmore's foundational model to incorporate the specific materiality of unconventional hydrocarbon extraction—such as long-distance commuting and contemporary recovery processes—thereby generating updated theoretical insights and testable predictions for other energy-impacted communities.
