# 10.1111_gere.12244

As part of the Peruvian Amazon Rural Livelihoods and Poverty (PARLAP) project, we conducted a large-scale census of 174 Amerindian and *ribereño* communities and towns within the Napo River basin in northeastern Peru between 2013 and 2014. Our methodological approach employed social network theory to examine the structure and spatial configuration of intercommunity connections, specifically focusing on three types of social interaction: crop seed acquisition, cooperative labor sharing, and intercommunity soccer matches. Data were gathered through focus-group interviews using a structured questionnaire administered by a local field team traveling by boat across the basin. We identified "partner" communities based on the flow of seeds and labor, as well as the frequency of soccer matches, while recording community locations via GPS. By organizing these linkages into matrices for relational analysis, we analyzed the sociospatial networks of each practice. Our analysis specifically tested for effects of homophily—the propensity for ties to form between communities of the same ethnicity—and the presence of "weak ties" or bridging interactions between upland (*terra firme*) and lowland (floodplain) communities, while accounting for the influence of distance and settlement patterns.
