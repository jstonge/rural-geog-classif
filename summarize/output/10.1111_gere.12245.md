# 10.1111_gere.12245

To identify and illustrate the manifestations of "virtual rurality" and the "rural idyll" within the American imagination, we employ a blend of qualitative and quantitative research methods. Our approach frames the phenomenon not as attachment to a specific location, but as an attachment to a generic type of place, drawing on psychological concepts of generic place attachment and geographical theories of the rural idyll. We gather qualitative evidence through field observations, a review of existing literature, and computer searches to identify visible portrayals of idealized rurality in popular culture, including advertising for real estate, lifestyles, and recreational opportunities. To complement these qualitative findings, we incorporate quantitative data from the U.S. Census of Agriculture (specifically comparing 1992 and 2012 data) to track trends in small-scale and micro-farming across various U.S. regions. While we do not follow a singular discourse analysis, our synthesis of these diverse data sources allows us to categorize six primary embodiments of the rural ideal—ranging from agritourism and hobby farming to urban agriculture and symbolic naming conventions—across a spatial scale that extends from deep rural areas to peri-urban and urban environments.
