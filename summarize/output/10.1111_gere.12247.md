# 10.1111_gere.12247

To examine the intersection of aquaculture expansion and the postproductive transition on the Maine coast, I conducted a comparative analysis of three primary aquaculture regions: the Damariscotta River, Cobscook Bay, and the area surrounding Blue Hill Bay. I conceptualized these regions as sites of rural restructuring, characterized by a shift from commodity production toward amenity-driven consumption and recreation. My primary data sources consisted of aquaculture lease hearing transcripts from the Maine Department of Marine Resources spanning 1981 to 2015. Of the 263 available transcripts, I analyzed 90 that included public hearings. Using NVivo11, I and two assistants employed inductive coding to quantify and categorize the specific concerns raised by the public—primarily coastal landowners—during these proceedings. I further bifurcated these hearings into "uncertainty" and "NIMBY" (Not In My Back Yard) groups based on the level of conflict and the stridency of objections. By analyzing variables such as navigation obstacles, environmental impacts, and aesthetic concerns, I sought to understand how divergent regional histories and geographies influence the negotiation between aquaculture farmers and amenity migrants in a postproductive landscape.
