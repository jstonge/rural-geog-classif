# 10.1111_gere.12264

We conducted a qualitative, exploratory study of country stores in rural Vermont to investigate how these built environments function as socio-spatial nodes within multifunctional rural landscapes. Our research, carried out in 2015 and 2016, focused on seventeen stores across fifteen towns, ranging in population from 298 to 5,009 residents. These sites represented a diverse array of local economies, including commuter towns and amenity-rich tourism areas. To gather data, we employed a multi-method approach consisting of site visits using a standardized data-collection form to document physical layout, geographic location, and merchandise; semistructured interviews with storekeepers and key stakeholders, including the director of a historic preservation organization; and an open-text online survey administered to community members attempting to establish a cooperative store. We specifically distinguished "country stores"—independently owned, long-standing establishments—from franchise convenience stores to isolate the specific socio-material roles of the former. By analyzing the range of goods, formal and informal services, and community engagements, we sought to identify the reflexive relationships between these stores and the broader physical and cultural geographies of their regions.
