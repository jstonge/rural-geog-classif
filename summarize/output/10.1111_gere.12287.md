# 10.1111_gere.12287

This comparative investigation examines the relationships between primary sector value chains and rural development in the Philippines, specifically focusing on four export-oriented products: bananas, coconut oil, tuna, and seaweed/carrageenan. To address the gap in literature regarding how smallholders and fishers are inserted into these chains, I employ an analytical framework that integrates the Sustainable Livelihoods Approach (SLA) with Global Value Chain (GVC) analysis. This integration allows for a multi-scalar examination of the "capitals pentagon," the vulnerability context, and the institutional political economy, facilitating an analysis of both horizontal and vertical coordination and the resulting trajectories of product and process upgrading.

The empirical analysis relies primarily on a comprehensive review of secondary sources, including academic literature, reports from consultants and NGOs, and Philippine media, to map value-chain structures and rural livelihood challenges. To supplement this, I conducted primary fieldwork in Guimaras Province in 2016, specifically targeting the seaweed value chain. This fieldwork comprised sixteen semi-structured interviews with key informants across six barangays and a survey of thirty-four seaweed-growing households on the island barangay of Panobolon. By combining these data sources, the study evaluates how changing value-chain dynamics impact the livelihoods of approximately 3 million households and identifies the systemic barriers to upstream upgrading.
