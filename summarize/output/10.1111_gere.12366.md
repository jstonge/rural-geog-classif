# 10.1111_gere.12366

To analyze the impact of social connections and contextual factors on the sense of belonging among rural-to-urban migrants in China, we conducted an empirical study utilizing data from the 2013 National Migrant Population Dynamic Monitoring Survey (MDMS). Our sample comprised 12,807 migrants aged 15–59 across eight purposively selected cities (including Shanghai, Wuhan, and Xi'an), drawn from approximately 750 neighborhoods using a three-stage probability proportionate to size (PPS) sampling technique. We measured the dependent variable, sense of belonging, by applying Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to six Likert-scale items covering place attachment, identity, and social bonding. To quantify social contacts, we categorized interactions into four groups: nonresidents, kin residents, nonkin residents, and local authorities. We further incorporated contextual variables, such as city population size and the availability of public services, alongside individual-level proxies for ties to the place of origin, including remittance amounts and landholding. To account for the nested nature of the data, we implemented multilevel linear regression models (MLMs), employing intraclass correlation (ICC) and likelihood ratio tests to validate the necessity of a multilevel approach over a single-level model.
