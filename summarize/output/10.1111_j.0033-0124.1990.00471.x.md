# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1990.00471.x

We explain the computer implementation of the mile marker addressing system across several counties in New Mexico to improve the location of rural residences. Our approach utilizes a public-private cooperative model where county governments provide personnel for field work and sign posting, while New Mexico State University provides computer mapping expertise and database construction. We collect data from two primary sources: mapped data, including USGS orthophoto quadrangles (1975–1982) with Public Land Survey System (PLSS) overlays and 7.5-minute topographic maps, and field survey data gathered through house-to-house interviews and observations. 

Using a manual digitizer and the state plane coordinate system, we enter road networks, dwellings, and political boundaries into a geographic database. This process involves an iterative field editing cycle where county personnel verify plotted maps for errors of omission or misalignment before final redigitization. The resulting system integrates geographic base files with a relational survey database containing resident information. We deliver these tools—including IBM-PC compatible hardware, specialized software, and a rural addressing atlas reduced to a scale of one inch to the mile—alongside technical training in cartography and software use to ensure local sustainability and integration with county clerk and assessor records.
