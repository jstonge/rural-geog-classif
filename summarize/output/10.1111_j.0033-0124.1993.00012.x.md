# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1993.00012.x

To examine the conflict between low-wage employment and high housing costs in the amenity town of Sedona, Arizona, we conducted a survey of the community's workforce in August 1991. Utilizing a systematic sampling approach, we selected every third business from the Sedona Chamber of Commerce membership list, distributing questionnaires to 64 businesses and 846 employees. From these, we obtained 286 completed responses (a 33.8% response rate). We categorized respondents into three groups based on their residence/workplace patterns: employees residing within Sedona city limits, those in the suburban Village of Oak Creek, and long-distance commuters from the broader laborshed, including towns such as Cottonwood, Clarkdale, Cornville, and Camp Verde. 

Our analysis focused on comparing these groups across several key variables, including demographic characteristics (age, marital status, number of children), socioeconomic indicators (household income, homeownership rates), and housing costs (mean property values and monthly rents). We employed ANOVA and chi-square tests to determine the statistical significance of differences between the groups. By analyzing the tradeoffs between commuting time and housing expenditures, we sought to evaluate the relationship between income levels and residential location within the context of a nonmetropolitan amenity environment.
