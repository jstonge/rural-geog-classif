# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1994.00178.x

This paper employs a dual retrospective and prospective approach to analyze the processes of agricultural restructuring, focusing primarily on the Canadian context. I begin with an empirical analysis of structural change in Canadian agriculture from 1971 to 1991, utilizing time series data from Statistics Canada to examine key variables such as farm numbers, average farm size, gross receipts, and debt-to-asset ratios. This quantitative assessment is used to identify trends of concentration, polarization, and financial instability within the sector. I then situate these empirical findings within a broader theoretical framework by synthesizing and comparing six contemporary development models of advanced capitalist economies, ranging from neoclassical "induced technical change" and political economy perspectives on capital penetration and uneven development to models emphasizing human agency and societal-environmental dialectics. By integrating this empirical evidence with a review of existing theoretical literature, I examine the role of national and international policy in shaping the current agricultural landscape and explore alternative development paths toward sustainable food and rural systems.
