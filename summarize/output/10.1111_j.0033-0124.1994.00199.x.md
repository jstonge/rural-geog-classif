# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1994.00199.x

We employ location-allocation models to evaluate the spatial accessibility of health care facilities in the Suhum District of Ghana, specifically testing the "add-more" policy recommendation against the alternative of optimizing facility location. Using 1984 census data, we identify 150 population centers as demand points, with population size serving as the proxy for demand. Because walking is the primary mode of travel, we utilize a distance-generating algorithm to calculate Euclidean distances between locations. 

Our methodological approach treats the three-level hierarchical health system (A, B, and C levels) as successively inclusive. We apply the p-median criterion to minimize average weighted travel distance for A-level services, comparing the actual system of 30 facilities against an optimal configuration. To assess the potential for improving accessibility without increasing resources, we vary the number of facilities ($p$) from 1 to 30 to determine the point of diminishing returns. Additionally, we utilize the maximum coverage criterion to evaluate the supervisory capacity of B-level facilities over A-level facilities, specifically testing the government's goal of maintaining a B-level facility within 8 km of every A-level facility. The Tietz and Bart heuristic algorithm is used to solve these location-allocation problems.
