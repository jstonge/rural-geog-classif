# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1995.00148.x

To address the paucity of statistical data regarding the cottage-owning population, we employ a multiple case study approach focusing on three distinct Canadian regions: the Rideau Lakes of Ontario, the Cultus Lake area of British Columbia, and the Beaubassin coastline of New Brunswick. These sites were selected to capture regional economic variations and represent the "rural-recreational countryside" within the broader urban field. Our methodology integrates two primary data sources to examine property distribution, ownership patterns, and socioeconomic profiles. First, we utilize local government property assessment and taxation records to identify seasonal-use cottage properties and determine the permanent residence of owners. Second, we administered a questionnaire survey in the summer of 1991 to a random sample of seasonal-use cottage owners and year-round rural residents. The sampling frame for each area was determined by the number of cottage-to-year-round conversions identified over the preceding decade. By comparing these two resident groups—defined by settlement type and occupancy pattern rather than a standard farm/non-farm dichotomy—we analyze key variables including age, education, and income to test assumptions regarding the socioeconomic differences between cottagers and local rural residents.
