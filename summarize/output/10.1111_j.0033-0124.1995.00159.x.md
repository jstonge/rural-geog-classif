# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1995.00159.x

We examined the relationship between human activities and forest-landscape structure along a 20-km wide, 140-km long belt transect extending from the urban core of New York City to rural northwestern Connecticut. To control for environmental variability, we positioned the transect within the glaciated Appalachian highlands and the oak-hickory vegetation association. We quantified urban structures and processes at both coarse (1:250,000) and fine (1:24,000) resolutions, utilizing data from the U.S. Bureau of Census, USGS planimetric and topographic maps, traffic volume reports, and aerial photography (1967–1991). Key urban variables included population density, annual growth rates, traffic volume, road length, and urban-residential land proportions. 

Using raster-based geographic information systems (IDRISI and MAP) and specialized spatial analysis software, we measured forest-landscape structure through parameters such as mean patch size, patch number, perimeter-area fractal dimensions for shape complexity, and the percentage of forest edges adjacent to urban land uses. We employed linear and logarithmic regressions to document trends in urban activity relative to distance from the city core and used correlations and partial correlations to determine the extent to which urban structures and processes explain variation in the distribution, size, and shape of native forest patches.
