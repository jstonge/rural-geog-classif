# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1995.00411.x

In this study, I employ a qualitative research approach to explore the complexities of spontaneous intra-rural migration in Sri Lanka, arguing that personal narratives can capture individual decision-making processes and contextual nuances that quantitative methods often overlook. Moving away from traditional macro-level statistical analyses, I utilize the narrative method—defined here as the collection and interpretation of individuals' stories—to illuminate the intersection of human agency and structural constraints. To exemplify this approach, I present a case study of "Amma," a female rural migrant. The data for this narrative were gathered through a series of informal interviews conducted via an interpreter, supplemented by my own field observations and accounts provided by the subject's son. Rather than a comprehensive life history, I focus specifically on the story of her migration to reveal the role of gender, family ties, and the specific socio-spatial characteristics of the rural Sri Lankan context. By treating the narrative as both a phenomenon and a method, I aim to achieve verisimilitude, providing a grounded, idiographic account that links individual action to the broader social and geographical environment.
