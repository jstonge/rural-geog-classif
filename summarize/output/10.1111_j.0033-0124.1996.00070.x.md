# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1996.00070.x

I examined the use of urban and rural habitats by eleven small arboreal bird species common to pine woodlands in the vicinity of Athens, Georgia, during the winters of 1989-1990 and 1990-1991. To determine population densities, I conducted a series of censuses along straight-line transects at four primary sites—two rural (Whitehall Forest and the Botanical Garden) and two urban residential developments (Pine View Drive and Davis Estates). I compared species densities between rural and urban sites using one-tailed Mann-Whitney U-tests and performed detectability analyses to ensure that distance from transects did not bias the results.

To assess habitat use, I mapped the primary sites into twelve categories based on canopy closure, dominant tree life form, and understory openness. I then used one-tailed $\chi^2$ tests with Yates' correction to determine if birds avoided highly disturbed habitats, such as those with open canopies or understories. Additionally, I quantified the flocking behavior of the brown-headed nuthatch by measuring its distance to the nearest Carolina chickadee or tufted titmouse at ten-minute intervals. To determine if variations in flocking were driven by interspecific attraction rather than resource distribution or bird density, I employed a simulation approach, randomly distributing fictitious birds onto maps based on observed habitat use and census data. Inter-bird distances were compared across sites using two-tailed Kolmogorov-Smirnov tests.
