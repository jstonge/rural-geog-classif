# 10.1111_j.0033-0124.1996.00284.x

We examine the electoral geography of the 1994 referenda on European Union membership in Norway, Sweden, and Finland to understand the spatial distribution of support and the factors mediating voter behavior. Utilizing a contextual approach that moves beyond neoclassical economic expectancies, we analyze voting patterns at the national and subnational levels, specifically focusing on the relationship between support for membership and population density across Nordic provinces and counties. We employ indicator-variable regression equations to quantify the correlation between population density and the strength of the "yes" vote, while mapping the geographic concentration of "no" votes against areas eligible for Objective 6 EU structural subsidies. By integrating these quantitative spatial analyses with a qualitative review of the political, social, and economic arguments raised during the campaigns—such as sovereignty, market access, and rural depopulation—we evaluate how local and national differences influenced the interpretation of EU membership. This multi-scalar analysis allows us to discern the tension between the urbanized core and the rural periphery, highlighting how geopolitical orientations and historical contexts shaped the final outcomes in each of the three nations.
