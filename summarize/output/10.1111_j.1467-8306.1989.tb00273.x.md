# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.1989.tb00273.x

In this presidential address, we review the extensive body of scholarly literature concerning the European colonization of rural North America to evaluate the descriptive and explanatory frameworks employed by historical geographers. We first synthesize seven primary descriptive themes—region, diffusion, landscape, first effective settlement, simplification, environmental perception, and humans as modifiers of the earth—which have traditionally served to document the "what" of Euroamerican settlement. We then critically examine the three dominant causal paradigms used to explain these patterns: environmental determinism, which emphasizes the shaping power of the New World wilderness; economic determinism, which focuses on resource annexation, market access, and capitalistic accumulation; and cultural determinism, which attributes societal evolution to imported ideologies and kinship networks. By analyzing these multi-generational approaches, we argue that while these models provide significant empirical insight, they often lack holistic explanatory power. Consequently, we propose the integration of cultural ecology, specifically the concept of preadaptation, as a potent but underutilized explanatory device to be used in conjunction with economic and cultural determinism to better interpret the processes of overseas farm settlement.
