# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.1992.tb01967.x

We explore the indigenous cultivated landscapes of Mesoamerica as they existed on the eve of the Columbian Encounter, aiming to illustrate the diversity of Amerindian agricultural technologies and management practices. To achieve this, we employ a transect-based approach, examining three specific routes that approximate the initial Spanish *entradas* through the region: the "Cortes transect," extending from the Gulf coast to central Mexico; the "Montejo transect," traversing the Yucatan peninsula from north to south; and the "Alvarado transect," climbing from the Pacific coastal plain into highland Guatemala. By analyzing these diverse environmental and sociopolitical terrains, we identify a multiplicity of cultivation systems, ranging from rainfed *temporal* and shifting cultivation to sophisticated hydraulically transformed wetlands and *chinampas*. We further sketch the major transformations occurring during the first phase of Spanish domination, focusing on the drivers of change—specifically Amerindian depopulation, the introduction of exotic biota and technologies, and the reordering of the rural economy. Through this synthesis, we evaluate the scale of environmental transformation and degradation associated with the transition from indigenous to hybrid landscapes in the sixteenth century.
