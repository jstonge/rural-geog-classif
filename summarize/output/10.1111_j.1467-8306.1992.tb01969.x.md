# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.1992.tb01969.x

We examine the processes of Spanish colonization and the subsequent alienation of indigenous landholdings in Central Mexico between 1521 and 1620. Our approach utilizes archival documentation of land grants (*mercedes*) as a primary tool to decipher regional settlement histories and evaluate the efficacy of the Spanish legal system in safeguarding Indian property rights. To explicate the granting of lands, we analyze three specific geographic examples: the Basin of Mexico, the district northwest of Puebla, and the Valley of Toluca. 

We trace the transition from an initial phase of exploitation based on the *encomienda*—a system of tribute and limited labor conscription—to a second phase centered on formal land ownership and agricultural expansion. We specifically analyze the failure of the Puebla experiment (1531–34), which attempted to establish a class of Spanish smallholders, and contrast it with the successful consolidation of large estates by the colonial elite. By synthesizing historical records with spatial considerations, we examine how the Indian demographic collapse and the policy of settlement amalgamation (*congregacion*) facilitated the transfer of land. Through this analysis, we identify structural similarities in the heart of Central Mexico while noting the unique settlement histories of peripheral areas.
