# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.1992.tb01974.x

This essay employs a historical and biographical analysis to examine the evolution of physical and cultural geography resulting from the Spanish encounter with the Americas between 1492 and 1590. The author challenges the notion of intellectual stasis during the Renaissance by demonstrating how the confrontation with the New World necessitated a shift from Medieval deductivism and Classical ontogenies toward empirical, inductive methods of observation and synthesis. To illustrate this transition, the study focuses on seven exemplary individuals—including Christopher Columbus, Fernando Colón, and Acosta—whose diverse backgrounds as sailors, soldiers, and scholars represent the multilinear development of the period's geographical thought. 

The methodological approach involves a critical re-appraisal of primary documentation, including voyage diaries, letters, and the extensive "Itinerary" survey conducted by Fernando Colón in Spain. By analyzing these sources, the author identifies key methodological spheres such as landscape and biotic taxonomy, cultural recording, and regional synthesis. The analysis specifically tracks the emergence of geomorphological and biogeographical observations and the role of normative urban planning in the Spanish government. Through these case studies, the paper argues that the practical necessity of describing unfamiliar environments fostered a spontaneous capacity for empirical description that often superseded traditional academic curricula.
