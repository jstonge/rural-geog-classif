This study adopts the perspective of cultural geography to examine the residential squares of London as symbolic landscapes that express shifting social values during the transition from late feudal to early capitalist modes of production. By analyzing the transformation of urban morphology from the seventeenth to the mid-nineteenth century, we seek to explain the relationship between social processes and landscape patterns, specifically focusing on the use of ornamental vegetation and the role of nature in the human environment. Our approach integrates architectural and landscape aesthetics with an analysis of the ongoing struggle between private and public control over urban open spaces. We examine the evolution of these squares—from paved, public plazas based on continental models to enclosed, private parks reminiscent of rural landscape parks—as expressions of elite tastes and a growing desire for class segregation and domestic isolation. By situating these gardens within the broader context of aristocratic estate development and the extinguishment of collective rights, we illustrate how these urban forms reflected changing concepts of land as capital and property, ultimately influencing the development of early public parks and the value systems underlying modern suburban living.
