# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.1994.tb01727.x

To examine the relationship between groundwater exploitation and community sustainability, we conducted a multi-scalar analysis of 32 western Kansas counties within the Ogallala region. We measured the association between reported groundwater use in 1990 and population change between 1980 and 1990, utilizing three distinct spatial scales: the county, the rural census subdivision, and the census-designated place. To improve upon previous studies that relied on irrigated acreage—which we argue masks actual water dependence—we employed reported water-use data from the Division of Water Resources of the Kansas State Board of Agriculture as our primary indicator of irrigation intensity. 

We performed multiple regression analyses to determine the relative importance of various independent variables, including the size of the largest place in 1980, per capita income, farm employment percentages, and changes in farm size and irrigated acres. By examining standardized regression coefficients (Beta weights), we sought to isolate the impact of groundwater use per square mile on population fluctuations while controlling for other socioeconomic factors. This methodological approach allows us to determine whether population growth is a function of general county-level location or is specifically contingent upon proximity to intensively irrigated areas and the achievement of certain population thresholds.
