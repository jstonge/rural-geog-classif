# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.1995.tb01793.x

We examined the interrelationships between meteorological conditions and landform controls on an estuarine sand beach located 15 km north of the mouth of Delaware Bay at Green Creek, New Jersey. To assess beach response during the post-storm recovery phase, we monitored wind, wave, and current dynamics across three storm sequences in October. We deployed a triangular array of pressure transducers to determine wave approach direction and significant wave height, alongside two Marsh McBirney electromagnetic current meters to measure cross-shore and longshore flow velocities. Wind speed and direction were recorded via an anemometer and wind vane mounted 6.0 m above the ground. 

Wave and flow data were collected at semidiurnal intervals during high tide, with frequency increasing to hourly during storm events. We utilized the BMDP software program for univariate and bivariate spectral analysis to define wave period and angle. Morphological changes were monitored using standard beach surveying techniques, including transit, stadia rod, and depth-of-activity rods across three shore-normal profile lines. Sediment characteristics were analyzed from surface samples using graphic measures. By controlling for wind speed, water depth, and wave height, we compared the effects of different wind directions—specifically north, west, and northeast—on nearshore processes and profile recovery.
