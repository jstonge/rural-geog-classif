# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.1996.tb01761.x

To document the rural landscape and structural manifestations of reprivatization in eastern Germany following the 1990 reunification, I employ a multi-methodological approach combining quantitative regional data with qualitative site-specific analysis. I utilize three primary data sources: questionnaires collected by a German agricultural research institute from a representative sample of 728 farms across eastern Germany, topographic and cadastral maps, and extensive field work conducted at nine farm or village sites. Through map analysis and field observations, I document changes in field patterns—specifically the number, density, and shape of fields—before and after reunification at six primary sites, including Heinersdorf, Rehmsdorf, Dedelow, and Hasenholz. To provide a comparative spatial context, I select two additional sites—Uelzen in western Germany and Emerald, Wisconsin—to contrast eastern German field patterns since 1990. The questionnaire data serve to provide the broader regional context for the farm and field changes observed in these case studies. This empirical framework allows me to relate recent landscape transformations to the preceding political and economic shifts of the past fifty years, specifically the Soviet land reforms (1945–1949) and the GDR’s subsequent collectivization (1950–1990).
