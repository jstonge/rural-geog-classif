# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.2004.00418.x

To operationalize recent calls for a more actor-oriented and ethnographically informed understanding of rural livelihoods, I reconstruct the dynamics of the regional dugout canoe trade in Central America's Mosquitia from the early 1600s to the late 1990s. My methodological approach combines diachronic historical analysis with contemporary ethnographic fieldwork conducted between 1994 and 2002, specifically focusing on the Patuca River basin in Honduras. To reconstruct the first three centuries of the trade, I draw upon a diverse array of primary archival sources, including 17th-century buccaneers' accounts and 18th- and 19th-century narratives from travelers and merchants. For the 20th century, I integrate missionary reports with testimonies recorded from canoe producers during my fieldwork. 

Drawing on the "social life of things" and theories of access within commodity chains, I treat the dugout canoe as a constant object of analysis to expose the multiscaled social and political systems through which it circulates. By analyzing the "biography" of this commodity, I examine key variables such as ethnic production specialization, the interaction between local and international trade circuits, and the constraints facing undercapitalized households. This trade-network approach allows me to move beyond binary calibrations of rural life—such as "autarkic" versus "market-integrated"—to reveal how remote rural peoples negotiate and resist asymmetrical relationships with global capital.
