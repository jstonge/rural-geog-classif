# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.2005.00450.x

To explore the complexities of a coupled human-environment system, we developed the Integrative Model for Simulating Household and Ecosystem Dynamics (IMSHED), an agent-based spatial model implemented using the Swarm toolkit. Our study focuses on the Wolong Nature Reserve in China, where we simulate the impact of a growing rural population on giant panda habitat. We employ a bottom-up approach, treating households as the fundamental decision-making units. These household agents are equipped with "knowledge" of themselves and their environment, interacting via artificial-intelligence rules to simulate the coevolution of human demographics and forest dynamics. 

The methodology integrates multidisciplinary data sources, including remote-sensing imagery for spatial heterogeneity and extensive socioeconomic data from in-person interviews with 220 households conducted between 1998 and 2002. The model incorporates three primary components: a demographic submodel tracking individual life histories and household formation (informed by structural equation modeling), a fuelwood demand module based on household size, age composition, and cropland area, and a spatial module simulating forest growth and harvesting. By linking individual-level behaviors to macroscopic landscape changes, we capture nonlinear interactions, feedbacks, and time lags across varying spatial and temporal scales to provide a tool for biodiversity conservation and policy analysis.
