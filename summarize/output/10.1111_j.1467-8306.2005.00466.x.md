# 10.1111_j.1467-8306.2005.00466.x

Drawing upon postcolonial studies and postcolonial geographies of North America, we examine the production of southern difference in the postbellum U.S. South between 1865 and 1880. We analyze a series of travel accounts penned by northern men and women to investigate how the region was scripted as an "imperial holding" of the North/nation, effectively translating a regional binary into a colonizer-colonized distinction. Our methodological approach focuses on the spatialities of description and the mechanisms of knowledge production within these narratives. Specifically, we identify and analyze three recurring textual themes—discourses of civilization, descriptions of nature, and discussions of whiteness—to illustrate how northern travelers positioned the South as a colonial appendage. We further interrogate how gender contoured the "civilizing mission" directed toward emancipated African Americans and the role of the imperial explorer in rural sites. By treating the South as a process rather than a fixed place, we employ a postcolonial lens to highlight the contradictions and imperfections of this internal imperialism, particularly where white rural poverty problematized the separation between the northern colonizer and the southern colonized.
