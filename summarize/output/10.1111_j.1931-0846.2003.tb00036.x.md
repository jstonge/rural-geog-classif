# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2003.tb00036.x

I examine the formation of social capital—defined as the norms of trust and reciprocity integral to social relations—and the ways in which it enables rural peoples' organizations to access rights and resources. I argue that this social capital is embedded within and given meaning through a constellation of shared understandings, or cultural capital. To illustrate this framework, I conduct a detailed case study of the Federation of Indigenous Organizations of Napo (FOIN), a lowland Quichua organization in the Ecuadorian Amazon. My approach probes the organizational history of FOIN, emphasizing the institutional relationships it has forged over time across multiple scales, from local community levels to provincial, regional, and transnational networks. I analyze how these connections have facilitated the federation's ability to manage development projects and make political claims against the state. Additionally, I employ a survey of seventy-four Quichua visitors to FOIN's offices in Tena to investigate the uneven distribution of social capital, specifically examining how variables such as gender, linguistic ability (Spanish vs. Quichua), and spatial proximity to the federation's headquarters influence access to organizational benefits. Through this analysis, I explore the role of FOIN in constructing the symbolic meanings within which its social relations and material practices are understood.
