# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2004.tb00178.x

Drawing upon a feminist political ecology framework, we conducted a qualitative study to explore the role of women in nature-society relations through the lens of "kitchenspace"—the integrated indoor and outdoor areas of the house-lot garden used for food preparation. Our research took place over eleven months between 2000 and 2001 across three semiurban communities in central Mexico: Xochimilco, Ocotepec, and Tetecala. These sites represent a gradient of urbanization and cultural identity, ranging from the periurban suburbs of Mexico City and Cuernavaca to the more isolated regional center of Tetecala. 

To capture the embodied experience of "dwelling" in these spaces, we employed a tripartite methodological approach consisting of participant observation, ethnographic interviews, and participatory mapping. We spent extensive time in approximately half a dozen homes per site, engaging in the daily labor of food preparation and the collective staging of religious fiestas. After an initial period of unstructured interviews and observation, we implemented structured interviews focusing on three key variables: gendered landscape spaces and adaptation to change, women's ecological knowledge regarding food gathering, and narratives of cultural identity. This approach allowed us to document the transmission of gendered knowledge and the symbolic connection to the land within increasingly urbanized contexts.
