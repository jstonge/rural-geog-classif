# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2004.tb00182.x

We conducted this study to examine how improvements in transportation infrastructure have influenced economic specialization within the woodworking industry of the Meseta Purepecha in west-central Michoacan, Mexico. We focused our analysis on seven towns, selected for their representation of varying levels of accessibility and their reliance on woodworking as a dominant craft industry. Our methodological approach combined longitudinal data with primary field research conducted in 2001 and 2004. To assess changes in production and market reach, we interviewed fifty-four craft producers (approximately eight per town), gathering data on production methods, raw material sources, and market destinations. To analyze the spatial distribution of the industry, we performed comprehensive street surveys in each of the seven towns during peak business hours (10:00 A.M. to 2:00 P.M.) in both 2001 and 2004 to map the location of craft storefronts. These current observations were then compared against historical data from the 1940s and 1950s to evaluate shifts in the types of crafts produced and the evolution of local and external market linkages relative to the development of the regional road network.
