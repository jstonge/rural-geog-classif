# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2010.00019.x

We examine the synanthropic relationship between the American crow (*Corvus brachyrhynchos*) and human populations in eastern North America through a multiscalar geographical lens. By integrating time scales ranging from the prehistoric to the observable present, we analyze how anthropogenic landscape modifications—specifically the fragmentation of closed forests—have driven the species' biogeography. Our approach utilizes a combination of historical observations, ornithological records, and archaeological evidence to correlate crow distributions with human land-use patterns. At the regional scale, we track the expansion of the species alongside the diffusion of maize (*Zea mays*) from the late Archaic period through Euro-American settlement, contrasting the crow's presence in humanized landscapes with the prevalence of ravens (*Corvus corax*) in wilderness areas. At the microscale, we analyze the foraging efficiency of crows within the agricultural cycle of the cornfield. Finally, we document the diverse behavioral and technological strategies employed by Native American peoples and Euro-American settlers to mitigate crop depredation, treating the crow as a biological indicator of the "pristine myth" and a reflection of the evolving human-environment interface.
