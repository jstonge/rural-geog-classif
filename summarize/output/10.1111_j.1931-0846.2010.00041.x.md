# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2010.00041.x

To assess the merit of unifying the concepts of creative destruction and trial by space, we conducted a detailed study of Creemore, a historic village in Ontario, Canada. Our methodological approach sought to determine how the village evolved from a productivist townscape into its current form as a postproductivist heritagescape. To capture the essence of Creemore as a locality and evaluate its evolutionary path, we drew upon a diverse array of data sources. We distributed a questionnaire to 300 households during the autumn of 2007 to elicit residents' opinions on the village's development, achieving a response rate of 25 percent. This primary data was supplemented by a comprehensive content analysis of print and online materials, including historical and contemporary municipal planning documents, reports and brochures from the Creemore Business Improvement Association, and articles from the local weekly newspaper, the *Creemore Star/Echo*, published since 1981. Additionally, we utilized historical business data from the 1977 Dun and Bradstreet Reference Book to establish a baseline for the village's commercial landscape. Together, these sources allowed us to analyze the interplay between the three facets of rural space—locality, representation, and everyday lives—and the motivational drivers of profit, preservation, and promotion.
