# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2014.12044.x

To examine how residents of the rural Mexican municipio of Coxcatlán, Puebla, construct garbage as a set of place-specific and demographically contingent issues, I employed a modified form of participatory risk mapping. Conducted between August and November 2011, the study sampled 433 respondents across twelve communities, targeting heads of households or the adults primarily responsible for waste management. I utilized a two-stage ordinal ranking system: respondents first identified the most important garbage-related issues—using open-ended prompts such as *asunto* (issue) or *lo que nos afecta* (that which affects us)—and subsequently rank-ordered these issues by importance. 

To analyze the data, I calculated an incidence score (the proportion of the sample mentioning a specific issue) and an importance index (a normalized rank derived from the number of issues listed per respondent). These values were plotted on two-dimensional coordinate grids to visualize the prevalence and severity of various issues. To further explore the nuances of these perceptions, I stratified the population by sex and spatial location (valley vs. sierra, municipal seat vs. outlying settlements, and center vs. peripheral colonias). Finally, I applied Pearson's Chi-Square statistics and binary logistic regression to test for statistically significant relationships between these spatial and demographic variables and the incidence of the most frequently cited garbage issues.
