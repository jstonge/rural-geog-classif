# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2016.12161.x

We conducted a case study of agricultural tourism in Michigan to evaluate the sector's economic contributions to the state, business operators, and employees. Our research design employed a mixed-methods approach, beginning with qualitative focus groups and depth interviews with specialty crop operators to inform the development of a systematic survey. We identified a statewide mailing list of 2,974 operations using Chamber of Commerce websites, directories, and online guides. The survey, administered via mail and Qualtrics during the summer and fall of 2013, collected data on firm demographics, product inventories, employment, advertising costs, and perceived economic benefits from 154 usable responses.

To assess the broader economic impact, we calculated descriptive statistics for twelve categories of operations and extrapolated these findings to a conservative statewide estimate of 4,000 operations. Furthermore, we utilized Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) multivariate regression analysis to identify drivers of firm success. The dependent variable was standardized as gross receipts per day of operation to allow for comparison across seasonal and year-round businesses. Independent variables included mean daily advertising costs, annual wages, the number of days open to the public, the percentage of sales from off-farm products, and a dummy variable to account for scale effects based on an annual sales threshold of $20,000.
