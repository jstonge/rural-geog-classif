# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2016.12182.x

Informed by feminist geopolitics, we employ a microscale, place-specific approach to examine the hidden, embodied costs of neoliberal restructuring and transnational migration borne by those "left behind." Our methodology integrates quantitative and qualitative data to disentangle how global political processes are experienced through localized, everyday practices. We draw upon socioeconomic data from a Mexican Migration Project (MMP) ethnosurvey of 150 randomly sampled households in Tierra Azul, a principal migration center in north-central Veracruz, to frame our analysis of gendered impacts. To supplement this, we conducted 63 semi-structured, in-depth interviews between 2007 and 2009 with 66 individuals—including community leaders, migrants, and family members—across 17 different communities in the Totonac region of rural Veracruz. Using purposeful and snowball sampling, we targeted information-rich informants, particularly nonmigrant women, children, and the elderly, to capture the narratives of de facto female-headed households. By contrasting female- and male-headed households and analyzing lived experiences of social reproduction and care work, we seek to challenge macroscale "migration for development" discourses that prioritize economic indicators over the psychosocial and corporal costs of family separation.
