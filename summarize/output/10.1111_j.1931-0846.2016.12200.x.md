# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2016.12200.x

This study explores the everyday lived experiences of the American exclave of Point Roberts, Washington, by examining its historical geography, evolving economic functions, and the social dynamics of its residential population. To capture the complexities of this hybrid borderland, I employed a mixed-methods approach combining quantitative analysis of public data sets with qualitative field research. Quantitative data were drawn from the American Community Survey, United States and Canadian censuses, Department of Homeland Security border crossing records, and the 2014 Whatcom County property tax parcel geodatabase. These were supplemented by close personal observations dating back to 1991, including a period of immersive participant observation while living in the community during the 2011–2012 academic year. To further develop a comprehensive picture of service provision and the perceptions of life in an exclave, I conducted semistructured interviews between 2011 and 2015 with key informants—including local business owners and Whatcom County planning and utility personnel—as well as members from ten U.S.-citizen households residing in Point Roberts on a year-round or part-time basis.
