# 10.1111_j.1931-0846.2016.12206.x

We employ a dynamic time series analysis using a household panel dataset collected across four survey waves (2007, 2008, 2010, and 2013) in three rural Vietnamese provinces: Ha Tinh, Thua Thien-Hue, and Dak Lak. These study areas were selected to represent diverse regional economic structures, ranging from the cash-crop specialization of the central highlands to the more diversified, industrialized economies of the north-central coast. To operationalize vulnerability to poverty, we utilize the absolute poverty line to generate binary dependent variables: the probability of remaining in poverty for previously poor households and the probability of falling into poverty for non-poor households. 

Our empirical framework utilizes region-specific logistic panel regressions to analyze the influence of becoming self-employed on these probabilities. We distinguish between opportunity-driven and necessity-driven self-employment; since motivational data were only available in the final wave, we approximate opportunity-driven self-employment using instrumental variables—specifically, the scale of initial investment and the number of hired non-family employees. We control for external shocks (categorized as individual, natural, and economic) and household asset endowments, including human, social, natural, and financial capital. This approach allows us to evaluate how the interaction between household motivations and regional economic environments—such as infrastructure quality and market proximity—shapes the likelihood of escaping poverty.
