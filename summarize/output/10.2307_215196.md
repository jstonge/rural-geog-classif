# 10.2307_215196

This study examines the historical and spatial distribution of the black population in southeastern Florida, focusing on the eastern portions of Palm Beach, Broward, and Dade counties. I trace the settlement patterns of blacks and their relationship to white populations from the post-Reconstruction era through the post-civil-rights period to explain the evolution of racial locations. Utilizing 1990 U.S. Census data, I analyze the distribution of the population across 630 census tracts, omitting twelve outlying tracts in Palm Beach County to focus on the regional ecumene. To test the accuracy of "belt" or "corridor" descriptions of black residential areas, I employ a zonal and transect analysis in Broward and Palm Beach counties, aggregating 364 census tracts into five north-south zones and twenty east-west transects. This methodology allows for the identification of population maxima and minima across a grid of one hundred cells. Finally, I apply the classical central-place model as an analytical device to demonstrate how racial bias and restricted mobility created separate sets of central-place hexagons for black residents, resulting in fragmented, disconnected communities separated by commercial districts or white residential zones.
