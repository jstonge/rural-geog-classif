# 10.2307_215199

This study examines the environmental and political history of the prickly pear cactus infestation in eastern Australia from 1880 to 1940. Utilizing a historical-geographical approach, I analyze the diffusion of the pest across Queensland and New South Wales, focusing on the interaction between climatic and edaphic characteristics—specifically subhumid regions with bentonitic soils—and human-mediated dispersal. The methodology relies on the synthesis of contemporary survey data, royal commission reports, and historical estimates to map the exponential growth and eventual collapse of the infestation, which at its peak rendered approximately 250,000 square kilometers of rural land useless. 

I evaluate the efficacy of governmental responses by categorizing interventions into indirect policies, such as land-tenure legislation and coercive leasehold regulations, and direct policies, including mechanical clearance, chemical poisoning, and the extermination of seed-dispersing native fauna. By tracing a pattern of "reactive escalation," I argue that early state-government strategies were uncoordinated and haphazard, often unwittingly promoting the spread of the pest. The analysis concludes by assessing the transition toward biological control, specifically the introduction of *Cactoblastis cactorum*, weighing the role of cohesive policy against opportunistic discovery in the eventual eradication of the menace.
