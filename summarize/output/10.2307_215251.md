# 10.2307_215251

To provide a model of urban tourism for small Caribbean islands—defined as those with populations under 400,000—I draw upon empirical evidence from eight principal port cities: St. John's (Antigua), Nassau (Bahamas), Bridgetown (Barbados), Roseau (Dominica), Castries (St. Lucia), Plymouth (Montserrat), St. George's (Grenada), and Kingstown (St. Vincent). My approach involves analyzing the spatial evolution of these centers from a "pretourism" phase, characterized by a lack of autonomous tourism structures, to a mature phase of development. By examining the intersection of cruise-ship activity, stayover tourism, and urban morphology, I develop a zonation model that depicts the geographical distribution of tourist activity. This model identifies five distinct zones: a specialized tourist zone adjacent to the docks, the central business district (further divided into transitional and peripheral areas), local neighborhoods, the resort strip, and the rural periphery. The analysis focuses on variables such as distance-decay patterns of tourist movement, the role of physical barriers, and the functional differentiation between retail-focused excursionist zones and accommodation-focused resort strips to generalize the patterns of urban tourism space across the region.
