To examine geographical variations in fertility rates across Africa and identify the underlying influences of the fertility transition, I employ a spatial-temporal framework based on demographic transition theory. Using country-level data from 1980 and 1993, I conduct a multivariate analysis to assess spatial variations and changes in fertility. The study area is divided into five regions—north, west, east, middle, and south—to account for differing sociocultural and economic characteristics. I utilize six measures of fertility as dependent variables, including crude birthrates and total fertility rates, and examine fourteen independent variables categorized as socioeconomic (e.g., GNP, primary-school enrollment, urban population percentage, and the Human Development Index for females) and demographic (e.g., infant mortality rates and contraceptive prevalence). To determine the levels of regional variation and account for observed spatial patterns, I employ a suite of quantitative techniques, including pairwise t-tests for means, analysis of variance (ANOVA), correlation analysis, and stepwise multiple-regression. These methods are used to operationalize a causal model where socioeconomic development is hypothesized as the primary driver of fertility decline.
