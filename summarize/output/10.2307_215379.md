This study examines the relationship between global market participation and household vulnerability to natural hazards in rural Western Samoa following Hurricane Ofa in February 1990. Utilizing a case study approach, I conducted fieldwork from April to August 1990 in two villages on the island of Savaii: Fusi, which had a longer history of market integration and higher rates of wage labor, and Vaipua, which was less integrated. To assess vulnerability and the persistence of the "moral economy," I employed a stratified sampling method, selecting fifteen households per village across high, average, and low socioeconomic categories. Data collection involved household interviews, land-use surveys, and general observations. Specifically, I conducted dietary surveys in May to track staple food consumption and a subsequent survey in June to quantify nonmonetary food gifts given and received. Additionally, a village-wide census from May through July was used to evaluate hurricane damage and the pace of repairs. By analyzing variables such as crop specialization (specifically taro versus coconut), the frequency of reciprocal food exchanges, and the reliance on imported staples, I sought to determine whether incorporation into the global economy had eroded traditional social coping mechanisms and increased the vulnerability of marginal households.
