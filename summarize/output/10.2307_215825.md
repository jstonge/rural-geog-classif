This study analyzes the territorial dimensions of the Shining Path insurgency in Peru from its inception in 1980 through the early 1990s. I employ a conceptual framework based on a three-stage military-political process—mobile warfare, guerrilla warfare, and the establishment of a territorial-based insurgent state—to evaluate the movement's progress toward statehood. The analysis focuses on the spatial evolution of the conflict, tracing the insurgency's origins in the southern Andean departments of Ayacucho and Apurimac and its subsequent expansion into the central Andes, the tropical rain forests of the upper Huallaga Valley, the southern altiplano of Puno, and the urban environment of Lima. 

To map these geographical dimensions, I utilize a combination of secondary data sources, including government declarations of states of emergency, reports from human rights organizations, and academic literature on regional socio-economics. Key variables analyzed include the exploitation of specific ecological zones—such as the high-altitude *puna* for tactical mobility—and the intersection of insurgent strategy with local economic structures, specifically the coca-producing economy of the Huallaga Valley. By examining the establishment of "liberated zones" and the imposition of alternative governance structures, I assess the insurgents' ability to maintain operations across diverse natural and social environments.
