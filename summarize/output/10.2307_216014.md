# 10.2307_216014

We examine the potential of beekeeping as a sustainable, low-cost strategy for promoting local self-reliance and supplementing incomes in rural southern Africa. Our approach integrates a broad regional analysis of the Southern African Development Community (SADC) with a specific case study of the Bondolfi Beekeeper's Association in Zimbabwe. We first explore the theoretical shift toward bottom-up development and indigenous technical knowledge, evaluating how beekeeping aligns with these self-reliance frameworks. We then synthesize existing literature and historical data to document traditional honey-hunting and beekeeping practices, specifically the use of bark hives in the savanna regions of Zimbabwe, Zambia, Tanzania, and Mozambique. Our analysis focuses on the biological characteristics of the *Apis mellifera scutellata* race and evaluates the socioeconomic variables associated with the practice, including gender empowerment, risk avoidance, and environmental impact. By contrasting the advantages of indigenous methods with the constraints of environmental degradation and market access, we assess the effectiveness of limited, non-dictatorial support from aid agencies in enhancing traditional productivity without undermining local autonomy.
