We employ a case study approach centered on the commune of Narpes in South Ostrobothnia, Finland, to examine the evolution of greenhouse horticulture as a mechanism for local economic adjustment. By analyzing the development of this agricultural specialization from its inception in 1916 through 1995, we seek to provide empirical evidence for an inductive theory of economic transition in depressed rural areas. Our analysis draws upon a variety of data sources, including historical accounts of early innovators, regional atlases, and statistical data on the number of greenhouses and total area under glass across thirty villages. We track key variables such as the diffusion of cultivation practices, shifts in building materials and heating methods, and the transition from small-scale operations to consolidated, modern structures driven by economies of scale and distant market demands. Furthermore, we compare the standard of living and income differentials between greenhouse cultivators and traditional farmers. This empirical evidence is then used to construct a theoretical model of regional agrarian change, illustrating how specialized industrial communities emerge in response to agricultural decline and population pressure.
