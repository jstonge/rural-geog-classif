# 10.2307_216062

In this study, we examine changes in the numbers and distribution of the population identified as Indian by the United States Bureau of the Census to determine their geography at the end of the twentieth century. Our analysis relies primarily on longitudinal census data spanning from 1890 to 1990, with a particular focus on the period between 1950 and 1990 to track shifts in regional concentration and urbanization. We evaluate population patterns across multiple spatial scales, including the national level, four census regions, nine census divisions, and individual states. 

Key variables in our analysis include total population counts, growth rates, and the proportion of residents living in urban areas versus recognized Indian lands—categorized as reservations, trust lands, tribal jurisdictional statistical areas (TJSAs), tribal designated statistical areas (TDSAs), and Alaskan national village statistical areas (ANVSAs). We specifically investigate the impact of shifting census methodologies, notably the transition to self-identification in 1960, and the influence of the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) relocation program on migration patterns. By comparing absolute numerical increases with relative growth rates across "Indian States" and non-Indian States, we seek to differentiate between growth driven by natural increase, migration, and changes in ethnic self-identification.
