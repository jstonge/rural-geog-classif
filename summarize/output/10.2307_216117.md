I examine the Municipal Pole for Agroforestry Production (Polo Municipal de Produção Agroflorestal) in Rio Branco, Acre, as a case study for addressing the socioeconomic challenges of rapid urbanization in western Amazonia. My research focuses on the initial project site in Floresta, an agrosilvopastoral system established in 1993 on degraded cattle pasture to resettle rural migrants and slum dwellers. To evaluate the project's impact on food security and self-determination, I conducted field research during the summer of 1995, which included interviewing forty-four of the forty-eight participating families. I analyzed key variables such as household demographics, lot sizes (averaging 3.6 hectares), and crop diversity, documenting a total of thirty-one perennial and twenty-eight annual species. By comparing current household conditions—specifically employment status and home ownership—with the participants' prior urban circumstances, I assess the viability of peri-urban agroforestry as a sustainable alternative to traditional low-income housing initiatives and government-directed colonization projects in the Brazilian Amazon.
