# 10.2307_216155

Drawing upon a political-ecological framework, I examine rural incendiarism not as mere pyromania, but as a tool of protest and a "weapon of the weak" utilized by disempowered agrarian populations. Through a comparative analysis of three distinct case studies—eighteenth- and nineteenth-century England, colonial Algeria, and the southern United States—I illustrate how the destructive use of fire emerges when established agrarian orders are disrupted by enclosure, colonial expropriation, or the rise of industrial forestry. My approach integrates historical literature, sociological reports, and geographical data to identify spatial and temporal patterns of arson, linking these occurrences to variables such as land tenure shifts, the privatization of commons, and the erosion of traditional subsistence rights. In the English context, I trace the geography of "rick burning" across specific cereal-farming counties; in Algeria, I analyze the clash between indigenous Mediterranean fire ecologies and French colonial forest codes; and in the American South, I evaluate the persistence of "spite burnings" against corporate landownership. By synthesizing these diverse examples, I argue that incendiarism represents a refractory response to the imposition of restrictive land-use policies and the transition from subsistence-based to capitalist space-economies.
