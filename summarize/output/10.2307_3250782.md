To examine the evolution of urban systems on the frontier of the Ecuadorian Amazon (the Oriente), we employ a comparative case-study approach focusing on the region's two largest boom towns, Puyo and Nueva Loja. Our methodology integrates a review of existing literature on South American frontier urbanization—specifically the populist and corporatist models proposed by Browder and Godfrey—with detailed analyses of local development factors. We analyze the interaction between exogenous forces, such as national colonization laws, international oil demand, and geopolitical conflicts with Peru, and endogenous characteristics, including geographical location and resource endowment. To assess the economic structure of these settlements, we utilize field surveys of commercial establishments and draw upon demographic data from the National Institute of Statistics and Census (INEC). By comparing the development of these pioneer towns with those in the coastal plain (the Costa) and the Brazilian Amazon, we evaluate the roles of rural-urban migration, local primacy, and spatial interaction. This approach allows us to characterize the Oriente's urban network as a series of specialized or transitional peripheries that are often more dependent on public-service employment and small-scale commerce than on integrated regional economic strength.
