We examine the production of the symbolic economy in Leavenworth, Washington, a rural community that transitioned from a declining resource-based economy to a thriving "theme town" by adopting a Bavarian identity in the 1960s. Drawing on the "cultural turn" in tourism research and the geography of consumption, we analyze how the town's built environment was radically transformed into a consumable landscape. Our approach utilizes a historical and conceptual framework to explore the material and symbolic issues surrounding this metamorphosis, specifically focusing on the role of "boosterism" and the "growth machine" in rural economic development. We evaluate the town's transformation through four interrelated conceptual lenses: the emulation of other successful theme towns, the construction of authenticity, the creation of visual difference, and the strategies of place marketing. By synthesizing historical accounts, local press archives, and observations of the physical landscape, we investigate how these strategies were employed to create an alluring destination and consider the subsequent implications for the identity and daily lives of Leavenworth's residents.
