# 10.2307_3250830

I conducted fieldwork from June 1999 to June 2000 in the villages of Muraviovka and Kuropatino, agricultural communities bordering the Muraviovka Nature Park in Russia's Amur Province. My research focused on evaluating the extent to which NGO-sponsored environmental education programs—specifically a conservation summer camp for local children—modified residents' attitudes and behaviors regarding the park, including activities such as poaching and fire setting. I employed a qualitative approach centered on semistructured interviews conducted in Russian. To mitigate the initial lack of candor and the suspicion associated with my role as an American researcher, I interviewed most respondents at least twice, framing questions differently over time to build rapport. These interviews took place across various settings, including private homes, workplaces, and social gatherings. A significant methodological challenge involved the pervasive role of alcohol in rural Russian social life; I observed how varying levels of alcohol consumption influenced the reliability and tone of informant responses, ranging from sober wariness to intoxicated braggadocio or candid criticism. To manage this, I established a personal threshold, arbitrarily ceasing note-taking after the fourth round of toasts during evening interviews.
