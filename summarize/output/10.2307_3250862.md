To evaluate the trajectory of the "rural revolution" in eastern North Carolina, I employ a longitudinal analysis of the region's transition from an agrarian to an industrial and mixed economy. Focusing on the North Carolina Coastal Plain—specifically the thirty-eight counties designated as rural by the 1990 census—I examine the shift in employment patterns from the 1960s through the late 1990s. To provide a granular assessment of these structural changes, I narrow my focus to a seven-county study area (Edgecombe, Greene, Lenoir, Nash, Pitt, Wayne, and Wilson) that served as the basis for previous research by Hart and Chestang in 1978.

My methodology integrates quantitative data from the North Carolina Employment Security Commission and the U.S. Bureau of the Census to track key variables, including manufacturing employment levels, wage structures, and plant-ownership patterns. I supplement this statistical analysis with qualitative case illustrations of two contrasting firms: Hampton Industries, representing the decline of domestic apparel manufacturing due to offshore competition, and Fullarton Computer Industries, representing the role of foreign direct investment. By synthesizing regional employment trends with specific corporate histories, I analyze how globalized manufacturing and the rise of service-based economies have reshaped the competitive advantage of this rural landscape.
