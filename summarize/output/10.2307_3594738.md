We compared plant and wildlife communities across the three principal rural land uses in the Mountain West: public protected areas, private-land livestock ranches, and exurban developments. Our study was conducted from May through August during 2000 and 2001 in the shrubsteppe and mixed-grass prairie foothills of the Front Range of the Rocky Mountains in northern Larimer County, Colorado. To reduce natural variability, we restricted our site selection to areas with similar soil types, elevations (1,740 to 2,200 meters), and plant communities, ensuring each site comprised at least 1,000 hectares to minimize the influence of surrounding land uses. Using orthophoto-quadrangle and plat maps for site identification, we randomly selected ninety-three points to survey songbirds and mammalian carnivores, and sixty-nine points to sample the plant community. We employed 75-meter fixed-radius point counts for songbirds, scent stations for mammalian carnivores, and 20-by-50-centimeter quadrats for plants. These methods allowed us to evaluate species richness, density, and cover, specifically contrasting native and nonnative species across the gradient of intensifying rural land use.
