In this study, I examine traditional databases and methods used to measure land-use dynamics to identify limitations in their utility for detailing changes at the urban fringe. I evaluate several commonly available data sources, including the U.S. Census of Agriculture, the National Resources Inventory (NRI), the USGS Land Use and Land Cover data set, and the National Land Cover Database (NLCD), as well as population and housing data from the U.S. Census. By comparing these sources—specifically through a case study in Colorado—I demonstrate how coarse analytical units, such as counties and Metropolitan Statistical Areas (MSAs), often result in the overbounding and underbounding of developed areas. I further analyze the degradation of the relationship between land cover and land use at lower intensities, noting that satellite-derived classifications frequently underestimate low-density exurban settlement. To address these deficiencies, I describe a method to map development patterns that captures change along the full urban-to-rural gradient, allowing for a more precise estimation of the extent and rate of land-use conversion beyond the urban fringe. Using this approach, I analyze historical and current trends from 1960 to 2000 across the conterminous United States, focusing on housing density as a key variable to track the encroachment of settlement on agricultural and natural resource lands.
