# 10.2307_4140919

To explore the ways in which urban-dwelling Hispanos incorporate their deep emotional attachment for rural areas into city life, I employed a qualitative landscape analysis centered on the upper Rio Grande region. Over seven years of fieldwork and informal interviews conducted throughout the rural villages and urban centers of New Mexico and southern Colorado, I amassed evidence of the elastic links between rural and urban places. Between the summers of 1998 and 1999 and the fall of 2000, I systematically analyzed the landscapes of Albuquerque, Española, Santa Fe, and Pueblo. My field observations of public space-places focused specifically on Zip-code areas where, according to 1990 U.S. census data, a plurality of residents were Hispanos. To verify the accuracy of these observations, I conducted numerous informal interviews with a diverse range of key informants, including housewives, retired couples, business owners, government officials, and local scholars. These findings were documented through extensive daily notes, photographs, and sketches, allowing me to synthesize the information and identify four primary cultural expressions—murals, mortuaries, music, and *acequias* (irrigation ditches)—that manifest rural place attachment within the urban fabric.
