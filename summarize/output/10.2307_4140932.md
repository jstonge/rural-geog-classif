I examine the forces contributing to agricultural expansion southwest of La Crete in northern Alberta's Peace River district, describing the transformation of the "Blue Hills" area from a forested plain to a cultivated landscape. My methodological approach integrates the analysis of provincial government records, including correspondence between the Agricultural Development Committee (ADC) and the Ministry of Public Lands, survey data on farmer demand, and land-selection draw results from 1980 to 1984. To quantify the magnitude of land-use change from 1970 through 2002, I developed a series of maps based on evidence obtained from aerial photographs, topographic sheets, and extensive fieldwork conducted between 1998 and 2001. These data sources allow me to track key variables such as the total acreage of cleared land, the proliferation of road networks, and the increase in occupied dwellings. Additionally, I incorporate qualitative insights from community members and historical accounts to assess how traditional Mennonite cultural values influenced land-parcel sizing and settlement patterns. By synthesizing these spatial and archival data, I evaluate the interplay between local initiative and provincial policy in the development of this marginal agricultural frontier.
